\title{Negation in Mano}
\abstract{This paper describes the negation system in Mano, a Southern Mande language. Negation in declarative, non-verbal and non-main clauses in Mano is asymmetric both because negation involves a change in the TAM constructions (constructional asymmetry) and because the inventory of negative constructions is reduced with respect to the inventory of affirmative constructions (paradigmatic asymmetry). In non-declarative constructions, several negative constructions do not have affirmative counterparts, resulting in paradigmatic asymmetry. Mano indefinite quantifiers distinguish between full and partial negation. Another unusual feature is a dedicated negative particle \textit{gɔ̰̀á̰̰} which is used only in conditional constructions. Mano does not have any negative derivation or negative coordinators and just one negative polarity item, \textit{féì} ‘nothing’.

\keywords{negation, Mano, Mande, asymmetric negation, quantifiers}}

\section{The language}\label{sec:mano:1}

Mano (or mááwè, Ethnologue: Maan, ISO: \href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/mev}{mev}, Glottocode: \href{https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/mann1248}{mann1248})  counts 370~000 speakers in Guinea and Liberia. Mano belongs to the Southern branch of the Mande language family, which has a debated status within the Niger-Congo phylum (\citealt{Vydrine2016}; \citealt{DimmendaalStorch2016}). 

Mano is spoken in South-Eastern Guinea and Northern Liberia. Guinean Mano counts about 66~000 speakers being one of 34 indigenous languages spoken in Guinea (\citealt{BahBangoura2017}). In Liberia, Mano is spoken by about 305~000 people, it is the fifth, out of 27, most spoken indigenous language of the country. In Guinea, three Mano dialects are distinguished: Zaan (\textit{zà̰à̰}), the easternmost dialect spoken around the Bossou town, Maa (\textit{màá}), the central dialect spoken in the city of Nzérékoré and to the south of it, and Kpeinson (\textit{kpéŋ́sɔ̰̀}), the Western dialect spoken around the Diécké town. In Liberia, there are also three dialects: the Northern dialect, Maalaa (\textit{máá lāā}), spoken around Sanniquellie, the Central dialect, Maazein (\textit{máá zè\'{ŋ}}) spoken around Ganta, and the Southern dialect, Maabei (\textit{máá bèí}), spoken around Saklepea. It is likely that there are several other varieties spoken to the south of Saklepea. Guinean and Liberian dialects are mutually understandable. While there is some variation between them, especially in the domain of phonology and lexicon \citep{Khachaturyan2018}, there is much less variation in morphosyntax and no attested variation in the negation system, which is the main topic of this paper. Most of the data is drawn from the central Guinean dialect, Maa.

The work on Mano is based on fifteen months of fieldwork conducted by the author between 2009 and 2019. Many examples are drawn from natural examples from a spoken corpus, but a small portion is elicited, all examples are marked correspondingly (nat. and el.). 
Some examples coming from the Bible translation \citep{UBS1978} are marked (UBS) and were checked with a native speaker for grammaticality and natural sounding. A grammar can be found in \citet{Khachaturyan2015}, some typological findings were exposed in \citet{Khachaturyan2014grammaire,Khachaturyan2020}. The paper is based on the questionnaire
% \lv{@LSP: please cross-ref to the questionnaire as a chapter in the book}
provided by the editors of this volume \parencitetv{chapters/questionnaire} and follows its structure.

Mano is characterized by a fixed S-O-V-X word order, where X stands for all oblique arguments and adverbs. The word order in the noun phrase is Gen – N – Adj, followed by number words, quantifiers, and demonstratives, respectively. 
While Mano segmental morphology is relatively poor, it has a rich system of tonal morphemes, especially on the verb and on the auxiliary (see Appendices with sample paradigms and \sectref{sec:mano:2.1} for a discussion of the use of the forms).

\section{Clausal negation}\label{sec:mano:2}
\subsection{Standard negation}\label{sec:mano:2.1}

Mano predications are divided into two major types: predicator clauses, where the head is an invariable predicator, and constructions with auxiliaries which function as a site of subject indexation. The word order in the latter is \textsc{S-aux-O-V,} where \textsc{aux} is an auxiliary. 
The subject NP is optional, so the subject may be expressed solely by a person and number index on the auxiliary. The list of auxiliaries is given in the Appendix (\tabref{tab:mano:app:1}). When the direct object is expressed by a \textsc{3sg} pronoun, a portmanteau form of the auxiliary is typically used, although a combination of a non-portmanteau form and a pronoun is also possible; such portmanteau forms are marked with >3\textsc{sg} \REF{ex:mano:1}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:1}
    \gll  māà mā.   \\
     \textsc{1sg.prf>3sg} understand   \\
    \glt ‘I have understood it.’ (nat.)
\z

The auxiliaries are not the only markers of TAM.
As is typical for languages of Sub-Saharan Africa \citep[343]{Welmers1973}, in Mano, TAM values, including negation, are not expressed by specific markers: they are rather expressed by a construction as a whole which contains auxiliaries together with particles (such as the negative particle \textit{gbā} used in the negative aorist \REF{ex:mano:5}; \textit{nɛ́ŋ̀ } ‘not yet’ used in the negative perfect \REF{ex:mano:7}; or \textit{dō} ‘once, never’ used in the affirmative and negative experiential (\ref{ex:mano:12}, \ref{ex:mano:13})) and verbs in a particular morphological form (for a sample of verbal forms, see Appendix, \tabref{tab:mano:app:2}).

In declaratives, there is only one negative auxiliary series (glossed by \textsc{neg}) which, combined with different grammatical means, is used to form constructions of negative imperfective \REF{ex:mano:3}, aorist \REF{ex:mano:5}, and perfect \REF{ex:mano:7}, while in the affirmative zone, there are three dedicated auxiliaries used for these purposes. Thus, affirmative imperfective is formed with the imperfective auxiliary \REF{ex:mano:2}, affirmative aorist is formed with the past auxiliary \REF{ex:mano:4} and affirmative perfect is formed with the perfect auxiliary \REF{ex:mano:6}. 

\ea\label{ex:mano:2}Affirmative imperfective\\
    \gll \'{ŋ}\`{ŋ} nàà à ká.\\
         \textsc{1sg.ipfv} love\textsc{:ipfv} 3\textsc{sg} with    \\
    \glt ‘I want that.’ (nat.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:3}Negative imperfective\\
    \gll \`{ŋ}\'{ŋ} nāā nɛ̀ŋwɔ̀ gèà ká.\\
         1\textsc{sg.neg} love lie say.\textsc{ger} with   \\
    \glt ‘I don’t like lying.’ (nat.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:4}Affirmative aorist\\
    \gll ē túó.\\
         \textsc{3sg.pst} be.afraid     \\
    \glt ‘She got scared.’ (nat.)
\z

\newpage
\ea\label{ex:mano:5}Negative aorist\\
    \gll \`{ŋ}\'{ŋ} gbā wìì zɛ̄.\\
         \textsc{1sg.neg} \textsc{neg} animal kill    \\
    \glt ‘I did not kill an animal.’ (nat.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:6}Affirmative perfect\\
    \gll āà kā lɔ́ kā sɔ̄ŋ́ gbùò ká. \\
         \textsc{3sg.prf} \textsc{2pl} buy \textsc{2pl} price big with \\
    \glt ‘He has bought you with a big price.’ (nat.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:7}Negative perfect (‚not yet‘)\\
    \gll Zɛ́zú lɛ̀ɛ́ nū nɛ́ŋ̀ . \\
     Jesus 3\textsc{sg.neg} come yet    \\
    \glt ‘Jesus has not come yet.’ (nat.)
\z

In addition to the aforementioned basic constructions, Mano has future and imperfect constructions (affirmative and negative). The future construction derives from the imperfective construction: the auxiliary verb \textit{ló} ‘go’ is put in the imperfective, followed by the content verb in the gerund form (\ref{ex:mano:8}--\ref{ex:mano:9}). The imperfect derives from the aorist construction: the auxiliary verb \textit{kɛ̄ } ‘be, do’ is put in the aorist, followed by the content verb in the infinitive (\ref{ex:mano:10}--\ref{ex:mano:11}). 

\ea\label{ex:mano:8}Affirmative future\\
    \gll lɛ́ɛ̀ lō yɛ́-ɛ̀ ē zí.   \\
         3\textsc{sg.ipfv} go:\textsc{ipfv} break-\textsc{ger} \textsc{3sg.refl} back   \\
    \glt \textsc{‘}He will come back.’ (nat.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:9}Negative future\\
    \gll lɛ̀ɛ́ ló wàā ɓèlà yī.   \\
    3\textsc{sg.neg} go suffering eat.\textsc{ger} there \\
    \glt ‘She will not suffer there.’ (nat.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:10}Affirmative imperfect\\
    \gll ɲɛ́nɛ́ ē kɛ̄ gá̰á̰-pɛ̀lɛ̀.\\
     sun 3\textsc{sg.pst} do drag-\textsc{inf}    \\
    \glt ‘The day (of the festivities) was approaching.’ (nat.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:11}Negative imperfect\\
    \gll lɛ̀ɛ́ gbā kɛ̄ ló-pɛ̀lɛ̀ ē mɛ̀ŋ́. \\
         3\textsc{sg.neg} \textsc{neg} do go-\textsc{inf} \textsc{3sg.refl} behind  \\
    \glt ‘He was not going back.’ (nat.)
\z

The experiential affirmative construction is formed by adding the particle \textit{dō} ‘once’ to the perfect construction \REF{ex:mano:12}. The negative experiential construction is formed by replacing the particle \textit{nɛ́ŋ̀ } ‘not yet’ with \textit{dō} ‘once’ in the negative perfect construction \REF{ex:mano:13}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:12}Affirmative experiential\\
    \gll wāà gɛ̰̀ dō.     \\
         \textsc{3pl.prf>3sg} see once     \\
    \glt ‘They have once seen it.’ (nat.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:13}Negative experiential\\
    \gll kòó mī dò gɛ̰̀ dō zèē.  \\
         \textsc{1pl.neg} person \textsc{indf} see once here  \\
    \glt ‘We have never seen anyone here.’ (nat.)
\z

The final declarative construction is prospective \REF{ex:mano:14}, which cannot be negated.

\ea\label{ex:mano:14}Prospective\\
    \gll yèlè é kpílì kɛ̄.    \\
         3\textsc{sg.prosp} \textsc{3sg.cnjv} feast do    \\
    \glt ‘He is going to organize a feast.’ (nat.)
\z


In addition, imperfective and resultative constructions are formed on non-verbal models. Affirmative imperfective is based on the existential auxiliary and affirmative resultative is based on the affirmative predicator \textit{lɛ̄ }, while the negative imperfective and the negative resultative are based on the general stative negator, the copula \textit{wɔ́}. These constructions are properly introduced in \sectref{sec:mano:2.3}.

To conclude this section, negation in Mano is asymmetric (in the sense proposed by \citealt{Miestamo2005}) both because negation involves a change in the TAM constructions (constructional asymmetry) and because the inventory of negative constructions is reduced with respect to the inventory of affirmative constructions (paradigmatic asymmetry). The main marker for standard negation is the negative auxiliary series.

\subsection{Negation in non-declaratives}\label{sec:mano:2.2}

Just like in declarative constructions, in non-declaratives, including directives, Mano employs specific TAM constructions which differ from one another in the choice of the auxiliary (and sometimes in the construction-initial particle), the verb typically remaining in its lexical form. Thus, the imperative and the basic optative constructions employ a subjunctive auxiliary \REF{ex:mano:15}; in the second person singular imperative, the auxiliary is not expressed. The prohibitive construction employs the prohibitive auxiliary series \REF{ex:mano:16}. 

\ea\label{ex:mano:15}Imperative\\
    \gll kà gó sɔ̀ŋ̄ ŋwɔ̀ gɛ̀nɛ̀.   \\
         1\textsc{pl.sbjv} leave character problem:\textsc{cnstr} in.front.of   \\
    \glt ‘Stop behaving badly!’ (lit. ‘Go away from the problem of behavior!’) (UBS)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:16}Prohibitive\\
    \gll ɓáá wéè mā.\\
         \textsc{2pl.proh>3sg} speech hear     \\
    \glt ‘Don’t listen to him!’ (lit. ‘Don’t hear his speech!’) (nat.)
\z

Optative and prohibitive constructions can also be formed with a subjunctive auxiliary together with dedicated optative (\textit{fɔ̰́kɛ̄}, \REF{ex:mano:17}) or prohibitive (\textit{yékɛ̀}, \REF{ex:mano:18}) construction-initial particle. 
The prohibitive particle is also used in purpose constructions (‘so that X doesn’t do Y’).

\ea\label{ex:mano:17}Optative\\
    \gll fɔ̰́kɛ̄ kó ló à píé yīī bà zàá. \\
         \textsc{opt} 1\textsc{pl.cnjv} go \textsc{3sg} at dream in in\\
    \glt ‘We should come to him in the dream.’ (nat.)
\z
{
\ea\label{ex:mano:18}Prohibitive\\
    \gll yékɛ̀ ó ló ō mɛ̀ŋ́ Hɛ́lɔ̀ píé. \\
         \textsc{proh} \textsc{3pl.cnjv} go \textsc{3pl} behind Herod at \\
    \glt ‘They should not return to Herod.’ (UBS)
\z}

Another prohibitive construction is formed with a paraphrase, literally meaning ‘I am not telling you to do X’ Its exact pragmatic value is unknown, however, because of is non-explicit form, it could be connected to the politeness function of hedging and avoiding direct commands \REF{ex:mano:19}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:19}Prohibitive\\
    \gll \`{ŋ}\'{ŋ} gèē ká gbóó ɓō!\\
         1\textsc{sg.neg>3sg} say 2\textsc{pl.cnjv} crying implement    \\
    \glt ‘Don’t cry!’ (lit. ‘I am not telling you to cry.’) (nat.)
\z

There are no dedicated hortative constructions, but imperatives, optatives and prohibitives may have a hortative interpretation. Thus, \REF{ex:mano:17} can be read as a mutual encouragement: the spirits of a person’s deceased parents are encouraging one another to come to him in a dream. 

A particular modal construction – the dubitative – is used without an inverse polarity counterpart. It could be interpreted as a negative polarity construction, as it means that an event occurred from the perspective of the subject, but the external observer disagrees that it indeed took place. Thus, the construction expresses a negation, from an outsider’s perspective, of the state of affairs as seen by the protagonist whose point of view is expressed \REF{ex:mano:20}. 

\ea\label{ex:mano:20}Dubitative\\
    \gll àá là wéé-pɛ̀ wɛ̄ yīè mā gbāāɓō.\\
     3\textsc{sg.dub} \textsc{3sg.poss} speak-thing:\textsc{cnstr} \textsc{dem} good hear now\\
    \glt ‘She thought that she heard her beautiful musical instrument (but in fact it was a fish).’ (nat.)
\z


To sum up, Mano non-declaratives show constructional asymmetry patterns: there is a negative optative periphrastic without an affirmative counterpart; there is also a dubitative construction which is also implicitly negative. Non-declarative clauses are summarized in \tabref{tab:mano:2}.

\subsection{Negation in non-verbal predications}\label{sec:mano:2.3}

Most stative non-verbal predications in the present are formed with an existential auxiliary.\footnote{More details in non-verbal predication in Mano can be found in \citet{KhachaturyanForthcoming}.} In contrast to other auxiliary constructions, there is no verb in non-verbal constructions with existential auxiliaries \REF{ex:mano:21}. In the past, however, an overt verb \textit{kɛ̄} ‘be’ is used as part of the aorist construction \REF{ex:mano:22}. The verb can also be used in the sense ‘become’, used in non-stative functions and put in the perfect \REF{ex:mano:23} or imperfective construction \REF{ex:mano:24}. Examples below illustrate the proper inclusion function, but the same patterns can apply to other functions expressed by non-verbal construction listed further below in this section.

\ea\label{ex:mano:21}
    \gll à dàā lɛ̄ dɔ̄mì ká.\\
         \textsc{3sg} father \textsc{3sg.ex} chief with\\
    \glt ‘His father is a chief.’ (el.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:22}
    \gll à dàā ē kɛ̄ dɔ̄mì ká.\\
         \textsc{3sg} father \textsc{3sg.pst} be chief with\\
    \glt ‘His father was a chief.’ (nat.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:23}
    \gll à dàā āà kɛ̄ dɔ̄mì ká.\\
         \textsc{3sg} father \textsc{3sg.prf} be chief with\\
    \glt ‘His father has become a chief.’ (el.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:24}
    \gll à dàā lɛ́ɛ̀ kɛ̀ dɔ̄mì ká.\\
         \textsc{3sg} father \textsc{3sg.ipfv} be:\textsc{ipfv}  chief with\\
    \glt ‘His father becomes a chief.’ (el.)
\z

All negative stative non-verbal predications in the present are formed with a general negative stative copula \textit{wɔ́} \REF{ex:mano:27} \citep{Veselinova2013neg.exist}. Non{}-stative and/or non-present non-verbal predications are negated according to the models of standard negation given in \sectref{sec:mano:2.1}. For example, negative stative predication in the past is formed like negative aorist \REF{ex:mano:25}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:25}
    \gll à dàā lɛ̀ɛ́ gbā kɛ̄ dɔ̄mì ká.\\
         \textsc{3sg} father \textsc{3sg.neg} \textsc{neg} be  chief with\\
    \glt ‘His father was not a chief.’ (el.)
\z

Stative non-verbal constructions as listed in \citet{Veselinova2014}, that is, equation, proper inclusion (also called classification, \citealt{Haspelmath2021}), attribution, locative and possessive predications are given below with their affirmative and negative counterparts (\ref{ex:mano:2627}--\ref{ex:mano:3435}).

\ea\label{ex:mano:2627} Equation\\
    \ea\label{ex:mano:26}
        \gll gɔ̰́ wɛ̄ lɛ̄ ŋ̄ dɛ̰̄ ká  \\
             man:\textsc{h} \textsc{dem} \textsc{3sg.ex} \textsc{1sg} husband with  \\
        \glt ‘This man is my husband.’ (el.)
    \ex\label{ex:mano:27}
        \gll gɔ̰́ wɛ̄ wɔ́ ŋ̄ dɛ̰̄ ká  \\
             man\textsc{:h} \textsc{dem} \textsc{cop.neg} \textsc{1sg} husband with  \\
        \glt ‘This man is not my husband.’ (el.)
    \z
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:2829} Proper inclusion  \\
    \ea\label{ex:mano:28} 
    \gll Sèé lɛ̄ kàlàmɔ́ɔ̀ ká.    \\
         Se \textsc{3sg.ex} teacher with    \\
    \glt ‘Se is a teacher.’ (el.)
    \ex\label{ex:mano:29}
    \gll Sèé wɔ́ kàlàmɔ́ɔ̀ ká.    \\
         Se \textsc{cop.neg} teacher with    \\
    \glt ‘Se is not a teacher.’ (el.)
\z\z 

\ea\label{ex:mano:3031} Attribution\\
\ea\label{ex:mano:30}
    \gll gɔ̰́ā̰nɛ́ɛ́ vɔ̀ ō tííkpé.    \\
         cat \textsc{pl} 3\textsc{pl.ex} small    \\
    \glt ‘Cats are small.’ (el.)
\ex\label{ex:mano:31}
    \gll gɔ̰́ā̰nɛ́ɛ́ vɔ̀ wɔ́ tííkpé.    \\
         cat \textsc{pl} \textsc{cop.neg} small    \\
    \glt ‘Cats are not small.’ (el.)
\z\z
\newpage
\ea\label{ex:mano:3233} Location\\
\ea\label{ex:mano:32}
    \gll ī kɛ́ì.     \\
         2\textsc{sg.ex} house.\textsc{loc}     \\
    \glt ‘You are in the room.’ (el.)
\ex\label{ex:mano:33}
    \gll ɓī wɔ́ kɛ́ì.     \\
         \textsc{2sg.emph} \textsc{cop.neg} house.\textsc{loc}     \\
    \glt ‘You are not in the room.’ (el.)
\z\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:3435} Possession\\
\ea\label{ex:mano:34} 
    \gll gɔ̰́ā̰nɛ́ɛ́ vɔ̀ ō ŋ̄ kɛ̀lɛ̀.   \\

    \glt ‘I have cats.’ (lit. ‘Cats are in my hand.’) (el.)
\ex\label{ex:mano:35}
    \gll gɔ̰́ā̰nɛ́ɛ́ vɔ̀ wɔ́ ŋ̄ kɛ̀lɛ̀.   \\
         cat \textsc{pl} \textsc{cop.neg} \textsc{1sg} hand   \\
    \glt ‘I don’t have cats’ (lit. ‘Cats aren’t in my hand.’) (el.)
\z\z 

Proper inclusion construction is formed, in addition to the argument and the auxiliary (or the copula), with a postpositional phrase with a comitative postposition \textit{ká}
expressing the set into which the subject NP is included. Such constructions have the predicator \textit{lɛ́ } instead of the existential auxiliary (more details on the predicator can be found below). The difference between these two types of proper inclusion constructions most likely pertains to the level of information structure, as reflected in the translation of \REF{ex:mano:36} and \REF{ex:mano:37}.          person:\textsc{h} \textsc{dem.vis} \textsc{3sg.ex} handkerchief\textsc{[fr]} with\\
    \glt ‘This thing (lit. person) is a handkerchief.’ (el.)
\z

In addition to the constructions predicating location, classification, or an attribute of a referent, Mano also has constructions predicating existence. These constructions have functions of predicating existence, the function dubbed \textit{hyparctic} by \citet{Haspelmath2021}. Like other non-verbal constructions, in the affirmative, they are formed with the existential auxiliary \REF{ex:mano:38}, and in the negative \REF{ex:mano:39}, with the copula \textit{wɔ́}. They also contain an existential particle \textit{ɓē{\textasciitilde}wē.}


\ea\label{ex:mano:38}Affirmative hyparctic predication\\
    \gll bīē vɔ̀ ō ɓē.    \\
         elephant \textsc{pl} \textsc{3pl.ex} \textsc{ex}    \\
    \glt \textsc{‘}Elephants exist.’ (el.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:39}Negative hyparctic predication\\
    \gll bīē vɔ̀ wɔ́ ɓē.    \\
         elephant \textsc{pl} \textsc{cop.neg} \textsc{ex}    \\
    \glt ‘Elephants do not exist.’ (el.)
\z

Another type of constructions with only one argument are used for referent identification or presentation \citep{Killian2022}. These two functions have a lot of overlap and cannot be clearly delineated. One of the ways to pull the functions apart is based on the referent’s properties, whether it is physically available at the interactive scene. In identifying constructions, the referent does not need to be physically present. In the affirmative, identifying constructions are formed with the predicator\footnote{A note on terminology. I consider copulas a type of predicators which indicate “the stative link between the two argument positions of a nonverbal clause” \citep[9]{Haspelmath2021}. Only the copula wɔ corresponds to this definition, as it obligatorily has two syntactic arguments, including in identification constructions where semantically, there is only one argument. Predicators \textit{lɛ́, lɛ̄, gɛ̰̀} and \textit{wɔ́} are typically used with only one argument -- the subject, although they may occasionally take a locative argument. For this reason, they are not considered to be copulas.} \textit{lɛ̄ } \REF{ex:mano:40} and in the negative, with the copula \textit{wɔ́} and a postpositional phrase with the postposition \textit{ká} and dummy 3\textsc{sg} pronoun \textit{à} \REF{ex:mano:41}. The 3\textsc{sg} existential auxiliary \textit{lɛ̄ } and the predicator \textit{lɛ̄ } are homonymous, but the predicator is used with arguments of all persons and numbers \REF{ex:mano:40}, while the auxiliary is used only with a \textsc{3sg} argument \REF{ex:mano:28}: the \textsc{3pl} subject requires the \textsc{3pl} auxiliary \textit{ō} \REF{ex:mano:30}. 

\ea\label{ex:mano:40}Affirmative identification\\
    \gll kō gɔ̰̄à̰ vɔ̀  lɛ̄.  \\
        \textsc{1pl} man.\textsc{pl} \textsc{pl} \textsc{ident} \\
    \glt ‘It is us, men.’ (nat.)
\z
\newpage
\ea\label{ex:mano:41}Negative identification\\
    \gll à líésímìà wáá ká.\\
     \textsc{bridg} chief.\textsc{pl} \textsc{cop.neg>3sg} with\\
    \glt ‘It is not the chiefs.’ (nat.)
\z

\textit{Wɔ́} \REF{ex:mano:42} and \textit{gɛ̰̀} \REF{ex:mano:43} are typically used in presentative constructions which serve to orient to physically available referents. \textit{Gɛ̰̀}, deriving from the verb \textit{gɛ̰̀} ‘to see’, is used with objects visible to both the speaker and addressee, while \textit{wɔ́} does not have that restriction. There is no negative equivalent of presentative constructions.

\ea\label{ex:mano:42}Presentative with predicator \textit{wɔ́}\\
    \gll yē wɔ́ ɔ̄.\\
         \textsc{3sg.emph} \textsc{pres} \textsc{dem}\\
    \glt ‘There he is!’ (nat.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:43}Presentative with predicator \textit{gɛ̰̀ }\\
    \gll dìì dōó gɛ̰̀, ɓū wɛ́lɛ́ ɓɔ̀ɔ́ dōó gɛ̰̀.  \\
        cow one \textsc{pres.vis} rice bone bag one \textsc{pres.vis} \\
    \glt ‘Here is one cow, here is one bag of rice.’ (nat.)
\z

Presentative constructions can also be used to predicate location, in which case a locative argument is added after the predicator, as in \REF{ex:mano:44}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:44}Presentative and locational with predicator \textit{wɔ́}\\
    \gll gìtáà bḭ́ḭ́ wɔ́ dìŋ̄ yā.\\
         guitar\textsc{[fr]} image \textsc{pres} there \textsc{dem}\\
    \glt ‘There is the image of the guitar.’ (nat.)
\z 

Predicator \textit{lɛ́ } is used both with physically available and non-available referents and, therefore, for presentation and identification. Its general function is drawing attention to a referent; as examples below and in \sectref{sec:mano:2.4} demonstrate, it can also be used as a focalizer or a relativizer (more on the link between these functions, see \citealt{KhachaturyanOzerov2021}). In example \REF{ex:mano:45}, presentation is implicit as the speaker is referring to the village where he resides and where he is located at the moment of speech.

\ea\label{ex:mano:45} Identification/presentative with predicator \textit{lɛ́}\\
    \gll à kɛ̄ sɛ̀lɛ̀ dìè lɛ́ wɛ̄.\\
         3\textsc{sg} do village\textsc{:cnstr} true \textsc{att} \textsc{dem}    \\
    \glt ‘(We lie a lot), but this is the true village of liers.’ (lit. ‘The true village of doing it is.’) (nat.)
\z

Predicators \textit{lɛ́} and \textit{wɔ́} are obligatorily followed by a demonstrative or a spatial adjunct. Whenever a demonstrative is used, the affirmative predicator \textit{wɔ́} can be distinguished from the negative predicator \textit{wɔ́}, but the examples where \textit{wɔ́} is followed by a locative adjunct are potentially ambiguous between a presentative reading and a negative location reading. 

Presentative and identifying functions can be combined with the hyparctic function, when the existence of an object is predicated at the same time when the referent is presented or identified. In such cases, the predicator \textit{gɛ̰̀} or \textit{lɛ́ } is used, followed by the existential marker \textit{ɓē{\textasciitilde}wē}. The context of example \REF{ex:mano:46} is that a careless driver hits a motorist. He visits him in the hospital, most possibly, for negotiations of damage reimbursement. He brings relatives or friends with him. By uttering \REF{ex:mano:46}, the speaker does not only present the people, but also communicates the very fact that he has brought some people with him. 

\ea\label{ex:mano:46}
    \gll ŋ̀ mīā gɛ̰̀ ɓē. \\
         \textsc{1sg.poss} person.\textsc{pl} \textsc{pres.vis} \textsc{ex}\\
    \glt ‘Here are my people.’ (nat.)
\z

Whether or not the existence of the object demonstrated is predicated depends on the existing presuppositions and the referent’ status in the common knowledge of discourse participants. Thus, simple presentative constructions, such as \REF{ex:mano:45}, can be interpreted as hyparctic, as well. In \REF{ex:mano:45}, the demonstrative can be replaced by the existential marker \textit{ɓē}.

Identifying and presentative predicators (\textit{lɛ́, wɔ́, gɛ̰̀} but not \textit{lɛ̄ }) can be followed by a clause (typically with a final demonstrative, which, however, can be omitted, as in \REF{ex:mano:47}, the contexts of omission are not clear). The clause may serve to facilitate identification.

\ea\label{ex:mano:47}
    \gll yē lɛ́ ē kìē zɛ̄ kpá.\\
         \textsc{3sg.emph} \textsc{att} \textsc{3sg.pst} chimpanzee kill long.time.ago\\
    \glt ‘(The man that is here, it is my son,) It is him who killed the chimpanzee in the times past.’ (nat.)
\z

Constructions with a clause can also be used in the focus function, which is a cross-linguistically rather typical extension of identification constructions \citep{Killian2022}. A common case are thetic (all-new) utterances where one NP – typically the subject – is framed with a demonstrative predicator. Example \REF{ex:mano:48} is a response to the question, who wants my goat. Usually, big animals are needed in times of big events, most often funerals, so implicitly, the question is, whose funeral is the goat needed for. By saying “It is my in-law who has died”, the speaker of \REF{ex:mano:48} responds to that implicit question. At the same time, the whole utterance in \REF{ex:mano:48} is new information and functions as a thetic utterance. See also \REF{ex:mano:61} as an example of focalization of a locative adjunct and \REF{ex:mano:85} as an example of direct object focalization.

\ea\label{ex:mano:48}
    \gll mā lɛ̄, ŋ̄ yɔ́ɔ̀ lɛ́ à gā-à lɛ̄ ɛ̄. \\
     \textsc{1sg.emph} \textsc{ident} \textsc{1sg} in.law \textsc{att} \textsc{3sg} die-\textsc{ger} \textsc{ident} \textsc{dem}\\
    \glt ‘It is me, my in-law died.’ (lit. ‘It is my in-law that its dying is.’)
\z

Negative identification can also be combined with a clause in the cleft function \REF{ex:mano:49}. 

\ea\label{ex:mano:49}
    \gll nɛ́fú tɔ́ɔ̄ wáá ká kɛ̄ é làà wɛ̄ kpɔ́ ē sɔ́nɔ́. \\
         child \textsc{dem.vis} \textsc{cop.neg>3sg} with \textsc{purp} \textsc{3sg.cnjv} knife \textsc{dem} put \textsc{3sg.refl} near\\
    \glt ‘It is not the child who puts the knife near himself.’
\z

In contrast with affirmative identification constructions (\ref{ex:mano:47}, \ref{ex:mano:48}), in negative identification constructions an indicative construction is not used, a purpose construction with a conjunctive auxiliary is used instead \REF{ex:mano:49}. There is no negative equivalent of presentative constructions. For more detail on identifying and presentative constructions, see \citet{KhachaturyanForthcoming}.

As mentioned in \sectref{sec:mano:2.1}, imperfective and resultative constructions are also formed on non-verbal models. The imperfective construction in (\ref{ex:mano:53}--\ref{ex:mano:54}) is formed based on the locative construction (and indeed, the infinitive suffix -\textit{pɛ̀lɛ̀} derives from the noun \textit{pɛ́lɛ́} ‘place’ in a low-tone construct form).

\ea\label{ex:mano:53}Affirmative imperfective\\
    \gll sà̰ā̰ lɛ̄ ló-pɛ̀lɛ̀ líé. \\
         work 3\textsc{sg.ex} go-\textsc{inf} front    \\
    \glt ‘The work is advancing.’ (nat.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:54}Negative imperfective\\
    \gll mā wɔ́ ló-pɛ̀lɛ̀ lūú. \\
         \textsc{1sg.emph} \textsc{cop.neg} go-\textsc{inf} bush    \\
    \glt ‘I am not going to the bush.’ (nat.)
\z

Resultative constructions derive from identifying constructions where the subject position is occupied by a VP nominalization with a gerund (\ref{ex:mano:55}--\ref{ex:mano:56}).

\ea\label{ex:mano:55}Affirmative resultative\\
    \gll  à ɓɔ̄-ɔ̀ lɛ̄. \\
          3\textsc{sg} leave-\textsc{ger} \textsc{ident}    \\
    \glt ‘He has left.’ (lit. ‘His leaving is.’) (el.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:56}Negative resultative\\
    \gll à ɓɔ̄-ɔ̀ wáá ká. \\
         \textsc{3sg} leave-\textsc{ger} \textsc{cop.neg>3sg} with    \\
    \glt ‘He has not left.’ (lit. ‘His leaving isn’t with it.’) (el.)
\z

Non-verbal predications manifest both constructional and paradigmatic asymmetry. Constructional asymmetry arises from the fact that all non-verbal predications in the affirmative are formed with an existential auxiliary, with the exception of presentative constructions which are formed with presentative predicators and identification constructions which are formed with the identification or attention-drawing predicator. The negative equivalents, however, are all formed with the copula. Paradigmatic asymmetry is manifested by the lack of negative equivalent of presentative constructions.

\subsection{Negation in non-main clauses}\label{sec:mano:2.4}

By default, negation in non-main clauses is formed along the patterns of standard negation. Some non-main clauses in Mano do not have a negative counterpart. This is the case of the conjoint construction used to express a sequence of events in a narrative or simultaneously occurring events \REF{ex:mano:57}. When sequences of events are negated, the usual negative construction is used as in  \REF{ex:mano:58}.
% \lv{I used a slightly different formulation. Please let me know if you accept it.}

\ea\label{ex:mano:57}
    \gll ē nū wáà sōō sì pɛ̄lɛ̄í.  \\
         3\textsc{sg.pst} come \textsc{3pl.cj} yelling take:\textsc{cj} village  \\
    \glt ‘He came and they (immediately) started to yell in the village.’ (nat.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:58}
    \gll lɛ́ɛ̀ gbóó ɓō tó làá zṵ́ ó nɛ̀ɛ̄nɛ́ɛ́. \\
         \textsc{3sg.ipfv} sobbing implement:\textsc{ipfv} and \textsc{3sg.neg>3sg} accept \textsc{3pl.cnjv>3sg} console\\
    \glt ‘Rachel is weeping and refuses (lit. does not accept) to be comforted.’ (UBS)
\z

Similarly to directive constructions in the main clause (see \sectref{sec:mano:2.2}), affirmative and negative purpose clauses are both formed with the conjunctive auxiliary. The affirmative construction also includes the initial purpose particle \textit{kɛ̄} \REF{ex:mano:59} and the negative construction includes the prohibitive particle \textit{yékɛ̀} \REF{ex:mano:60}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:59}
    \gll monpère dò wɔ́ kō fèŋ́ kɛ̄ é làmɛ́sà sàlà ɓō. \\
         priest[\textsc{fr}] \textsc{indf} \textsc{cop.neg} \textsc{1pl} between \textsc{purp} \textsc{3sg.cnjv} Mass[\textsc{fr}] sacrifice:\textsc{cnstr} implement\\
    \glt ‘There is no priest among us to celebrate the Mass.’ (lit. ‘There is no priest among us so that he implements the sacrifice of the Mass.’) (nat.) 
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:60}
    \gll óò lō ō kɔ̀ yà ī wì yékɛ̀ ī gà̰ é gḭ̀ī. \\
         \textsc{3pl.ipfv} go:\textsc{ipfv} \textsc{3pl} hand sit \textsc{2sg} under \textsc{proh} \textsc{2sg} foot \textsc{3sg.cnjv} hurt\\
    \glt ‘They will support you (lit. put their hands under you) so that your foot does not hurt.’ (nat.)
\z

In relative clauses and focus constructions, the relativized or the focused constituent is framed by the predicator \textit{lɛ́} and its variants, \textit{tɛ́}, \textit{nɛ́}, used after a nasal vowel or a syllable starting with a nasal consonant, and a floating high tone. In these constructions, the imperfective auxiliary is replaced by the subjunctive auxiliary both in the imperfective and in the future constructions \REF{ex:mano:61}. Negative relative clauses keep their negative auxiliary, which is another instance of paradigmatic asymmetry. Indeed, in the affirmative, there is a contrast between an imperfective construction used in the main and in the relative clause(or in a focus construction) and in the negative, there is no such contrast as shown in \REF{ex:mano:62}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:61}
    \gll [lɛ̀ lɛ́ ò ɓò kɔ̀nɔ̀ yī  ā] yē lɛ́ è lɛ́ɛ́ ɓèlè yī ā. \\
         place  \textsc{att} \textsc{3pl.sbjv} goat stick\textsc{:ipfv} there  \textsc{dem} \textsc{3sg.emph}  \textsc{att} \textsc{3sg.sbjv} leaf eat:\textsc{ipfv} there \textsc{dem} \\
    \glt ‘Where you attach the goat, this is where it eats the leaves.’ (nat.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:62}
    \gll [mí nɛ́ lɛ̀ɛ́ ɓā ē ɓɛ̄ɛ̄ yīè ká ā] gbɔ̀ɔ̄ lɛ̄ kpɔ̀á yílí gáná yí.   \\
         person:\textsc{h}  \textsc{att} \textsc{3sg.neg} bear.fruit \textsc{3sg.refl} fruit good with \textsc{dem} axe \textsc{3sg.ex} put.\textsc{ger>}with tree root in  \\
    \glt ‘The person who does not bear good fruit, the axe is already put at the roots.’ (nat.)
\z

Some dependent clauses can take a non-finite form: this is the case of purpose constructions which can contain a gerund if the subjects of the dependent and the main clauses corefer \REF{ex:mano:63}. Yet negative purpose clauses are expressed only by finite prohibitive constructions \REF{ex:mano:64}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:63}
    \gll kò lō dɛ̀ vɔ̄-ɔ̀ ī ŋwɛ́ŋ̀.  \\
         \textsc{1pl.sbjv} go:\textsc{ipfv} divination send-\textsc{ger} \textsc{2sg} because.of  \\
    \glt ‘Let’s go to make a divination for you.’ (nat.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:64}
    \gll í gó lɛ̀ wɛ̄ mɔ̀, yékɛ̀ í ī gà̰ gbɛ̰̀ sɛ́lɛ́ mɛ́síà wɛ̄ yí. \\
         \textsc{2sg.cnjv} leave place \textsc{dem} on \textsc{proh} \textsc{2sg.cnjv} \textsc{2sg} foot put ground holy \textsc{dem} in \\
    \glt ‘You must leave so that you don’t put your feet on the holy ground.’ (nat.)
\z

Conditional clauses are particularly interesting with respect to negation. There is a dedicated negative conditional marker \textit{gɔ̰̀á̰̰} which is used in negative protasis (real, \REF{ex:mano:67}, and counterfactual, (\ref{ex:mano:69}--\ref{ex:mano:70})) and in counterfactual negative apodosis \REF{ex:mano:70}. In addition, there are dedicated conditional verbal forms (two different tonal forms combined with the conditional suffix -\textit{á}) used only in affirmative protasis, real \REF{ex:mano:65} and counterfactual \REF{ex:mano:68}, as well as a counterfactual form -\textit{à} used in negative protasis (real, \REF{ex:mano:67}, and counterfactual, (\ref{ex:mano:69}--\ref{ex:mano:70}), as well as in counterfactual apodosis (affirmative, (\ref{ex:mano:68}--\ref{ex:mano:69}), and negative, \REF{ex:mano:70}).
As all these examples show, there is a large array of dedicated conditional forms and constructions. The only cases where usual declarative constructions are used are real apodosis, which features imperfective and future constructions in the affirmative \REF{ex:mano:65} and in the negative \REF{ex:mano:66}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:65}Real conditional, affirmative protasis, affirmative apodosis\\
    \gll [ká mā-á ā] káà lō yéí sí-à. \\
         \textsc{2pl.cnjv>3sg} hear:\textsc{cond-cond} \textsc{dem} \textsc{2pl.ipfv} go:\textsc{ipfv} laughter take-\textsc{ger}\\
    \glt ‘If you hear it, you will laugh.’ (el.)
\z


\ea\label{ex:mano:66}Real conditional, affirmative protasis, negative apodosis\\
    \gll [í gɛ̄lɛ̀ dà bɛ̰̀ɛ̰̄ bɛ̀ɛ́ ā yí ā,] lɛ̀ɛ́ ló dō pɛ́ɛ̀. \\
         2\textsc{sg}.\textsc{cnjv} rock fall also bag \textsc{dem} in \textsc{dem} 3\textsc{sg}.\textsc{neg} go once tear.\textsc{ger}    \\
    \glt ‘Even if you put a rock in the bag, it will not tear apart.’ (el.)
\z

         \textsc{2sg.cnjv} \textsc{cond.neg} do-\textsc{cntf} \textsc{dem} person \textsc{indf} different \textsc{3sg.ipfv} go \textsc{3sg} do-\textsc{ger}    \\

    \gll [ɓà kà̰-á̰ yālá ā] ɓáá gā-à ɲɛ́nɛ́ pèèlɛ̄ píé. \\
         2\textsc{sg.sbjv>3sg} cut:\textsc{irr{}-cond} yesterday \textsc{dem} \textsc{2sg.proh} die-\textsc{cntf} sun two to \\
    \glt ‘If you had cut it, you would have died in two days.’ (el.)
\z

    \glt ‘Without you (lit. if it hadn’t been you) I would have suffered.’ (el.)
\z

    \gll [è gɔ̰̀á̰ pɛ́ ɓɛ̄ sí-à ā] lɛ̀ɛ́ gɔ̰̀á̰ félà gbāā. \\
         \textsc{3sg.sbjv} \textsc{cond.neg} thing:\textsc{h} \textsc{dem} take-\textsc{cntf} \textsc{dem} \textsc{3sg.neg} \textsc{cond.neg} become.clear.\textsc{cntf} now    \\ 
    \glt ‘If she hadn’t taken this thing, her skin (lit. she) would not have become so fair.’ (el.)
\z


Note also that the meaning ‘without’ in \REF{ex:mano:69} is expressed by a periphrastic construction: ‘without you’, lit. ‘if it hadn’t been you’.

Another interesting part are cases of polarity mismatch: the prohibitive auxiliary occurs in the construction of counterfactual affirmative apodosis \REF{ex:mano:68}. In addition, the subjunctive auxiliary occurs in the counterfactual protasis, both affirmative \REF{ex:mano:68} and negative \REF{ex:mano:69}. Conjunctive auxiliary, too, occurs in both affirmative (\ref{ex:mano:65}--\ref{ex:mano:66}) and negative \REF{ex:mano:67} real protasis, but this type of polarity mismatch is reminiscent of directive strategies, where the conjunctive auxiliary can be used in both imperative and prohibitive constructions.

\section{Non-clausal negation}\label{sec:mano:3}
\subsection{Negative replies}\label{sec:mano:3.1}

Polar questions are formed with the same pattern as the declarative clause but with vowel lengthening at the end. Negation can be contained in the question. Negative response (\textit{kpàāò} ‘no’) indicates the reversal of the polarity value in the question and positive response (\textit{\`{ŋ}\`{ŋ}} ‘yes’) indicates its confirmation. In other words, a “no” response to a question with an affirmative construction implies negation \REF{ex:mano:71}, while a positive answer to a question containing negation confirms the negation \REF{ex:mano:72}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:71}
    \begin{xlist}
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:mano:71:a}
        \gll kō kò nɔ́ɔ̀ kɛ̀nɛ́ì kṵ́-pɛ̀lɛ̀ sɛ̀ɛ̀? \\
         \textsc{1pl.ex} \textsc{1pl.poss} child.\textsc{pl} small take-\textsc{inf} well.\textsc{q}  \\
        \glt ‘Do we treat our small children well?’ 
    \exi{B: }\label{ex:mano:71:b}
        \gll  kpàāò. \\ 
              no \\
        \glt ‘No (we don’t treat them well).’ (nat.)
    \end{xlist}
\z 

\ea\label{ex:mano:72}
    \begin{xlist}
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:mano:72:a}
         \gll lɛ̀ɛ́ wìì ɓèlèè? \\
         \textsc{3sg.neg} meat eat.\textsc{q} \\
    \glt ‘She doesn’t eat meat?’ 
    \exi{B: }\label{ex:mano:72:b}
        \gll ŋ̀ŋ̀. \\ 
             yes \\
        \glt ‘Correct (she doesn’t eat meat).’ (nat.)
    \end{xlist}
\z 


\subsection{Negative indefinites and quantifiers}\label{sec:mano:3.2}
Negative and indefinite quantifiers in Mano belong to the class of determinatives and are always part of a noun phrase. They can be used with generic nouns, such as \textit{mī} ‘person’ or \textit{pɛ̄ } ‘thing’ \REF{ex:mano:73}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:73}
    \gll yé kò mī dò kɔ̄āā kɛ̄ ŋwɔ́ dò kɛ̄-pìè... \\
         when 1\textsc{pl.ip} person \textsc{indf} \textsc{1pl.ex} do thing \textsc{indf} do-\textsc{inf}       \\
    \glt ‘If we are doing something with someone...’ (lit. ‘We and somebody we do something’) (nat.)
\z

There are four indefinite quantifiers: \textit{dò}%\lv{Lawali is asking for a gloss here and you suggest \textsc{indf}. However, I don't see why the gloss should be there. You have said that these words are all indefinite quantifiers. Besides, Matti does not approve of glossing abbreviations in translations. I suggest we skip this.}
which has the same distribution as \textit{gbɛ̄ɛ̄}, but also \textit{dōódōó} and \textit{gbṵ́ṵ̀} whose distribution differs in minor details. All of them can be used in affirmative contexts with specific referents, in irrealis non-specific contexts, in conditionals (as in \REF{ex:mano:73}), as well as in negation, indirect (\ref{ex:mano:74}--\ref{ex:mano:75}) and direct (\ref{ex:mano:76}--\ref{ex:mano:77}).

\ea\label{ex:mano:74}
    \gll ŋ̀ŋ́ yí dɔ̄ ékɛ̄á mī dò nū-à lɛ̄. \\
         \textsc{1sg.neg>3sg} interior know if person \textsc{indf} come-\textsc{ger} \textsc{ident}\\
    \glt \textsc{‘}I do not know if anybody came.’ (el.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:75}
    \gll ŋ̀ŋ́ yí dɔ̄ ékɛ̄á mī gbṵ́ṵ̀ nū-à lɛ̄. \\
         \textsc{1sg.neg>3sg} interior know if person some come-\textsc{ger} \textsc{ident}\\
    \glt \textsc{‘}I do not know if anybody came.’ (el.)
\z

Crucially, while \textit{dò} and \textit{gbɛ̄ɛ̄} are used in the context of direct full negation \REF{ex:mano:76}, meaning that there does not exist a referent with a property X, \textit{dōódōó} and \textit{gbṵ́ṵ̀} are used in the context of direct partial negation \REF{ex:mano:77}, meaning that there exist referents which do not have a property X.

\ea\label{ex:mano:76}Direct full negation\\
    \gll mī dò lɛ̀ɛ́ gbāā nū. \\
         person \textsc{indf} \textsc{3sg.neg} \textsc{neg} come   \\
    \glt ‘Nobody came.’ (lit. ‘There doesn’t exist a person who came.’) (el.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:77}Direct partial negation\\
    \gll mī gbṵ́ṵ̀ lɛ̀ɛ́ gbāā nū. \\
          person some \textsc{3sg.neg} \textsc{neg} come  \\
    \glt ‘Somebody did not come.’ (lit. ‘There exist at least one person who did not come.’) (el.)
\z

Mano also has a dedicated negative quantifier \textit{fɛ́ì{\textasciitilde}féì} ‘nothing’ which can be reinforced \textit{féìféìféì} ‘absolutely nothing’. It is used in the contexts of direct full negation \REF{ex:mano:78}.

    \gll féì wáá kɛ̀lɛ̀. \\

\textit{Féì} can be used in exclamations meaning ‘nonsense!’ \REF{ex:mano:79}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:79}
    \gll à lìī dìè nì wáà gèè kéēé: féì! \\

\subsection{Negative derivation and case marking}\label{sec:mano:3.3}

There is no negative derivation in Mano. There is no dedicated word for ‘without’ either, the meaning is expressed by a periphrastic construction (see \REF{ex:mano:69}).

\section{Other aspects of negation}\label{sec:mano:4}

\subsection{Scope of negation}\label{sec:mano:4.1}

Focus constructions in Mano (VP, argument focus or thetic constructions) are formed with the marker \textit{lɛ́}: for example, with argument focus, the focalized NP is placed at the beginning of the utterance, followed by \textit{lɛ́}, followed by the predication where the syntactic position of the focalized constituent is filled with a pronoun (or an auxiliary, in case of subject focalization). Polarity does not have an effect on focus constructions, except for the choice of the auxiliary discussed in \sectref{sec:mano:2.3} and \sectref{sec:mano:2.4}. \REF{ex:mano:80} illustrates focalization in a negative utterance. See also \REF{ex:mano:49}, a negative focus construction.

\ea\label{ex:mano:80}
    \gll Rosalie lɛ́ lɛ̀ɛ́ fàā Mado lɛ́ɛ̀ fàā. \\
         Rosalie  \textsc{att} \textsc{3sg.neg} grow Mado \textsc{3sg.ipfv} grow:\textsc{ipfv}\\
    \glt ‘It is Rosalie who does not grow, Mado grows.’ (nat.)
\z

The effects of quantifiers on the scope of negation are discussed in \sectref{sec:mano:3.2}. The quantifier \textit{dò} is used in contexts of full negation, meaning that there does not exist a referent with a property X, while \textit{dōódōó} and \textit{gbṵ́ṵ̀} are used in the context of direct partial negation, meaning that there exist referents which do not have a property X.


\subsection{Negative polarity items}\label{sec:mano:4.2}

Negative polarity items include the quantifier \textit{fɛ́ì{\textasciitilde}féì} ‘nothing’ which can be reinforced \textit{féìféìféì} ‘absolutely nothing’. See \sectref{sec:mano:3.2} for illustrations.

\subsection{Marking of NPs in the scope of negation}\label{sec:mano:4.3}

There are no differences in marking of NPs in the scope of negation. Mano does not have case, so negation does not affect it.

\subsection{Reinforcing negation}\label{sec:mano:4.4}

Negation can be reinforced with the quantifier \textit{féìféìféì} ‘absolutely nothing’. See \sectref{sec:mano:3.2} for illustrations.

\subsection{Negation, coordination and complex clauses}\label{sec:mano:4.5}

Clausal coordination in Mano proceeds by coordinating full clauses, there is no argument reduction. Consider coordinated affirmative clauses (\ref{ex:mano:81}--\ref{ex:mano:82}).

\ea\label{ex:mano:81}
    \gll kɛ̄ɛ̄ ō dò ō ō kɔ̀ yà à wì ɛ̰́ɛ̰́ ō ō zò dɔ̄ à mɔ̀. \\
         but \textsc{3pl} \textsc{indf} \textsc{3pl.pst} \textsc{3pl} hand sit \textsc{3sg} under and \textsc{3pl.pst} \textsc{3pl} heart install \textsc{3sg} on\\
    \glt ‘But some of them accepted him and they believed in him.’
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:82}
    \gll gɔ̰́ yāā lɛ́ɛ̀ wèè ɛ̰́ɛ̰́ léé ā lɛ́ɛ̀ wèè. \\
         man \textsc{dem} \textsc{3sg.ipfv} speak\textsc{:ipfv} and woman \textsc{dem} \textsc{3sg.ipfv} speak:\textsc{ipfv}\\
    \glt ‘The man and the woman speak.’ (lit. ‘The man speaks and the woman speaks.’)
\z

Similarly, in negative coordination, the full clauses have to be repeated, there is no ‘neither... nor’ equivalent (\ref{ex:mano:83}--\ref{ex:mano:84}). 

\ea\label{ex:mano:83}
    \gll lɛ̀ɛ́ gbā zṵ́ é ō kpílì kɛ̄, lɛ̀ɛ́ gbā bɛ̰̀ɛ̰̄ ō bílí gɛ̰̀. \\
         3\textsc{sg.neg} \textsc{neg} agree 3\textsc{sg.cnjv} \textsc{3pl} feast do \textsc{3sg.neg} \textsc{neg} too \textsc{3pl} dead.body see  \\
    \glt ‘He neither accepted to organize their funerals, nor saw their corpses.’ (lit. ‘He did not accept to organize their funerals, he did not see their corpses.’) (nat.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:84}
    \gll donc gɔ̰̄ wɔ́ ɓē, ɛ̰́ɛ̰́ lēē wɔ́ ɓē kɛ̄ é lēē nɛ̀fú wɛ̄ pá gbāā. \\
     so[\textsc{fr}] man \textsc{cop.neg} \textsc{ex} and woman \textsc{cop.neg} \textsc{ex} \textsc{purp} 3\textsc{sg.cnjv} woman child:\textsc{cnstr} \textsc{dem} hit now \\
    \glt ‘So there is no man nor woman (lit. there is no man and there is no woman) to hit the girl.’ (nat.)
\z

There is no construction for contrastive negation. Again, a full predication must be repeated \REF{ex:mano:85}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:85}
    \gll ŋ̀ŋ́ gbā bū dà mīī mɔ̀, wìì lɛ́ mā zɛ̄ ɛ̄.   \\
         1\textsc{sg.neg} \textsc{neg} gun fall person on meat  \textsc{att} \textsc{1sg.pst>3sg} kill \textsc{dem}   \\
    \glt ‘I did not shoot at a person but at an animal.’ (lit. ‘I didn't shoot at a person, it was an animal that I killed.’) (nat.)
\z

There is a dedicated prohibitive particle \textit{yékɛ̀}, see \sectref{sec:mano:2.2}. A dedicated negative auxiliary \textit{gɔ̰̀á̰̰} is used in conditional clauses, see \sectref{sec:mano:2.4}.

\subsection{Miscellaneous aspects of negation}\label{sec:mano:4.6}
This section will focus on the phenomenon known as the reversal of polarity.
In informal conversations, especially in questions, affirmative utterances are sometimes used when a negative polarity is intended, and negative utterances are sometimes used when an affirmative polarity is intended. Such pragmatic reversal of polarity can be interpreted as a friendly mockery when the interlocutors have enough common ground to understand that they mean the inverse of what they say. For example, \REF{ex:mano:86} is an affirmative question pragmatically implying a reading that would be a negative declarative with the same content. Conversely, \REF{ex:mano:87} is a negative question pragmatically implying an affirmative declarative reading.

\ea\label{ex:mano:86}
    \gll zèē kɔ́nɔ́ bɛ̰̀ɛ̰̄, lɛ́ɛ̀ tōō? \\
         here food too \textsc{3sg.ipfv} stay\textsc{:ipfv.q}   \\
    \glt ‘(A woman’s sister in law complains that the former started to prepare a festival dish late. The former woman says:) The food here, does it last?’ (meaning: of course it doesn’t, it will always be eaten, whenever we cook it).’ (nat.)
\z

\ea\label{ex:mano:87}
    \gll òó gèē zénīí kā ló-pɛ̀lɛ̀ Gúù kɛ̀ɛ̀?  \\
     \textsc{3pl.neg} say still 2\textsc{pl.ex} go-\textsc{inf} Goo isn’t.it \\
    \glt ‘(A woman observes that her sister in law is preparing to cook fish. She knows that the latter’s mother, who lives in the Goo village, likes fish a lot.) Don’t they say furthermore that you are going to the Goo village?’ (nat.)
\z

Negative imperfective constructions are also used as a directive strategy \REF{ex:mano:88}.

\ea\label{ex:mano:88}
    \gll ɓàá nɔ̄ mɔ̄ŋ́ tḭ̄á̰ ŋ̄ lɛ̀ɛ̄ɛ̄? \\
         1\textsc{sg.neg>3sg} give so yet \textsc{1sg} to.\textsc{q} \\
    \glt ‘Give it to me for now!’ (lit. ‘Don’t you give it to me for now?’)
\z

There are two polarity reversal cases in conditional clauses. First, the affirmative counterfactual apodosis construction employs a negative polarity auxiliary, namely, the prohibitive. Second, the subjunctive auxiliary, which is affirmative, is used for affirmative, but also for negative counterfactual protasis. For a fuller discussion, see \sectref{sec:mano:2.4}.

A paraphrastic directive strategy, where the speaker, instead of issuing a command ‘do X’, says ‘I am not telling you to do X’. could be considered a case of negative transport (see \sectref{sec:mano:2.2}).

Metalinguistic negation is not covered in this paper because of lack of data.

\section{Summary}\label{sec:mano:5}

Negation in Mano is highly asymmetric (in the sense proposed by \citealt{Miestamo2005}) both because negation involves a change in the TAM constructions (constructional asymmetry) and because the inventory of negative constructions is reduced with respect to the inventory of affirmative constructions (paradigmatic asymmetry). Thus, in declaratives, there is only one negative auxiliary which, combined with different lexical and grammatical means, is used to form constructions of negative habitual, negative aorist, and negative perfect, while in the affirmative zone, there are three different auxiliaries used for these purposes. The prospective construction exists only in the affirmative. For negative non-verbal constructions there is only one predicator, the negative copula \textit{wɔ́}, while affirmative non-verbal clauses contain either an existential auxiliary or either of the three available affirmative predicators \textit{lɛ̄ }, \textit{wɔ́} or \textit{gɛ̰̀}, and presentative constructions exist only in the affirmative polarity. Yet in non-declaratives, the paradigmatic asymmetry favors the negative domain: there is a periphrastic prohibitive with no imperative equivalent (‘I am not saying that you do X’, meaning ‘Don’t do X’) and a dedicated dubitative construction with negative pragmatics.



One of the striking features of negation in complex clauses are constructions used in conditional clauses since they employ a special negative particle \textit{gɔ̰̀á̰̰} which is used only in real and counterfactual negative protasis and in counterfactual negative apodosis.

Another interesting feature concerns quantification. The semantic map of quantifiers in Mano violates the adjacency principle predicted by the quantification typology. Moreover, indefinite quantifiers differ with respect to the scope of direct negation: for some, such as the most commonly used indefinite quantifier \textit{dò}, the whole predication is under the scope of negation (‘nobody came’), while for others, such as the quantifier \textit{gbṵ́ṵ̀}, the negation is partial (‘some people did not come’).

Mano does not have any negative derivation nor dedicated negative coordinators. Negative polarity items are limited to \textit{féì} ‘nothing’. 

More information on negation in Southern Mande languages can be found in \citet{Vydrine2009}. On negation, and in particular, negative final particles, in some other Mande languages, see \citet{Idiatov2012,Idiatov2015}. On negation in several other West African languages, see \citet{CyfferEtAl2009}.
