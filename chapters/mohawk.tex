\title{Negation in Mohawk}
\abstract{The patterns of negation in individual languages are often not unrelated to other structural characteristics. Mohawk, an Iroquoian language of northeastern North America, is polysynthetic, with a high proportion of verbs in spontaneous speech. This trait has implications for its inventory of negative constructions. Most of the kinds of non-verbal clauses mentioned in the Questionnaire for Describing the Negation System of a Language by \textcitetv{chapters/questionnaire} are expressed in verbal predications in Mohawk, and are accordingly negated like other verbal clauses, with a construction that has evolved via a Jespersen cycle. There are no non-finite clauses, so most dependent clauses are negated in the same way as main clauses. There is no nominal case, so negation does not affect case marking, and negation has no effect on determiners. There is no negative nominal derivation. The language does offer features of interest in the interplay between negation and tense, aspect, and modality.
\keywords{aspect, modality, Mohawk, polysynthesis, tense}

\section{The language} 

Mohawk (ISO: \href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/moh}{moh}, Glottocode: \href{https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/moha1258}{moha1258}) is an Iroquoian language indigenous to northeastern North America. It is currently spoken mainly in six communities in modern Canada and the United States, in  Quebec, New York State, and Ontario. The number of first-language speakers is estimated at around 900, but there are more highly competent second-language speakers, and their numbers continue to grow. Dialectal variation across the six communities is minor: primarily the pronunciation of an affricate and a liquid, and certain lexicalizations. Morphological and syntactic structure are essentially the same in all. Just three lexical categories are distinguished in terms of their morphological structures: verbs, nouns, and particles.

Verbs can be relatively complex. All contain specification of the core arguments in a pronominal prefix, and apart from imperatives, an aspect suffix. They may also contain additional prefixes, suffixes, and/or an incorporated noun stem. Examples are in (\ref{ex:moh:1}--\ref{ex:moh:4}).\footnote{Examples are given here in the community orthography. Most symbols are close to their IPA counterparts. Nasalized vowels are represented with digraphs <en> [ʌ̨] and <on> [ų], the palatal glide [j] with <i>, and glottal stop with an apostrophe <‘> [ʔ]. Stress with high or rising tone is indicated with an acute accent <á>, and stress with falling tone with a grave accent <à>. Vowel length is indicated with a colon <:> after the vowel: <a:>, <en:>, etc.}

There  are nearly 60 pronominal prefixes: one paradigm for grammatical agents like the masculine singular \textit{{}-ha-} ‘he’ in \REF{ex:moh:1} ‘he escaped’, one for grammatical patients like the neuter \textit{io-} ‘it’ in \REF{ex:moh:2} ‘it is dry’,  and one for transitive combinations, like the masculine singular acting on multiple third persons \textit{{}-hshakoti-} ‘he>them’ in \REF{ex:moh:3} ‘he spooked them’. Paradigm choice is semantically based but lexicalized with each verb stem. Verb stems fall into two categories: 1) event verbs, which have imperative, habitual, perfective, and stative forms, and 2) stative-only verbs, which have only stative forms. In the imperative, habitual, and perfective aspect forms of event verbs, grammatical agents are generally in control, and grammatical patients are not in control but significantly affected. In the stative aspect forms of these verbs, which often function as perfects, single participants of intransitives are identified with grammatical patient prefixes. In stative-only verbs, the pronominal paradigm often reflects a distinction between inherent and resultant states.

Multiple derivational suffixes can co-occur. These include reversives, inchoatives, causatives, instrumental, benefactive, and directional applicatives, a facilitative, an ambulative, a purposive, and distributives. Final post-aspectual verb suffixes can further distinguish progressives, remote pasts, and continuatives.

Morphological verbs can function as in other languages as predicates. They can also serve as clauses in themselves. All are finite. Ideas conveyed in non-finite clauses in many other languages are expressed in finite clauses in Mohawk, as illustrated in \REF{ex:moh:5}. \ea\label{ex:moh:9}
    \glll o’tá:ra’ \\ 
        o-’tar-a’ \\
         \textsc{n}-clay-\textsc{ns} \\
    \glt ‘clay, chimney, clan’
\z

    \glll akhwá:tsire’ \\ 
         ak-hwatsir-e-’ \\
         \textsc{1sg.al.poss}-family-\textsc{ep-ns} \\
    \glt ‘my family’
\z 

Noun stems may be simple roots, as in \REF{ex:moh:9} and \REF{ex:moh:10} above, or formal nominalizations, as in \REF{ex:moh:11}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:11}

Morphological nouns are used only as referring expressions. Various enclitics may be added to words functioning as nominals, such as diminutives, distributives, and decessives (used when referring to deceased persons).

Particles are by definition monomorphemic, though they may be compounded. They serve a variety of syntactic and discourse functions. Some examples are in \REF{ex:moh:12}.

The language could be characterized as head marking, since arguments are identified pronominally in every verb and there is no nominal case. There is no unmarked, syntactically-defined basic constituent order. All orders are pragmatically significant: apart from various discourse particles, constituents appear essentially in decreasing order of newsworthiness.

The description of negation in Mohawk in this chapter is based on the questionnaire provided by the editors (\citetv{chapters/questionnaire}) and mirrors its structure.

\section{Clausal negation}\label{sec:moh:2}

\subsection{Standard negation}\label{sec:moh:2.1}

Standard negation is expressed by a negative particle \textit{iah} near the beginning of the clause and a prefix on the verb. There are two negative constructions. The first consists of the negative particle \textit{iah} and the negative verb prefix \textit{te’-}. This is used if there are no prepronominal prefixes in the verb, or only a repetitive or cislocative. An assertion and its negative counterpart with this construction are in \REF{ex:moh:13} and \REF{ex:moh:14}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:13} Assertion\\
    \gll Khe-nòn:we’-s. \\
         \textsc{1sg>3}-like-\textsc{hab} \\
    \glt ‘I like her.’ 
\z

\ea\label{ex:moh:14} Standard negation\\
    \gll \textbf{Iah} \textbf{te}-khe-nòn:we’-s.\\
         \textbf{not} \textbf{\textsc{neg}}\textsc{-1sg>3}-like-\textsc{hab}\\
    \glt ‘I \textbf{don’t} like her.’
\z

The second negative construction consists of the negative particle and contrastive verb prefix \textit{th-}, used if there are other prepronominal prefixes in the verb. An assertion and its negative counterpart with this second construction are in \REF{ex:moh:15} and \REF{ex:moh:16}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:15} Assertion\\
    \glll Iesakwéhtha’. \\
         ie-sa-akw-eht-ha’ \\
         \textsc{trl-rpt-1excl.pl.agt}-stop.by-\textsc{hab} \\
    \glt ‘We go back there.’
\z

{\ea\label{ex:moh:16} Standard negation\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{th}iesakwéhtha’. \\
         \textbf{iah} \textbf{th}-ie-s-akw-eht-ha’\\
         \textbf{not} \textsc{\textbf{cntr}-trl-rpt-1excl.pl.agt}-stop.by-\textsc{hab} \\
    \glt ‘We \textbf{don’t} go back there.’
\z}

Outside of negative contexts, the contrastive marks surprising information. Both the negative and contrastive prefixes occur in various context-conditioned phonological forms.

For the affirmative and negative pairs discussed so far, the verb forms are the same, apart from the negative or contrastive prefix. There are several contexts in which they are not perfect counterparts, however.

Perfective aspect verbs co-occur with one of three prefixes: the factual, the future, or the irrealis (often called the optative or indefinite tense in the literature). Factual perfectives are usually interpreted as past tense. Their negative counterparts are not formed simply with the negative particle plus a prefix on the factual perfective verb, however. Rather than saying that something did not happen with a factual perfective (‘He did not see it’), speakers use a stative form of the verb, interpretable as a perfect (‘He is/was in the state of not having seen it.’). Past affirmative and negative counterparts are in \REF{ex:moh:17} and \REF{ex:moh:18}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:17} Factual perfective\\ 
    \glll \textbf{Wa}hatkáhto\textbf{’}.\\
         \textbf{wa}-ha-atkahto-\textbf{’}\\
         \textbf{\textsc{fact}}-\textsc{m.sg.agt}-see-\textbf{\textsc{pfv}} \\
    \glt ‘He \textbf{saw} it.’
\z
\ea\label{ex:moh:18} Negative stative\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{te}hotkáhth\textbf{on} \\
         \textbf{iah} \textbf{te-}ho-atkahth-\textbf{on}\\
         \textbf{not} \textbf{\textsc{neg}}\textsc{-m.sg.pat}-see\textsc{-}\textbf{\textsc{stat}} \\
    \glt ‘He \textbf{hasn’t} seen it.’
\z

Future perfectives are used for future events. Their negative counterparts are also not formed simply with the negative particle plus a prefix on the future perfective verb. An irrealis perfective is used instead. Future affirmative and negative counterparts are in \REF{ex:moh:19} and \REF{ex:moh:20}.

{\ea\label{ex:moh:19} Future perfective\\
    \glll \textbf{En}hatkáhtho’. \\
         \textbf{en-}ha-tkáhtho-’\\
         \textbf{\textsc{fut}}\textsc{-m.sg.agt}-see-\textsc{pfv} \\
    \glt ‘He \textbf{will} see it.’
\z}

{
\ea\label{ex:moh:20} Negative irrealis\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{tha:}hatkáhtho’. \\
         \textbf{iah} \textbf{th-aa-}ha-atkahtho-’\\
         \textbf{not} \textbf{\textsc{cntr-irr}}\textsc{-m.sg.agt}-see\textsc{-pfv} \\
    \glt ‘He \textbf{won’t} see it.’
\z  }

Future perfectives are also used for recurring events in the past. In these uses as well, negative counterparts are inflected as irrealis. Describing her childhood, one speaker noted that her family would never begin to eat before her father said grace. She used a future perfective verb for ‘before my father would pray’, but an irrealis perfective for the negative ‘you could not’ in \REF{ex:moh:21}. 

\ea\label{ex:moh:21} Customary past events \\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{th}aón:ton’ tahsatskà:kon’, \\ 
         \textbf{iah} \textbf{th-aa}-w-aton-’ t-aa-hs-at-ska’nhon-’ \\ 
         \textbf{not} \textbf{\textsc{cntr-irr}}-\textsc{n.agt}-be.possible-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{dv-irr-2sg.agt-mid}-dine-\textsc{pfv} \\
    \glt ‘You could\textbf{n’t} eat’\\  \medskip
    \glll tsik \textbf{en}haterén:naien’ ne \\ 
          tsi=ok \textbf{en}-ha-ate-renn-a-ien-’ ne \\
          at=only \textbf{\textsc{fut}}\textsc{-m.sg.agt-mid}-song-\textsc{lnk-}lay-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{art} \\
        \glt 
    {\qquad}  \glll rake’níha. \\ 
          rake-’ni=ha \\
          \textsc{m.sg>1sg}-be.father.to=\textsc{dim} \\
    \glt ‘before my father prayed.’ (Kanatí:res Grace Franks, W) 
\z



The factual and future prefixes never co-occur with negative prefixes.

Since irrealis perfectives are used for events with no specified time, they are somewhat less common in main clauses than factuals and futures, but they do occur, often translated ‘might, would, could, should’ etc. In \REF{ex:moh:22} Mr. Deer was talking about a tornado.

{\ea\label{ex:moh:22}Irrealis\\
    \glll \textbf{A}ón:ton’ \\ 
         \textbf{aa}-w-aton-’ \\ 
         \textbf{\textsc{irr}}\textsc{-n.agt}-be.possible-\textsc{pfv} \\
    \glt ‘It \textbf{might} be possible,’\\ \medskip
    \glll \textbf{a:}kì:ron’ \textbf{a}:kanatarihsión:ko’. \\ 
          \textbf{aa}-k-ihron-’ \textbf{aa}-ka-natar-ihsionko-’\\
          \textbf{\textsc{irr}}\textsc{-1sg.agt-}say-\textsc{pfv} \textbf{\textsc{irr}}\textsc{-n.agt}-town-destroy-\textsc{pfv}\\
    \glt  ‘I’\textbf{d} say, that it \textbf{could} demolish the town.’ (Joe Awenhráthon Deer, Kw)
\z}

In most contexts, irrealis perfectives are negated in the standard way, with the negative particle \textit{iah} and a contrastive prefix, as in \REF{ex:moh:23}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:23}Negative irrealis\\
    \glll \textbf{Iah} \textbf{th}aón:ton’ \\ 
         \textbf{iah} \textbf{th}-aa-w-aton-’ \\ 
         \textbf{not} \textsc{\textbf{cntr}-irr-n.agt}-be.possible-\textsc{pfv} \\
    \glt ‘I could\textbf{n’t}’\\ \medskip 
    \glll  a:kì:ron’ teiona’tarákhen.\\ 
      aa-k-ihron-’ te-io-na’tar-akh-en\\
      \textsc{irr-1sg.agt}-say-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{dv-n.pat}-bread-be.twins-\textsc{stat}\\
    \glt  ‘say the bread has two lumps.’ (Margaret Kahentawákhon Mcdonald, A)
\z 

But when irrealis perfectives are used with a deontic sense to mean ‘should’, they are negated with a different particle \textit{tóhsa’}, here glossed \textsc{neg}, and no prefix on the verb, as in \REF{ex:moh:24}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:24}Negative Perfective\\
    \glll Tówa’ nòn:wa’ \textbf{tóhsa’} aétewake’.\\
         towa’ nonhwa’ \textbf{tohsa’} aa-etewa-k-e-’\\
         maybe now \textbf{\textsc{neg}} \textsc{irr-1incl.pl.agt}-eat-\textsc{ep-pfv}\\
    \glt ‘Maybe we should\textbf{n’t} eat it.’ (Sonny Edwards, A)
\z

This patterning is reminiscent of the A/Cat subtype of \citegen{Miestamo2013-wals114} inventory of Asymmetric Standard Negation strategies, with structural differences between affirmative and negative counterparts based on verb categories such as tense, aspect, and modality.

\subsection{Negation in non-declaratives}\label{sec:moh:2.2}

Prohibitives, that is, negative imperatives, are formed with the second negative marker \textit{tóhsa’}. Like \textit{iah}, this particle occurs at or near the beginning of the clause. There is no negative or contrastive prefix on the verb, as can be seen in \REF{ex:moh:25} and \REF{ex:moh:26}.

{\ea\label{ex:moh:25}Command\\
    \glll Ia’tsiá:ken’n!\\
         ia’-ts-iaken’n\\
         \textsc{trl-2sg.imp}-go.out\\
    \glt ‘Go out!’
\z}


\ea\label{ex:moh:26}Basic prohibitive\\
    \glll \textbf{Tóhsa’} ia’tsiá:ken’n. \\
         \textbf{tohsa’} ia’-ts-iaken’n\\
         \textbf{\textsc{neg}} \textsc{trl-2sg.imp.agt}-go.out\\
    \glt ‘\textbf{Don’t} go out!’
\z

Basic imperatives differ from other verbs in lacking an aspect suffix, but they do contain a second person pronominal prefix that distinguishes singular, dual, and plural addressees. The form of the singular agent in indicatives is \nobreakdash-\textit{hs(e)-} with loss of the \textit{h} word-initially or after a consonant. It thus most often appears as \textit{s(e)-}. The indicative and imperative second person singular agent prefixes are descended from different forms, *-(h)s- and *-č-. They have merged to \textit{s(e)}{}- in most contexts, but the imperative never appears with an initial \textit{h}, and before a high front vowel or glide it is still an affricate, spelled <ts>. In some dialects this is pronounced as an alveo-palatal [ʧ]; in others it is now pronounced as an alveolar affricate [ʦ], and a following glide has disappeared.

There is also a second prohibitive construction, with the future prefix \textit{en-}. This is also formed with \textit{tóhsa’}, as in \REF{ex:moh:27}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:27}Future Prohibitive\\
    \glll \textbf{Tóhsa’} i\textbf{en}hsiá:ken’n(e’). \\
         \textbf{tohsa’} i-\textbf{en}-hs-iaken’n-e-’\\
         \textbf{\textsc{neg}} \textsc{trl-\textbf{fut}-2sg.agt-}go.out-\textsc{ep-pfv}\\
    \glt ‘\textbf{Don’t} go out.’
\z

For some speakers, the future prohibitives lack an aspect suffix, like basic imperatives, but for others, they end in a perfective suffix, like future perfective statements.  For all speakers, the form of the second person singular agent pronoun in this construction is that used with indicatives. Speakers find it difficult to articulate the precise difference in meaning and use between the basic and future prohibitives. Though a difference in time is often involved, speakers agree that forms with the future prefix are not simply delayed imperatives as might be expected, and they are not necessarily more polite. 

Basic prohibitives are used for immediate situations, where there is no time for discussion. If a man is about to put a forkful of food into his mouth, but it is suddenly discovered that there might be something wrong with it, he might get the warning in \REF{ex:moh:28}.

\newpage
\ea\label{ex:moh:28} Basic prohibitive\\
    \glll \textbf{Tóhsa’} í:sek!\\
         \textbf{tohsa’} i-s-ek\\
         \textbf{\textsc{neg}} \textsc{proth-2sg.imp.agt}-eat \\
    \glt ‘\textbf{Don’t} eat it!’ (Á:nen Konwaroniá:wi Deer, Kw)
\z

But if someone has made a cake for a bake sale, and the man comes in and spies it on the counter, he would be more likely to get the warning in \REF{ex:moh:29}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:29}Future prohibitive\\
    \glll \textbf{Tóhsa’} \textbf{én}hsek(e’)! \\
         \textbf{tohsa’} \textbf{en}-hs-ek-e-’\\
         \textbf{\textsc{neg}} \textsc{\textbf{fut}-2sg.agt}-eat-\textsc{ep-pfv}\\
    \glt ‘\textbf{Don’t} eat it!’ (Annette Kaia’titáhkhe’ Jacobs, Kw)
\z

Speakers note that these future forms are more likely to be used for longer-term prohibitions: ‘Don’t plan to ...’. Kanáhstatsi Nancy Howard suggests that the future prohibitive might imply a warning: ‘You’d better not ...’, as in \REF{ex:moh:30}. 

\ea\label{ex:moh:30} Future prohibitive\\
    \glll \textbf{Tohsa’} wakerónhak i\textbf{en}hsia:ken’n(e’). \\
         \textbf{tohsa’} wake-ron-hak i-\textbf{en}-hs-iaken’n-e-’\\
         \textbf{\textsc{neg}} \textsc{1sg.pat}-warn-\textsc{cont} \textsc{trl-}\textbf{\textsc{fut}}\textsc{-2sg.agt}-go.out-\textsc{ep-pfv}\\
    \glt ‘\textbf{Don’t} you dare go out!’ (Kanáhstatsi Nancy Howard, Ks)
\z

Addressees might expect a consequence if they went ahead and acted, and would be likely to ask for a reason. The context in \REF{ex:moh:31} is typical.

\ea\label{ex:moh:31}Future prohibitive\\
    \glll \textbf{Tóhsa’} \textbf{én}hsek(e’)  tanon’ ensanonhwákten! \\
         \textbf{tohsa’} \textbf{en}-hs-ek-e-’ tanon’ en-sa-nonhwakt-en-’\\
         \textbf{\textsc{neg}} \textbf{\textsc{fut}}\textsc{-2sg.agt}-eat-\textsc{ep-pfv} and \textsc{fut-2sg.pat}-sick-\textsc{ben.appl-pfv}\\
    \glt ‘\textbf{Don’t} eat it or you’ll get sick.’ (Annette Kaia’titáhkhe’ Jacobs, Kw)
\z

The basic prohibitive construction thus falls into the second prohibitive type defined by \citet{AuweraEtAl2013}, with an imperative verb form but a different negative particle than that used in standard negation. The future prohibitive falls into their fourth type, with a future tense verb either with no aspect suffix like imperatives, or a perfective suffix like other future verbs, and the different negative particle. 

Hortatives are formed like commands, with a first person inclusive dual or inclusive plural pronominal prefix and no aspect suffix, as in \REF{ex:moh:32}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:32}Basic hortative\\
    \glll Thó tetewá:ta’n. \\
         tho te-tewa-t-a’n \\
         there \textsc{dv-1incl.pl.agt}-be.standing-\textsc{inch}\\
    \glt ‘Let’s stand there.’
\z

Negative hortatives are formed in the same two ways as prohibitives. Basic negative hortatives consist of the second negative particle and a basic imperative verb, as in \REF{ex:moh:33}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:33}Basic negative hortative \\
    \glll \textbf{Tóhsa’} thó tetewá:ta’n. \\
         \textbf{tohsa’} tho te-tewa-t-a’n\\
         \textbf{\textsc{neg}} there \textsc{dv-1incl.pl.agt}-be.standing-\textsc{inch}\\
    \glt ‘Let’s not stand there.’ (Annette Kaia’titáhkhe’ Jacobs, Kw)
\z

Future negative hortatives consist of the second negative particle and a future tense verb, with or without a perfective aspect suffix, depending on the speaker. As with commands, listeners may expect a reason, as in \REF{ex:moh:34}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:34}Negative future hortative \\  
    \glll \textbf{Tóhsa’} thó tentewá:ta’n(e’). \\ 
         \textbf{tohsa’} tho t-en-tewa-t-a’n-e-’ \\
         \textbf{\textsc{neg}} there \textsc{dv-fut-1incl.pl-}stand-\textsc{inch-ep-pfv} \\
    \glt ‘Let’s not stand there.’\\ \medskip
    \glll Ratikwen’tsheráhrhohs. \\
         rati-kwen’tsher-a-rho-hs \\
         \textsc{m.pl.agt}-paint-\textsc{lnk-}coat-\textsc{hab}\\ 
    \glt ‘They’re painting.’ (Annette Kaia’titáhkhe’ Jacobs, Kw)
\z 

Negative questions and answers are formed with the standard negation construction. Polar questions begin with the questioned element, which is the constituent in focus, in initial position, followed by the question particle \textit{ken}, as in \REF{ex:moh:35}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:35}Polar question\\
    \glll Iowísto \textbf{ken}? \\
         io-wisto \textbf{ken}\\
         \textsc{n.pat}-be.cold.\textsc{stat} \textbf{\textsc{q}}\\
    \glt ‘Is it cold?’
\z

Their negative counterparts include the basic negative particle \textit{iah} and a negative or contrastive prefix on the verb, as in \REF{ex:moh:36}. 

\ea\label{ex:moh:36}Negative polar question\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{ken} \textbf{te}iowísto? \\
         \textbf{iah} \textbf{ken} \textbf{te}-io-wisto\\
         \textbf{not} \textbf{\textsc{q}} \textsc{\textbf{neg}-n.pat}-be.cold.\textsc{stat}\\
    \glt ‘Is\textbf{n’t} it cold?’
\z

Negative questions convey a positive presupposition. Answers address the proposition, not the presupposition. The exchange in \REF{ex:moh:37} was prompted when Mrs. Curotte noticed that her daughter was about to leave without a jacket.

\z

A negative question with a negative response is in \REF{ex:moh:38}.

\subsection{Negation in stative predications}\label{sec:moh:2.3}

There is no adjective category in Mohawk. Most meanings expressed by adjectives in other languages are expressed by verbs in Iroquoian languages. To a certain extent, stative verbs denoting permanent qualities occur with grammatical agent prefixes, and those denoting temporary situations occur with grammatical patient prefixes, but paradigm choice is lexicalized with each verb. The two are negated with the standard construction, consisting of the negative particle \textit{iah} and either the negative \textit{te’-} or the contrastive \textit{th-} prefix on the verb. A description and its negative counterpart are in \REF{ex:moh:39} and \REF{ex:moh:40}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:39}Description\\
    \glll Wahétken.\\
         w-ahetk-en\\
         \textsc{n.agt}-be.bad-\textsc{stat} \\
    \glt ‘It was terrible.’
\z

\ea\label{ex:moh:40} Negative description\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{te}wahétken.\\
         \textbf{iah} \textbf{te}-w-ahetk-en\\
         \textbf{not} \textbf{\textsc{neg}}\textsc{-n.agt}-be.bad-\textsc{stat}\\
    \glt ‘It was\textbf{n’t} terrible.’ (Kanatí:res Grace Franks, W)
\z

There may or may not be an incorporated noun evoking the entity described. Examples \REF{ex:moh:41} and \REF{ex:moh:42} contain the incorporated noun stem \textit{{}-hsenn-} ‘name’.

\ea\label{ex:moh:41}Description with incorporated noun\\
    \glll Kahsenní:io. \\
         ka-hsenn-iio\\
         \textsc{n}-name-be.nice.\textsc{stat}\\
    \glt ‘It’s a nice name.’ (Dorris Montour, Kw)
\z

\ea\label{ex:moh:42}Negative description\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{te}kahsenní:io.\\
         \textbf{iah} \textbf{te}-ka-hsenn-iio\\
         \textbf{not} \textsc{\textbf{neg}-n-}name-be.nice.\textsc{stat}\\
    \glt ‘It’s \textbf{not} a nice name.’
\z

Location is also predicated with verbs and negated like other verbs, as in \REF{ex:moh:43} and \REF{ex:moh:44}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:43}Locative predication\\
    \glll Shé:kon tkanónhsote’.\\
         shekon t-ka-nonhs-ot-e-’\\
         still \textsc{cis-n.agt}-house-stand-\textsc{ep-stat}\\
    \glt ‘The house is still there.’ (Joe Awenhráthon Deer, Kw)
\z

\ea\label{ex:moh:44}Negative locative predication\\
    \glll \textbf{Iah} áro’k \textbf{te}kontì:teron’ ne kítkit. \\
         \textbf{iah} aro’k \textbf{te}-kont-i’teron-’ ne kitkit\\
         \textbf{not} yet \textbf{\textsc{neg}}\textsc{-z.pl.agt}-dwell-\textsc{stat} \textsc{art} chicken \\
    \glt ‘The chickens were\textbf{n’t} in there yet.’ (Ima Johnson, O)
\z

Existence is similarly predicated with verbs and negated accordingly, as in \REF{ex:moh:45} and \REF{ex:moh:46}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:45}Existential predication\\
    \glll Tóhka’s ki’ nikahéhtaien’ thí:ken. \\
         tohkara’=se’s ki’ ni-ka-heht-a-ien-’ thiken\\
         several=formerly in.fact \textsc{par-n.agt}-garden-\textsc{lnk-}lie-\textsc{stat} that\\
    \glt ‘There were several gardens there.’ (Joe Awenhráthon Deer, Kw)
\z

\ea\label{ex:moh:46}Negative existential predication\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} kwi’ ne’ lektic \textbf{te}kahahseró:tahkwe’ ne: thò:ne.\\
         \textbf{iah} ki’+wahi’ ne:’ {lektic} \textbf{te}-ka-hahser-ot-ahkwe’ ne: thohne\\
         \textbf{not} in.fact+\textsc{tag} that {electric} \textbf{\textsc{neg}}\textsc{-n}-light-stand-\textsc{pst} that there\\
    \glt ‘There were \textbf{no} electric lights at that time.’ (Josie Jacobs Day, Kw)
\z

Possession is also predicated with verbs and negated with the standard construction. The most common verb of possession is \textit{{}-ien}, which is also the verb meaning ‘to be sitting or lying’. An example of basic predicative possession is in \REF{ex:moh:47}, and of negative predicative possession in \REF{ex:moh:48}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:47}Predicative possession: Margaret Kahentawákhon McDonald, A\\
    \glll Nia’té:kon rotí:ien’. \\
         n-ia’-te-k-on roti-ien-’ \\
         \textsc{par-trl-dv-n-}amount.\textsc{stat} \textsc{m.dpl.pat}-have-\textsc{stat}\\
    \glt ‘They have a lot of things.’
\z

\newpage
\ea\label{ex:moh:48}Negated predicative possession: John Maracle, O\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{te}hó:ien-’ ne tewatokwáhtha’. \\
         \textbf{iáh} \textbf{te}-hó:-ien-’ ne te-w-atokw-áht-ha’ \\
         \textbf{not} \textsc{\textbf{neg}-m.sg.pat}-have-\textsc{stat} \textsc{art} \textsc{dv-n.agt}-scattered-\textsc{caus-hab}\\
    \glt ‘He \textbf{didn’t} have a spreader.’
\z

As noted earlier, many words used as referring expressions are morphological verbs. Most kinship terms originated as verbs, like that in \REF{ex:moh:49}. When they predicate a relationship, they are negated in the standard way, as in \REF{ex:moh:50}. 

\ea\label{ex:moh:49}Predicating a relationship\\
    \glll Shakotere’okòn:’a nè:’e.\\
         shako-atere’=okon’=a ne’e \\
         \textsc{m.sg>3dpl}-have.as.grandchild=\textsc{distr=dim} it.is\\
    \glt ‘He has them as grandchildren’ = ‘He is their grandfather.’
\z

\ea\label{ex:moh:50} Negation of a relationship\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{te}hshakotere’okon:’a.\\
         \textbf{iah} \textbf{te}-hshako-atere’=okon’=a\\
         \textbf{not} \textbf{\textsc{neg}}\textsc{-m.sg>3dpl}-have.as.grandchild=\textsc{distr=dim} \textbf{not} \textbf{not} does he have them as grandchildren \\
    \glt ‘He is \textbf{not} their grandfather.’ (He’s just a friend.)
\z

Nominal possessive constructions distinguish alienable and inalienable possession, but since these kinship terms are not morphological nouns, there is no comparable distinction. When such verbs function as predicates in equative constructions, a demonstrative is used as in \REF{ex:moh:51}, or the verb \textit{í:ken}’ ‘it is’ as in \REF{ex:moh:52}. In their negative counterparts, the verb ‘be’ is negated in the standard way: \textit{iah tè:ken’} ‘it is not’, as in \REF{ex:moh:53}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:51}Equation\\
    \glll Shakotere’okòn:’a thí:.\\
         shako-atere’=okon’=a thiken \\
         \textsc{m.sg>3dpl}-have.as.grandchild=\textsc{distr=dim} that\\
    \glt ‘That is their grandfather.’
\z

\ea\label{ex:moh:52}Equation\\
    \glll Shakotere’okòn:’a í:ken’. \\
         shako-atere’=okon’=a i-ka-i-’\\
         \textsc{m.sg>3dpl}-have.as.grandchild=\textsc{distr=dim} \textsc{proth-n.agt}-be-\textsc{stat}\\
    \glt ‘That is their grandfather.’
\z

\ea\label{ex:moh:53}Negative equation\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} shakotere’okòn:’a \textbf{tè:ken’}. \\ 
         \textbf{iah} shako-atere’=okon’=a \textbf{te’-ka-i-’} \\
         \textbf{not} \textsc{m.sg>3dpl}-have.as.grandchild\textsc{=distr=dim} \textbf{\textsc{neg-n.agt}-be-\textsc{stat}} \\
    \glt ‘That’s \textbf{not} their grandfather.’ (The guy next to him is.)
\z 

The morphological verb \textit{rató:rats} is literally ‘he hunts’, but it is also the term for ‘hunter’. The word in \REF{ex:moh:54} is a morphological verb, but it can be used either to predicate or to refer.

\ea\label{ex:moh:54}
    \glll rató:rats \\ 
         ra-atorat-s \\
         \textsc{m.sg.agt}-hunt-\textsc{hab}\\
    \glt ‘he hunts’ = ‘hunter’
\z 

When used to describe his activities, it is negated with the standard construction, as in \REF{ex:moh:55}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:55}
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} \textbf{te}ható:rats. \\ 
          \textbf{iah} \textbf{te}-ha-atorat-s\\
          \textbf{not} \textbf{\textsc{neg}}\textsc{-m.sg.agt}-hunt-\textsc{hab}\\
    \glt ‘He \textbf{doesn’t} hunt.’
\z 

For proper inclusion the negated verb ‘be’ is used, as in \REF{ex:moh:56}. 

\ea\label{ex:moh:56}Proper inclusion\\
    \glll \textbf{Iáh} rato:rat-s \textbf{tè:ken}.\\
         \textbf{iah} ra-atorat-s \textbf{te’-ka-i}\\ 
         \textbf{not} \textsc{m.sg.agt}-hunt-\textsc{hab} \textsc{\textbf{neg}-n-}be.\textsc{stat}\\
    \glt ‘He is \textbf{not} a hunter.’\\ 
\z

\subsection{Negation in non-main clauses}\label{sec:moh:2.4}

All Mohawk verbs are finite. Most dependent clauses are negated in the same way as independent sentences, with the particle \textit{iah} plus a negative or contrastive prefix on the predicate, as in \REF{ex:moh:57}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:57}Negative complement\\
    \glll Wa’onkhró:ri’ [tsi \textbf{iáh} \textbf{te}honhrónkha’].\\
         wa’-onk-hrori-’ tsi \textbf{iah} \textbf{te}-hon-aronk-ha’ \\
         \textsc{fact-fi>1sg}-tell-\textsc{pfv} that \textbf{not} \textsc{\textbf{neg}-m.pl.agt}-understand.a.language-\textsc{hab}\\ 
    \glt ‘They told me that they \textbf{don’t} understand.’ (Susie Lynch, T) 
\z

Irrealis verbs frequently occur in dependent clauses, as in \REF{ex:moh:58}.

\ea\label{ex:moh:58}Irrealis complement\\
    \glll Rotina’khwèn:’en, \\ 
         roti-na’khwen-’-en \\ 
         \textsc{m.pl.pat}-be.angry-\textsc{inch-stat} \\
    \glt ‘They were angry’\\ \medskip 
    \glll tsi \textbf{iah} \textbf{thá}on:ton’, \\ 
          tsi \textbf{iah} \textbf{th}-\textbf{aa}-w-aton-’ \\
          that \textbf{not} \textbf{\textsc{cntr-irr}}-\textsc{n.agt}-be.possible-\textsc{pfv} \\
    \glt ‘that they \textbf{could} \textbf{not}’\\ \medskip
    \glll   iahatiiá:ken’ne’. \\ 
           i-aa-hati-iaken’n-e-’ \\
           \textsc{trl-irr-m.pl.agt}-go.out-\textsc{ep-pfv}\\
    \glt   ‘go out.’ (Annette Kaia’titáhkhe’ Jacobs, Kw)
\z 
But as in main clauses, if there is a deontic sense, the second negative particle \textit{tóhsa’} is used, with no prefix on the verb, as in \REF{ex:moh:59}.

{\ea\label{ex:moh:59}Negative deontic irrealis\\

         ionkwa-ate-rien’tar-e-’ \\

    \glt ‘We knew’\\ \medskip 
    \glll  \textbf{tóhsa’} thó ni\textbf{a}iákwe’. \\ 
          \textbf{tóhsa’} tho ni-\textbf{aa}-iakwa-e-’ \\

    \glt  ‘that we were \textbf{not} supposed to go there.’ (Watshenní:ne’ Sawyer, Ks)
\z}

The same construction is used in negative purpose clauses ‘so that not’, ‘in order that not’, as in \REF{ex:moh:60}.

    \glll  \textbf{tóhsa’} iahshonkwakarón:ni’ \\

\subsection{Negative indefinites and quantifiers}\label{sec:moh:3.2}

Indefinite pronouns are negated with the standard construction, consisting of an initial negative particle, an indefinite word, and a negative or contrastive prefix on the predicate if the particle is \textit{iah}, or no prefix if the particle is \textit{tóhsa’}. In most cases the indefinite word is the same as in non-negatives. The equivalent of ‘no one’ is ‘not anyone, not someone, not who’. The interrogative \textit{ónhka’} ‘who’ can be seen in \REF{ex:moh:68}, the indefinite \textit{ónka’k} ‘someone, anyone’ in \REF{ex:moh:69} and \REF{ex:moh:70}, and the negative \textit{iah ónhka’} ‘no one’, literally ‘not anyone’, in \REF{ex:moh:70}.

\subsection{Negative derivation and case marking}

As noted earlier, there is no nominal case marking in Mohawk. There are also no particles or derivational affixes with meanings comparable to English \textit{without} or \textit{{}-less}, In many situations, ideas expressed with nominals in other languages are expressed with verbs. \section{Other aspects of negation}

\subsection{The scope of negation}

Issues of scope in the negation of indefinite and quantifier constituents were discussed in \sectref{sec:moh:3.2}. \subsection{Negative polarity}

There are few negative polarity items. One is the particle \textit{áre’kho}, which normally occurs in negative constructions to mean ‘not yet’. It is sometimes pronounced \textit{áro’khe} and is often shortened to \textit{áro’k}. In fast speech, the negative particle \textit{iah}, which occurs at or near the beginning of the clause, can be barely audible or even omitted.

\subsection{Miscellaneous aspects of negation}

Negative constructions within the Iroquoian family can be seen to have developed via Jespersen cycles (\citealt{Mithun1995affixation,Mithun2016}), a not uncommon trajectory. The family consists of two main branches, Southern Iroquoian, now represented only by Cherokee, and Northern Iroquoian. Within the Northern branch, the first group to separate became the Tuscarora-Nottoway, followed by the Wendat \linebreak (Huron)-Wyandot. The remaining Northern people, known as Iroquois, became the Seneca, Cayuga, Susquehannock, Onondaga, Oneida, and Mohawk.

The negative prefix \textit{te’-} was apparently grammaticalized early in the development of Iroquois verb morphology, because it is one of the innermost prefixes, occurring  immediately before the cislocative, repetitive, or pronominal prefix, as can be seen in \tabref{tab:moh:2}. It has cognates in all of the Iroquois languages. This early attachment is not surprising. Negation is extremely common in daily speech, and frequency can lead to the processing of a recurring sequence of words as a chunk, here a negative particle and a following verb, then fusion. But negation is also usually one of the most important parts of the message. Once fused with the verb, the negative prefix could no longer carry independent stress. And as more prefixes were added to the verb over time, the inner position of the negative prefix would have further reduced its salience.

Speakers of the various Iroquois languages remedied the mismatch between form and function in slightly different ways. For verbs with additional prepronominal prefixes, Seneca speakers reorganized the prefix order, moving the negative \textit{te’-} outward to a position before newer prefixes. Cayuga, Onondaga, Oneida, and Mohawk speakers opted instead to exploit an existing outer prefix for this purpose in these situations, the Contrastive \textit{th-}.

But even word-initial prefixes are less salient than separate words. They cannot be given emphatic stress, and they are usually small, in these languages often little more than a single syllable or less. Cayuga, Onondaga, Oneida, and Mohawk speakers began reinforcing their negative constructions with additional particles based on their words for ‘no’, the pattern still seen in the match between the \textit{iah} of the Mohawk negative construction and the answer \textit{iah} ‘no’. The origin of this particle is no longer discernible. These reinforcements apparently occurred after the languages had separated, since they are not cognate across the languages.

\section{Summary}

In concert with the typological profile of Mohawk as polysynthetic and dependent marking, with a preponderance of verbs and no non-finite clauses, the same constructions are used for most negation. The standard negative construction consists of a negative particle \textit{iáh} at or near the beginning of the clause, plus a negative prefix \textit{te’-} or contrastive prefix \textit{th-} on the verbal predicate. This construction is used for declarative clauses, questions, answers, and descriptive, locative, existential, and possessive predications. Equation and proper inclusion are negated with the negative particle \textit{iah} and the negated form of the verb ‘be’, with the negative prefix. They negate both independent and dependent clauses, as well as constituents, including indefinite pronouns and quantifiers.

There is, however, an interplay between the standard negation construction and tense, aspect, and modality. Event verbs are inflected for habitual, perfective, or stative aspect, except for commands. Negative factual perfectives (usually interpreted as past) differ slightly from their positive counterparts. Negation of past events is accomplished with negative statives, interpreted as perfects. Negation of future events and recurring events in the past is accomplished with negative irrealis perfectives. 

Commands, future imperatives, and hortatives are negated with a different marker \textit{tóhsa’} and no prefix on the verb. Irrealis perfectives with a deontic obligation sense (‘should, be supposed to’) or purposive sense (‘on order that’) are negated with the same particle \textit{tóhsa’} and no prefix on the verb.

Sentence fragments which lack a predicate are negated simply with a negative particle \textit{iah} or \textit{tóhsa’}.
