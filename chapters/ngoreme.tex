\title{Negation in Ngoreme}
\abstract{This chapter is an overview of various aspects of negation in Ngoreme, a Lacustrine Bantu language spoken in the Mara Region of Tanzania. Ngoreme marks standard negation via verbal morphology, either with the prefix \textit{ta}- following the subject marker (termed post-initial) or the prefix \textit{te}- preceding the subject marker (termed pre-initial). These forms are in free variation in standard negation, reflecting an ongoing process of change from the original post-initial marking to the pre-initial one, likely due to the influence of related languages to the north. We also describe negation in non-main clauses, where only the older post-initial marking can be used, and the use of various negative auxiliaries in certain contexts, such as prohibitives and the nondum (‘not yet’). We also cover non-verbal negation as well as various other related phenomena such as negative indefinites and reinforcement of negation. Finally, the chapter includes a separate discussion of diachrony.

\keywords{Mara, Bantu, negation, variation, language change}}

\section{The language}\label{sec:ngo:1}

Ngoreme (ISO: \href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ngq}{ngq}, Glottocode: \href{https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/ngur1263}{ngur1263}), known as \textit{ekéŋɡoɾeme} in the language, is a Lacustrine Bantu language spoken in the Mara Region of Tanzania by some 55,000 speakers (\citealt{HillEtAl2007}, \citealt{LoT2009}). The Mara Region covers an area between Lake Victoria and the Serengeti National Park, and the Ngoreme speakers are found in the central part of the area, neighbouring Kuria in the north, west and east, and the more closely related languages Ikizu, Ishenyi and Nata to the south. There is also a concentration of Ngoreme speakers found in the small town of Mugumu on the eastern edge of the region.

The Mara Region is an area of high linguistic diversity. Some twenty Bantu varieties are spoken in the area, along with the Nilotic languages Datooga (South Nilotic), Luo (West Nilotic) and Maasai (East Nilotic) \citep{HillEtAl2007}. There are also claims of a historical presence of Cushitic languages in the region (\citealt{Ehret1971}; \citealt{Schoenbrun1994}, \citeyear{Schoenbrun1997}).

Bantu languages, grouped in the Benue-Congo branch of the Atlantic-Congo phylum, are commonly referred to using a system of alphanumeric codes following Guthrie (\citeyear{Guthrie1948, Guthrie1967--71}) and updated by Maho (\citeyear{Maho2003,Maho2009}).\footnote{This system is based on the division of language into 16 geographic zones, each of which is assigned a letter. This descriptive classification is geographic rather than genetic in nature. In this paper, we make reference to both the Guthrie codes and the ISO coding systems to facilitate use both by those familiar with the Guthrie classification system and those who are used to the ISO system.} Under this system, Ngoreme is classified as JE401 although it is also often thought to constitute a subgroup with other languages under the title of the Mara languages (\citealt[9]{Nurse1999}; \citealt[519]{NurseMuzale1999}; \citealt[170]{NursePhilippson2003}), specifically along with Ikizu (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ngq}{ikz}; JE402), Ikoma Ishenyi, Nata (with shared codes \href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ngq}{ntk}; JE45) and Zanaki (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ngq}{zak}; JE44), with which it forms the South Mara branch. 
The other branch of Mara languages is the North Mara group of Gusii (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ngq}{guz}; JE42), Kabwa (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ngq}{cwa}; JE405), Kuria (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ngq}{kuj}; JE43) and Simbiti (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ngq}{ssc}; JE431) \citep{AunioEtAl2019}; the Suguti languages are also spoken in the Mara Region. All of these languages, with the exception of Gusii and Kuria in Kenya, are spoken within the Mara Region. While all of the Mara Bantu languages are classified in the Lacustrine (or Great Lakes) branch of Bantu (\citealt{Schoenbrun1994}, \citeyear{Schoenbrun1997}), it is not clear that they constitute a genealogical grouping (\citealt{Walker2013}; \citealt{Roth2018}). However, the unity of the South Mara branch seems to be confirmed, along with Ngoreme’s inclusion therein, despite a relatively strong influence from neighbouring Kuria (\citealt{RothGibson2019}).

Despite it being a relatively small language in terms of the number of speakers, the language is vibrant and spoken by people of all ages. At the latest by the time they reach primary school, Ngoreme-speakers are exposed to Swahili – the official and national language in Tanzania, as well as the medium of instruction in all government primary schools. Swahili is the \textit{lingua franca} used in all official dealings and in inter-ethnic communication. Although Ngoreme is a language of the home and of the village, Swahili may still be preferred as the medium of communication in towns and cities. There is no official Ngoreme language education at any level, nor is the language afforded any formal status in the country.

Two main dialects of Ngoreme have been identified, a southern dialect (Mwibara) and a northern dialect (Rogoro)\footnote{Although \textit{ɾoɣóɾo} actually means ‘east’ in Ngoreme.}, which are mutually intelligible. For the most part, dialectal variation is not central to this paper and is only marked when this adds something to the broader discussion. 

In phonological terms, Ngoreme is a fairly typical Bantu language. It exhibits a syllable structure in which the most common syllable is the open syllable CV(V) where the vowels are of the same quality (i.e., a long vowel). The consonant inventory is typical for the area and Bantu more generally; a dissimilation process called Dahl’s Law (\citealt{DavyNurse1982}) is active, whereby voiceless stops are voiced when the following syllable also has a voiceless consonant (\citealt[507]{AunioEtAl2019}). The voiced counterpart of /k/ in the system is the fricative /ɣ/, and the reflexes of Bantu \textit{*b} and \textit{*d} are /β/ and /ɾ/, respectively, unless prenasalized (resulting in /mb/ and /nd/, respectively). There is no /p/. Ngoreme is a tonal language and exhibits a privative tone system, i.e., one with an opposition between \textsc{h}, marked with an acute accent throughout this paper,\footnote{There is some variation and fluctuation in the tones in Ngoreme, and the system is not yet fully analysed. The examples in this paper are tone marked according to our best current understanding, but this should be considered a preliminary analysis. We are thankful to Lotta Aunio for her help with the tones.} and Ø, left unmarked; the tone bearing unit is the syllable \citep[196]{Aunio2017}.

Ngoreme has a 7-vowel system /i e ɛ a u o ɔ/, with an \textsc{atr} distinction in the mid-vowels (\citealt[503]{AunioEtAl2019}). However, the system seems to be in a state of transition towards a 5-vowel one /i ɛ a u ɔ/, with all mid-vowels being recategorized as $-$\textsc{atr}. This can be seen synchronically in the split in the lexicon in which only five vowels are used in verbs, while nouns have seven vowels, reflecting the earlier historic vowel system. There are also inconsistencies in \textsc{atr}{}-based vowel harmony processes \citep[506]{AunioEtAl2019}.

Ngoreme exhibits a dominant SVO constituent order and head-marking morphology. Bantu languages are known for their systems of noun classes which function as grammatical genders. Ngoreme is no exception to this and has 19 noun classes. By convention, these noun classes are numbered. In the typical Bantu fashion, classes 1–10 in Ngoreme represent singular-plural pairs. Classes above this include those that form their plurals in other distinct classes (e.g., class 11 which has most of its plurals in class 10, but also a handful in classes 6 and 14; cf. \citealt[61--62]{Laine2023}). There are also two diminutive classes (classes 12 and 19) and an augmentative class (class 20). Class 15 contains verbal nouns – or infinitives – which similarly have no singular versus plural distinction. Another widespread Bantu feature is the use of locative classes. Ngoreme has three locative classes (16,17,18) which represent specific, general and internal location, respectively.

Nominal forms typically comprise of the noun stem, a nominal prefix (as determined by the noun class) and a pre-prefix. This pre-prefix is also termed the augment in the wider literature on Bantu – a term that we too will employ here. The augment is glossed \textsc{aug}, but there is no connection to an augmentative function. Across the language family, the presence of the augment is dependent on a number of factors which often seem to relate to specificity, topicality and definiteness either synchronically or at least diachronically (\citealt{Blois1970}; \citealt{Velde2019}). The augment is also sensitive to negative polarity and focus. Since the augment does not typically appear in negative contexts, it is absent from many of the examples in the current paper (see further discussion in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.3}). The nominal prefix is often phonologically determined with variant forms appearing in different contexts. Nominal dependents such as adjectives, numerals, demonstratives and adnominals also exhibit agreement with the noun class of the nominal they modify.

The Ngoreme verb is constructed in the usual Bantu manner in which not all elements of the verb are obligatorily present, but those that are must appear in a specific order. The verbal complex is agglutinative, and prefixes and suffixes combine with an obligatory verb stem to encode a range of grammatical distinctions such as tense-aspect-mood, polarity and associated motion. The structure of the verb is outlined in \figref{fig:ngo:1} below.

This structure is exemplified in \REF{ex:ngo:1} where the pre-initial marker appears as \textit{te-} and encodes negation, the initial position contains the subject marker \textit{βa{}-} (which although indexing third person plural, also belongs to noun class 2 -- hence the “2” in the glossing) and the pre-radical appears as the past tense marker \textit{á-}. The radical is \textit{-ɣi} ‘go’ and the final positive hosts the perfective marker -\textit{iɾe}. Similarly, in \REF{ex:ngo:2} the prefix \textit{βa}{}- appears in the initial position and the imperfective aspectual prefix appears in the pre-radical position. However, in this example, the applicative marker \textit{{}-ɛɾ} also appears in the pre-final position.

\z

The language also employs auxiliary forms to encode a range of tense-aspect-mood distinctions (see also \sectref{sec:ngo:2.2}). In auxiliary constructions, an auxiliary form is typically followed (although see also \sectref{sec:ngo:4.9}) by a lexical main verb with temporal and aspectual information distributed across the two forms.

\section{Clausal negation}\label{sec:ngo:2}
\subsection{Standard negation}\label{sec:ngo:2.1}

Standard negation in Ngoreme is achieved by adding a negative prefix to the verb. The prefix is most typically \textit{te-} which appears before all other markers in the verbal complex, preceding the subject marker in the so-called pre-initial position (see \figref{fig:ngo:1} above). While affirmative main clause verb forms are commonly – albeit far from necessarily – marked with the focus marker \textit{N-}, this does not appear in negative constructions.\footnote{In Ngoreme, the pre-initial prefix \textit{N}{}- is typically used to convey verb focus or assertion about the event depicted in the (predicate) verb and may be omitted when such criteria are not met. However, there are some affirmative contexts where pre-initial \textit{N}{}- is obligatorily omitted. These include focus construction where focus is placed on some constituent other than the (predicate) verb (e.g., in content questions and answers), as well as relative and other subordinate constructions (i.e., in non-main clauses). Absence of a focus nasal may also be used to convey modal readings of uncertainty. See \citet{AunioEtAl2019}, \citet[35--36]{Roth2018}, \citet{BernanderLaine2020} for more elaboration on pre-initial \textit{N}{}- and its function(s) in Ngoreme and its closest relatives.} Compare the affirmative and negative examples in \REF{ex:ngo:3} and \REF{ex:ngo:4} below. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:3} 
    \gll m-ba-a-ɣí-íɾe mwanza \\ 
        \textsc{foc-sbj2{}-pst}{}-go-\textsc{pfv} Mwanza\\
    \glt ‘They went to Mwanza.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:4} 
    \gll te-βá-á-ɣi-iɾe mwanza \\ 
     \textsc{neg-sbj2-pst}{}-go-\textsc{pfv} Mwanza\\
    \glt ‘They did not go to Mwanza.’
\z 

There is, however, an alternative negative marker: a prefix \textit{ta-,} which can occur between the subject and \textsc{tam} markers in the \textit{post-}initial slot of the verbal complex. Thus, example \REF{ex:ngo:4} above can also be formed as in \REF{ex:ngo:5}:

\ea\label{ex:ngo:5} 
    \gll βa-tá-á-ɣi-iɾe mwanza \\ 
         \textsc{sbj2-neg{}-pst}{}-go-\textsc{pfv} Mwanza\\
     \glt ‘They did not go to Mwanza.’
\z 
 

The strategy involving the post-initial marker is the only available means of negating verbs in other South Mara languages, while the pre-initial \textit{te-} (or a related form) is used in the North Mara languages, including Kuria, with which Ngoreme has been in contact. The pre-initial marker thus appears to be an innovation in Ngoreme. The choice of marker in Ngoreme seems to be a matter of free variation: there is no difference in meaning, and the same speakers will alternate between the two forms in similar environments. While speakers are aware of dialectal differences and recognize certain grammatical variations as dialectal, they do not consider the choice of the negative prefix to represent such a variation, and speakers accept both forms.

The idea that the post-initial negation marker in Ngoreme is the older form and the pre-initial one an innovation is further supported by the fact that, of the two prefixes, in negated subordinate clauses, only the post-initial marker may be used (see also \sectref{sec:ngo:2.2}, \sectref{sec:ngo:2.4} and the discussion in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.8}). 
This is also true of copula verbs (see also \sectref{sec:ngo:2.3}). An additional twist in the position of the pre-initial negative marker in relation to the subject prefix arises when the subject is either 2\textsc{sg} or noun class 1, 3 or 9. In these cases, the subject prefix is a single vowel (\textit{o-, a-, o-} and \textit{e-,} respectively), and in negative verbs, this fuses with the negative marker resulting in the forms \textit{to-, ta-, to-} and \textit{te-}. In negative main clauses, these are the only forms available with these subjects. However, in subordinate clauses, these forms will obligatorily separate into a subject marker followed by the post-initial negative \textit{{}-ta-}: \textit{o-ta-} (\textsc{sbj2sg-neg),} etc. (see also \sectref{sec:ngo:2.4}).

The affirmative imperfective prefix is \textit{ɾa-},\footnote{The /ɾ/ is strengthened to /d/ when prenasalized.} as in \REF{ex:ngo:6}. However, in negated forms, an alternative imperfective marker \textit{(V)ko-}\footnote{<V> here stands for an unspecified vowel, that is, a vowel which merely lengthens the preceding vowel and lacks a vowel quality of its own. The marker \textit{Vko-} is used for both affirmative and negative imperfectives in Ngoreme’s closest relatives Ikoma, Ishenyi and Nata. The affirmative \textit{ɾa-} seems to be a Ngoreme innovation, and most likely derived from the progressive \textit{ɾa-} still found in the other languages. See \sectref{sec:ngo:2.3} on negative copulas for another instance where Ngoreme preserves an older form in negation.} is used together with the negative prefix, as in \REF{ex:ngo:7}. 
\ea\label{ex:ngo:6} 
     \gll ɾééɾo n{}-dá{}-ɣi ku{}-ɾɛm{}-a\\ 
        today \textsc{sbj1sg-ipfv}{}-go \textsc{inf}{}-farm-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘Today I am going farming.’
\z 
 
\ea\label{ex:ngo:7} 
    \gll ɾééɾo té{}-ŋ{}-ko{}-ɣi ku{}-ɾɛm{}-a \\ 
     today \textsc{neg-sbj1sg-ipfv}{}-go \textsc{inf}{}-farm-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘Today I am not going farming.’
\z 

Unlike many of its neighbouring languages and the Bantu languages more generally, Ngoreme lacks a morphological progressive form. Instead, a periphrastic progressive with a copular auxiliary is commonly used in the affirmative as in \REF{ex:ngo:8}. The negated equivalent of this form is most often a negated imperfective as in \REF{ex:ngo:9}, but it is also possible to negate the auxiliary, in which case auxiliary inversion does not occur (see \sectref{sec:ngo:4.9} for more on inverted auxiliary constructions). There is thus an asymmetric tendency here, but the alternation is not fully grammaticalized; see \tabref{tab:ngo:1} below, along with the accompanying note on paradigmatic asymmetry.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:8} 
    \gll ŋ-ko-ɾjɛɾ-a βa{}-aní\\ 
    \textsc{foc{}-inf}{}-run-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj}2-\textsc{prs.cop}
    \\
    \glt ‘They are running.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:9} 
    \gll te-βá-áko-ɾjɛɾ-a\\ 
    \textsc{neg-sbj2-ipfv}{}-run-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘They are not running.’
\z 

Perfective aspect in Ngoreme is coded with the marker \textit{{}-iɾe/-iɾi}, a reflex of a common Bantu suffix and the Proto-Bantu form *\textit{{}-ide}. The \textsc{tam} prefix slot is left empty. The variation in the translation of \REF{ex:ngo:10} shows that change-of-state verb like ‘sleep’ ({\textasciitilde} ‘fall asleep’) conjugated in this verb form may either gain a perfect(ive) or a (present) resultative reading, depending on clause-external issues of viewpoint or the general context in which the propositions are uttered. See \citet{BotneForthcoming} on this common behaviour of the Bantu perfect(ive) and \citet[54–70]{Roth2018} on Western Serengeti specifically.
In negation, either the pre- or post-initial negative prefix may be used, as illustrated in example \REF{ex:ngo:11a} and \REF{ex:ngo:11b}, respectively.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:10} 
    \gll m-bá-mis-iɾi\\ 
        \textsc{foc}{}-\textsc{sbj}2-sleep-\textsc{pfv}\\
    \glt ‘They have slept.’ (‘They are asleep.’ / ‘They slept.’)
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:11}
    \ea\label{ex:ngo:11a}
        \gll βa-tá{}-mis-iɾi \\
         \textsc{sbj2-neg}{}-sleep-\textsc{pfv} \\
        \glt ‘They have not slept.’ 
    \ex\label{ex:ngo:11b}
        \gll te-βá-mis-iɾi\\
          \textsc{neg-sbj2}{}-sleep-\textsc{pfv}\\
        \glt ‘They have not slept.’ 
     \z
\z

The past tense is formed by combining a tense prefix \textit{a-} with the perfective form, as illustrated in examples \REF{ex:ngo:12} and \REF{ex:ngo:13}. The past tense is thus only differentiated from the perfective by vowel length preceding the macrostem: \textit{βatámisiɾi} ‘they have not slept’, as in \REF{ex:ngo:11a}, or \textit{βatáámisiɾi} ‘they did not sleep’.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:12} 
    \gll n-a-a-ɾúɣ{}-iɾe e{}-βj{}-ákoɾi ít͡ʃo\\ 
        \textsc{foc-sbj}1\textsc{{}-pst}{}-cook-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{aug{}-}8-food yesterday\\
    \glt ‘He cooked food yesterday.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:13} 
    \gll tá-á-ɾuɣ-iɾe βj-ákoɾi ít͡ʃo\\ 
    \textsc{neg.sbj1{}-pst}{}-cook-\textsc{pfv} 8-food yesterday \\
    \glt ‘I did not cook food yesterday.’
\z 

Other morphological \textsc{tam} categories in Ngoreme include the habitual (\textit{háa-/ɾáa-}),\footnote{Ngoreme has two habitual markers, \textit{háá-} and \textit{ɾáá-}, apparently in free variation. \citet[73]{Roth2018} mentions the possibility that the latter is an emergent form analogically related to the habitual \textit{háá-} and the imperfective \textit{ɾa-}.} the (counterfactive) conditional (\textit{nɡa-}), the narrative (\textit{Vka-}) and the situative (\textit{ko-}).\footnote{Ngoreme is analysed as having a situative formed with the prefix \textit{ko-} by \citet{Roth2018}. See, however, \citet[171–174]{Laine2023} for a critical discussion.} Of these, only habitual forms, as in \REF{ex:ngo:14} and \REF{ex:ngo:15}, are found negated in our data: All instances make use of the post-initial marker.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:14} 
    \gll βa-ta-ɾáá-ɾjɛɾ-a\\ 
     \textsc{sbj}2-\textsc{neg-hab}{}-run-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘They don’t run (regularly).’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:15} 
    \gll tu-ɾá-β-a tu-ta-ɾáá-βin-a \\ 
         \textsc{sbj1pl-ipfv-}be\textsc{-fv} \textsc{sbj1pl-neg-hab}-sing-\textsc{fv}\\
     \glt ‘We will not be singing (regularly).’
\z 

In narrative and situative contexts, other negated forms, mainly the past and imperfective, respectively, are used instead. In conditionals, a negated auxiliary formally similar to the periphrastic progressive is used as in example \REF{ex:ngo:16} below.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:16} 
    \gll ti-n-ááɾe ko-ɣí musoma hata kama o-mu-nɛnɛ a-nɡa-n-tɔm-íɾe\\ 
        \textsc{neg-sbj1sg-pst.cop} \textsc{inf}{}-go Musoma even if \textsc{aug}{}-1-fat \textsc{sbj1-cond-om1sg}{}-send\textsc{{}-pfv}\\
    \glt ‘I would not have gone to Musoma even if the boss had sent me.’
\z

As shown, there is quite a bit of paradigmatic asymmetry in Ngoreme (see \citealt{Miestamo2005}, \citeyear{Miestamo2013-wals113}), particularly in the imperfective domain, where only the habitual and the imperfective proper are regularly negated as such. The periphrastic progressive exhibits some variability; as discussed above, it is typically negated using the morphological imperfective, but the auxiliary verb can be negated instead, retaining the progressive construction. The asymmetry in Ngoreme seems to be a fairly clear case of neutralization of \textsc{tam} distinctions, the most common situation in asymmetric paradigms (e.g., \citealt[54ff]{Miestamo2005}).

\subsection{Negation in non-declaratives}\label{sec:ngo:2.2}

Ngoreme speakers employ different strategies for expressing negative imperatives, here also referred to as prohibitives. In accordance with a common trait across Bantu (cf. \citealt{DevosVanOlmen2013}; \citealt[191--193]{Nurse2008}; \citealt{Güldemann1999}), prohibitives are most commonly expressed in Ngoreme with a complex construction consisting of an auxiliary and a full verb occurring in the infinitive form.


As illustrated in (\ref{ex:ngo:19}--\ref{ex:ngo:21}), two different verbs are employed as prohibitive auxiliaries, viz. -\textit{tiɣ}{}-, with the source meaning ‘stop, leave off, avoid’, and \nobreakdash-\textit{Vt͡ʃ}\nobreakdash- with the source meaning ‘come’.\footnote{Recall that the <V> here stands for an unspecified vowel, that is, a vowel which merely lengthens the preceding vowel and lacks a vowel quality of its own. Note that the diacritic on /t͡ʃ/ follows IPA convention for an affricate consonant and does not span over the <V>.} When functioning as a prohibitive auxiliary, \nobreakdash-\textit{Vt͡ʃ}- furthermore contains the standard negative prefix \textit{ta}{}-.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:19} 
    \gll o-tíɣ{}-ɛ ko-ɾút-a e-siɣáɾa\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-stop-\textsc{sbjv} \textsc{inf}{}-pull-\textsc{fv} \textsc{aug}{}-9.cigarette \\
    \glt ‘Don’t smoke (the cigarette)!’
\z 


\ea\label{ex:ngo:20} 
    \gll tíɣ{}-a ɣw-ikáɾa há-no\\ 
      stop-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-become\_seated 16-\textsc{dem}\\
    \glt ‘Don’t sit here!’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:21} 
    \gll u-tá-át͡ʃ-a ko-ɾ{}-a e-ɲáma\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-\textsc{neg}{}-come-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-eat-\textsc{fv} \textsc{aug}{}-9.meat\\
    \glt ‘Don’t eat meat!’
\z 


Notice that these constructions also differ with regard to the final vowel, the construction with \nobreakdash-\textit{tiɣ}\nobreakdash- being variably inflected with the final subjunctive \textit{\nobreakdash-ɛ} \REF{ex:ngo:19} or the vowel \nobreakdash-\textit{a} of the basic imperative verb form \REF{ex:ngo:20}, in which case it also lacks subject marking. Whereas the former is typically used for milder and more polite commands and may be used for directives which include performers other than the second person, the latter is used only for second person singular directives and typically conveys a stronger command. This systematization is in accordance with the common division of labour within the affirmative imperative-hortative system in Bantu (cf. \citealt{DevosVanOlmen2013}; \citealt{NurseDevos2019}), as illustrated in \REF{ex:ngo:22} and \REF{ex:ngo:23} below. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:22} 
    \gll tuk-á\\ 
     dig-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘Dig!’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:23} 
    \gll βa-tuk-ɛ\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}2-dig-\textsc{sbjv}\\
    \glt ‘They should dig.’
\z 

The construction with \nobreakdash-\textit{Vt͡ʃ}\nobreakdash-, which employs a morphological construction with the post-initial negator and a final vowel \nobreakdash-\textit{a}, formally adheres to what is the basic, negative-imperative verb form \textsc{sbj}{}-\textit{ta}{}-B-verb in Ngoreme’s closest Western Serengeti relatives. As further discussed in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.8}, however, this is not the case in Ngoreme, where the reflex of this synthetic non-declarative negative verb form has been reinterpreted with “ironic” non-negative readings.

There is a strong tendency of a functional division of labour between the two auxiliaries which revolves around the notion of “retrospective” versus “prospective” negation (cf. \citealt{Auwera2010}). This functional partition is connected to the lexical semantics of the respective etymons of these negative auxiliaries, in line with \citegen{Hopper1991} theory of persistence in grammaticalized (or grammaticalizing) elements. Thus, whereas \nobreakdash-\textit{tiɣ}\nobreakdash- typically is used retrospectively for commanding the addressee to stop the continuation of an already initiated activity, as represented in the second infinitive verb, the construction with \nobreakdash-\textit{Vt͡ʃ}\nobreakdash-, with its de-venitive (cf. \citealt{Dahl2000}), prospective reading, more readily denotes that the addressee should not commence in the event depicted in the full verb altogether. This fact may be illustrated with the minimal pairs shown in \REF{ex:ngo:24} and \REF{ex:ngo:25} below.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:24} 
    \gll o-tíɣ{}-ɛ ku-ɣɔɾ{}-a\\ 
     \textsc{sbj}2sg-stop-\textsc{sbjv} \textsc{inf}{}-buy-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘Don’t buy!’\\ (the addressee is in the shop and s/he has already started buying items)
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:25} 
    \gll u-tá-át͡ʃ-a ku-ɣɔɾ{}-a\\ 
        2sg-\textsc{neg}{}-come-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-buy-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘Don’t buy!’ \\ (the addressee has not started the act of buying but is still on his/her way to the shop) 
\z 

The two negative auxiliaries described in this section are additionally used in other non-standard, or marked clause types, where it would seem that this retrospective-prospective division continue to hold and influence their usage patterns to some extent. This fact is further discussed in \sectref{sec:ngo:2.4}. 

As mentioned, it is possible to form a directive construction by the use of the (standard) post-initial negative morpheme \textit{ta}{}- plus the basic affirmative imperative verb form, a construction which, however, has been reinterpreted with non-negative senses. Notice however, that some Ngoreme speakers contrast this essentially affirmative expression with an actual negative imperative verb form, which includes an additional formative \textit{ka(a)}\nobreakdash- attached in a position between the negative prefix and the verb stem \REF{ex:ngo:26}.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:26} 
    \gll u-ta-káá{}-ɣi na a{}-βá-nto a{}-βa-sinéénu \\ 
        \textsc{sbj2sg}-\textsc{neg}{}-\textsc{pot}{}-go \textsc{com} \textsc{aug}{}-2-people \textsc{aug-}2-bad\\
    \glt ‘Don’t go ({\textasciitilde}get acquainted) with bad people.’ 
\z 

This prefix does not appear in non-negative constructions in Ngoreme. However, as further discussed in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.8}, it is used in several of the languages of the Northern Mara Bantu subgroup as a more general modal {\textasciitilde} irrealis {\textasciitilde} non-indicative marker (hence the glossing as \textsc{pot} = potential).

 For some speakers of Ngoreme (possibly exclusively speakers of the northern Rogoro dialect), the prohibitive construction with -\textit{Vt͡ʃ}\nobreakdash- once again has to include the additional prefix of the form \textit{ka(a)}{}-, as in \REF{ex:ngo:27}. Notice however, that due to Dahl’s Law, a type of voicing dissimilation synchronically productive in Ngoreme (cf. \sectref{sec:ngo:1} above), the velar plosive of the formative \textit{ka(a)}\nobreakdash- becomes a voiced /\textit{ɣ}/ in this construction. Additionally, the underlying sequence of three vowels yielded by the combination of this form with this verb stem, viz. °/\textit{kaa}{}-\textit{Vt͡ʃ}{}-/, is reduced to a sequence of two vowels in actual speech.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:27} 
    \gll u-ta-ɣá-át͡ʃ-a ko-ɾút-a e-siɣáɾa\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-\textsc{neg}{}-\textsc{pot}{}-come-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-pull-\textsc{fv} \textsc{aug}{}-9.cigarette\\
    \glt ‘Don’t smoke the cigarette.’
\z 

Note finally that no special negators exist for interrogative clauses. It may be mentioned, however, that the post-initial strategy is preferred in content interrogation as the predicate is extra-focal in such contexts (see the discussion around example \REF{ex:ngo:80} in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.3}).

\subsection{Negation in stative predications}\label{sec:ngo:2.3}

Affirmative constructions in the present tense involving equation, proper inclusion and attribution (which have, along with locatives, existentials and possessives, been labeled “stative predications” by e.g. \citealt[111ff]{Payne1997}; see also \citealt{Veselinova2014}) are all formed the same way in Ngoreme, with the copula \textit{nV=/N-}, as in \REF{ex:ngo:28}.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:28} 
    \gll e-ɣi-ɣɛsɔ ki-no ŋ=kj-éɣe \\ 
     \textsc{aug-}7-knife 7-\textsc{prox.dem} \textsc{cop=7-}sharp\\
    \glt ‘This knife is sharp.’
\z 

Locative predication on the other hand requires a fully inflected copula verb agreeing with the subject. There are three copula verbs lending their forms to different temporal reference points: \textit{{}-Vɾe, -Vni} and \textit{{}-ɾaβa}\footnote{The “future copula” is quite transparently the verb ‘to be(come)’ in the imperfective, but it functions as part of the copula paradigm, always has a future reading, and must be used for future stative predications. Incidentally, Ngoreme has no morphological future tense for other verbs. For more on the cross-linguistically common grammaticalization of become-verbs into future markers (see \citealt{Dahl2000} and \citealt[86--87]{KutevaEtAl2019}).} for past, present and future, respectively (see \citealt{BernanderLaine2020} for more on the copula verbs in Western Serengeti). The copula verbs take a subject marker, often preceded, like other verbs, by a focus marker. With copula constructions, however, the focus marker (formally similar and historically related to the ascriptive copula) is somewhat more productive, and may often appear on a different constituent as in \REF{ex:ngo:29}, exemplifying a present tense locative predication.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:29} 
    \gll ɲááwa ŋ=kó-mo-te a-aní\\ 
     1a.cat \textsc{foc=loc17}{}-3-tree 1\textsc{{}-prs.cop}\\
    \glt ‘The cat is in the tree.’\footnote{‘1a' in this and other glossed examples refers to the so called noun class 1a, comprising a set of nouns ‘‘that lack a prefix in the singular [but] trigger class 1 agreement. It typically contains proper names, some kinship terms, personified animals
and borrowings." \citep[240]{Velde2019}.}
\z 
    %\hc[inline,size=tiny]{what does 1a stand for in the gloss line? this is not in the abbrevs list (occurs a few other times too), should probably be changed to smallcaps as well.}

Other types of stative predications also require a copula verb in the past and future tense. Copula verbs are also used for all stative predications when negated, in which case a negative prefix is added. As with other verbs, either the pre- or post-final negator may be used with copula verbs, and there is no distinction in meaning between, say, \textit{te-ɣa-ɾe} or \textit{ɣa-ta-ɾe} ‘it (CL6) is not’. In present and future forms, the copula verb itself changes when negated. The present tense \textit{{}-Vni} becomes \-\textit{{}-ɾe,} as in \REF{ex:ngo:30} below, reflecting the fact that the affirmative present tense copula is an innovation in the Western Serengeti languages, and the older \textit{{}-ɾe} form, reconstructed for Proto-Bantu as \textit{*dɪ̀} \citep{BastinEtAl2002}, is preserved in negatives. In the future form, \textit{{}-ɾaβa} becomes \textit{{}-kuβa,} with the affirmative imperfective prefix changing to the corresponding negative marker – again an older one – as with other verbs.

Examples of negated stative predications can be seen in the examples below which show equation (\ref{ex:ngo:30}--\ref{ex:ngo:31}), proper inclusion \REF{ex:ngo:32} and attribution (\ref{ex:ngo:33}--\ref{ex:ngo:34}).

\ea\label{ex:ngo:30} 
    \gll t͡ʃóóni ta-ɾé mɾa waito \\ 
        John \textsc{sbj}1.\textsc{neg-}\textsc{prs.cop} brother our \\
    \glt ‘John is not my brother.’ (lit. ‘our brother')
\z

\ea\label{ex:ngo:31} 
    \gll ɣá-no te-ɣá-ɾe ma-hɛmba\\ 
     6-\textsc{prox.dem} \textsc{neg-sbj6-}\textsc{prs.cop} 6-millet\\
    \glt ‘This is not millet.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:32} 
    \gll eeni té{}-n{}-de mwánafunzi \\ 
     I \textsc{neg-sbj}1\textsc{sg{}-prs.cop} student\\
    \glt ‘I am not a student.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:33} 
    \gll eeni té-n-de m-támbe \\ 
     I \textsc{neg{}-sbj1sg{}-prs.cop} 1-tall\\
    \glt ‘I am not tall.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:34} 
    \gll e{}-ɣi{}-ɣɛsɔ kí{}-no te{}-ké{}-ɾe kj-éɣe\\ 
     \textsc{aug-}7-knife 7-\textsc{prox.dem} \textsc{neg}{}-7-\textsc{prs.cop} 7-sharp\\
    \glt ‘This knife is not sharp.’
\z 

Negative locative predications are seen in (\ref{ex:ngo:35}--\ref{ex:ngo:37}) below.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:35} 
    \gll e-ɣi-tá$\beta ɔ$ te-ké-ɾe ko-méza \\ 
        \textsc{aug-}7-book \textsc{neg}{}-7-\textsc{prs.cop} \textsc{loc17}{}-table\\
    \glt ‘The book is not on the table.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:36} 
    \gll te-βá-ɾe sokoni\\ 
        \textsc{neg}{}-2-\textsc{prs.cop} at\_the\_market\\
    \glt ‘They [people] are not at the market.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:37} 
    \gll ɲááwa tá-ɾe ko-mó-te\\ 
     1a.cat \textsc{sbj}1.\textsc{neg-}\textsc{prs.cop} \textsc{loc17}{}-3-tree\\
    \glt ‘The cat is not in the tree.’
\z 

In present tense third person singular contexts, subject marking may be left out in favour of the invariable negative copula \textit{teɾe} (or occasionally \textit{taɾe}). There is some variation as to whether the form \textit{teɾe} is deemed acceptable also with human subjects, with some speakers preferring \textit{taɾe,} marked for noun class 1.\footnote{There is some obscurity with these forms, since \textit{taɾe} is both a form unmarked for subject with the post-initial negator, and the class 1 form, where the negative prefix (either \textit{te-} or \textit{ta-}) and the subject marker \textit{a-} coalesce into an indivisible whole. On the other hand, \textit{teɾe,} the non-agreeing form with the pre-initial negator, is homonymous with the form marking a class 9 subject.} 

Existentials in Ngoreme are formed and negated with a dedicated existential construction involving the same copula verbs as other stative predications (cf. \citealt{BernanderLaine2020}). A type of locative existential is formed by adding a locative pro{}-form (in the terminology of \citealt{BentleyEtAl2013} and \citealt{Bentley2017}) to a plain locational clause, namely the locative verbal clitic \textit{=ho} (class 16) or \textit{=mu} (class 18). These are added to the copula verb, as exemplified in the affirmative existential in \REF{ex:ngo:38} and the negated one in \REF{ex:ngo:39}. Note that the word order in existentials is flexible and plays no role in negation (see \citealt{BernanderLaine2020} for more).

\ea\label{ex:ngo:38} 
    \gll βa-ani=hó a-βá-nto a-βa-néne a-βá-áɾu\\ 
    \textsc{sbj2-prs.cop=loc16} \textsc{aug}{}-2-person \textsc{aug}{}-2-fat \textsc{aug}{}-2-many\\
    \glt ‘There are many fat people.’
\z

\ea\label{ex:ngo:39} 
    \gll a-má-nt͡ʃe ɣa-tá-ɾe=mu mó-ndoa\\ 
        \textsc{aug-}6-water \textsc{sbj6-neg-}\textsc{prs.cop=loc18} \textsc{loc18}{}-bucket.9\\
    \glt ‘There is no water in the bucket.’
\z 

Possessive predication most typically utilizes a specific possessive verb \textit{{}-ɣɔɔtɛ} (from \textit{kuɣɔɔta,} ‘to catch, to hold’). This can be seen in the examples below where \textit{{}-ɣɔɔtɛ} appears with the first person singular marker in \REF{ex:ngo:40} and can be negated as shown in \REF{ex:ngo:41} where the negative prefix \textit{téé-} also appears. The possessive verb \textit{{}-ɣɔɔtɛ} can also be used for inanimate possessors, as in \REF{ex:ngo:42} and \REF{ex:ngo:43}. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:40} 
    \gll né-ŋ-ɡɔɔtɛ e-séése\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{sg}{}-have \textsc{aug-}9.dog\\
    \glt ‘I have a dog.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:41} 
    \gll té-ŋ{}-ɡɔɔtɛ séése\\ 
        \textsc{neg-sbj1sg}{}-have 9.dog\\
    \glt ‘I don’t have a dog.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:42} 
    \gll e-ɲúmba é-no n-é{}-ɣɔɔtɛ e{}-ɣe-séku\\ 
        \textsc{aug-}9.house 9-\textsc{prox.dem} \textsc{foc-sbj9}{}-have \textsc{aug-}7-door\\
    \glt ‘This house has a door.’ 
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:43} 
    \gll e-ɲúmba é-no te{}-ɣɔɔtɛ ɣe-séku\\ 
        \textsc{aug-}9.house 9-\textsc{prox.dem} \textsc{sbj}9.\textsc{neg}{}-have 7-door\\
    \glt ‘This house doesn’t have a door.’
\z 

Alternatively, a comitative construction with the comitative element \textit{nV,} prefixed with the subject marker, can be used. Both this and the \textit{{}-ɣɔɔtɛ} form can be negated with either a pre- or post-final negator. Note the absence of the augment in the negative construction in example \REF{ex:ngo:45}, in contrast to example  \REF{ex:ngo:44}.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:44} 
   \gll níí-ne e-séése\\ 
        \textsc{1sg-com} \textsc{aug-}9.dog\\
    \glt ‘I have a dog.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:45} 
    \gll n-tá-na séése\\ 
        \textsc{1sg-neg-com} 9.dog\\
    \glt ‘I don’t have a dog.’
\z 

\subsection{Negation in non-main clauses}\label{sec:ngo:2.4}

Different strategies of non-main clause negation can be found in Ngoreme. The first strategy is simply to use standard morphological negation. For almost all non-main clauses, however, only the post-initial prefix may be employed. For example, the relative clause in \REF{ex:ngo:46} contrasts with the negated main clause in \REF{ex:ngo:47} where either a pre- or post-initial prefix may be used. (The relativizer originates from a demonstrative as can be seen in example \REF{ex:ngo:46} where the identical element occurs twice, representing both meanings.)

\ea\label{ex:ngo:46} 
    \gll βí{}-no ne=e-βj-ákoɾi βí-no n-tá-áɣo-tun-a\\ 
    8-\textsc{dem} \textsc{cop=aug}{}-8-food 8\textsc{{}-rel} \textsc{sbj1sg-neg-ipfv}{}-want-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘This is food that I don’t want.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:47} 
    \gll e-βj-ákoɾi βí-no té{}-ŋ-ko-βe-tun-a\\ 
        \textsc{aug}{}-8-food 8-\textsc{dem} \textsc{neg-sbj1sg-ipfv-om8}{}-want-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘This food, I don’t want (it).’
\z 

Relative clauses are in general construed with the post-initial negative prefix. Furthermore, several types of adverbial clause constructions are derived from a relative construction with the locative noun class 16 relativizer \textit{háno}. Consider example \REF{ex:ngo:48} which relativizes a locative head noun subject – viz. the generic \textit{ahasɛ} ‘place’, but which could also be analysed as functioning as a locative clause. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:48} 
     \gll tu-ɾa-ɣí musóma a{}-há-sɛ há-no tu-ta-mɛɲ{}-iɾe\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{pl}{}-\textsc{ipfv}{}-go Musoma \textsc{aug-}16-place 16-\textsc{rel} \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{pl}{}-\textsc{neg}{}-live-\textsc{pfv}\\
     \glt ‘We are going to Musoma, a place where ({\textasciitilde}at which) we don’t live.’
\z 

The construction with the locative relativizer recurs in several negated adverbial clause constructions, not only in negative locative clauses or temporal clauses like \REF{ex:ngo:52}. It also occurs in “anti-circumstantial” clauses – viz., abessive-like ‘without’-constructions (cf. \sectref{sec:ngo:3.3}), but where the absentee is not a nominal referent but an event coded in a dependent clause (cf. \citealt{MauriSansò2019}; \citealt{BernanderEtAl2023a}) as in \REF{ex:ngo:49}, as well as (at least some types of) negative conditional clauses, as evident in \REF{ex:ngo:50}. Being formally equal to relative constructions, these types of adverbial clauses are also negated with the post-initial prefix.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:49} 
    \gll te-mú-n-saiɣ{}-iɾe ha-no mu-tá-maɲ{}-iɾe\\ 
        \textsc{neg-sbj}2\textsc{pl-obj}1\textsc{sg}{}-like-\textsc{pfv} 16-\textsc{rel} \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{pl}{}-\textsc{neg}{}-know-\textsc{pfv}\\
    \glt ‘You detest me (lit. don’t like me) without knowing me.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:50} 
    \gll há-no a-ta-n-dɛh-iiɾe e-náuɾi te-ŋ{}-ku-ɣáɾɔk-a ká\\ 
    16-\textsc{rel} \textsc{sbj}1-\textsc{neg}{}-\textsc{obj}1\textsc{sg}{}-pay-\textsc{appl:pfv} \textsc{aug-}9.ticket \textsc{neg-sbj}1-\textsc{ipfv}{}-return-\textsc{fv} home \\
    \glt ‘If he does not pay me for my ticket, I will not go back home.’
\z 

As mentioned in \sectref{sec:ngo:2.1}, the subject markers for 2\textsc{sg} and noun classes 1, 3 and 9 fuse with the negative prefix, yielding the opaque forms \textit{to-, ta-, to-} and \textit{te-,} and in these cases there is no distinction between post- and pre-initial negators. In relative clauses as well as in other subordinate clauses derived from relatives, however, these fused forms become separated, reflecting the use of post-initial \textit{ta-} in all non-main clause cases, with which it is clearly connected. In example \REF{ex:ngo:51}, this is illustrated with a canonical verb for class 9, while \REF{ex:ngo:52} shows the 2\textsc{sg} prefix separated from the negative prefix on a copula verb.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:51} 
    \gll ha-no n=a-há-sɛ ha-no é{}-ŋ-ka i-tá-áko-ɣwɛm-a\\ 
    16-\textsc{dem} \textsc{cop=aug-16}{}-place 16\textsc{{}-rel} \textsc{aug}{}-9-lion \textsc{sbj9-neg-ipfv}{}-hunt-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘This is an area where the lion doesn’t hunt.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:52} 
    \gll n-aa-ɣí-íɾe wáát͡ʃo ká ha-no u-ta-aɾé=ho \\ 
        \textsc{sbj1sg-pst-}go\textsc{{}-pfv} yours home 16-\textsc{rel} \textsc{sbj2sg-neg-pst.cop-16.loc}\\
    \glt ‘I went to your place when you weren’t there.’
\z 

Negated reason clauses, indicated with the subordinator \textit{ɣo kuβa} not by a relativizer, are an exception to the hegemony of post-initial negative morphology in non-main clauses. As seen in \REF{ex:ngo:53}, a reason clause may instead be negated with pre-initial negation, probably as it is not derived from a relative clause but more likely is paratactic in origin. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:53} 
    \gll ta-a-ɣu-tóɾ{}-a kw-ándɛk-a ɣó kuβa ta-a-na e-kaɾámu\\ 
        \textsc{neg}{}-\textsc{sbj}1-\textsc{ipfv}{}-can-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-write-\textsc{fv} for that \textsc{neg}{}-\textsc{sbj1}{}-\textsc{com} \textsc{aug-}9.pencil\\
    \glt ‘She cannot write because she has no (lit. is not with a) pencil.’
\z 

In addition to these instances of morphological negation, the two negative auxiliary verbs (discussed in detail in \sectref{sec:ngo:2.2}) also have an extended use in non-main clauses beyond being prohibitives. The functional retrospective-prospective division between them, as described around examples \REF{ex:ngo:24} and \REF{ex:ngo:25} in \sectref{sec:ngo:2.2} above, seems to continue to influence the choice of a particular negative verb here as well. This fact may be illustrated with the following two purpose clauses, where the choice of auxiliary verb depends on the context, i.e., whether the full verb in the dependent clause refers to a prospective or retrospective event. Thus, the proposition in \REF{ex:ngo:54} would not be felicitous with \nobreakdash-\textit{tiɣ}{}- as that would suggest that the participants have already been captured. Following a similar line of reasoning, the construction with \textit{\nobreakdash-Vt͡ʃ{}-} is dispreferred in \REF{ex:ngo:55} as the proposition is meant to highlight the termination of a situation which has already started and continues to hold.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:54} 
    \gll βa-ɣá{}-át͡ʃ{}-a há-no βa-tá-át͡ʃ-a ku-ɣɔɔt-ú na a{}-βa-síɾikaɾe\\ 
     \textsc{sbj}2-\textsc{narr}{}-come-\textsc{fv} 16-\textsc{dem} 2-\textsc{neg}{}-come-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-catch-\textsc{pass} \textsc{com} \textsc{aug-}2-police\\
    \glt ‘They came here so that they would not get caught by the police.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:55} 
    \gll βa-ɾa-sáám-ɛɾ{}-a há-no musoma βa-tíɣ{}-ɛ ku-ɲáŋki-βu na a{}-βa-táŋki\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}2-\textsc{ipfv}{}-move-\textsc{appl-fv} 16-\textsc{dem} Musoma \textsc{sbj}2-stop-\textsc{sbjv} \textsc{inf}{}-annoy-\textsc{pass} \textsc{com} \textsc{aug-}2-state\\
    \glt ‘They are moving here to Musoma so that they will not be harassed by the state (anymore).’ 
\z 

Affirmative purpose clauses are inflected in the subjunctive form in Ngoreme, as is common across Bantu (see \citealt{NurseDevos2019}; \citealt{Devos2008}). This is particularly the case when the main clause and the subordinate clause do not refer to the same participant(s), as in \REF{ex:ngo:56}. The use of non-declarative strategies for the negation of purpose clauses could thus be seen as a direct reflection of the affirmative situation, where the subjunctive is used both for milder commands in the imperative-hortative domain as well as for subordination.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:56} 
    \gll n{}-tw{}-a{}-tánw{}-iɾe ɔkɔɔrí taata a{}-kɔɾ{}-ɛ kw{}-ɛɣ{}-i\\ 
        \textsc{foc-sbj}1\textsc{pl}{}-\textsc{pst}{}-leave-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{comp} 1a.father \textsc{sbj}1-do-\textsc{sbjv} \textsc{inf}{}-sweep-\textsc{caus}\\
    \glt ‘We left so that father could tidy up.’
\z 

Note however, that when there is co-referentiality between the subject participant(s) of the main and dependent clause, affirmative purpose clauses may be expressed with the infinitive in Ngoreme. In this sense, examples \REF{ex:ngo:54} and \REF{ex:ngo:55} above could just as well be argued to illustrate negated non-finite dependent clauses. Compare \REF{ex:ngo:54} with the affirmative reason clause in \REF{ex:ngo:57} below. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:57} 
    \gll βa-ɣá-át͡ʃ{}-a há-no ko-nííβ{}-a \\ 
        \textsc{sbj}2-\textsc{narr}{}-come-\textsc{fv} 16-\textsc{dem} \textsc{inf}{}-create-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘They came here to create a new life (for themselves).’
\z 

Thus, non-finite clauses may be negated, but only through the negative (auxiliary) verb strategy (that is, morphological negation is not accepted here). An infinitive functioning as a nominal head of a clause may also be negated, again with negative verbs, as illustrated in \REF{ex:ngo:58} and \REF{ex:ngo:59}. In this case, both verbs occur in the infinitive form. Notice that there is an augment prefix on the negative verb \textit{oɣutíɣa} ‘to stop’ and \textit{okwáŋɡa} ‘to refuse’ but not on the lexical verbs (cf. \sectref{sec:ngo:4.3}) and furthermore that the adjective agrees with the verbs. Because of the distinct nominal components of the infinitive in these particular constructions, they are here glossed as (noun class) 15, i.e., the noun class that infinitives are associated with.\footnote{For more on this dual nature of the Bantu infinitives, see e.g., \citet{Schadeberg2003}.} Interestingly, it seems that the construction with \nobreakdash-\textit{Vt͡ʃ}\nobreakdash- cannot be used in this context. Instead, another inherently negative verb, namely \textit{\nobreakdash-aŋɡ\nobreakdash-} ‘refuse’, is used along with \nobreakdash-\textit{tiɣ}\nobreakdash-.\footnote{The marker -\textit{ang} is also used by some speakers even for expressing negative imperatives (\sectref{sec:ngo:2.2}) and negative subordinates (this section). Its usage in these domains appears to be much more widespread in the other Western Serengeti languages, however.} \\

\ea\label{ex:ngo:58} 
    \gll o{}-ɣu-tíɣ{}-a ko-ɲó a{}-ma-ro ŋ=ɡu-t͡ʃómu \\ 
        \textsc{aug}{}-15{}-stop-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-drink \textsc{aug-}6-homebrew \textsc{cop}=15-good\\
    \glt ‘Not to drink ({\textasciitilde}not drinking) (locally brewed) beer is good.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:59} 
    \gll  o{}-kw-áŋɡ-a ɣu-sám-a a{}-má{}-ro ŋ=ɡu-t͡ʃómu\\ 
          \textsc{aug-15}{}-refuse-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-try{}-\textsc{fv} \textsc{aug-}6-homebrew \textsc{cop}=15-good\\
    \glt ‘Not to taste ({\textasciitilde}not tasting) (locally brewed) beer is good.’
\z 

A final category of non-main clause negation strategies that occur in Ngoreme is Swahili borrowings ({\textasciitilde}code-switching). These include \textit{badala (ya)} ‘instead of’ + infinitive verb for the expression of substitutive clauses and \textit{bila (ya)} ‘without’ + infinitive verb which constitute an alternative negative strategy for \mbox{(anti-)}\allowbreak circumstantial clauses (cf. the further discussion on abessive-like constructions in \sectref{sec:ngo:3.3}).\footnote{Note that these subordinators have been borrowed into Ngoreme from Swahili which itself had borrowed them from Arabic. It has been argued for Swahili (\citealt{Lodhi1994}, \citealt{Wald2001}, \citealt{Schadeberg2009}) that their incorporation came with a more profound structural re-configuration where more analytic structures consisting of subordinators and nominalized verbs came to replace synthetic structures with negative verbal morphology. These Swahili loans are relatively marginal clause types in Ngoreme but we include them here and in \tabref{tab:ngo:2} in the interests of completeness.}

We have no examples of lexically idiosyncratic negatives in Ngoreme. 

\subsection{Other clausal negation constructions – the nondum}\label{sec:ngo:2.6}


The conceptual category of ‘not yet’, sometimes referred to as a \textit{nondum} (e.g., \citealt{VeselinovaDevos2021}) is included in the onomasiological grid of phasal polarity, i.e., “structured means of expressing polarity in a sequential perspective” (\citealt[40]{Baar1997}; cf. \citealt{Kramer2017}). Two different constructions may be used (interchangeably, as far as we have been able to tell) to mark this concept in Ngoreme. The constructions are formally linked to the conceptually related positive phasal polarity expressions of ‘still’ and ‘already’ respectively (cf. \citealt{Kramer2017}, \citealt{VeselinovaDevos2021}). 

As illustrated in \REF{ex:ngo:60}, one kind of nondum-construction in Ngoreme consists of the copula-like form \textsc{sbj}\nobreakdash-\textit{kééɾe} ‘still’ (consisting of a subject marker and most likely the persistive prefix *\textit{kɪ́-} + copula *\nobreakdash-\textit{dɪ̀}, all parts reconstructible for Proto-Bantu, e.g., \citealt[246--249]{Nurse2008}) and a full verb occurring in the infinitival form. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:60} 
    \gll a{}-má-ándasi ɣa-kééɾe ɣu-tɛɾɛk-u\\ 
        \textsc{aug-}6-doughnut \textsc{sbj}6-still \textsc{inf}{}-cook-\textsc{pass}\\
    \glt ‘The maandazis ({\textasciitilde}doughnuts) have not been prepared yet.’
\z 

Note that there is no explicit negative material used in this nondum-construc\-tion, intriguing from a typological perspective as negatives without negators are a rare trait cross-linguistically (cf. \citealt{Miestamo2010}). It is not clear whether ‘not yet’ is a conventionalized meaning or whether such a negative reading is (still) only inferred from the combination of \textit{kééɾe} ‘still’ with an infinitive verb, viz. ‘be still to X’ > ‘be not yet X’ (cf. \citealt{Güldemann1998}; \citealt[147--148]{Nurse2008}; \citealt{VeselinovaDevos2021}). One indication that the meaning of \textsc{sbj}{}-\textit{keere} is not just compositional is the fact that it conveys a negative interpretation when occurring alone as in the negative answer in \REF{ex:ngo:61}.\footnote{The form \nobreakdash-\textit{keere} denotes the positive meaning ‘still’ when operating on finite verbs or non-verbal predicates. The reading of negative or positive continuation thus depends on the constructional context (viz. infinite vs finite) and not the internal phase structure of the predicate (verb), as in some Malayo-Polynesian languages \citep[see][]{VeselinovaEtAlFC}.}

\ea\label{ex:ngo:61}
    \begin{xlist}[Q: ]
    \exi{Q: }\label{ex:ngo:61A}
        \gll w{}-iɣɔti-iɾe \\ 
            \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-become\_satiated-\textsc{pfv}\\
         \glt ‘Are you full?’
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:ngo:61B}
        \gll ŋ{}-kééɾe\\ 
            \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{sg}{}-still\\
         \glt ‘Not yet.’
    \end{xlist}
\z 

Another kind of nondum-construction in Ngoreme consists of the negated verb \nobreakdash-\textit{maɾ}\nobreakdash- ‘finish’ – also used in the conceptually related expression of ‘already’ (see \citealt{BernanderEtAl2023b}), as well as marking ‘before’ (example \REF{ex:ngo:90}, \sectref{sec:ngo:4.5}) – plus a second verb expressing the main event of the proposition.\footnote{Both verb forms in this construction are odd. Most likely they are retentions of synchronically obsolete TA conjugations. \textsc{sbj}-a-B-a is most likely a remnant of a perfect aspect verb form (and hence tentatively glossed as such) and \textsc{sbj}-Ø-B-a a (dependent) present tense, found in other Mara languages (e.g. neighbouring Kuria; \citealt[286--287]{Cammenga2004}).} As shown in examples \REF{ex:ngo:62} and \REF{ex:ngo:63}, both the pre-initial and the post-initial negative prefix may be employed in forming this type of nondum-expression.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:62} 
    \gll tu-tá-á-maɾ{}-a tu-ɣɔɾ{}-a e-ŋɔmbɛ je-ɾe\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{pl}{}-\textsc{neg-prf}{}-finish-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{pl}{}-buy-\textsc{fv} \textsc{aug-}9.cow 9-\textsc{dem}\\
    \glt  ‘We haven’t bought this cow yet.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:63} 
    \gll ti-ná-á-maɾ{}-a m-máɲ{}-a ku-ɣámb-a e{}-kí{}-iŋɡeɾesa\\ 
        \textsc{neg}{}-\textsc{sbj}1\textsc{sg}{}-\textsc{prf}{}-finish-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{sg}{}-(come\_to)know-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-speak-\textsc{fv} \textsc{aug-}7-English\\
    \glt ‘I don’t know (lit. haven’t come to know) (how) to speak English yet.’
\z 

Finally, it should be mentioned that both markers associated with the expression of ‘not yet’ may be combined, yielding a more emphatic reading of disbelief, irritation and counter-expectation, similar to that of ‘still not’ in English (cf. \citealt[2]{Baar1997}). Example (\ref{ex:ngo:64}) is a case in point.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:64} 
    \gll ɾóóbi kana o-kééɾe u-tá-á{}-maɾ{}-a o-swáɾ{}-a\\ 
        Rhobi oh \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-still \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-\textsc{neg-prf}{}-finish-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-wear\_clothes-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt  ‘Oh Rhobi, are you \textsc{still not} dressed?!’ (I have told you three times already) 
\z 

\section{Non-clausal negation}\label{sec:ngo:3}
\subsection{Negative replies}\label{sec:ngo:3.1}

While affirmation \textit{ee} and denial \textit{a-a} are used by Ngoreme-speakers, the language does not have dedicated one-word affirmative or negative replies. As such, answers to polar questions typically require a full sentence in response, as in \REF{ex:ngo:65b} below.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:65}
    \begin{xlist}[Q: ]
    \exi{Q: }\label{ex:ngo:65a}
        \gll βóóno é{}-ŋ{}-ka te-eku{}-ŋɔɔn{}-a he\\
             now \textsc{aug}{}-9-lion \textsc{neg.9-ipfv}{}-roar-\textsc{fv} \textsc{neg}\\
        \glt‘Is the lion not roaring?’ 
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:ngo:65b}
        \gll n-ku-ŋɔɔn-a j-eni\\
             \textsc{foc-inf}{}-roar-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj9-prs.cop}\\
        \glt ‘[Yes,] it is roaring.’
    \end{xlist}
\z 
 
As regards replies to negative questions, answering the question in \REF{ex:ngo:65a} above with \textit{a-a} means that the lion is indeed not roaring; answering with \textit{ee} contrasts the negation in the question, meaning that the lion \textit{is} roaring.

\subsection{Negative indefinites and quantifiers}\label{sec:ngo:3.2}

In line with the broader cross-Bantu pattern, Ngoreme does not have indefinite pronouns; instead, words such as \textit{omónto} ‘person’, \textit{eɣénto} ‘thing’ or \textit{ahásɛ} ‘place’ can be used to mean ‘someone’, ‘something’ and ‘somewhere’. The word best translated as ‘somebody’ is \textit{ŋáni,} which refers to a particular, known somebody, as seen in \REF{ex:ngo:66}. Most likely, this is a borrowing originally from Luo, a West Nilotic language (cf. \citealt[529]{Tucker1994}).

\ea\label{ex:ngo:66} 
    \gll ŋáni a-aní há-áɾe\\ 
        someone \textsc{sbj1-prs.cop} 16-\textsc{dist.dem}\\
    \glt ‘Someone (particular) is there.’
\z 

The word \textit{ŋáni} can also be used in a negative context as in \REF{ex:ngo:67}, but the meaning remains that of a particular someone, and not ‘nobody’.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:67} 
     \gll há-áɾe ŋáni tá{}-ɾe=ho\\ 
        16-\textsc{dist.dem} someone \textsc{neg-}\textsc{prs.cop=loc16}\\
     \glt ‘(A certain) someone is not there.’
\z 

For expressing ‘nobody’, the aforementioned word \textit{omónto} ‘person’ is used with a negative verb, as in \REF{ex:ngo:68}. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:68} 
     \gll mó{}-nto tá{}-á{}-t͡ʃ{}-iɾe\\ 
        1-person \textsc{neg.sbj1-pst}{}-come-\textsc{pfv}\\
    \glt ‘Nobody came.’
\z 

Similarly, the word \textit{eɣénto} ‘thing’ can be used to mean both ‘something’ and ‘nothing’ as in \REF{ex:ngo:69} and (\ref{ex:ngo:70}--\ref{ex:ngo:71}), depending on the polarity of the verb. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:69} 
    \gll né{}-ŋ{}-ɡɔɔtɛ e{}-ɣe{}-nto\\ 
        \textsc{foc-sbj1sg}{}-have \textsc{aug}{}-7-thing\\
    \glt ‘I have something {\textasciitilde} I have a/the thing.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:70} 
    \gll té{}-ŋ{}-ɡɔɔtɛ ɣe{}-nto\\ 
        \textsc{neg-sbj1sg}{}-have 7-thing \\
    \glt  ‘I don’t have anything.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:71} 
    \gll té{}-ŋ{}-ɡu-tɔɾ{}-a ku-ɾɔɾ{}-a ɣe-nto \\ 
         \textsc{neg-sbj1sg-ipfv}{}-be\_able-\textsc{fv} \textsc{inf}{}-see-\textsc{fv} 7-thing\\
    \glt ‘I can’t see anything.’
\z 

Finally, in \REF{ex:ngo:72}, the word \textit{ahásɛ} ‘place’ is used to convey the meaning ‘nowhere’. Note that in all of these negated contexts, the augment vowel disappears. It may, however, be used to mark specificity or referentiality even under negation, as in \REF{ex:ngo:73}, where a reading of ‘nowhere’ is not available. For more on the augment and the scope of negation, see \sectref{sec:ngo:4.3}.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:72} 
    \gll tááta tá-á-ɣi-iɾe ha-sɛ\\ 
        1a.father \textsc{neg.sbj1{}-pst}{}-go-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{16-}place\\
    \glt ‘My father didn’t go anywhere.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:73} 
    \gll éwe tá{}-á{}-ɣi{}-iɾe a{}-há{}-sɛ\\ 
        3\textsc{sg} \textsc{neg.sbj1-pst}{}-go-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{aug-}16-place\\
    \glt ‘(S)he didn’t go to a (certain) place.’
\z 

In \sectref{sec:ngo:4.4}, the reinforcing negative particle \textit{he} is examined. Among its other meanings, it can also have a reading of ‘never’, as shown in \REF{ex:ngo:74}.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:74} 
    \gll e{}-saaɾiɣɔɔkɔ té{}-éku{}-ɾaɣɔɾ-a he\\ 
        \textsc{aug-}9.cock \textsc{sbj}9.\textsc{neg-ipfv}{}-crow-\textsc{fv} \textsc{neg}\\
    \glt ‘The cock never crows.’
\z 

\subsection{Negative derivation and case marking}\label{sec:ngo:3.3}

Ngoreme does not have special means for achieving negative derivation or abes\-sive-like morphemes. Instead, other phrases or full clauses may be employed. For example, to express an abessive meaning of ‘without something’ a juxtaposed negative possessive clause is used, as in \REF{ex:ngo:75}.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:75} 
    \gll a{}-βá{}-nto βa{}-no βa-ɾa-ɾááɣɛɾ{}-a βa-tá{}-ána βi{}-t͡ʃííko\\ 
        \textsc{aug-}2-person 2-\textsc{prox.dem} \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{{}-ipfv}{}-eat-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj}2-\textsc{neg-com} 8-spoon\\
    \glt ‘These people eat without spoons.’ (lit. ‘These people eat, they do not have spoons.’)
\z 

Alternatively, the Swahili preposition \textit{bila} (originally from Arabic \textit{bilā}) is often used for ‘without’: \textit{bila {\textasciitilde} βiɾa βi-t͡ʃiiko} ‘without spoons’. See also \sectref{sec:ngo:4.5} on negative coordination of the type \textit{neither...nor.} 

\section{Other aspects of negation}\label{sec:ngo:4}
\subsection{The scope of negation}\label{sec:ngo:4.1}

In Eastern Bantu languages the pre-initial prefix is often portrayed as having scope over the whole proposition whereas the post-initial prefix and negative auxiliaries have scope over the verbal (macro-)stem / lexical complement, marking different types of non-standard negation (\citealt[193--194]{Nurse2008}; \citealt{Güldemann1999}). This division is not clear-cut in Ngoreme as the post-initial prefix may also be used in standard negation where it has scope over the whole proposition. The diachronic factors at play that further explains this synchronic situation in Ngoreme are discussed in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.8}. See also \sectref{sec:ngo:4.3} for marking of NPs in the scope of negation. As far as we are aware, prosodic prominence is not employed in the marking of negative scope in Ngoreme.

\subsection{Negative polarity}\label{sec:ngo:4.2}

We have not come across any specialized negative polarity items in Ngoreme. The presence versus absence of the augment is a polarity phenomenon discussed in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.3}. 

As discussed in \sectref{sec:ngo:3.2}, Ngoreme has no specific indefinite pronouns. Instead, meanings such as ‘someone’, ‘something’ and ‘somewhere’, and their negative counterparts, are expressed through appropriate nouns (‘person’, ‘thing’, ‘place’). While Ngoreme also doesn’t necessarily distinguish between English examples such as ‘we don’t have food' and ‘we don’t have \textit{any} food (at all)’, a similar reading can be achieved by the reinforcing particle \textit{he} as described in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.4} below.

\subsection{Marking of NPs in the scope of negation}\label{sec:ngo:4.3}

As touched upon already in \sectref{sec:ngo:1}, \sectref{sec:ngo:3.2} and \sectref{sec:ngo:4.2}, augments – i.e., pre-prefixes attached to nominal class prefixes on nouns – are typically omitted when the nouns appear within the scope of negation. Compare \REF{ex:ngo:76} with \REF{ex:ngo:77} (see also \REF{ex:ngo:69} vs. \REF{ex:ngo:70}).

\ea\label{ex:ngo:76} 
    \gll o-ɾa-βúsuɾ-a a-ma-hɛmba\\ 
        \textsc{sbj2sg-ipfv}{}-plant-\textsc{fv} \textsc{aug}{}-6-millet\\
    \glt ‘You plant (the) millet.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:77} 
    \gll te-tó-óko-ɾuɣ-a ma-hɛmba\\ 
        \textsc{neg-sbj1pl-ipfv-}cook-\textsc{fv} 6-millet\\
 \glt ‘We are not cooking millet.’
\z 

However, as has been described for several other Bantu languages (see e.g. \citealt{Givon1978}, \citealt{Velde2019}, \citealt{Miestamo2014}), the augment may be attached to a noun phrase also under negative scope to signal specificity (or referentiality). Consider the two examples below, seen also above in \sectref{sec:ngo:3.2}. While the augment is not normally present under negation as in \REF{ex:ngo:78}, in \REF{ex:ngo:79} it is used for specificity; a reading of ‘anywhere/nowhere’ is not possible here. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:78} 
    \gll tááta ta-a-ɣi-iɾe ha-sɛ\\ 
        1a.father \textsc{neg-pst}{}-go-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{16-}place\\
\glt ‘My father didn’t go anywhere.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:79} 
    \gll éwe tá{}-á{}-ɣi-iɾe a-há{}-sɛ\\ 
        3\textsc{sg} \textsc{neg.sbj1-pst}{}-go-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{aug-}16-place\\
    \glt ‘(S)he didn’t go to a (certain) place.’
\z 

There does not seem to be such strong incompatibility effects between negation and focus in Ngoreme, as described for other (West) African languages, such as Saharan Kanuri (\citealt{Cyffer2009}; see also \citealt{Miestamo2014}). The focus nasal marker cannot occur in the pre-initial position of the verb when negated (cf. \sectref{sec:ngo:2.1}). However, a negated verb does seem to be able to co-occur with the nasal when it is used for (term) focus on a (fronted) noun phrase. This is shown in \REF{ex:ngo:80a}, where ‘father’ is an answer to an information question (and hence inherently focused; cf. e.g. \citealt[282--286]{Lambrecht1994}). As seen in \REF{ex:ngo:80b}, negation may also co-occur with a type of alternative focus formation where the nasal is attached to a resumptive pronoun. Notice also that the negation of the verb is conveyed by the post-initial prefix.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:80}
    \ea\label{ex:ngo:80a}
        \gll n=tááta a{}-tá{}-á{}-maɾ{}-a a{}-m{}-tóóri\\
             \textsc{foc}=1.father \textsc{sbj1-neg-prf}{}-finish-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj1-obj}1-help\\
        \glt ‘\textbf{Father} has not helped her yet.’
    \ex\label{ex:ngo:80b}
        \gll tááta n=éwe a{}-tá{}-á{}-maɾ{}-a a{}-m{}-tóóri\\
             1.father \textsc{foc=}3\textsc{sg} \textsc{sbj1-neg-prf}{}-finish-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj1-obj}1-help\\
        \glt ‘\textbf{Father} has not helped her yet.’\\
        \medskip(Both a and b answer the question: ‘\textbf{Who} has not helped her cleaning yet?')
    \z
\z

\subsection{Reinforcing negation}\label{sec:ngo:4.4}

The main element used for reinforcing negation is the particle \textit{he}. It is only used in main clauses where it appears in a clause-final position. At least for some Ngoreme speakers, it may also have a more specific reading of ‘completeness’. This would link to the etymology proposed by \citet{Rose2001} for a corresponding particle in neighbouring Kuria as being a borrowed Luo completive ideophone. For example, the addition of \textit{he} to the negative existential example \REF{ex:ngo:38} – here re-stated as \REF{ex:ngo:81} – would imply that there is not a single drop left in the bucket. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:81} 
    \gll a{}-má{}-nt͡ʃe ɣa-tá{}-ɾe=mu mo-ndóa he\\ 
        \textsc{aug-}6-water \textsc{sbj6-neg-}\textsc{prs.cop=loc18} \textsc{loc18}{}-bucket \textbf{\textsc{neg}}\\
    \glt‘There is no ({\textasciitilde} complete absence of) water in the bucket.’ 
\z 

We have also already seen in \REF{ex:ngo:74} (\sectref{sec:ngo:3.2} above), that \textit{he} may be used to convey ‘never’ – a stronger or more emphatic denial in more general terms. As further discussed in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.8}
, it appears that \textit{he} in this role as an emphatic negative reinforcer has been enrolled in an ongoing Jespersen’s Cycle-like grammaticalization process. 

\subsection{Negation, coordination and complex clauses}\label{sec:ngo:4.5}

The most common coordination strategy in Ngoreme is the use of a coordinator \textit{na} (formally identical to the comitative marker). This coordinator is used with both affirmative \REF{ex:ngo:82} and negative clauses \REF{ex:ngo:83}. While the coordinator remains the same regardless of the polarity of the clauses, both verbs in coordinated negative clauses need to be fully inflected. This contrasts with the situation in affirmative cases as in \REF{ex:ngo:82}, where the latter clause may have an infinitive verb form when the subject and tense-aspect-mood information is shared across in the two clauses.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:82} 
    \gll n{}-tw{}-a{}-ɾí{}-íɾe e{}-βj-ákoɾi no kó{}-ɲo a{}-má{}-ro\\ 
        \textsc{foc-sbj1pl-pst}{}-eat-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{aug-}8-food and \textsc{inf}{}-drink \textsc{aug-}6-alcohol \\
     \glt ‘We ate food and drank alcohol.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:83} 
    \gll tu{}-tá{}-á{}-ɾaaɣ{}-iɾe na tu{}-tá{}-á{}-ɲw{}-eɾe\\ 
        \textsc{sbj1pl{}-neg-pst}{}-eat-\textsc{pfv} and \textsc{sbj1pl-neg-pst}{}-drink-\textsc{pfv}\\
    \glt ‘We did not eat or drink.’
\z 

In \REF{ex:ngo:84} two negated clauses with different subjects are coordinated. Example \REF{ex:ngo:85} has an affirmative clause followed by a negative one. The coordinator \textit{na} is used in both cases, as above.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:84} 
    \gll te{}-n{}-áá{}-maɾ{}-a n{}-duɣ-a βj-ákoɾi\\ 
         \textsc{neg-sbj1sg}{}-\textsc{prf}{}-finish-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj1sg}{}-cook-\textsc{fv} 8-food \\
    \glt ‘I have not cooked food’\\\medskip
    \gll na a{}-βá-ána te-βá-á{}-maɾ-a βa-ɾaaɣɛɾ-a\\
        and \textsc{aug}{}-2-child \textsc{neg-sbj2-prf}{}-finish-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj2}{}-eat-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt ‘and the children have not eaten.’
\z 


\ea\label{ex:ngo:85} 
    \gll n-aa-ɾú-úɾe musoma na tááta tá-á-ɣi-iɾe ha-sɛ\\ 
        \textsc{sbj1sg{}-pst}{}-leave{}-\textsc{pfv} Musoma and 1a.father \textsc{neg-pst}{}-go{}-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{16-}place\\
     \glt ‘I left Musoma and my father didn’t go anywhere.’
\z 

Often, a disjunctive coordinator \textit{au,} borrowed from Swahili, is used instead of the more general coordination marker \textit{na}. Another loanword, \textit{waaɾa} ‘neither’ (from Sw. \textit{wala,} originally from Arabic \textit{wa-lā} ‘and not’) can be used for coordinating negative clauses coordinator \REF{ex:ngo:86}, although the same meaning could be communicated with \textit{na}. 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:86} 
    \gll tá-á-hɔnt͡ʃɔk-iɾe ika waaɾa tá-á-tɛm-iɾe e-síímu\\ 
         \textsc{neg.sbj1-pst}{}-return-\textsc{pfv} home neither \textsc{neg.sbj1-pst}{}-hit-\textsc{pfv} \textsc{aug-}9.phone\\
    \glt ‘He didn’t come home, nor did he call.’
\z 

\subsection{Miscellaneous aspects of negation}
\subsubsection{Metalinguistic negation}\label{sec:ngo:4.6}

In \REF{ex:ngo:87}, we see an example of metalinguistic negation. As can be seen on the basis of this example, it is possible to say that Amani does not have three children when stressing the fact that, in fact, he has more.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:87} 
    \gll amááni tá{}-ɾe βá{}-ána βa-tato a-ɣɔɔtɛ, m=ba-ne\\ 
        Amani \textsc{neg-prs.cop} 2-child 2-three \textsc{sbj}1-have \textsc{cop=}2-four\\
    \glt ‘Amani doesn’t have three children, but four.’
\z 

The example is formally interesting. The subject is followed by a negated copula, in turn followed by an affirmative clause \textit{(βáána βatato aɣɔɔtɛ} ‘he has three children’) falling under the scope of negation; the entire sentence is literally something along the lines of “Amani, it is not [that] he has three children, it is four”. Note that the ‘affirmative’ clause shows behaviour typical of negated contexts (see \sectref{sec:ngo:2.1}): the object, \textit{βáána} ‘children’, has no augment vowel, and there is no focus marker on the possessive verb. The negative copula is glossed as having a pre-initial negator alone, but, as mentioned in \sectref{sec:ngo:2.3}, there is an alternative analysis of agreement with the subject, since a form unmarked for subject is homonymous with class 1 agreement.

While interesting, this is an isolated example in our data, and should not be considered definitively indicative of all metalinguistic negation in Ngoreme. 

\subsubsection{Non-negative uses of negatives and expletive negation}\label{sec:ngo:4.7}

As mentioned briefly in \sectref{sec:ngo:2.2} on negation in non-declaratives, the use of the (standard) post-initial negative morpheme \textit{ta}{}- plus the basic affirmative imperative verb form does not convey negation in Ngoreme (unlike its Western Serengeti relatives) but has developed a specialized reading. Rather, it functions as a polar, affirmative directive (together with a sense of urging or imploring), roughly translatable as ‘why don’t you X ’{\textasciitilde} ‘do X’. This is illustrated in examples \REF{ex:ngo:88} and \REF{ex:ngo:89}.

\ea\label{ex:ngo:88} 
     \gll u-tá{}-ɣɔɾ{}-a n-ɡo-ku-ɣáɲ-a to{}-oní  \\ 
        \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-\textsc{neg}{}-buy-\textsc{fv} \textsc{foc}{}-\textsc{inf}{}-\textsc{obj}2\textsc{sg}{}-wait-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{pl}{}-\textsc{prs.cop}\\
    \glt ‘Go ahead and buy (now), we are waiting for you!’ 
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:89} 
    \gll n-da-ku-sáβ{}-a u-ta-tánɔɾ{}-a o-ɾu há-no\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{sg}{}-\textsc{ipfv-obj}2\textsc{sg}{}-beg-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-\textsc{neg}{}-leave-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj}2\textsc{sg}{}-go\_away 16-\textsc{dem}\\
 \glt ‘Please, why don’t you just leave!’
\z 

Another case of a non-negative use of a negative construction in Ngoreme relates to temporal-adverbial ‘before’-clauses. As illustrated in \REF{ex:ngo:90}, these are not expressed with a conjunction but rather an auxiliary construction consisting of the verb \nobreakdash-\textit{maɾ}\nobreakdash- (‘finish’) and the post-initial negative prefix, \textit{tá}{}- in the example below.


\ea\label{ex:ngo:90} 
    \gll w-i-ku-ɣɔɾ{}-a t͡ʃí{}-ɲaaɲa e{}-ke-ɾúútu ɣi-tá-á{}-maɾ{}-a ke-hík-a \\ 
         \textsc{sbj}2sg-\textsc{itv-inf-}buy-\textsc{fv} \textsc{aug-}10.tomatoes \textsc{aug-}7-darkness \textsc{sbj}7-\textsc{neg-prf}{}-finish-\textsc{fv} \textsc{sbj}7-arrive-\textsc{fv}\\
     \glt ‘(You should) go and buy tomatoes before it gets dark.’
\z 

Notice that this construction of temporal subordination is in essence formally identical and hence most likely metonymically related to one of the ‘not yet’/ nondum-constructions discussed in \sectref{sec:ngo:2.6}, which adheres to a cross-linguis\-tically common pattern of polysemy (\citealt[275--276, 291]{Baar1997}) which has also been observed to occur in Bantu \citep{VeselinovaDevos2021}.

\subsubsection{Diachronic observations}\label{sec:ngo:4.8}

From a historical-comparative point of view it would seem that the negation system of Ngoreme is in a state of flux relative to its more conservative Western Serengeti relatives. As indicated above, there are signs of ongoing change in negation strategies associated with both standard and non-standard clauses in Ngoreme. 

To begin with, in relation to the pre-initial negator in the standard negation domain, Ngoreme seems to be moving away from its relatives and is instead employing a more canonical Bantu pattern of negation. This pattern can, in a simplified manner, be characterized as consisting of a functional division of labour between a pre-initial negator used for standard clauses and a post-initial negator used for non-standard clauses (cf. \citealt{Güldemann1996}, \citeyear{Güldemann1999}; \citealt[179--201]{Nurse2008}; cf. also the interrelated note on scope in \sectref{sec:ngo:4.1}).\footnote{\citet{Güldemann1999} suggests that the pre-initial negator is introduced for explicit correction or contradiction of a previous utterance. However, no specific evidence for this has been found for Ngoreme.} \citet{Güldemann1999} distinguishes four specific “distribution patterns” of pre-initial and post-initial negation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Post-initial only, in all clause types
    \item Post-initial in all clauses \& pre-initial alternations in main clauses
    \item Post-initial in non-declaratives + non-main clauses \& pre-initial in main clauses
    \item Pre-initial only (or at least pre-dominantly)
\end{enumerate}

Note that these four “patterns” are not categorically suggested as (diachronic) stages, but rather as synchronically attested tendencies across the Bantu speaking area. However, given the comparative data from the Lacustrine languages (\citealt[288--294]{Güldemann1996}, \citealt[fn. 43]{Güldemann1999}), it is clear that pre-initial negation is a newer development in this genealogical group with the variation of distributional patterns detected between the different languages instantiating different stages of maturation. Thus, whereas the Western Serengeti relatives are all still in Stage 1, Ngoreme is to be categorized somewhere between Stage 2 and 3. The in-between status in this language emanates from the facts provided in this paper. That is, on the one hand, the post-initial clearly is the default choice for standard negation in Ngoreme without any pragmatic overtones. On the other, however, there are still cases in the language where the post-initial negative prefix is used for standard negation. One example of this is the use of the post-initial with the auxiliary {}-\textit{maɾ}{}- ‘finish’ for expressing ‘not yet’ (cf. \sectref{sec:ngo:2.6}). Compare \citet{Rose2001} who suggests a similar ‘not-yet’-construction with a post-initial negator in neighbouring Kuria to be a relic exactly along this line of reasoning. In this respect, it is also interesting to note that the nondum-expression with a post-initial negative prefix cannot be used together with the emphatic post-verbal particle \textit{he}. Conversely, the construction with pre-initial negation may not be used in subordinate (temporal adverbial) clauses where -\textit{maɾ}{}- + \textsc{neg} is used for expressing ‘before’ (cf. \sectref{sec:ngo:4.7}).

Along with the innovation of pre-initial negation, Ngoreme also seems to have been enrolled in a Jespersen’s Cycle-like process, i.e., the grammaticalization scenario where an element initially introduced for reinforcing negative expressions turns into the negative marker itself (see e.g., \citealt{Auwera2010}, \citealt{DevosAuwera2013}). Interestingly, this development in Ngoreme adheres to an areal phenomenon, being a shared trait with its North Mara neighbours and even varieties of the Greater Luhya cluster spoken further north in Kenya (cf. \citealt{DevosAuwera2013}). The strategy of negation with the particle \textit{he} (cf. \sectref{sec:ngo:4.4}) in main clauses in Ngoreme is reminiscent of the inceptive stages of Jespersen’s Cycle where the post-verbal particle is neither obligatory nor neutral but is still used to add a sense of emphasis to the proposition. Ngoreme thus contrasts with its northern neighbours Simbiti (\citealt{WalkerOverton2018}) and Kuria (e.g., \citealt{Rose2001}) which have moved further along the cycle of obligatorification and bleaching. For Kuria, \citet{Rose2001} even mentions contexts where the pre-initial negative prefix rather than the negative post-verbal particle is the optional component. An additional indication of the less grammaticalized status of this negative construction in Ngoreme relative to its northern neighbours is the fact that the use of \textit{he} has not spread (i.e., generalized) to non-declarative and non-main clauses. 

There are ongoing changes that also seem to take place in the domain(s) of non-standard negation. As seen in \sectref{sec:ngo:2.2} and \sectref{sec:ngo:2.4}, inherently negative verbs have been recruited into auxiliaries, increasingly replacing the original post-initial negator. This is not surprising from a Bantu perspective as negation in non-declaratives is “a major conduit through which innovation occurs” \citep[191]{Nurse2008}. In this regard, it may further be noticed that as auxiliary constructions of the form found in Ngoreme – i.e., with a finite auxiliary and infinite full verb – move further along the pathway of verb-to-affix grammaticalization, the erstwhile auxiliary tends to end up as a post-initial prefix, and thus re-initiate the same patterns described above (cf. \citealt[261--284]{Güldemann1996}, \citeyear{Güldemann1999}; see also \citealt[194--196]{Nurse2008}). 

There is some semantic evidence that the mentioned auxiliaries are dedicated prohibitives and not merely “indirect prohibitive speech acts” (\citealt[89]{Auwera2010}). To begin with, they are back-translated as prohibitives into Swahili (the meta-language during elicitation sessions) by the consultants and thus they are arguably “harmonic” with a simplex prohibitive verb form. Furthermore, there are indications that these verbs have undergone semantic bleaching/relaxation relative to their etymons. Hence, as negators they may describe non-agentive events and operate on second verbs in direct opposition to their original lexical semantics (e.g. \nobreakdash-\textit{Vt͡ʃ}\nobreakdash- + ‘go’ or \nobreakdash-\textit{tiɣ}\nobreakdash- + a resultative achievement verb like ‘sit’) as well as co-occur with their own lexical etymons, e.g. \textit{tíɣa kotíɣa} ‘don’t stop’ (cf. \citealt[60--61]{Heine1993}, \citeyear{Heine2002}). However, we have encountered little if any formal evidence for these auxiliaries having a more grammatical(ized) status in relation to their lexical sources. Hence, following \citet[61]{Heine1993}, they can be seen as (still) being in a “budding stage”.

With regard to etymology, the use of the verb \nobreakdash-\textit{tiɣ}\nobreakdash- ‘stop, leave off’ as a prohibitive is not surprising from a typological point of view, given its “inherently negative” cessative lexical meaning (cf. \citealt[358--359]{Aikhenvald2010}; \citealt{Auwera2010}). The reflex of this specific verb is also a typical source for a prohibitive across the Bantu family (see e.g., \citealt[183]{Nurse2008} who lists *\nobreakdash-\textit{tíg}\nobreakdash- as “one of the auxiliaries most involved in negation”), including for several languages closely related to Ngoreme, e.g., Kwaya (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/kya}{kya}; JE251; \citealt{Sillery1932}; \citealt{Odom2016}). The strategy of forming a prohibitive auxiliary by combining (morphological) negation plus ‘come’ is much rarer, but not unheard of for Bantu (cf. Myene (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/mye}{mye}; B11) mentioned by \citet{DevosVanOlmen2013}) and can be taken to adhere to a general cross-linguistic trend of ‘come (to)’ being recruited to express affirmative directives as well as various grammatical expressions with prospective readings (cf. \citealt[92--94, 101--107]{KutevaEtAl2019} and references therein). Examples from Ngoreme’s closest relatives include the cognate ‘come’-verb being recruited into a ‘when’-subordinator in Ikoma \citep{Robinson2015}, and as a future tense marker in Nata (cf. \citealt{Osa-GomezdelCampo2014}).
 
It may further be hypothesized that the introduction of prohibitive auxiliary verbs into Ngoreme was connected to the functional lacuna which the reinterpretation of the synthetic negative imperative form as expressing an affirmative proposition gave rise to in the language. In their cross-Bantu study of imperatives and prohibitives, \citet{DevosVanOlmen2013} note a similar phenomenon in Ngoreme’s neighbour Kuria (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/kuj}{kuj}; JE43) (see also \citealt[293]{Cammenga2004}). They also report a similar pattern in Gikuyu (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/kik}{kik}; E51) and Kamba (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/kam}{kam}; E55), two languages spoken in proximity to the Mara languages. These authors suggest that the polar, affirmative expression can be derived from the conventionalization of an “ironic” use of the negative imperative, an explanation that is plausible also for this feature in Ngoreme.
 
The bi-morphemic construction combining the negative prefix -\textit{ta}{}- and the formative \textit{ka(a)\nobreakdash-} (derived from a verb meaning ‘can’ according to \citealt[90]{Rose2001}; citing \citealt[230]{Cammenga1994}) seems to be an innovation in the neighbouring North Mara subgroup which most likely spread from there to the adjacent Ngoreme speakers. Ngoreme’s two closest neighbours to the north, viz. Kuria and Simbiti, both use the potential in affirmative verb forms. They also regularly form negative imperatives with this prefix included (see \citealt[23]{Sillery1936}; \citealt[17]{WalkerOverton2018}). Examples \REF{ex:ngo:91} and \REF{ex:ngo:92} below are from Simbiti (\href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ssc}{ssc}; JE431). 

\protectedex{
\ea\label{ex:ngo:91} Simbiti (North Mara, Tanzania) \citep[17]{WalkerOverton2018}\\ 
\ea
    \gll to-ta-kaa-bhin-a\\ 
        \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{pl}{}-\textsc{neg-pot}{}-dance-\textsc{fv}\\
    \glt‘We shouldn’t dance.’ 
\ex\label{ex:ngo:92} Simbiti (North Mara, Tanzania) \citep[17]{WalkerOverton2018}\\ 
    \gll n{}-to{}-kaa{}-bhin{}-a \\ 
        \textsc{foc-sbj1pl-hyp-}dance\textsc{{}-fv} \\
    \glt ‘We might dance.’
\z \z }

Notice that a similar construction is, once again, also attested in Gikuyu and Kamba. \citet[27]{DevosVanOlmen2013} note for these languages that “the […] basic prohibitival strategy is used not in its regular form but in combination with the andative (or motional) prefix *\textit{ka}{}- (cf. \citealt[109]{Meeussen1962})”. They go on to suggest that this conventionalization of a bi-morphemic construction gave way to the dedicated ironic-affirmative use of the original prohibitive form (\citealt[47--48]{DevosVanOlmen2013}). It may be suspected that these “andative” prefixes are in some way reflexes of the same non-indicative marker presented here for the Northern Mara languages and Ngoreme. Thus, this chain of reanalysis of prohibitive constructions might represent a larger areal trait. More comparative research would be needed, however, in order to draw any firm conclusions on whether these formatives are cognates or not and how to account for the functional and formal (vowel length) discrepancies. 

\subsubsection{A note on word order}\label{sec:ngo:4.9}

Ngoreme exhibits a typologically and comparatively unusual word order in compound constructions in which the main verb appears before the auxiliary. This is unusual both from a typological perspective where SVO languages are expected to exhibit auxiliary-verb order (\citealt{Dryer1992}; \citealt{Greenberg1966universals}) and from a comparative perspective since Bantu languages overwhelmingly exhibit auxiliary-verb order (\citealt{Gibson2019, Gibson2021}). This has been observed in the Bantu language Simbiti also spoken in the Mara Region, neighbouring languages Gusii and Kuria, as well as the central Tanzanian languages Rangi and Mbugwe (\citealt{GibsonMarten2019}, \citealt{Gibson2019}, \citealt{RothGibson2019}). Of interest for the current discussion is the observation that in Ngoreme, while declarative main clauses exhibit verb-auxiliary order in specific tense-aspect contexts, this word order is reversed in the negative counterparts of these clauses, yielding auxiliary-verb order. 

This can be seen on comparison of the examples below which show verb-auxiliary order in the affirmative in example \REF{ex:ngo:93} and auxiliary-verb order in its negative counterpart in example \REF{ex:ngo:94} (repeated from \REF{ex:ngo:17} and \REF{ex:ngo:18} above).

\ea\label{ex:ngo:93} 
    \gll ɾééɾo ŋ-ko-ɣi too-ni taɾisaɾaamu\\ 
        today \textsc{foc}{}-\textsc{inf}{}-go \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{pl}{}-\textsc{prs.cop} {Dar es Salaam}\\
     \glt ‘Today we are going to Dar es Salaam.’ \citep[278]{Gibson2019} 
\z 

\ea\label{ex:ngo:94} 
    \gll ɾééɾo tu-tá-ɾe ko-ɣi taɾisaɾaamu\\ 
        today \textsc{sbj}1\textsc{pl}{}-\textsc{neg{}-prs.cop} \textsc{inf}{}-go {Dar es Salaam}\\
 \glt ‘Today we are not going to Dar es Salaam.’
\z

As described above, Ngoreme shows interesting variation in its marking of standard negation on the verb, with a pre-initial marker (before the subject marker) \textit{te-} and a post-initial marker (following the subject marker) \textit{ta-}, in free variation. In subordinate clauses, however, only post-initial \textit{ta-} may be used, contributing to a body of evidence for this being the older form. Along with the post-initial prefix, different negative verbs with inherent negative semantics occur as negators in both subordinate as well as non-declarative contexts. Similar patterns are well known across Bantu languages (see especially \citealt{Güldemann1996, Güldemann1999}). Along with these developments and other diachronic phenomena, such as Jespersen’s Cycle, Ngoreme is an interesting language within the Mara subgroup, and features prominently in other present and future work on the variation and development of negation strategies across the Mara Bantu languages (e.g. \citealt{Laine2023}, \citealt{BernanderEtAl2023b}).
