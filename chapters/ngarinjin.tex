\title{Negation in Ngarinyin}
\abstract{Ngarinyin is an Australian Aboriginal language of the Worrorran family, belonging to the West Kimberley region. Its morphological expression of negation is very regular and demonstrates the close relation between negation and modality. Following \citegen{chapters/questionnaire} typological questionnaire, the chapter sketches the basic properties of negation in Ngarinyin within and across clause boundaries and proposes several diachronic and semantic connections between negation markers and (other) particles, interjections and lexical expressions.
\keywords{Aboriginal languages (Ngarinyin); Worrorran; negation; modality}

\section{The language} \label{SecIntroNgarinyin}

%\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.81\textwidth}
%  \centering

Ngarinyin (ISO: \href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ung}{ung}, Glottocode: \href{https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/ngar1284}{ngar1284}) is a Worrorran (non-Pama-Nyungan) language of the Kimberley region in North-Western Australia (\citealt{Rumsey1982}; \citealt{Spronck2015}.\footnote{In earlier work I referred to the language as `Ungarinyin', following the preference of language consultants involved and in order to distinguish the language name from the ethnonym for the Ngarinyin people. Here, I will use the label `Ngarinyin' for both, in accordance with common practice in the literature and with contemporary Ngarinyin community practice.} Although once spoken in a dialect chain spanning most of the central Kimberley region, the language is currently critically endangered. However, efforts from Ngarinyin elders to record stories and conversations with missionaries and linguists since the 1930s have resulted in a rich archive of spoken narratives, personal histories and spontaneous speech. In collaboration with remaining speakers, younger generations are applying these materials in language teaching programmes in schools and in individual training.

Ngarinyin is mostly agglutinative, non-configurational and head-marking. The language is syntactically nominative-accusative, but does not distinguish morphological nominal or accusative case. It has a genitive and a dative case, alongside several locational cases, but most nominal morphology is optionally marked and frequently left out in discourse. Nouns agree with one of five noun classes, masculine, feminine, collective/plural and two neuter classes, roughly corresponding to locational and temporal concepts.

Verbal constructions either consist of a single verb root or constitute `coverb' (also `preverb') constructions, as also found elsewhere in the Kimberley (\citealt{SchulzeBerndt2000}; \citealt{McGregor2002}; \citealt{Bowern2010}). In a coverb construction, a minimally inflecting preverbal lexeme is combined with a fully inflected auxiliary verb, chosen among a (mostly) closed set of about 15 verb roots, cf. coverb \textit{kunin} ‘cover.up’ and verb \textit{y\textsubscript{2}i} ‘be’ in example (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingi}) below. Inflecting verbs carry pronominal object/subject prefixes and allow several other verbal prefixes (irrealis, future, imperative, `identifiable' subject)\footnote{For a description of the identifiable subject marker, glossed here as \textsc{def} `definite subject', see \citet{Spronck2020}.}, but are mostly suffixing (reflexive, tense, optative, dual/paucal, continuative aspect, direction, indirect object/oblique). Morpheme boundaries are often blurred by the assimilation of consonants and vowels, following strengthening and lenition rules as proposed by \citet{Rumsey1982}, indicated in the morphemic transcriptions below by subscript numbers 1 and 2 (for details, see \citealp[17--24]{Rumsey1982}). Note that none of the subscript numbers appearing in the glosses of examples in this chapter have semantic significance.

\hspace*{-1pt}Ngarinyin narratives and conversation predominantly consist of simple clauses and (in)subordinate clauses formed through one single general subordinating strategy (see \sectref{ExNonMainClauses}), as has been reported for other Australian languages (cf. \citealt{Hale1976}; \citealt{Nordlinger2006}). Further complex clause marking is limited to a construction used in reported speech, thought and intention, cf. examples (\ref{ExNEGinRS}) and (\ref{ExNGuruNGinke}) \citep{Spronck2015} and some conditional clauses headed by the conjunction \textit{wana} `if'. The anaphoric `temporal neuter' pronoun \textit{di} `then' can head clauses with an approximate conjunctive function, but Ngarinyin does not have explicitly marked coordinating constructions.

The language has been described in a dictionary that remains one of the largest of any Australian language \citep{CoatElkin1974} and two grammars, an early and mostly superseded description in \citet{CoateOates1970} and a detailed description in \citet{Rumsey1982}. \citet{Spronck2015} contains a more recent sketch grammar and observations about complex clauses, modality and discourse.

Examples cited in this chapter derive from four sources: original field recordings by the author (indicated with a label consisting of numbers and letters plus a time stamp). In examples cited from this source, the label indicates the time and type of the recoding and the name of the language consultant (in case of, e.g. example (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingi}), the label 100903-25NGUN, 2:25-2:26 indicates that the recoding was made on 3 September 2010, that it was the 25th file on that day, that the speaker was `NG', Pansy Nulgit, and that the recording consisted of Unelicited Narrative. The sentence is spoken between 2'25'' and 2'26'' in the file.) A fuller description of the labels and a list of recordings is given in \citet{Spronck2015} and the corpus is archived at the Endangered Language Archive (ELAR). A second source of examples are transcripts made by missionary linguist Howard Coate between the 1930s and 1970s \citep{Coate1930-1971}. These examples are followed by a label consisting of the letter C and a document number, a page number and a line number in the corpus. The full corpus of transcripts and matched sound recordings will be deposited at the archive of the Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies in Canberra, once it is completed. A third source of examples are published sources, which are referenced in the customary way. Glosses and translations of these examples may have been modified, as indicated for the relevant examples. In the case of examples cited from the dictionary \citet{CoatElkin1974}, the relevant dictionary entry is provided as well (cf. \ref{ExCoatElkin1974WaIRR}). Finally, in cases where clear minimal pairs are lacking in the corpus, some examples have been constructed by the author (marked as `\textit{constructed}'), as in (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingiPOS}).

The discussion of negation in the following sections closely follows the questionnaire provided by the editors \parencitetv{chapters/questionnaire}.
\section{Clausal negation} \label{SecClausalNEG}
\subsection{Standard negation}

Ngarinyin standard negation, `i.e. the basic way(s) a language has for negating declarative verbal main clauses'
\citep[1]{Miestamo2005} is constructed by the preverbal particle \textit{wa} `not' and irrealis inflection on the verb, as in (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingi}). The positive version of this sentence, without negation particle and irrealis form is given in (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingiPOS}).

\begin{exe}
\ex
\begin{xlist}
\ex \label{ExWaKuninbaNginkingi}
    \glll Wa kuninba nginkingi.\\
    wa kunin-ba ngin-wa\textsubscript{2}-y\textsubscript{2}i-ngi\\
    \textsc{neg} cover.up-\textsc{iter} 1\textsc{sg}-\textsc{irr}-be-\textsc{irr.pst}\\
    \glt `I did not cover up [my belongings].' (100903-25NGUN, 2:25-2:26)
\ex \label{ExWaKuninbaNginkingiPOS} 
    \glll Kuninba nginyi.\\
    kunin-ba ngin-y\textsubscript{2}i-nyi\\
    cover.up-\textsc{iter} 1\textsc{sg}-be-\textsc{pst}\\
    \glt `I covered up.' (\textit{constructed})
\end{xlist}
\end{exe}

According to the typology in \citet{Miestamo2013-wals113}, the opposition between (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingi} and (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingiPOS}), in which the irrealis marker doubles as a negative expression, classifies Ngarinyin as a language displaying asymmetric negation. Note that in (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingi}) the negation particle \textit{wa} appears before the coverb \textit{kunin} `cover up', demonstrating that the coverb and the following inflecting verb form a single verb phrase. Apart from some clitics and, occasionally, modifiers, no non-verbal elements appear between the negation particle and the inflecting verb. As the minimal pair in (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingi}) and (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingiPOS}) shows, in addition to the irrealis prefix, the negative/irrealis form can also be distinguished from the positive/realis form through some tense inflections, in this instance a dedicated irrealis/realis past tense. This only occurs in the verbal paradigms of a limited amount of verb roots.

The common standard negation construction in (\ref{ExWaKuninbaNginkingi}) has two less frequent variants. The first type is shown in (\ref{ExSub3}), where the negation particle \textit{wa} takes the minimally different form \textit{way}.


\protectedex{
\ea\label{ExSub3}
\glll Karrabirri  mindi way irrkungurli.\\
karrabirri  mindi way irr-w\textsubscript{2}a\textsubscript{2}-ngurli-{\o}\\
boomerang \textsc{n}\textsubscript{m}-\textsc{ana} \textsc{neg} \textsc{3m.pl.obj:3pl.sbj-irr}-give-\textsc{prs}\\
\trans `They do not give them (any) boomerangs.' (111015-02PNNKDDJEUD, 14:42-14:45)
\z
}

Whether \textit{way} is an older form of \textit{wa}, an innovation or (dialectal) variation is unknown, but the form does not seem to imply any semantic contrast.\footnote{In (\ref{ExSub3}), the variant may also be phonologically conditioned, although \textit{way} is not restricted to assimilation contexts.}

Occasionally, the particle is left out entirely and negation is marked by the irrealis alone, as in (\ref{ExNegNoWa}) (also see \ref{ExNGuruNGinke}).\footnote{For an example of the modal irrealis function of this marker, see (\ref{ExCoatElkin1974WaIRR}) below and the brief discussion in \sectref{subsModality}.}

\ea\label{ExNegNoWa}
    \glll Darak anya nyingan darak nyinkayirri. \\
        darak a\textsubscript{1}-a-nya nyingan darak nyin-w\textsubscript{2}a\textsubscript{2}-a-yirri\\
        go.in 3\textsc{m.sg}-go-\textsc{dist} 2\textsc{sg} go.in 2\textsc{sg}-\textsc{irr}-go-\textsc{cont}\\
    \trans `He enters, but you don't.' (lit. `He enters, you are not entering.') \citep[118, line 285]{Coate1966}
\z

Rather than acting as the primary marker of negation, \textit{wa}(\textit{y}) therefore mostly seems to serve to disambiguate negated verbs. In cases where such disambiguation is unnecessary, the irrealis suffices as negation marking: note that a likely reason why the negative marking is sufficiently implied in (\ref{ExNegNoWa}) is because of the contrast set up with the preceding positive clause. It should be noted, however, that the disambiguating function of the negation particle \textit{wa} is often necessary, since the irrealis does express modal functions suggested by the name of the category. This can be illustrated by the insubordinated verbal construction in (\ref{ExChapStance1AppBowerbirdLine04}) in which the irrealis signals a hypothetical event.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExChapStance1AppBowerbirdLine04}
\glll Anjaku rimij nginkenungarri ngin maji buluk ba wura jadarn.\\
anja-ku rimij ngin-w\textsubscript{2}a\textsubscript{2}-y\textsubscript{2}i-{\o}-nu-ngarri ngin maji buluk ba\textsubscript{2}-a wura jadarn\\
what-\textsc{dat} steal 1\textsc{sg.sbj-irr}-be-\textsc{prs}-2\textsc{sg}.\textsc{io}-\textsc{sub} 1\textsc{sg} \textsc{must} look.around \textsc{imp}-go sideways properly\\
\trans `Why would I steal from you? You should look around properly.' (100903-24NGUN, 0:13-0:17)
\end{exe}

The irrealis may also express epistemic modality, either by itself (as in \ref{ExCoatElkin1974WaIRREpistemic}) or in combination with a range of modal particles that, like the negation particle, appear in preverbal position. The minimal pair in (\ref{ExCoatElkin1974WaIRR}), adapted from the Ngarinyin dictionary \citet{CoatElkin1974}, shows the semantic contrast between the epistemic interpretation (without particle \textit{wa}) and the negative interpretation (with particle \textit{wa}) particularly clearly.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExCoatElkin1974WaIRR}
\begin{xlist}
\ex \label{ExCoatElkin1974WaIRREpistemic}
\glll Ngoy angkuma.\\
ngoy a-w\textsubscript{2}a\textsubscript{2}-ma-{\o}\\
breathe 3\textsc{m.sg}-\textsc{irr}-do-\textsc{prs}\\
\trans `He might breathe.'
\ex \label{ExCoatElkin1974WaIRRNegation}
\glll Wa ngoy angkuma.\\
wa ngoy a-w\textsubscript{2}a\textsubscript{2}-ma-{\o}\\
\textsc{neg} breathe 3\textsc{m.sg}-\textsc{irr}-do-\textsc{prs}\\
\trans `He won't breathe.' \citep[427, entry: \textit{ŋoj}]{CoatElkin1974}
\end{xlist}
\end{exe}

\subsection{Negation in non-declaratives}

The verbal marking in non-declarative negative clauses is the same as in declarative ones, i.e. all involve irrealis inflection. The expression of the preverbal particle is affected by illocution, however: in interrogative clauses, the question clitic \textit{=ka} attaches to the negative particle \textit{wa}, as in (\ref{ex:ngarinjin:6}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:ngarinjin:6}
\glll Waka laybiru kurrkengi?\\
wa=ka laybiru kurrk-yi-ngi\\
\textsc{neg}=\textsc{q} know 2\textsc{pl}.\textsc{irr}-be-\textsc{pst:irr} \\
\trans `Did you not know?' \citep[470, entry: \textit{waga}]{CoatElkin1974}
\end{exe}

In interrogative clauses, the ambiguity between a negative and a modal interpretation of the irrealis prefix holds to a similar degree as in declarative clauses, which explains the prevalent, although not absolute, occurrence of the preverbal negation marker in both. This ambiguity is neutralised in irrealis marked imperatives, however, which in my corpus do not occur with a preverbal \textit{wa}, cf. (\ref{exNegIMP}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{exNegIMP}
\glll Nyulwankurr kurrke.\\
nyulwankurr kurrk-y$_1$i\\
humbug 2\textsc{pl}.\textsc{irr}-be\\
\trans `Don't bother me.' (100831-01NGUN, 2:52-2:54)
\end{exe}

Plural imperatives, as in (\ref{exNegIMP}), are simply formed with the second plural irrealis prefix and no tense marking on the verb and the preverbal particle omitted.

Even though Ngarinyin has a dedicated imperative prefix for singular intransitive imperatives, as illustrated in the first clause in (\ref{ExChapStanceKajinkaIMP}), expression with this prefix cannot be explicitly negated since imperatives cannot combine with an irrealis form. Instead, the second person irrealis prefix can be used, without tense inflection. Instead of the negative particle \textit{wa}, several other preverbal particles are available, such as the modal particle \textit{kajinka} `cannot' in (\ref{ExChapStanceKajinkaIMP}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExChapStanceKajinkaIMP}
\glll Mok buwa kajinka buk nyingkalu!\\
mok bu-w\textsubscript{1}a kajinka buk nyinga\textsubscript{2}-w\textsubscript{2}a\textsubscript{2}-a-{\o}=lu\\
hide \textsc{imp}-fall \textsc{cannot} come.out 2\textsc{sg}-\textsc{irr}-go-\textsc{prs}=\textsc{prox}\\
\trans `Hide, you cannot come out!' (110925-NGUN, 2:45-2:48)
\end{exe}

Another particle that may take the preverbal position in negative imperatives, in the absence of the regular negation marker \textit{wa}, is \textit{burray}, cf. (\ref{ExRumsey1982NEGBurray}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExRumsey1982NEGBurray}
\glll Burray dalu winjaw ngabun!\\
burray dalu winja\textsubscript{2}-a\textsubscript{2}-w\textsubscript{1}u ngabun\\
\textsc{emph:neg} pour.out 3\textsc{n}\textsubscript{w}.\textsc{obj:2sg.sbj-irr}-act.on water\\
\trans `Don't pour out the water!' \citep[168]{Rumsey1982}
\end{exe}

The particle \textit{burray} in (\ref{ExRumsey1982NEGBurray}) highlights the negative imperative and is therefore glossed here as `emphatic negation' marker, but it can (diachronically) be analysed as \textit{burr-ay} `3\textsc{pl}-nothing'. Various inflected forms of \textit{-ay} are found in Ngarinyin in negative roles beyond negative verbal clauses, as illustrated in the next sections.

\subsection{Negation in stative predications}

Ngarinyin formally distinguishes two types of stative clauses, each with a separate marking strategy for negation. Stative clauses also appear to involve a third strategy that occurs both under verbal and non-verbal stative predication.

Verbal stative clauses, commonly formed with the verb -\textit{\textit{y}}$_1$\textit{i}- `be' or another stative intransitive verb, display the most frequent negation strategy in Ngarinyin, with the negation particle \textit{wa} and irrealis inflection, as in (\ref{exWaJukurlNyerrke}), (\ref{exWaDeyjBarrkumara}) and (\ref{exNgabundaAngke}).\footnote{Impressionistically, the verbal strategy appears to be slightly more used for the first three types of stative clauses listed in the questionnaire, i.e. that of equation, proper inclusion and attribution, and the non-verbal one introduced below in locative predication, existential predication and possessive predication but the correlation is not absolute, as, e.g., the locative stative clause in (\ref{exNgabundaAngke}) illustrates.}

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{exWaJukurlNyerrke}
  \begin{xlist}
  \ex 
    \glll Jukurl nyarri. \\
        jukurl nyarr-y\textsubscript{1}i \\
        happy \textsc{2pl}-be \\
    \glt ‘We are happy.’ (\textit{constructed}) 
  \ex
    \glll Wa jukurl nyerrke.\\
    wa jukurl nyerrka$_2$-y$_1$i\\
    \textsc{neg} happy 2\textsc{pl.excl:irr}-be\\
    \trans `We are not happy.' (090812JENGPDg, 1:00-1:03, \textit{edited})
  \end{xlist}
\end{exe}

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{exWaDeyjBarrkumara}
\begin{xlist}
  \ex 
    \glll Deyj badmara.\\
    deyj barr-ma-ra \\
    crack \textsc{3pl}-do-\textsc{pst}\\
    \glt ‘They are cracked.’ (\textit{constructed})
  \ex  
    \glll Wa deyj bargumara.\\
    wa deyj barrka$_2$ma-ra\\
    \textsc{neg} crack 3\textsc{pl:irr}-do-\textsc{pst}\\
    \trans `They are not cracked.' (C26:14)
  \end{xlist}
\end{exe}

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{exNgabundaAngke}
\begin{xlist}
  \ex 
    \glll Ngabunda e.\\
    ngabun-ra a\textsubscript{1}-y\textsubscript{1}i \\
    water-\textsc{loc} \textsc{3m.sg}-be \\
    \glt ‘He is in the water.’ (\textit{constructed})
  \ex
    \glll Ngabunda angke.\\
    ngabun-ra angka$_2$-y$_1$i\\
    water-\textsc{loc} 3\textsc{m.sg:irr}-be\\
    \trans `He is not in the water.' (\textit{constructed})
  \end{xlist}
\end{exe}

As is common in stative constructions cross-linguistically, `be'-verbs are optional in most stative constructions in Ngarinyin and are frequently left out, as in (\ref{exNginRimijbarda}). For the standard marking of negation, however, the irrealis marker requires a verbal host and therefore a form of the verb -\textit{y}$_1$\textit{i}- `be', or equivalent (\ref{exNginRimijbardaNEG}).

\begin{exe}
\ex
\begin{xlist}
\ex \label{exNginRimijbarda} \glll Ngin rimijbarda.\\
ngin rimij-barda\\
1\textsc{sg} steal-\textsc{nmlz}\\
\trans `I am a thief.' (100903-21NGUN, 3:10-3:11)
\ex \label{exNginRimijbardaNEG}
\glll Ngin rimijbarda ngangke.\\
ngin rimijbarda ngangka$_2$-y$_1$i-{\O}\\
1\textsc{sg} steal-\textsc{nmlz} 1\textsc{sg:irr}-be-\textsc{prs}\\
\trans `I am not a thief.' (\textit{constructed})
\end{xlist}
\end{exe}

Instead of a verbal predicate, Ngarinyin may also use the dedicated negative predicate -\textit{ay} `no(thing)' in stative and possessive clauses, as is the case in other Australian languages \citep[135]{Veselinova2013neg.exist}. Like nominal predicates in Ngarinyin more generally, this predicate takes a pronominal prefix that agrees with its head noun or pronominal subject and it can host nominal affixes, like case suffixes, but it does not display any verbal categories, as illustrated in B's response in (\ref{ExNoDogs}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExNoDogs}
\begin{xlist}
\exi{D:} \glll Kanangkurr burrnangka jirri?\\
kanangkurr burr-nangka jirri?\\
dog 3\textsc{pl}-\textsc{gen} 3\textsc{sg}.\textsc{ana}\\
\trans `Does he have dogs?'
\exi{B:} \glll Kanangkurr burraynangka.\\
kanangkurr burr-ay-nangka\\
dog 3\textsc{pl}-nothing-\textsc{gen}\\
\trans `He has no dogs.' (C62:8:24--27)
\end{xlist}
\end{exe}

The negative predicate -\textit{ay} `no(thing)' is quite versatile in Ngarinyin and seems to serve as the source construction for other, more crystallised forms in the language, like the particle \textit{burray} illustrated in (\ref{ExRumsey1982NEGBurray}) and in \sectref{SecNonClausalNEG}.

In addition to the \textit{wa} + irrealis marking strategy and the negative predicate \textit{-ay} `no(thing)', Ngarinyin appears to have (had) a third negation strategy, which is attested in stative constructions, simply formed by the question clitic =\textit{ka}. This strategy is found both in non-verbal clauses, as in (\ref{Ex-KaNEG}) and verbal stative constructions, as in the second clause in (\ref{Ex-KaNEGVerb}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{Ex-KaNEG}
\glll Bidibidika budmareri.\\
bidibidi=ka burr-ma-ra-yirri\\
small=\textsc{q} 3\textsc{pl}-do-\textsc{pst}-\textsc{cont}\\
\trans `They are not small, they were saying.' (\citealp{CoatElkin1974}, entry: \textit{bidibidi})
\end{exe}

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{Ex-KaNEGVerb}
\glll Didi emularu maniyangarri embularuka malwa \\
di-di {\O}-emularu ma$_1$-aniyangarri {\O}-embularu=ka malwa\\
\textsc{n}$_m$.\textsc{ana-red} 3\textsc{m.sg}-foot \textsc{n}$_m$-good 3\textsc{m.sg}-foot=\textsc{q} be.bad\\

\glll  mingkengingarri.\\
       mangk-y$_1$i-ngi-ngarri\\
      3\textsc{n}$_m$:\textsc{irr}-be-\textsc{pst}-\textsc{sub}\\

\trans `That's how his feet were good, his feet were not ugly.' (C6:6:70)
\end{exe}

The usage of the question particle as in (\ref{Ex-KaNEG}) and (\ref{Ex-KaNEGVerb}) is rare in my contemporary text corpus of Ngarinyin, but does occur occasionally and is also reported by \citet{Rumsey1982}.

\subsection{Negation in non-main clauses}\label{ExNonMainClauses}

Ngarinyin has two clearly identifiable complex clause constructions: a general subordinate construction and a construction for attributing speech, thought and intentions. In both constructions the marking of negation is the same as in simple clauses.

The Ngarinyin subordinate construction is signalled by the verbal suffix \textit{-ngarri} \textsc{sub} and often appears to carry an adverbial function (translated as `because' or `when/while'), rather than signal clear dependency relation with a main clause, as illustrated in (\ref{ExNEGinSUB-positiveVersion}). A similar structure has been reported for other Australian languages (\citealt{Hale1976}; \citealt{Nordlinger2006}). This construction also frequently occurs in discourse without an accompanying main clause, carrying a pragmatic backgrounding function instead of a syntactic one \citep[cf.][]{Mithun2008extension}. Negation in clauses marked with \textit{-ngarri} is indicated with the negative particle \textit{wa} and the irrealis prefix, like negation in other Ngarinyin verbal constructions, as in (\ref{ExNEGinSUB}).

\begin{exe}
\ex
\begin{xlist}
\ex \label{ExNEGinSUB-positiveVersion}
\glll jarl birrinngarri\\
jarl burr-y\textsubscript{1}i-n-ngarri\\
put.in.oven 3\textsc{pl}-be-\textsc{prs}-\textsc{sub}\\
\trans `When/Because they baked it' (\textit{constructed})
\ex \label{ExNEGinSUB}
\glll wa jarl burrkengarri\\
wa jarl burrka\textsubscript{2}-y\textsubscript{1}i-ngarri\\
\textsc{neg} put.in.oven 3\textsc{pl}:\textsc{irr}-be-\textsc{sub}\\
\trans `When/Because they don't bake it' (C55:10:177)
\end{xlist}
\end{exe}

Like in simple clauses, the negative particle \textit{wa} may be left out in subordinate constructions, which, therefore, do not appear to differ in any way from negation in non-subordinated clauses.

The same assessment applies to the second type of complex clause construction in Ngarinyin, illustrated in (\ref{ExNEGinRS}) and (\ref{ExNGuruNGinke}). This construction consists of a clause or subclausal element (representing an utterance, thought or intention), followed by an inflected form of the verb -\textit{ma}- `do', which in this context is commonly translated as `say', `think' or `want' (for details of this construction, see \citealp[157--166]{Rumsey1982} and \citealp{Spronck2015}).

\subsection{Negative lexicalisations}

Negative lexicalisations in Ngarinyin appear to follow the most common semantic patterns \citet{Veselinova2013lexicalization, Veselinova2013neg.exist} describes, including concepts such as `be unknown/not know' (as in \ref{ExNotKnow}), `not understand', `not present', `not sacred', etc. As (\ref{ExNotKnow}) and the contrasting positive example of `know' in (\ref{ExPositiveKnow}) illustrate, however, negative lexicalisations neither receive a specific form of marking, nor stand in a paradigmatic relation with their positive counterpart.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExNotKnow}
\glll Nyinilimbani.\\
nyin-ilimba-ni\\
\textsc{2sg.obj:1sg.sbj}-not.know-\textsc{pst}\\
\trans `I did not know you.' (\citealp{CoatElkin1974}, entry: -\textit{alimba})
\end{exe}

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExPositiveKnow}
\begin{xlist}
\exi{A:}
\glll Jirringka laybiru nyindinangka?\\
jirri-nga$_1$=ka laybiru nyinda$_2$-y$_1$i-nangka\\
m.\textsc{ana}-only=\textsc{q} know 2\textsc{sg}-be-3\textsc{sg:obl}\\
\trans `Do you know only him?'
\exi{B:}
\glll Jirringa laybiru nginangka.\\
jirri-nga$_1$ laybiru ngi$_2$-y$_1$i-nangka\\
m.\textsc{ana}-only know 1\textsc{sg}-be-3\textsc{sg:obl}\\
\trans `I know only him.'
\end{xlist}
\end{exe}

In the absence of any specific formal marking of negative concepts as in (\ref{ExNotKnow}), negative lexemes in Ngarinyin do not constitute a distinguishable class of verbs and their relevance for negation primarily emerges in translation. A relevant cultural observation in this respect is that licensed distribution of knowledge according to life stages and (kinship) affiliation is central to membership of the Ngarinyin community and absence or presence of an object or entity may have direct consequences on daily life. For example, not belonging to a certain social group, not talking to an avoidance relative or feeling the presence of deceased ancestors in a place or situation, all affect concrete decisions in the behaviour and actions of individuals, as they do in other Australian Indigenous societies (cf. \citealt{Fleming2014}; \citealt{Merlan2020}). Lexemes applying to such cases may require a negative translation in English, but are therefore not necessarily felt as less real or less part of the physical world for a Ngarinyin speaker, which might explain that these concepts do not form a dedicated lexical class.

The only other lexicogrammatical domain closely related to negation in Ngarinyin is that of modality, with which negation shares its main coding features. For example in (\ref{ExChapStanceKajinkaPRS}) the irrealis receives a modal interpretation, signalled by the particle \textit{kajinka} `cannot', appearing in the same slot as the preverbal negation particle \textit{wa}.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExChapStanceKajinkaPRS}
\glll Kajinka ni wungkuminda.\\
kajinka ni wunga\textsubscript{2}-w\textsubscript{2}a\textsubscript{2}-minda-{\o}\\
\textsc{cannot} think 3\textsc{n}\textsubscript{w}.\textsc{obj:3sg.sbj-irr}-take-\textsc{prs}\\
\trans `He can't think about it.' (100809-01NGUN, 3:01-3:03, \textit{elicited})
\end{exe}

Given the similarities between the expression of modality and negation in Ngarinyin and other Worrorran languages, some authors have questioned whether negation in these languages actually constitutes a separate construction class, or, e.g., rather forms a subtype of modality. I will briefly address this discussion in \sectref{subsModality}.

\section{Non-clausal negation} \label{SecNonClausalNEG}

\subsection{Negative replies}

As the brief dialogue in (\ref{ExPositiveKnow}) illustrates, positive replies may be formed in Ngarinyin by echoing a turn of the addressee. Alternatively, speakers may use the interjection \textit{yow} `yes' to confirm a positive reply (as in \ref{exAkaPositive} below). The negative counterpart of these strategies is constructed in a parallel fashion: a speaker may deny a proposition simply with a negative clause, or by using the interjection \textit{burray} `no', as in (\ref{Ex090812JENGPDiBurray1}).

        warda wunja\textsubscript{2}-w\textsubscript{1}u-n ngurr-ba-ngarri?\\

The second turn in (\ref{Ex090812JENGPDiBurray1}), by speaker JE, uses the interjection \textit{wije} `different', which is here interpreted as `absolutely not'. This use of \textit{wije} `different' appears not to be dialectal, however, the interjection \textit{burray} `no', which is a negative reply to the question \textit{warda wunjon ngurrbangarri} `do you like hitting?', is frequently used in this function. The interjection likely derives from the inflected form \textit{burr-ay} `3\textsc{pl}-no' and, as \citet{Veselinova2013neg.exist} points out, the use of negative existentials is quite common cross-linguistically. The interjection \textit{burray} `no' in (\ref{Ex090812JENGPDiBurray1}) is a fully conventionalised, non-variable form, rather than an inflected negative existential predicate (hence the different gloss) and, as illustrated in \sectref{subsNegativeQuantifers} also has additional quantifying functions.

In addition to \textit{burray} `no', which has the positive equivalent \textit{yow} `yes', Ngarinyin also has an interjection that is not part of a positive-negative pair, but exclusively constitutes a negative strategy: \textit{aka} `not so'. This interjection denies an expectation raised in the preceding clause, often in interaction, as in (\ref{ExKojBaBudmanangkaWaWarda}).

In the situation described in the narrative fragment in (\ref{ExKojBaBudmanangkaWaWarda}) the drinkers (`they') invite the protagonist to join them for a beer, leading to the socially desirable response to accept, which is rejected by the particle \textit{aka} `not so'. \subsection{Negative indefinites and quantifiers} \label{subsNegativeQuantifers}

Ngarinyin has two negative expressions used as independent pronouns and adnominal quantifiers: \textit{malyan} `no', `nothing' and \textit{burray} `no', `nothing, `nobody'.

In the brief dialogue in (\ref{Ex11101502-PNNKDDJEUDNeg}) the speakers contemplate the loss of traditional knowledge and JE's first turn shows \textit{malyan} `nothing' both as part of the predicative construction `there is nothing' and the independent expression `nothing', `none'. While \textit{malyan} `no', `nothing' appears to be exclusively used for objects, \textit{burray} `no, nothing, nobody' can also refer to human beings, either as an independent pronoun or as a modifier. Even in the latter function it does not agree with the noun it modifies, however, as illustrated in (\ref{ExBurrayYila}).

\begin{exe}

    \glll Wa mara bundawi dubalangarri burray yila.\\

An alternative interpretation of the phrase \textit{burray yila} `no children' in (\ref{ExBurrayYila}) could be a predicative translation like `there are none, children', in which case \textit{burray} may be analysed as the predicative construction \textit{burr-ay} `3\textsc{pl}-no'. In contemporary Ngarinyin, however, \textit{burray}, both in its attributive and pronominal function, does not appear in any other inflectional form, so should therefore best be treated as a single, non-segmented morpheme.

Ngarinyin does not have a common strategy for deriving negative nouns or modifiers, but can approximate such derivations through the negative use of the question clitic (as in \ref{Ex-KaNEG}), or, more commonly, through collocations with the form {-\textit{aynangka}} `not having', as in (\ref{ExNoDogs}).

An example in which the suffix -\textit{nangka}, which may signal both genitive case and nominalisation, occurs in both functions together with the predicate \textit{-ay} `no' is shown in (\ref{ExMunduwarriAynangkanangka}).

\begin{exe}

\trans `He has no lice.' (lit. `(He is) a person not having lice') (100722-04NGUS, 1:49-1:51)
\end{exe}

\section{Other aspects of negation} \label{SecOtherAspects}

\subsection{The scope of negation}

Where negation is marked with a verb inflection in Ngarinyin, it necessarily scopes over the entire clause. Or, at least, the language does not allow to formally distinguish clausal negation from negation that only scopes over a verb construction. Hence, both translations in (\ref{exNEGscope}) are equally appropriate.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{exNEGscope}
\glll Yali wa banyirrkumerernnyirri.\\
yali wa ba-nyirrka\textsubscript{2}-mara-y\textsubscript{1}i-nyirri\\
kangaroo \textsc{neg} \textsc{3pl.obj-1pl.excl.sbj:irr}-take-\textsc{irr.pst-du}\\
\trans `We did not catch (any) kangaroos.' / `We caught no kangaroos.' (100722-08NGUS, 3:58-4:00)
\end{exe}

As far as I am aware, speakers do not differentiate between these scope interpretations, nor do they do so through prosodic means. Highlighting narrow negative scope is of course possible under contrastive negation and in verbless clauses, in which negative verbal predicates and the negation marking question clitic scope over their associated head noun.

\subsection{Marking of NPs in the scope of negation}

Elements within the scope of the negative inflection (or a negative modifier) do not receive any special marking, such as a case form. This observation fits with the use of case in Ngarinyin more generally: although the language has a set of semantic cases to express relational and locational meanings, these are not conditioned by their syntactic environment and their use is always optional \citep[cf.][19--25]{Spronck2015}.

\subsection{Reinforcing negation}

An emphatic suffix that exclusively attaches to verbs is -\textit{yali} \textsc{emph}. It may occur on negative verbs as well, thereby reinforcing the negative meaning expressed, as illustrated in (\ref{ExNEGyali}).

\ea \label{ExNEGyali}
    \glll Wa durr irrkumareyali ru-di nangkurr\phantom{---} irrwini.\\
    wa durr irrka\textsubscript{2}-mara-{\o}-yali ru-di nangkurr irr-w\textsubscript{1}u\textsubscript{1}-ni\\
    \textsc{neg} strike \textsc{3m.sg.obj:1pl.incl.sbj:irr}-do-\textsc{prs}-\textsc{emph} only-\textsc{n}\textsubscript{w}.\textsc{ana} miss \textsc{3m.sg.obj:1pl.incl.sbj}-act.on-\textsc{pst}\\
    \trans `We do not hit him at all, we completely missed him.' (C58:2:37-38)\footnote{The difference in tense between these clauses may be an error, but such changes are also common in mythological narratives, in which the narrator frequently alternates between presenting the events as part of an eternal here-and-now and a past, ancestral world.}
\z

The most common intensifier in Ngarinyin is the suffix -\textit{nga} `only', `just', which attaches to (pro)nominal elements. It frequently combines with \textit{malyan} `no', `nothing' to form \textit{malyannga} `nothing at all'.

\subsection{Negation, coordination and complex clauses}

Ngarinyin discourse does not often involve explicit coordination, but negative meanings can be interpreted across clauses, as in the case of the example of contrastive negation in (\ref{exContrastiveNegation}).

\ea
\label{exContrastiveNegation}
    \glll Mangarri wa minjarl burrkume me wa minjarl burkume, burray, nalijanga.\\
    mangarri wa minjarl burrka$_2$-ma-y$_1$i me wa minjarl burrka$_2$-ma-y$_1$i, burray, nalija-nga\\
    food \textsc{neg} eat 3\textsc{pl:irr}-do-\textsc{irr:pst} food \textsc{neg} eat 3\textsc{pl:irr}-do-\textsc{irr:pst} no tea-only\\
    \trans `They did not eat one type of food or another type of food. Nothing, only tea.' (111013-01NGUN: 2:13-2:16)
\z

In this example, the positive phrase \textit{nalijanga} `only tea' contrasts with the preceding negative clauses \textit{mangarri}/\textit{me wa minjarl burrkume} `he did not eat food'.\footnote{For further discussion of this construction, see example (\ref{exNegativeCoordination}).} In my analysis, this constitutes a pragmatic contrast, rather than a distinct syntactic construction.

Contrastive negation is also exemplified in (\ref{ExNegNoWa}), repeated for convenience below as (\ref{ExNegNoWaREPEATED}), which has the same structure as standard clausal negation elsewhere, but the presence of the free pronoun, which is often omitted in Ngarinyin, results from the pragmatic prominence of the subject, \textit{\uline{nyingan} darak nyinkayirri} `\uline{you} do not enter (he does)'.


\ea \label{ExNegNoWaREPEATED}
    \glll Darak anya nyingan darak nyinkayirri.\\
    darak a\textsubscript{1}-a-nya nyingan darak nyin-w\textsubscript{2}a\textsubscript{2}-a-yirri\\
    go.in 3\textsc{m.sg}-go-\textsc{dist} 2\textsc{sg} go.in 2\textsc{sg}-\textsc{irr}-go-\textsc{cont}\\
    \trans `He enters, but you don't.' (lit. `He enters, you are not entering.') \citep[118, line 285]{Coate1966}
\z

These examples show that, even though contrastive negation does not form a coherent syntactic construction, it may affect the expression of various elements in the clause.

In complex clauses, a negated main clause scopes over the subordinated clause, as in (\ref{ExWaWardaWanyirrkoBudmangarriJindaPicture})


\protectedex{\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExWaWardaWanyirrkoBudmangarriJindaPicture}
{Commenting on a picture from the Family Problems Picture Task} \citep{SanRoqueEtAl2012}. \\
    \glll Wa wardawa nyirrko budmangarri jinda {picture}.\\
    wa warda-wa nyirr-w$_2$a$_2$-w$_1$u bud-ma-{\o}-ngarri jinda {picture}\\

Ngarinyin does not explicitly mark coordinated clauses, but direct apposition as in (\ref{exNegativeCoordination}) may be interpreted as coordination. Since negative inflection occurs on every finite verb, negation marking in both `coordinated' and subordinated sentences is marked on each negated clause.

\ea \label{exNegativeCoordination}
    \glll Mangarri wa minjarl burrkume me wa minjarl burkume.\\

As these examples illustrate, negation marking in Ngarinyin complex clauses does not differ from marking in simple clauses.

\subsection{Miscellaneous aspects of negation}
\subsubsection{Negative transport}

Ngarinyin does not display negative transport, i.e. the negative interpretation of an embedded clause on the basis of negation in a matrix clause (e.g. `I don't think that it will rain today' for `I think that it will not rain today').

Two syntactic factors preclude this phenomenon from happening in the language: the absence of a construction that explicitly marks thought as opposed to speech \citep[97]{Spronck2015} and the lack of a common strategy to embed propositions, i.e. a class of propositional attitude constructions \citep[133]{Spronck2015}.

\subsubsection{Non-negative uses of negatives and expletive negation}

I am not aware of any occurrences of expletive negation in Ngarinyin, but the corpus contains instances of the use of \textit{\textit{waka}} \textsc{neg=q} in tag questions. Although (\ref{exTagQuestionWAKA}) has been taken from a historical corpus and the example probably dates from the 1960s, English was already widespread in the Ngarinyin community at the time, so it is possible that this particular use represents an English calque.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{exTagQuestionWAKA}
\glll Aw ru dambun wungunjura waringarri waka?\\
aw ru dambun wa$_1$-ungunja-ra waringarri waka\\
\textsc{inj} only place n$_w$-where-\textsc{loc} East \textsc{neg=q}\\
\trans `Ah, only the place is somewhere East, isn't it?' (C62:84)
\end{exe}

Phasal polarity is not marked differently than negation in general, with an added temporal adverb. Compare the following example from (\citealt{CoateOates1970}, orthography updated to the Ngarinyin spelling used in the rest of this chapter).

\ea\label{ex:nga:additional}

    then \textsc{3obj:3pl.sbj}-spear-\textsc{pst} {cry.out} \textsc{3m.sg}-take-\textsc{pst} spear while \textsc{neg} know \textsc{3pl.irr}-be-\textsc{pst-3sg.obj} white \\
\glt `He cried out when he was being speared, for as yet they didn't know the white man' \citep[82]{CoateOates1970}

\subsubsection{Diachronic notes and observations: re-cycling \textit{wa}} \label{subsDiachrony}

Both the preverbal negation particle and the inflectional negation marker (irrealis), which \citet{Rumsey1982} transcribes as w\textsubscript{2}a\textsubscript{2}, have the same underlying phonological form. Even the question clitic =\textit{ka}, which is also used to mark (non-verbal) negation (as in \ref{Ex-KaNEG}), may be derived from /wa/, since in Rumsey's (\citeyear{Rumsey1982}) notation -\textit{w}\textsubscript{2} may `strengthen' to a velar nasal. This leads to the intriguing possibility that the three main elements involved in the marking of negation in Ngarinyin have all derived from the same diachronic source. Speculatively, this could have taken the form of a `Jespersen (re)cycling' process (\citealt{Jespersen1917}; \citealt{Dahl1979}), whereby, e.g., the negation particle could have developed into an inflectional element and clitic through several steps of grammaticalisation.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Hypothesis: Jespersen (re)cycling of \textit{w}\textsubscript{2}\textit{a}\\
  irrealis inflection = question clitic \citep[128]{Rumsey1982} = negation particle?
\end{itemize}

While this scenario would make it easier to explain why the question clitic \textit{=ka} \textsc{q} is part of the inventory of negation markers in Ngarinyin as well, it is not supported by solid evidence in the absence of sufficient long term historical data.

Unfortunately, comparative data within the Worrorran language family \citep{McGregorRumsey2009} also does not offer further clues for either confirming or rejecting the hypothesis either since the pattern of similarity between the preverbal particle and the inflectional marking of negation seems to be unique to Ngarinyin.

In one of the closest neighbours of Ngarinyin, Worrorra (ISO: \href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/wro}{wro}, Glottocode: \href{https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/worr1237}{worr1237}), the negation particle has a similar form as in Ngarinyin but the verbal inflection is not cognate, cf. (\ref{exWorrorra}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{exWorrorra} {Worrorra} (Worrorran, Kimberley region)\\
    \glll Waa jukurljukurl banganineerri.\\
    waa jukurl-jukurl ba-nga=ni-n-nu-eerri\\
    not happy-happy \textsc{cnte}-1=be-\textsc{npst}-\textsc{2dat}-\textsc{prog}\\
    \trans `I'm not happy to see you.' \citep[195]{Clendon2014}
\end{exe}

In Wunambal, which \citet{McGregorRumsey2009} identify as another branch of the Worrorran family, the preverbal particle resembles the negation particle of Worrorra's Eastern neighbour, the unrelated language Kija, rather than the negation particle of Worrorra and Ngarinyin.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{exWunambal} {Wunambal} (Worrorran, Kimberley region) \\
    \glll Nguwa minja nyaanama.\\
    nguwa minja nyaa-na=ma\\
    \textsc{neg} eat 1\textsc{excl:pl}-\textsc{neg}=do\\
    \trans `We don't eat (it).' \citep[184, cf. Kija (Jarrakan) \textit{nguwan} NEG]{Carr2000}
\end{exe}

Even though the functions of irrealis and negation marking are shared by the inflectional elements in all three languages of the family, the Worrorra and Wunambal inflections do not appear to be cognate with the Ngarinyin one.

Also in the Worrorra dialect Unggumi (\ref{exUnggumi}) and the Wunambal dialect Gambera (\ref{exGambera}) negation marking appears to have a different origin. The preverbal particle differs from Ngarinyin in both languages.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{exUnggumi} {Unggumi} (Worrorran, Kimberley region) \\
    \glll Nyindi mudan jay bungunu.\\
    nyindi mudan jay bu-ngu-nu\\
    2\textsc{sg} \textsc{neg} laugh \textsc{neg}?-2\textsc{sg}-do?\\
    \trans `You are not laughing.' (Coate1676B, 9:45--9:47, \textit{tentative gloss})
\ex \label{exGambera} {Gambera} (Worrorran, Kimberley region) \\
    \glll Gabo nguwa buniyangi.\\
    gabo nguwa bu-ni-ya-ngi\\
    no \textsc{neg} 3\textsc{sg}-\textsc{neg}-go-\textsc{pst}\\
    \trans `No, he did not go.' (Coate1675B, 47:01--47:03, \textit{tentative gloss})
\end{exe}

Only in Worrorra does the preverbal particle seem cognate, whereas the form of the inflectional marker differs from that of Ngarinyin in all of its closest relatives. These observations may indeed support a scenario in which negation marking in Ngarinyin has undergone a process that is rather idiosyncratic within the Worrorran family and involves the particle \textit{wa}, but none of the clues introduced here constitute definitive evidence.

\subsubsection{Negation and modality} \label{subsModality}
Across the Worrorran language family, epistemic modal marking and negation show strong similarities, as is reflected in the glossing of inflectional elements involved in negation in these languages. In Worrorra, \citet{Clendon2014} glosses the inflection on negative verbs (and epistemic modals) as `counterfactual', suggesting that negation falls within the class of modal structures. \citet{Carr2000}, noting a similar overlap between negative and epistemic meanings in Wunambal decides to gloss the inflectional element involved in both as `negation', given the relative frequency of the negative function.

The decision between these glosses is somewhat arbitrary: in all cases the inflectional element that in Ngarinyin I have glossed as `irrealis', following \citet{Rumsey1982}, reflects an event that does/did \textit{not occur in the real world}. This core meaning can be brought out particularly poignantly in Ngarinyin by considering the modal particles that do and do not combine with irrealis inflection. The particles that are shaded \colorbox{light-gray}{grey} in \tabref{TabModeMarkersReordered} all combine with the irrealis, like the negation marker. The particles without colouring combine with the regular indicative.\footnote{The one particle shown in a box in the table, \fbox{\textit{yaku}} `try', has a different mood restriction: it only combines with future tense or the imperative. For further exemplification of each of these markers, see \citet{Spronck2015}.}

\begin{table}
\centering
    \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{llll}
    \lsptoprule
     & {negative} & {neutral} & {positive} \\
     \cmidrule [\heavyrulewidth]{2-4}
     {epistemic} & \colorbox{light-gray}{\textit{kajinka}} `cannot' & & \colorbox{light-gray}{\textit{biyarra}} `can' \\
     & & \textit{marri} `would' &\\
     & & \textit{=karra} `maybe' & \\
     \cmidrule{2-4}
     {neutral} & \colorbox{light-gray}{\textit{wa}} `not' & &\\
     & \colorbox{light-gray}{\textit{burray}} `no', `not' &  &\\
     \cmidrule{2-4}
     {deontic} & \textit{menya} `should not (have)' & & \colorbox{light-gray}{\textit{biya}} `ought to' \\
     & & \textit{maji} `should' & \\
     & & \fbox{\textit{yaku}} `try' & \\
    \lspbottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\caption{The Ngarinyin mood markers ordered by modal meaning and polarity} \label{TabModeMarkersReordered}
\end{table}

Each of the particles that are not restricted to irrealis constructions indicate modal meanings about events that either do occur or need to occur, according to the attitude indexed by the modal, whereas those particles combining with irrealis inflection exclusively reflect non-occurring or hypothetical events. The non-irrealis particles \textit{marri} `would' and \textit{maji} `should' signal events that customarily occur or \textit{have to} occur, events with the irrealis particles \textit{kajinka} `cannot', \textit{burray} `not' and \textit{wa} `not' do not occur and the irrealis particles \textit{biyarra} `can' and \textit{biya} `ought (to have)', indicate that an event is held to be merely possible or should have occurred (but didn't), respectively. (The clitic \textit{=karra} `maybe' is included for completeness but is not a preverbal particle and does therefore not condition irrealis mood.)

Given these distributions, the one apparent outlier seems to be \textit{menya} `should not (have)', which expresses a negative meaning but does not combine with the irrealis. For a closer inspection of the semantics of this particle, consider (\ref{ExChapStance1AppYilurrunLine038}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ExChapStance1AppYilurrunLine038}
    \glll Bala wungo amara arri ngawa menyi merr amanga.\\
    bala wunga\textsubscript{1}-w\textsubscript{1}u-{\o} a\textsubscript{1}-ma-ra arri ngawa menya merr a\textsubscript{1}-ma-nga\\
    spread 3\textsc{n}\textsubscript{w}.\textsc{obj:1sg.sbj}-act.on-\textsc{fut} 3\textsc{m.sg}-do-\textsc{pst} about rain alas get.near \textsc{3m.sg.obj:3sg.sbj}-take-\textsc{pst}\\
    \trans `He wanted to spread it out, but when there was rain it got close to him, although it shouldn't have.' \citep[2, \textit{retranscribed and glosses added}]{Coate1970}
\end{exe}

The situation described in (\ref{ExChapStance1AppYilurrunLine038}) makes it clear that the deontic meaning `should not' applies to an event the speaker would like not to have occurred, \textit{but it did}. For this reason, I have glossed the particle as `alas' in (\ref{ExChapStance1AppYilurrunLine038}); it reflects an event that is presented as having taken place in the real world and therefore belongs with the non-irrealis particles. Although the status of the irrealis as a modal category appears to be varied and controversial in some languages \citep{Mithun1995relativity}, in Ngarinyin irrealis marking seems to be highly consistent: the category applies to events that are portrayed as not actually occurring. This naturally includes negated events.

\section{Summary}

In summary, Ngarinyin has a small number of highly regular strategies for marking negation: both clauses and non-verbal phrases can be negated, although the division between the two is not always fully transparent.

As a general characterisation of the semantics of negation in Ngarinyin, I would suggest that it seems to be concerned with three separate semantic dimensions.

First, and most prominently, the relation between (verbally marked) negation and modality appears to hinge on a core meaning of the inflection involved in both categories: the prefix analysed as `irrealis' signals that the event denoted by a verb so marked should not be interpreted as taking place in the actual world. If one would like to characterise this meaning as a pole of a scale it would be a scale of reality vs. irreality, as is further demonstrated by the relation between negative meanings, irreality and modality illustrated in \sectref{subsModality} below.

Second, the non-verbal strategy does not receive irrealis marking and is either signalled with the non-verbal predicate \textit{-ay} `no, none' or the question clitic \textit{=ka}, attaching to the negated referent. The main semantic distinction made with these strategies could perhaps be qualified as an opposition between absence and presence.

Finally, in the realm of discourse and argumentative dialogue, the interjection \textit{aka} `not so' (as in \ref{ExKojBaBudmanangkaWaWarda}) carries a function that could be qualified as `disalignment' \citep{DuBois2007}, contradicting the interlocutor's expectations or an implicature raised by a sentence. The particle \textit{burray} `no', which may both occur as a preverbal negation particle and an interjection, connects `discourse' negation with clausal negation \textit{and} non-verbal negation, demonstrating that the same lexical element can operate in all three negation types in Ngarinyin.

The marking strategies identified in this chapter are summarised in \tabref{TabSummary}, shown here along with their specific functions.

\centering

    \lsptoprule

    \textit{wa} \textsc{neg} + \textsc{irr}- & any type of clausal negation \\
    \textsc{irr}- & clausal negation (but potentially ambiguous) \\
    \textit{=ka} \textsc{q} (+ \textsc{irr}-) & sub-clausal or non-verbal negation \\
    \textit{-ay} `nothing' & negative predication \\

This chapter illustrated the various constructions and strategies in which these markers and inflections appear and noted that negation in Ngarinyin is highly consistent. It also demonstrates close interaction with the semantic (and morphological) domain of modality.
