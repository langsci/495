\title{Negation in Kambaata}

\author{Yvonne Treis\affiliation{CNRS-LLACAN}}%HC orcid link doesn't work \orcid{0000-0002-0685-8716}
\abstract{The Ethiopian language Kambaata (Cushitic) has five distinct negative inflectional suffixes that negate (i) declarative main verbs and non-verbal predicates, (ii) imperatives, (iii) jussives and benedictives, (iv) converbs and (v) relative verbs. Affirmative and negative verb forms often do not match each other in a one-to-one relation, but paradigmatic and constructional asymmetries can be observed. Depending on the verb type, aspectual and modal distinctions are neutralized under negation, the number of different subject indexes is reduced, the distinction between same subject and different subject forms is lost, and changes occur in the morphological makeup of verb forms. Finally, not all affirmative paradigms have dedicated negative counterparts. Most noteworthy from a typological point of view are Kambaata’s negative relative verbs. Unlike affirmative relative verbs, which have exclusively verbal morphology, negative relative verbs are almost perfect verb-adjective hybrids with their combination of verbal and adjectival inflection. They index the person, gender, number and honorificity of their subject, and agree in case and gender with their head noun. Apart from inflectional means of negation, Kambaata has a productive caritive derivation -\textit{beel} ‘-less’ that generates adjectives from nouns.

\keywords{Cushitic, inflectional negation, derivational negation, asymmetry, negative participle}}

\section{The language}\label{sec:treis:1}
\subsection{General information}\label{sec:treis:1.1}

The Kambaata language (ISO: \href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/ktb}{ktb}, Glottocode: \href{https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/kamb1316}{kamb1316}) is spoken by the Kambaata, Xambaaro and Donga people, who are settled around the Hambarrichcho massif in southern Ethiopia, about 300\,km southwest of the Ethiopian capital Addis Ababa. According to the last published census, Kambaata (endonym: \textit{Kambaatissáta}) has more than 600,000 speakers \citep[91]{CSA2007}, the large majority of whom live in the Kambaata-Xambaaro Zone of the Southern Nations, Nationalities and Peoples’ Region. Kambaata belongs to the Highland East Cushitic branch of the Cushitic family (Afroasiatic phylum). There are possibly slight regional differences in prosody, grammar and lexicon (whose exact nature is still to be explored) across the Kambaata-speaking area, but these do not impact mutual intelligibility. The immediate neighbors of the Kambaata are speakers of other Highland East Cushitic languages (Hadiyya and Alaaba) and Ometo languages of the Omotic family (Wolaitta and Dawro). Amharic, the Ethiopian lingua franca, is the most important second language of Kambaata speakers. Kambaata is used as a medium of instruction in public primary schools and is taught as a subject up to grade 12. In 2018, Wachamo University started a Kambaata language BA program on its Duu\-raame campus. Despite having been taught in schools since 1997, Kambaata remains an overwhelmingly oral language, while Amharic is the written language in private and official contexts. Overall, Kambaata is still linguistically vital. Intergenerational transmission seems stable in rural areas yet threatened in towns. Amharic is dominant in certain communication contexts, such as social media, TV, religious services, political meetings, offices, courts, schools, and parts of the retail and gastronomic industries. Kambaata is increasingly being restricted to communication within the family, with friends and neighbors.

The official Kambaata orthography is based on the Roman script (\citealt[73--80]{Treis2008}; \citealt{Alemu2016}) and follows the spelling conventions of the Oromo Qubee orthography. The official Kambaata orthography is adopted for all data transcription in this contribution with one important addition: Phonemic stress is marked by an acute accent. The following Kambaata graphemes are not in accordance with IPA conventions: <ph> /p’/, <x> /t’/, <q> /k’/, <j> /dʒ/, <c> /tʃ’/, <ch> /tʃ/, <sh> /ʃ/, <y> /j/ and <’> /Ɂ/. Geminate consonants and long vowels are marked by doubling the grapheme, e.g. <shsh> /ʃ:/ and <ee> /e:/. Nasalization is marked by a macron, e.g. <ā> /ã/. The data for this paper comes from my recorded corpus of narratives and conversations (marked by the recorded speaker’s initials, the date, and the file number or name), my field notes of volunteered or elicited data as well as a corpus of locally published written Kambaata texts. The structure of the paper follows the questionnaire provided by the editors (\citetv{chapters/questionnaire}).

\subsection{Typological profile}\label{sec:treis:1.2}

Kambaata is agglutinating-fusional and strictly suffixing. Its constituent order is consistently head-final; hence all modifiers precede the noun in the NP, and all dependent clauses precede independent main clauses. The last constituent in a sentence is usually a fully finite main verb or a copula. The following open word classes are defined on morphosyntactic grounds: nouns, adjectives, verbs, ideophones, and interjections.

Kambaata is a nominative-accusative language. The nominative is the subject case; the accusative marks direct objects and certain adverbial constituents, and also serves as the citation form of nouns and adjectives. Nouns are marked for gender (masculine vs. feminine); the assignment of grammatical gender is mostly arbitrary, with the exception of nouns referring to human beings and higher animals, where it is sex-based. Nouns distinguish nine case forms (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative, ablative, instrumental, locative, oblique, predicative), all of which are marked by a segmental suffix and a specific stress pattern.  

Adnominal adjectives, numerals and demonstratives agree with their head noun in case and gender. The case system of adnominals is reduced to three forms, namely nominative, accusative and oblique, with the oblique form marking agreement with non-nominative/non-accusative head nouns.

All verbs, with the exception of verbal nouns, carry simple or bipartite subject indexes, as exemplified by the structures of an affirmative declarative main verb in \figref{fig:treis:1} and a jussive main verb in \figref{fig:treis:2}.

Verbs inflect for aspect (imperfective, perfective, perfect, progressive), mood (declarative, imperative, jussive, benedictive, apprehensive), polarity (affirmative, negative) and dependency status (main verbs, relative verbs, converbs, purposive verbs); the morphemes realizing these grammatical categories are placed in the slot after the first subject index. Inflectional morphemes tend to fuse into inseparable portmanteau morphemes and are therefore often unsegmented in the examples. Direct and indirect objects can be expressed by suffixed pronouns at the right edge of the inflected verb. The use of these pronominal object suffixes is partly pragmatically determined and depends on the referential prominence of the object. If a declarative verb is marked for past tense, the free morpheme \textit{íkke} is the last element of the verbal complex (\figref{fig:treis:1}). Derivational morphemes (passive, causative and middle morphemes, or combinations of these) are found between root and inflection. The inflectional potential of dependent clause verbs is less rich than that of main verb forms: certain person/gender and aspectual distinctions are neutralized, they cannot be marked for mood, and only some of them allow for object suffixes.

Ideophones and interjections are morphologically invariant. Interjections constitute utterances of their own. Ideophones are integrated into the clause through light verbs, \textit{y}- ‘say’ or \textit{ih}- ‘be(come)’ in intransitive clauses and \textit{a’}- \~{} \textit{ass}- ‘do’ in transitive clauses.

\subsection{Earlier research on negation}\label{sec:treis:1.3}

Borelli’s linguistic data appendix (\citeyear[463--482]{Borelli1890}) is the earliest published source on the Kambaata language. Though the section on negative verbs remains empty in his questionnaire, there are five negative verb forms in his list of common phrases (\textit{phrases usuelles}), e.g. the negated imperfective \textit{ossaamba} [ossa’áamba’a] ‘I don’t sleep’, the negated non-imperfective \textit{amé timba} [ameetimbá’a] ‘I did not go, have not gone, am not going’, and the negated imperative \textit{agoti} [ággooti] ‘don’t drink’.\footnote{English translations mine.} \citet{Leslau1952} gives the first coherent presentation of negative paradigms. Some relevant examples in \citet[90--108]{KorhonenEtAl1986} demonstrate slight morphological differences in the non-imperfective negation of Kambaata (proper) and Xambaaro as well as the closely related language Alaaba. As far as more recent publications are concerned, \citet{Treis2012a} is a detailed discussion of the negation of relative verbs, \citet[86--90]{Treis2012c} deals with negative converbs, and \citet{Treis2012b} analyzes clausal negation in Kambaata from a comparative Highland East Cushitic perspective. 

\section{Clausal negation}\label{sec:treis:2}

As in many languages in the world, the affirmative and negative verb forms and paradigms do not neatly match each other in a one-to-one relation. Rather, paradigmatic and constructional asymmetries \citep{Miestamo2013-wals113} can be observed: Not all affirmative paradigms have dedicated negative counterparts, aspectual and modal distinctions may be neutralized under negation, and negation may trigger changes in the morphological makeup of verb forms. Kambaata has five inflectional negation suffixes (\tabref{tab:treis:1}), which will be treated in the following subsections (\sectref{sec:treis:2.1}--\sectref{sec:treis:2.4}). Negative morphemes are always located after the subject indexes and aspectual morphology, and before tense marking; their position relative to mood and object marking depends on the type of verb they combine with. Information on the order of morphemes will be given in the respective sections below.

\subsection{Negation in non-declaratives}\label{sec:treis:2.2}

Main verbs in non-declarative sentences are marked for imperative, jussive/\hspace{0pt}benedictive or apprehensive mood. Imperatives and jussives/benedictives have dedicated negation strategies. The apprehensive, a paradigm of main verb forms expressing warnings and threats \citep{TreisForthcoming}, has no corresponding negative paradigm.

\subsubsection{Negation of imperatives}\label{sec:treis:2.2.1}

\subsection{Negation in non-main clauses}\label{sec:treis:2.4}

In complex sentences only the last verb is usually a fully finite main clause verb (or a copula),\footnote{Exceptions to this rule are sentences with coordinated final main verbs and with non-final main verbs in embedded reported speech. Furthermore, contrastive clauses with \textit{bagáan} ‘but’ (\sectref{sec:treis:4.5}) and the apodosis of hypothetical and counterfactual conditional clauses contain main verb forms.}  while verbs in preceding clauses are either based on converbs, relative verbs, purposive verbs or verbal nouns. Negation strategies in non-main clauses differ significantly from those in main clauses (\sectref{sec:treis:2.1}--\sectref{sec:treis:2.3}): A dedicated negative converb is used for converb clauses (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.1}), a negative participle for relative and purposive clauses (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.2}, \sectref{sec:treis:2.4.3}), and a periphrastic strategy for the negation of verbal nouns (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.4}).

\subsubsection{Negation of converb clauses}\label{sec:treis:2.4.1}

Kambaata makes a distinction between perfective (general) and imperfective (explicitly simultaneous) converbs; both affirmative converb types are obligatorily marked for switch-reference (-\textit{yan} \textsc{ds})\footnote{In \REF{ex:treis:21}, for instance, the switch from a \textsc{1pl} to a \textsc{2sg} subject is marked by the \textsc{ds} marker on the imperfective converb.}  if the subject changes between the converb and the superordinate clause \citep{Treis2012c}. In the converb paradigms, five persons are distinguished (in contrast to main verbs, which distinguish seven) (\tabref{tab:treis:8}). Perfective converbs consist essentially of a verb stem and the first subject index; in addition, the \textsc{1sg/3m} form is marked by the palatalization and gemination of single stem-final consonants or – after stem-final clusters – by a stressed vowel suffix -\textit{í}. Imperfective converbs consist of a verb stem, the indexes of the first subject index slot and an ending -\textit{án}. The parentheses in the top half of \tabref{tab:treis:8} indicate that affirmative converbs can only receive object suffixes in their different subject form.\footnote{An exception is that same subject converbs may be used as final verbs in questions; in this function, they can carry object suffixes.}

\subsubsection{Negation of relative clauses}\label{sec:treis:2.4.2}

Affirmative relative verbs are based on declarative main verbs and differ from them, as far as the segmental realization is concerned, only minimally -- note only that if the inflectional complex ends in -\textit{’u} (see e.g. -\textit{táa-’u} \textsc{3f.ipfv} in \tabref{tab:treis:2}) this element is dropped. There are regular stress differences between perfective and imperfective main vs. relative verbs; in contrast, the stress pattern is identical for perfect and progressive main and relative forms (see \citealt{Treis2012a} for details). Relativization is ubiquitous in Kambaata. Many subordinate clauses -- e.g. temporal \REF{ex:treis:60}, causal, conditional \REF{ex:treis:24}, concessive, similative \REF{ex:treis:9} -- are relative-based. Cleft sentences are a common focusing device; here, the focused information is expressed in a non-verbal predicate, the backgrounded information in a headless relative clause \REF{ex:treis:21}. While affirmative relative verbs – like declarative main verbs – distinguish between four aspectual values (imperfective \REF{ex:treis:3}, perfective \REF{ex:treis:61}, perfect \REF{ex:treis:22}, progressive), these values are neutralized under negation. 
The relative negator is -\textit{umb} (\textsc{neg.rel}) and its position in the verb is illustrated in \figref{fig:treis:3}. Note the absence of an aspect morpheme.

\subsubsection{Negation of purposive clauses}\label{sec:treis:2.4.3}

Kambaata has two purposive paradigms, i.e. dependent verb forms used in purpose and certain complement clauses and marked for switch-reference \citep{Treis2010}. The verb forms in -\textit{óta} are used in same subject contexts, e.g. \textit{hab-óta} (forget-\textsc{[3m.]\hspace{0pt}purp.\hspace{0pt}ss}) ‘so that he (= \textsc{ss}) forgets’; the verb forms in ´-\textit{unta} in different subject contexts, e.g. \textit{bá’-unta} (spoil-\textsc{[3m.]purp.ds}) ‘so that he (= \textsc{ds}) is spoilt’. There is no negative purposive paradigm. Instead negative purpose clauses are based on negative relative clauses (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.2}) plus the similative marker =\textit{g} ‘manner; like’ (see \citealt{Treis2017} on the multifunctionality of =\textit{g}). The distinction between \textsc{ss} and \textsc{ds} purposive is neutralized under negation; see (\ref{ex:treis:25}--\ref{ex:treis:26}), where the same verb form is used in same and different subject contexts.

\subsubsection{Negation of verbal noun clauses}\label{sec:treis:2.4.4}

Verbal nouns consist of a verbal stem plus a case/gender marker -\textit{ú} (\textsc{m.acc}) in the citation form, e.g. \textit{ta'mm-ú} ‘using' \REF{ex:treis:27}, and inflect like any other noun \citep{Treis2012a}. They may function as arguments and as adverbial constituents, and are used, among other things, as final verbs in purpose, manner and complement clauses. Verbal nouns cannot be  negated morphologically but require a periphrastic negation with \textit{hoog}- ‘not do’. The negative verb takes the verbal noun as a direct object \REF{ex:treis:27}.

\subsection{Negative indefinites and quantifiers}\label{sec:treis:3.2}

Kambaata does not have any inherently negative indefinites. If interrogative pronouns are combined with the additive suffix (‘also, even’),\footnote{Dependent on the quality of the final vowel and the stress pattern of the host, the additive suffix is either realized as a lengthening of the final vowel or as -\textit{nii}.} they can serve as free-choice indefinite pronouns (‘\textsc{wh}-ever’, ‘any-\textsc{wh}’) 
in affirmative contexts and be translated as negative indefinite pronouns under negation (‘nobody’, ‘nothing’, ‘nowhere’, ‘never’, etc.) \citep{Treis2015}; see the additive-marked interrogative phrases in (\ref{ex:treis:38}--\ref{ex:treis:39}).\footnote{As predicted by the semantic map in \citet[64]{Haspelmath1997}, who was pointed out to me by an anonymous reviewer, Kambaata's additive interrogative pronouns are also used in indirect negation (e.g. ‘I don't think that anyone (lit. even who) knows the answer’) and in comparatives (e.g. ‘She knows about it more than anyone (lit. than even who)’).}

\gll Fanqashsh-ú-s \textbf{ay-í-i} dag-áno-ba’a.\\

\subsection{Negative derivation}\label{sec:treis:3.3}

Kambaata has a caritive derivational morpheme -\textit{beel}, which generates denominal adjectives with the meaning ‘[N]-less, [not having] N’. It either attaches to the nominal stem or the genitive form and is followed by the case and gender markers -\textit{ú} (\textsc{m})/-\textit{úta} (\textsc{f}) in the citation form (accusative). \citet[277]{Treis2008} has too hastily characterized the caritive derivation as “seldom used”,\footnote{In \citet{Treis2008}, the label “privative" rather than “caritive" is used for the derivation with -\textit{beel}.} whereas \citegen{Alemu2016} dictionary shows that it is in fact fairly productively applicable – at least in a certain genre, namely written texts (including schoolbooks) \REF{ex:treis:41}.\footnote{\citet{Alemu2016} alone contains 128 different caritive adjectives, most of which are used in the monolingual definitions.}

\eabox{\label{ex:treis:41}\small
\begin{tabular}{lllll}
    \textit{bonx-beel-ú(ta)} & ‘leafless, without leaves’ &<& \textit{bonx-á}& ‘leave(s)’ \\
    \textit{muum-beel-ú(ta)} & ‘hairless, without hair’ &<& \textit{muumm-í} &‘hair’ \\
    \textit{seer-beel-ú(ta)} &‘illegal, without rules’ &<& \textit{seer-á} &‘rule, law’ \\
\end{tabular}
}

The use of the caritive derivation in speech remains little attested, and clausal constructions of the type ‘which does not have N’ are clearly preferred. Furthermore, most caritive adjectives attested in my written corpus are best considered semantically transparent adhoc creations and the result of the generalization of a hitherto only weakly productive derivational schema. Examples of caritives that are not adhoc creations but widely shared across the speech community are given in \REF{ex:treis:42}. Note that many of these established caritives are lexically idiosyncratic derivations.

\section{Other aspects of negation}\label{sec:treis:4}
\subsection{The scope of negation}\label{sec:treis:4.1}

The negation of a final main or relative verb generally has scope over a preceding same subject general converb – e.g. \textit{beeqq-am-éen} in \REF{ex:treis:44}.

\ea\label{ex:treis:44}
\gll (…) 	nugguss-áan-ch-u				he’-áni-yan				maal-á	beeqq-am-éen						it-éenno-\textbf{ba’a}.\\
{}		circumcise-\textsc{agt-sgv-m.nom}	live-\textsc{[3m.]ipfv.cvb-ds}	meat-\textsc{m.acc} share.\textsc{mid-pass-3hon.pfv.cvb}	eat-\textsc{3hon.ipfv-\textbf{neg}}\\
\glt ‘(…) if there is a (newly) circumcised (boy in the household), one (does not) share the meat among (the family members) and does not eat it (together).’ (Recorded conversation, EK2016-02-23\_001)
\z 

The scope of negation is often narrowed down to a specific constituent (an NP, a clause etc.) through a cleft construction \REF{ex:treis:45}; see also \REF{ex:treis:63}. The focussed constituent is the non-verbal predicate (marked by a copula), which is negated by \textit{-ba(’a)} (\textsc{neg}); the backgrounded part of the sentence is expressed in a headless, nominative-marked relative clause.

\ea\label{ex:treis:45} 
\gll Lag-áakk-at kaa’ll-itaa’í-i(hu) maranch-áa xall-íiha-ab-\textbf{ba’a}.\\
river-\textsc{plv-f.nom} be.used-\textsc{3f.ipfv.rel-nmlz.m.nom} transport-\textsc{f.dat} only-\textsc{m.dat-cop.id-\textbf{neg}}\\
\glt ‘The rivers are not only used for the transport (of goods).’ (lit. ‘It is not only for the transport that the rivers are used.’) (\cite{KEB1989}{:} 3.54)
\z 

Whereas all the examples presented so far in this chapter show that the inflectional negators are affixed to the predicate, an ellipsis construction from a recorded conversation and elicited data triggered by this natural example prove that the standard negator can also directly attach to (pro)noun phrases in order to restrict the scope of negation \REF{ex:treis:46}.\footnote{As pointed out to me by an anonymous reviewer, languages are often more liberal in the placement of negative markers in ellipsis constructions than in independent clauses, e.g. \textit{I invited John but not Bill} vs. *\textit{I invited not Bill}.} 

\ea\label{ex:treis:46}
\gll  Antab-eechch-úta min-í aag-ís-i, gamball-áb-\textbf{ba’a}, wojj-úta!\\
chicken-\textsc{sgv-f.acc} house-\textsc{m.acc} enter-\textsc{caus}-\textsc{2sg.imp} black-\textsc{f.acc-\textbf{neg}} white-\textsc{f.acc}\\
\glt ‘Bring the hen into the house; not the black one, the white one!’ (Elicited 2019)
\z 

\subsection{Negative polarity}\label{sec:treis:4.2}

The additive-marked indefinite pronouns discussed in \sectref{sec:treis:3.2} can count as negative polarity items, as they cannot be used in affirmative, non-free choice contexts, e.g. in reference to specific individuals, as in \textit{Someone was looking for you}.

\subsection{Marking of NPs in the scope of negation}\label{sec:treis:4.3}

Case marking (\sectref{sec:treis:1.2}) is not affected under negation. Other effects that negation may have on the marking of NPs, e.g. the possible interaction between negation and the use of various pragmatically determined focus and attitude morphemes, have not yet been examined and would make an interesting subject for future research.

\subsection{Reinforcing negation}\label{sec:treis:4.4}

Negation is reinforced by the adverbials \textit{hór-a(-n)} \REF{ex:treis:47} or \textit{hór-a-nii} \REF{ex:treis:48}, which are oblique case forms of the adjective \textit{hor-á} (\textsc{m})/\textit{hor-áta} (\textsc{f}) ‘all’. The bracketed \textit{-n} is a focus morpheme (\textsc{emph}) whose functional range is yet to be investigated; \textit{-nii} is an allomorph of the additive morpheme. The adverbials are used in positive contexts to express ‘all, completely’; under a negative verb, they are translatable as ‘not at all, really not, definitely not, never’. Alternatively, the synonymous \textit{hinát-e-n} (totality-\textsc{m.obl-emph}) ‘at all’ can be used \REF{ex:treis:29}.

\protectedex{\ea\label{ex:treis:47} 
\gll \textbf{Hór-a-n}			dag-g-im-bá’a 			(…). 	\textbf{Hór-a}		\textbf{hór-a}			dag-g-im-bá’a. \\
\textbf{all-\textsc{m.obl-emph}}	know-\textsc{2sg-nipfv-neg} {} \textbf{all-\textsc{m.obl}}	\textbf{all-\textsc{m.obl}}	know-\textsc{2sg-nipfv-neg}\\
\glt (One player to another in a riddling session:) ‘You have no clue (…), so so wrong!’ (lit. ‘You don’t know at all, at all!’) (Recorded conversation, TH2003-06-04\_xinkuta)
\z 
}

\ea\label{ex:treis:48} 
\gll Aat-teenantá=r-u 						\textbf{hór-a-nii} 			kot-ún-ka-’nne.\\

\z

\subsection{Negation, coordination and complex clauses}\label{sec:treis:4.5}

Kambaata does not have any special negative coordinators. Main, complement and adverbial clauses are coordinated with the additive morpheme (\textsc{add}) irrespective of their polarity; see the coordinated benedictive clauses in \REF{ex:treis:52} and the coordinated verbal noun clauses in \REF{ex:treis:53}. In the same way as other modifiers in the noun phrase, relative clauses are coordinated with \textit{-na} (\textsc{coord}), irrespective of their polarity; in \REF{ex:treis:53}, the gods are modified by a negative and an affirmative relative clause in coordination.\footnote{Compare with the coordination of two affirmative relative clauses in \REF{ex:treis:12}.}

\ea\label{ex:treis:52} 
\gll Maadd-u-'né-e horajj-áata haráar-u-\textbf{nii} gáa'nn-u-\textbf{nii}. \\

\glt ‘And may your eating place always be wide (i.e. with many family mem-\hspace{0pt}bers) and fat (i.e. with plenty of food); may it never be narrow and thin.’ (Blessing on the \textit{masaala}-holiday, Volunteered 2003)
\z 

\ea\label{ex:treis:53}

\subsection{Miscellaneous aspects of negation}\label{sec:treis:4.6}
\subsubsection{Negation through inference}\label{sec:treis:4.6.1}

There are (at least) two constructions that regularly invite a negative inference even in the absence of an overt negative morpheme. The first construction contains the equative demonstrative (and by extension: intensifier) \textit{kank-á} ‘that much/\hspace{0pt}many; very much/many’. If used predicatively (lit. ‘it is that/very much’) \REF{ex:treis:55}, the demonstrative often invites a negative interpretation (‘it is not that/very much’) (see also \citealt[195]{Treis2019}).

\ea\label{ex:treis:55} 
\gll Gíir-at		katam-í			mann-íi			aass-itáa			tám-it	\textbf{kánk-a-anta}.\\
fire-\textsc{f.nom}	town-\textsc{m.gen}	people-\textsc{m.dat}	give-\textsc{3f.ipfv.rel}	use-\textsc{f.nom} \textbf{that.much-\textsc{f.pred-f.cop.asc<emph>}}\\
\glt ‘The use that fire has (lit. gives) for townspeople is not that much (lit. is that much).’ (\cite{KEB1989}{:} 4.56)
\z 

The apprehensive verb form \citep{TreisForthcoming} is a fully grammaticalized main verb paradigm that is used in warnings of looming dangers and in threats (\ref{ex:treis:56a}-\ref{ex:treis:56c}). 

\ea
\ea\label{ex:treis:56a}
\gll bu<m>bókkoomm \\
burn<\textsc{1pl>appr}\\
\glt ‘(take care, otherwise) we might burn’
\ex\label{ex:treis:56b}
\gll eeb-bókkoont \\
bring-\textsc{2sg.appr}\\
\glt ‘(take care, otherwise) you might bring/cause (something)’
\ex\label{ex:treis:56c}
\gll ag-ókkoo-kke\\
drink-\textsc{3m.appr-2sg.obj}\\
\glt ‘(take care, otherwise) (it) might drown (lit. drink) you’
\z
\z

Whereas the second person apprehensive can be used as a warning of an imprudent realization of an undesirable event, as in \REF{ex:treis:56b}, it is also very common to employ the second person apprehensive as a negative command and thus as synonymous to the negative imperative (\sectref{sec:treis:2.2.1}). See \REF{ex:treis:57}, where the second person apprehensive and the negative imperative occur in the same context, and \citet[§3.3]{TreisForthcoming} for details.

\ea\label{ex:treis:57}  
\gll Bookk-íta 	it-téen-oochch-iyye; (…) 	resh-á-ssa-n	\textbf{ul-teenókkoonta}.\\
pigs-\textsc{f.acc}	eat-\textsc{2pl-neg.imp-2pl.imp}	{} carcass-\textsc{m.acc-3pl.poss-emph} \textbf{touch-\textsc{2pl.appr}}\\
\glt ‘Don’t eat pork; (…) don’t touch their carcasses.’ (Deuteronomy 14: 8; lit. translation from the Kambaata version) \citep{BibleSociety2023}
\z 

\subsubsection{Metalinguistic negation}\label{sec:treis:4.6.2}

Kambaata does not have a dedicated strategy for metalinguistic negation. Instead, as \REF{ex:treis:58} proves, the contrastive construction (cf. \ref{ex:treis:54}) is employed. The disputable word choice is negated in clause 2. 

\ea\label{ex:treis:58} 
\gll Wóm-m-at gashsh-itáa \textbf{bagáan} (.) mát-ua=rr-íi ann-á ik-káa-ba'a.	\\
	king-\textsc{plv-f.nom} reign-\textsc{3f.ipfv} \textbf{\textsc{cntr}} {} one-\textsc{m.obl}=\textsc{nmlz-m.gen} owner-\textsc{m.acc} be(come)-\textsc{3f.ipfv-neg}\\
\glt (The businessman interrupts the little prince mid-sentence and corrects his word choice:) ‘They rather “reign over” (their kingdom); kings do not “own” anything.’ \citep[47]{Saint-Exupéry2018}
\z 
A different conversational pattern equivalent to metalinguistic negation is illustrated in \REF{ex:treis:59}. A does not negate B's word choice, as in \REF{ex:treis:58}, but first quotes it in a question. The preferred wording is then expressed in an isolated \textit{bagáan}-clause.

\protectedex{\ea\label{ex:treis:59} 
\gll Árr-u 			fúll-ee’u					y-áano-ndo? 	Hór-a		buss-áyyoo			\textbf{bagáan}.\\
sun-\textsc{m.nom}	come.out-\textsc{3m.prf}	say-\textsc{3m.ipfv-q} all-\textsc{m.obl}	burn-\textsc{3m.prog}	\textbf{\textsc{cntr}}\\
\glt (A says: ‘The sun is shining (lit. has risen).’ – B corrects A's word choice.) ‘The sun is not shining but really burning.’ (lit. ‘Does one say “The sun has come out”? Rather “(It) really burns”.’)  (Elicited 2019)
\z }


\subsubsection{Non-negative use of negative constructions}\label{sec:treis:4.6.3}

This section discusses two non-negative uses of negative constructions. Firstly, the fossilized \textsc{3m} perfective converb form, \textit{hóogg} lit. ‘(he) not being’, of the lexically negative verb \textit{hoog-} ‘not do' (\sectref{sec:treis:2.5}) serves as a disjunctor between phrases and clauses (‘or’), especially in locally produced written material \REF{ex:treis:60}, where it is possibly triggered by the need to find a handy translation for Amharic \textit{wäy} ‘or’ in Kambaata.

Kambaata has a construction that bilingual speakers regularly translate as a negative question in English. It is marked by question intonation and the morpheme -\textit{bay} \REF{ex:treis:61}. However, questions marked by -\textit{bay} are not intended to solicit an answer. They are possibly best characterized as rhetorical questions and are, as such, also found in the middle of monologues. They can express (i) a strong expectation on the side of the speaker that the addressee will fully agree, (ii) the speaker’s surprise that the addressee has expressed the opposite, forgotten about something or not realized something obvious \REF{ex:treis:61}, or (iii) the speaker’s comment about a surprising encounter or discovery \REF{ex:treis:62}. So even though the construction comes in the form of a negative question, it is a strongly non-negative assertive statement.

The morpheme -\textit{bay} is possibly segmentable into the standard negator -\textit{ba} (\sectref{sec:treis:2.1}) plus an undetermined -\textit{y}. However, even though speakers tend to translate it as a negative interrogative in English or provide it as a rough equivalent of the -\textit{ba-ndo}-form in (\ref{ex:treis:8}--\ref{ex:treis:9}), the categorization of -\textit{bay} as a negator is not entirely certain. On the one hand, it is attested after the non-imperfective morpheme -\textit{im´} \REF{ex:treis:61}, which is an aspect category exclusive to negative polarity (recall \tabref{tab:treis:3}), and thus this combination is an argument in favor of interpreting -\textit{bay} as a negator. On the other hand, -\textit{bay} is also attached to progressive verbs \REF{ex:treis:62}, where the standard negator never occurs. Furthermore, -\textit{bay} can combine with an already negated verb, the result being a double negative question (lit. ‘Wasn’t it not by chance?’), which serves as a strongly assertive negative statement \REF{ex:treis:63}, often after a surprising discovery (e.g. ‘Oh, it was not by chance!’). If the standard negator (\textsc{neg}) in \REF{ex:treis:63} were dropped, the sentence would again be an assertive affirmative statement (lit. ‘Wasn’t it by chance?’ for ‘Oh, it was by chance!’).

\subsubsection{Diachronic notes and observations}\label{sec:treis:4.6.4}

The diachrony of the Kambaata negative morphology is still unexplored. A study of verbal negation in Highland East Cushitic \citep{Treis2012b} has shown that the languages apply fairly divergent means of standard negation. There are four non-cognate standard negators: (i) Hadiyya -\textit{yyo}, Libido -\textit{ssho}, (ii) Kambaata and Alaaba -\textit{ba’(a)}, K’abeena -\textit{ba}, Gedeo -\textit{baa} and -\textit{bo’}, (iii) Sidaama \textit{di}=, and (iv) Burji -\textit{ey’i}. Given that the Highland East Cushitic languages are closely related, some standard negators must be fairly recent innovations. Negators with initial \textit{b} are found in the standard negation of Kambaata, Alaaba, K’abeena and Gedeo as well as in Hadiyya and Libido, which have a dedicated negative existential verb \textit{bee’e} ‘not exist’. \textit{Bee’e} is probably cognate with Kambaata  -\textit{ba’(a)} (\textsc{neg}). \citet[22]{Sasse1982} expresses the tentative hypothesis that the Highland East Cushitic negators with initial \textit{b} are “borrowed from Omotic” – but without providing any supporting evidence.

Non-morphological means, i.e. periphrases with a lexically negative verb, are needed to negate verbal nouns. As shown in \sectref{sec:treis:2.1} and \sectref{sec:treis:2.3}, only negated imperfective main verbs and non-verbal predicates are fully symmetrical to their affirmative counterparts. Everywhere else the following paradigmatic asymmetries are observed: 

\begin{itemize}
  \item Reduction or neutralization of aspectual distinctions – see non-\hspace{0pt}imperfective main verbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.1}), converbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.1}) and relative verbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.2}).
  \item Reduction of the set of subject indexes – see non-imperfective main verbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.1}) and relative verbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.2}).
  \item Neutralization of a modal distinction – see jussives/\hspace{0pt}benedictives (\sectref{sec:treis:2.2.2}).
  \item Neutralization of the same subject/\hspace{0pt}different subject distinction – see converbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.1}) and purposive verbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.3}).
\end{itemize}

The following constructional asymmetries are observed:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Different means of subject indexing:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Type 1: Bipartite subject indexing is reduced to simple subject indexing – see non-imperfective main verbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.1}) and relative verbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.2}).
        \item Type 2: Covert subject indexing in the affirmation vs. overt subject indexing in the negation – see imperatives (\sectref{sec:treis:2.2.1}).
    \end{itemize}
    \item Optional object marking in the negation, but incompatibility of object marking in the affirmation – see converbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.1}).
    \item Fully verbal morphology in the affirmation, but verb-adjective hybrids in the negation – see relative verbs (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.2}).
    \item Neutralization of the distinction between relative-based and purposive-based purpose verb forms (\sectref{sec:treis:2.4.3}).
\end{itemize}

Apart from inflectional means of negation, Kambaata has a productive caritive derivation -\textit{beel} ‘-less’ that generates adjectives on the basis of nouns (\sectref{sec:treis:3.3}). Kambaata has no negative indefinite pronouns and instead uses additive-marked interrogatives and quantifiers to express ‘nobody, nothing, nowhere etc.’ under negation (\sectref{sec:treis:3.2}).
