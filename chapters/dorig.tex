\title{Negation in Dorig}

\abstract{Dorig, an Oceanic language spoken on Gaua island in northern Vanuatu, shows a~wealth of constructions for encoding negative polarity. Verbs contrast 14~positive TAM categories with 9~negative; together, these form a “\textsc{tamp}” [tense--aspect--mood--polarity] system made of 23~portmanteau categories. All negative \textsc{tamp} morphemes are formally discontinuous, and synchronically non-compositional. Standard negation shows several forms of structural asymmetries, both constructional and paradigmatic, across polarities. Besides standard negation, Dorig has separate constructions for negating non-verbal predicates, existentials, locatives, and imperatives. While this study highlights the intricacy of negative structures in~this~particular language, it~places them in their typological and areal contexts. Thanks to comparative tables showing all negative morphemes in the 17 languages of the Torres
and Banks islands, it becomes clear that Dorig is mostly representative of regional patterns in north Vanuatu.

\keywords{negation, asymmetry of TAM systems, non-verbal predicates, Oceanic languages, Vanuatu}

\section{The language}\label{sec:dor:1}

The present chapter examines the grammar of negation in Dorig, an Oceanic language of Vanuatu. Like other chapters in this volume, this study follows closely the structure of the typological questionnaire designed by the editors (\citetv{chapters/questionnaire}) – including in the order and numbering of sections.

\subsection{Context and sources}\label{sec:dor:1.1} 

Dorig (ISO: \href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/wwo}{wwo}, Glottocode: \href{https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/weta1242}{weta1242}) is one of five languages spoken on the island of Gaua, in the Banks islands of northern Vanuatu (see \figref{fig:dor:map}).
Like all the 138 indigenous languages of the Vanuatu archipelago \citep{FrançoisEtAl2015exceptional}, Dorig belongs to the Oceanic subgroup of the Austronesian phylum. More specifically, Dorig forms part of a dialect chain that runs around the island of Gaua – itself a portion of the broader Torres–Banks linkage \citep[182]{François2014}.

The language’s 300 speakers live mostly in the village of \textit{Dōrig} [ⁿdʊriɣ] on Gaua’s south coast; they entertain social and linguistic ties with their immediate neighbours on the island. The two languages genealogically closest to Dorig, as measured using Historical Glottometry, are Nume and Koro (\citealt[56]{François2016a}; \citealt[79]{KalyanFrançois2018}).

The grammar of negation shows considerable cross-linguistic variation across the vast Oceanic family (see \citealt{HovdhaugenMosel1999}), to say nothing of the broader Austronesian phylum (see \citealt{VossenAuwera2014}) – so the present study should not be taken as representative of such large ensembles. That said, Dorig can be seen as quite typical of the grammatical structures found in its local environment of Vanuatu (especially the Torres \& Banks languages) as it shares most of their semantic categories and formal tendencies; and yet, Dorig presents several structural features that make it an original system.

Apart from a wordlist under the obsolete glossonym Wetamut \citep{Tryon1976}, nothing was known of Dorig until I conducted fieldwork on it, as part of my 2003 survey of Banks languages. I was only able to stay in the Dorig area for nine days altogether (4–12 August 2003), with no opportunity of returning there since, due to uneasy access; a second trip scheduled in 2011 was finally cancelled due to the lack of reliable transportation.

My 2003 stay allowed me to record substantial data, thanks to my in-depth knowledge of neighbouring languages, and to the “conversational questionnaire” I had designed for that purpose \citep{François2019}. This data collection method was supplemented by language immersion, as I began to speak and understand the language in its daily context, taking field notes and recording the spontaneous speech of native speakers. Out of 151′ of various recordings, I transcribed 67′ of narratives: this yielded a corpus of 13 texts totalling 14,300 words, partially published as \citet{François2008}, and archived online.\footnote{My audio recordings are freely accessible at \href{https://pangloss.cnrs.fr/corpus/Dorig?lang=en&seeMore=true&mode=pro}{https://pangloss.cnrs.fr/corpus/Dorig}.} The examples cited in this study come either from my field notes or from that text corpus.\footnote{Labels of the type [\textsc{af.bp3}.30b] -- as in sentences \REF{ex:dor:koro}, \REF{ex:dor:give}, \REF{ex:dor:women} -- refer to my field notes; these are archived at \url{https://www.odsas.net/}. Labels of the type [Drg.d04.Kava:41] -- as in~\REF{ex:dor:potential}, (\ref{ex:dor:seeher}--\ref{ex:dor:phone}), \REF{ex:dor:worry}, \REF{ex:dor:stillwet} -- are taken from my field questionnaire, which can be accessed at \url{https://tiny.cc/AF_Q_Dorig}.} Whenever possible, I~will provide permanent (\textsc{doi}) links to sentences in their original context.\footnote{If an example is followed by an anchor icon \texttt{⚓} and a string of characters, adding that string to the prefix \url{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-000} yields a valid \textsc{doi} identifier. For example, {\{\texttt{⚓}3195\#S5\}} yields the \textsc{url} \url{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003195\#S5}.} 

This is the first ever publication dedicated entirely to the Dorig language. That said, I have presented various aspects of it in my comparative studies of the Torres–Banks area: this includes information on Dorig’s phonology, with a \mbox{focus} on its CCVC syllabic template (\citealt[407–408]{François2010}); on its vowel system (\citealt[461, 491]{François2005a}); on its noun articles \citep{François2007}; its possessive morphology (\citealt[486]{François2005a}); its space system \citep{François2015}; and its personal pronouns \citep{François2016a}. As for the data I will provide on other languages of the Banks and Torres Islands [\sectref{sec:dor:5}, Appendix], their main source will be my own field notes and publications; the reader is also referred to the description of Vera’a by \citet{Schnell2011}, and the grammar of Vurës by \citet{Malau2016}.

Dorig has a CCVC syllabic template, with optional consonants (\citealt[407]{François2010}): e.g. \textit{āv} [aːv] ‘fire’, \textit{loq} [lɔk​͡pʷ] ‘wet’, \textit{wrēt} [wrɪt] ‘squid’, \textit{rqa} [rk​͡pʷa] ‘woman’, \textit{tger} [tɣɛr] ‘disappear’, \textit{\={m}kār} [ŋ​͡mʷkaːr] ‘flying fish’. 

Several prefixes have a form \textsc{c(v)}- with an elidable vowel: e.g. \textit{m(e)-} ‘Perfect’, \textit{s(o)-} ‘Irrealis’, \textit{v(a)-} ‘Stative’, \textit{v(e)-} ‘Attributive’. The prefix vowel normally elides when the first syllable of the phonological word can accommodate a \textsc{c-} prefix into the maximal \textsc{ccvc} template: \textbf{\textit{m}}(\textit{e})-~+~\textbf{\textit{t}}\textit{ur} ‘stand’ \textrm{${\rightarrow}$}~\textit{m-tur} [\textbf{mt}ur] ‘stood’. By~contrast, when a verb already starts in a consonant cluster (e.g. \textbf{\textit{tv}}\textit{ig} ‘bury’), the prefix will surface as \textsc{cv-}, revealing its underlying vowel (e.g. \textit{m}\textbf{\textit{e}}\textit{-tvig} ‘buried’,\textit{ s}\textbf{\textit{o}}\textit{-tvig} ‘will bury’). 

Morphemes of the form \textsc{c(v)}- with an elidable vowel qualify as prefixes, because their surface shape is determined by syllabification rules that apply at the higher level of the phonological word. In addition, Dorig also has \textsc{cv} morphemes whose surface form is independent of the next morpheme: I will analyse them as proclitics, or simply particles. For~example, while the Irrealis \textit{s(o)-} is a prefix, the homophonous Sequential aspect, with its fixed shape \textit{so} [sɔ], is better analysed as a particle rather than an affix. Compare \textit{s(o)-} ‘Irrealis’ in \REF{ex:dor:bewitch} with \textit{so} ‘Sequential’ in \REF{ex:dor:old}: these are two distinct morphemes, in shape and in meaning.

         \textsc{1sg} \textsc{irr}-bewitch all \textsc{3pl} \textsc{3pl} \textbf{\textsc{irr}}-die \\

\z

Dorig is an SVO language with fixed word order. Simple verbal clauses follow the general template in \figref{fig:dor:order}, where the pointy brackets indicate the limits of the verb phrase:

\label{fig:dor:order}

The predicate head is always the first (most leftward) lexical element after the subject. As for the (emically defined) class “postverb”, it includes words whose function is to modify the verb head inside the verb phrase.\footnote{This lexical class of VP-internal “postverb” (sometimes called “VP-internal adverb” or “\mbox{adjunct}”) is found in all northern Vanuatu languages (cf. \citealt[137--142]{François2004}; \citealt[316]{François2017}; \citealt[91]{Schnell2011}; \citealt[122--124]{Malau2016}; \citealt{Rangelov2022}; \citealt{FrançoisKrausseFC}).} Postverbs are optio\-nal, and immediately follow the predicate head. They may correspond to English manner adverbs – cf. \REF{ex:dor:tavul} \textit{tavul} ‘well’ – or floating quantifiers – \REF{ex:dor:bewitch} \textit{bas} ‘all’ – among others. The postverbal slot can also be occupied by a second verb, in a serial verb construction – as in \REF{ex:dor:follow} or \REF{ex:dor:worry} below. 

Dorig collapses into a single paradigm the categories of Tense, Aspect, Mood, and Polarity. It is thus best described as a “\textsc{tamp}” system\footnote{\citet[461]{Malau2016} also describes the neighbouring language Vurës as having a “\textsc{tamp}” system, for \textit{Tense-Aspect-Mood-Polarity}.} – hence the slots label\-led \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{1} and \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2} in \figref{fig:dor:order}. A given predicate inflects for only one \textsc{tamp} category at a time: e.g. a verb takes either the (positive, realis) Perfect \textit{m(e)-} or the (positive) Irrealis \textit{s(o)-}, but it cannot combine them. All \textsc{tamp} morphemes will be listed in \tabref{tab:dor:tamp} [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.1}]. Note that \textsc{tamp} is always overt, and never encoded by zero; thus a sentence like \REF{ex:dor:natekni} is ungrammatical – by contrast with overtly-marked predicates like (\ref{ex:dor:home1}--\ref{ex:dor:home2}):

\ea\label{ex:dor:natekni} 
    \gll  *Na tek ni.\\ 
           \textsc{\textsc{1sg}} see \textsc{\textsc{3sg}}\\
    \glt 
\z

\noindent When the verb is transitive, its object usually follows \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2} as in \REF{ex:dor:lala} or \REF{ex:dor:cone}; but it can also exceptionally precede it, as in \REF{ex:dor:river}. 

As~we’ll soon see, standard negation in Dorig always takes the form of bipartite morphemes, whose elements occupy the same slots as \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{1} and \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2} in \figref{fig:dor:order}.
Throughout this study, I will make the choice to gloss \textsc{tamp} morphemes, whether positive or negative, as bipartite – e.g. ‘\textsc{pot}\textsubscript{1}-… \textsc{pot\textsubscript{2}}’ for~the Potential in~\REF{ex:dor:lala} – even when one of their components can also occur on its own. This analytic decision is meant to avoid the trap of searching for compositionality when we’re in~fact dealing with entrenched, grammaticalised units of phraseology (\citealt[31]{François2003}). This view can be taken as a \textit{constructional} approach to morphosyntax – in the sense of the \textit{Construction grammar} (\citealt{FillmoreEtAl1988}; \citealt{Croft2001}; \citealt{BarðdalEtAl2015}); it will also guide my analysis of negation morphology.

\section{Clausal negation}\label{sec:dor:2}
\subsection{Standard negation}\label{sec:dor:2.1}
\label{bkm:Ref36662877}\label{bkm:Ref130726661}
\citeauthor{Miestamo2005} (\citeyear[39]{Miestamo2005}, \citeyear[553]{Miestamo2007}) defines \textit{standard negation} as “the basic means that languages have for negating declarative verbal main clauses”. Dorig has not one, but several morphemes that meet this definition, depending on the Tense-Aspect-Mood value of the verb.


\subsubsection{Negation in declarative verbal main clauses: overview}\label{sec:dor:2.1.1}
\label{bkm:Ref36399531}\label{bkm:Ref66638886}
An important characteristic of Dorig is that polarity (positive vs. negative) is really in-built inside the \textsc{tamp} markers. For example, the Potential \textit{s(o)-… lala} we saw in \REF{ex:dor:lala}, or the Imperfective \textit{t(o)-… ti} in \REF{ex:dor:cone}, are incompatible with negation; they really stand for “positive potential” and “positive imperfective” respectively. Their negative counterpart is a different morpheme altogether, which is not compositional. And crucially, the relation between positive and negative \textsc{tamp} categories is not a straightforward one: as~we’ll see in \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.2}, the Dorig language shows a rich array of asymmetries between polarities.

\tabref{tab:dor:tamp} shows the complete \textsc{tamp} system of Dorig, and gives a preliminary idea of how declarative verbal main clauses deal with polarity. The ellipsis “…” represents here the \textit{verbal group}, i.e. the verbal head with its postverb(s), as per the formula in \figref{fig:dor:order}. Whatever precedes “…” in \tabref{tab:dor:tamp} corresponds to \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{1} (whether a prefix or a particle); whatever follows it fits in the \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2} slot. Some morphemes occur only in \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{1}, others only in \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2}; discontinuous morphemes have morphological material on both sides of the verbal group.\footnote{Vowels in round brackets correspond to the morpheme’s underlying vowel, subject to regular elision [\sectref{sec:dor:1.2}]. The consonant \textit{(v)} in \textit{(v)te} is simply optional [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.6}]. As for the square brackets seen in the Imperative, they indicate that the form is zero in the singular, but takes the form \textit{ar} with a non-singular agent [\sectref{sec:dor:2.2.2}]. Finally, the abbreviation \textsc{red} in the Prohibitive indicates that the verb head must show its reduplicated form [\sectref{sec:dor:2.2.2}].}

The table’s left-hand side lists the 14 affirmative \textsc{tamp} markers: e.g. the Imper\-fec\-tive \textit{t(o)-… ti} shown in (\ref{ex:dor:cone}--\ref{ex:dor:river}) above. Evidently -- as will be discussed in \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.4} -- there is no one-to-one correspondence across polarities, neither in terms of morphology nor semantics.

As \tabref{tab:dor:tamp} shows, standard negation in Dorig always takes the form of a discontinuous morpheme, of the type \{\textsc{neg}\textsc{\textsubscript{1} }… \textsc{neg\textsubscript{2}}\}. This type of negative morpheme, known in the literature as “double negation” \citep[88]{Dahl1979}, is present in about 10~percent of the world’s languages \citep{Dryer2013-wals112} – cf. \textit{ne… pas} in Standard French. So-called double negation is widespread in Vanuatu [see \sectref{sec:dor:5.1}]: for instance, about nearby Vera’a [\figref{fig:dor:map}], \citet[31]{Schnell2011} notes: “All negative \textsc{tam} markers are circummorphemes”. The label “double negation” is misleading, as it seems to suggest a construction where polarity would be somehow encoded twice (cf. English \textit{I~cannot not call him}); yet this is not what happens with the morphemes we are discussing here. I~prefer to simply describe them as discontinuous markers, in~a way parallel to some of the positive \textsc{tamp} morphemes [\sectref{sec:dor:1.2}].

The two elements of each negative morpheme occupy the same slots as the \textsc{tamp} markers \{\textsc{tamp}\textsc{\textsubscript{1} }… \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2}\} in \figref{fig:dor:order} [\sectref{sec:dor:1.2}]. The object phrase, whether it is an NP or a pronoun, is usually located outside the boundaries of negation, after \textsc{neg\textsubscript{2}}, just like we saw in \REF{ex:dor:lala} for the positive potential. Sentences \REF{ex:dor:fire1} and \REF{ex:dor:follow1}, taken from my corpus, illustrate two of the negative morphemes cited in \tabref{tab:dor:tamp}. 

\REF{ex:dor:fire1} shows the Nondumitive \textit{sowse}\ldots\ \textit{te} ‘not yet’ [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.7}], with a nominal object. The positive counterpart of \REF{ex:dor:fire1} would be the iamitive \REF{ex:dor:fire2} [see \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.7}]:

\ea\label{ex:dor:fire}
    \ea\label{ex:dor:fire1} {\small [({negative}) \textsc{nondumitive}]} \\
        \gll  Tōlkma \textbf{sowse} wdō\={n} \textbf{te} o āv.\\ 
              \textsc{1excl.tri} \textsc{ndum\textsubscript{1}} set.up \textsc{ndum\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{art} fire\\
        \glt ‘We haven’t set up the fire yet.’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0007437#S31}{\{\texttt{⚓}7437\#S31\}}
    \ex\label{ex:dor:fire2} {\small [(positive) \textsc{iamitive}]} \\
        \gll  Tōlkma \textbf{me-}wdō\={n} \textbf{nok} o āv.\\
              \textsc{1excl.tri} \textsc{iam\textsubscript{1}}\textsc{-}set.up \textsc{iam\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{art} fire\\
        \glt ‘We have set up the fire already.’
    \z 
\z 

The Negative potential \textit{(v)te}\ldots\ \textit{late} in \REF{ex:dor:follow1}, just like its positive equivalent \REF{ex:dor:follow2} \textit{s(o)-}… \textit{lala}, is~carried by a complex predicate (serial verb), and followed by a pronominal object: 

\ea\label{ex:dor:follow}

              \textsc{1sg} \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{1}} return follow \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{2du}\\
        \glt {\small [\textsc{negative} \textsc{potential}]} \quad ‘I won’t be able to follow you.’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003162#S31}{\{\texttt{⚓}3162\#S31\}}

              \textsc{1sg} \textsc{pot\textsubscript{1}}-return follow \textsc{pot\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{2du}\\

    \z

\subsubsection{Typological classification}\label{sec:dor:2.1.2}

\citet{Dahl1979} proposed a first typological classification of negative strategies in the world. With respect to the morphological types of exponents, Dorig negative morphemes pertain to the type he calls “circumfixal” \citep[100]{Dahl1979}.

As far as word order is concerned, \citet{Dryer2013-wals144} classified languages in terms of the negator’s position with respect to the clause’s subject, object and verb. Dorig would belong to his subsection 144F “\textit{Obligatory double negation in SVO languages}”. Within that group, it falls under type \#2 \textit{SNegVNegO} when \textsc{neg\textsubscript{1}} is a particle, or under the similar type \#7 \textit{S[Neg-V]NegO} when \textsc{neg\textsubscript{1}} is a prefix.

Throughout his publications, \citet{Miestamo2005,Miestamo2007,Miestamo2013-wals114,Miestamo2013-wals113} has studied the many forms of symmetry and asymmetry found in the marking of negation in languages. Symmetrical negation is one where “affirmative and negative structures are identical except for the presence of the negative marker(s)” (\citealt[72]{Miestamo2005}); all formal contrasts that correlate with~negation – whether morphological, syntaxic or semantic – are then considered types of “asymmetry”. 

On the basis of the first facts we have seen, we can already make some observations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \tabref{tab:dor:tamp} above makes it clear that standard negation is never symmetrical: the form taken by the portmanteau \textsc{tamp} morpheme changes altogether across polarities, so there is no case where positive and negative differ only by the presence of a negator. In~this respect, Dorig belongs to the subtype \citeauthor{Miestamo2005} (\citeyear[170]{Miestamo2005}, \citeyear{Miestamo2013-wals114}) calls “Asymmetric standard negation only” [type Asy]; this is his least common category, found only in 17~percent of his typological sample.
    \item In negative clauses, the verb is always finite, just like in positive ones. Dorig does not show asymmetry in the finiteness status of the verb (subtype A/Fin in \citealt[73]{Miestamo2005}, \citealt{Miestamo2013-wals114}).
    \item Word order is identical across polarities: Dorig is syntactically symmetrical (cf. \citealt[153]{Miestamo2005}).
    \item The marking of Tense-Aspect-Mood categories differs across polarities: this pertains to \citegen[112]{Miestamo2005} A/Cat type – i.e. asymmetry in the marking of grammatical categories. Note that this is even true when there is a perfect semantic correspondance, in terms of paradigm, between the positive and the negative. For example, the nondumitive in \REF{ex:dor:fire1} is semantically the exact counterpart of the positive iamitive \REF{ex:dor:fire2}, and yet their morphological exponents have nothing in common.\footnote{This configuration is parallel to the one \citet[117]{Miestamo2005} reports for Central Siberian Yupik, or for the Songhay language Koyraboro Senni.} This is a case of \textit{constructional asymmetry} (\citeyear[52]{Miestamo2005}) in the expression of tense-aspect-mood (A/Cat/TAM, \citeyear[116]{Miestamo2005}).
    \item Another major type of asymmetry found in Dorig is \textit{paradigmatic asymmetry}. The layout of \tabref{tab:dor:tamp} shows the pervasive mismatch between positive and negative \textsc{tam}, and the lack of one-to-one correspondance across polarities. Some \textsc{tam} categories that exist in the positive don’t have any equivalent in the negative [Sequential \textit{so}, cf.~\REF{ex:dor:old}]; and some semantic contrasts made under one polarity are lost or neutralised in the other [see \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.4}].
    \item In Miestamo’s fine-grained quantitative typology, Dorig would belong to his category “A~in both C~and~P” (\citeyear[172]{Miestamo2005}), i.e., Asymmetry both in constructions and in paradigms.
    \item Finally, section \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.5} will discuss yet another type of asymmetry relevant for Dorig: the one related to the “reality status” of the clause (A/NonReal). As we'll see, one possible analysis is to describe it as a a case of “paradigmatic displacement” (A/NonReal/Displc, \citeyear[98]{Miestamo2005}).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Declarative statements in the realis domain}\label{sec:dor:2.1.3}

Let us now examine in more detail the way standard negation works in Dorig. For the sake of expository convenience, I will examine separately two distinct domains in modality or “reality status”, respectively the \textsc{realis} and the \textsc{irrealis}. These are defined semantically, after \citet{Elliott2000}. The realis domain targets states of affairs whose temporal anchoring precedes or includes the moment of utterance, encompassing the domains ‘past’ and ‘present’. By~contrast, the \mbox{irrealis} domain corresponds to “unrealized state[s] of affairs” \citep{Cristofaro2012}, events which are only virtual at the moment of utterance. While many Oceanic languages contrast realis vs. irrealis explicitly using modal morphology, Dorig and its neighbours leave the opposition unmarked; or rather, they incorporate that modal contrast in a paradigm that also encodes oppositions of tense and aspect. In other words, Dorig really uses a \textit{set} of realis \textsc{tamp} markers on the one hand, and a \textit{set} of irrealis ones on the other hand – as suggested in the first column of \tabref{tab:dor:tamp}.\footnote{\label{fnt:ftn9} The identical form between (realis) Stative \textit{v(a)-} and (irrealis) Counterfactual \textit{v(a)-} is best analysed, synchronically, as a mere matter of homophony, as there is no semantic link between the two \textsc{tamp} categories other than a common source of grammaticalization (<*\textit{va} ‘thing’). The~only \textsc{tamp} morpheme that really straddles the semantic boundary between the two modal domains [cf. \tabref{tab:dor:tamp}] is the form \textit{qra}, glossed here ‘Dilatory’. This aspectual category, sometimes labelled ‘Time focus’ (\citealt[199--216]{François2003}; \citeyear[221--223]{François2021}), is found across north Vanuatu languages. Everywhere it is compatible with realis and irrealis readings: in a realis context, the Dilatory aspect takes on an inaugural meaning (‘do X for the first time’); in an irrealis one, it forms a dilatory future, i.e. a~future tense with a pragmatic orientation towards later ‘will do X later’ – as in \REF{ex:dor:qra}. The common denominator of these two uses is a semantic mechanism that can be glossed ‘\textit{only at time T, and not earlier}’ (whence the labels “Time focus” or “Dilatory”). The realis use of \textit{qra} would be negated using the Negative realis, while its irrealis reading would correspond to the Negative future: this can be taken as evidence that the contrast of reality status (realis vs. irrealis) is in fact operational in Dorig, in spite of the ambivalence of the positive morpheme \textit{qra}.} 

In~Dorig, the negation of declarative verbal main clauses takes different forms depending on the aspect and modality of the clause; as a result, no individual morpheme can be identified as \textit{the} marker of negation. \tabref{tab:dor:tamp} however suggests some regularities, or at~least some trends. Essentially, negative morphemes in declarative realis statements (top half of \tabref{tab:dor:tamp}) tend to involve a postverbal particle (\textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2}) \textit{tēmē}, which I will provisionally gloss ‘\textsc{neg.ind}’ for ‘Negative indicative’. By~contrast, irrealis statements (negative versions of the potential, conditional, imperative…) often involve a postverbal particle \textit{te}.

As a first approximation, one could thus propose that negation in Dorig \mbox{involves} a general opposition between \textit{tēmē} (${\approx}$realis) and \textit{te} (${\approx}$irrealis).\footnote{The etymology of these two negative elements will be discussed in \sectref{sec:dor:5.2}.} However, there are exceptions to this binary contrast: \textit{tēmē} is sometimes found in irrealis (future) contexts, and \textit{te} in some realis (nondumitive) statements. In~order to describe the behaviour of negation in the system, it is better to delve, step by step, into the semantics of each of the negative morphemes listed in \tabref{tab:dor:tamp}. 

\subsubsection{Paradigmatic asymmetries}\label{sec:dor:2.1.4}

The present section will first describe the paradigmatic asymmetries that characterise \textsc{tamp} categories in the realis domain. Section \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.5} will then discuss the paradox that the negation used with semantically realis statements seems to be borrowed from the irrealis domain. The issue of phasal negatives will be examined in a separate section \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.7}.

In the affirmative, the Stative particle \textit{v(a)-} serves to assign a stative property (whether an adjective or a stative verb) to the subject:

\ea\label{ex:dor:tavul}
        \gll  Na \textbf{va-} vrēgēl tavul na vara-n.\\ 
              \textsc{1sg} \textsc{stat-} know well \textsc{art} country-\textsc{3sg}\\
        \glt ‘I know her country well.’
\z
This is a purely aspectual marker, underspecified with respect to tense. While its default interpretation is the present, it can equally refer to a past situation: thus \textit{na~va-vrēgēl} in \REF{ex:dor:tavul} can translate ‘I~know (now)’ or ‘I~knew (then)’.
     
In order to negate a sentence like \REF{ex:dor:tavul}, one cannot just combine the Stative \textit{v(a)-} with the negation \textit{tēmē}: such a sentence \REF{ex:dor:statneg} is rejected as ungrammatical.
     
    \ea\label{ex:dor:statneg}
        \gll   *{Na} \textbf{{va-}vrēgēl} {tavul} \textbf{{tēmē}} {na} vara-n.\\ 
              \textsc{1sg} \textsc{stat-}know well \textbf{\textsc{neg.ind}} \textsc{art} country-\textsc{3sg}\\
        \glt [\textit{intended}: ‘I don’t know her country well.’] 
    \z
Instead, the only way to negate the Stative \textit{va-} is to use the Negative realis \textit{s(o)-… tēmē} (glossed \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{1-}\ldots\ \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{2}); as in \REF{ex:dor:statneg2}:

    \ea\label{ex:dor:statneg2}
        \gll  Na \textbf{so}-vrēgēl tavul \textbf{tēmē} na vara-n.\\
              \textsc{1sg} \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{1}}\textsc{-}know well \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{art} country-\textsc{3sg}\\
        \glt ‘I \textbf{don’t} know her country well.’ 
    \z


\noindent The principle illustrated in (\ref{ex:dor:tavul}--\ref{ex:dor:statneg2}) with Stative \textit{v(a)-} also applies to other \mbox{realis} \textsc{tam} categories. Thus, a Perfect \REF{ex:dor:home1} \textit{m(e)-} becomes \REF{ex:dor:home2} \textit{s(o)-… tēmē} when negated:

\ea\label{ex:dor:home}
    \ea\label{ex:dor:home1}
        \gll  Na \textbf{m}-tek ni a gvur.\\ 
              \textsc{1sg} \textbf{\textsc{prf}}\textsc{-}see \textsc{3sg} \textsc{loc} house\\
        \glt ‘I saw him at home.’
    \ex\label{ex:dor:home2}
        \gll  Na \textbf{s}-tek \textbf{tēmē} ni a gvur.\\
              \textsc{1sg} \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{1}}-see \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{3sg} \textsc{loc} house\\
        \glt ‘I \textbf{didn’t} see him at home.’
    \z
\z 

A clause in the Imperfective will also replace its discontinuous marker \textit{t(o)-\ldots~ti} with the combination \textit{s(o)-\ldots\ tēmē}, as in~\REF{ex:dor:koro}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:koro} 
    \gll  Na l\={n}a ra ta Krō, radōn nēk \textbf{t}-ro\={n} tavul \textbf{ti}, radōn nēk \textbf{s}-ro\={n} tavul \textbf{tēmē}.\\ 
          \textsc{art} voice.of \textsc{hum.pl} \textsc{abl} Koro some \textsc{2sg} \textbf{\textsc{ipfv}\textsubscript{1}}-hear well \textbf{\textsc{ipfv}\textsubscript{2}} some \textsc{\textsc{2sg}} \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{1}}-hear well \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{2}}\\
    \glt ‘The language of Koro, some of it one understands easily, but some of it, one \textbf{doesn’t} understand easily.’ \quad  [\textsc{af.bp3}.18b] 
\z 

As a result, the negation \textit{s(o)-}… \textit{tēmē} is semantically ambiguous. A~sentence like \REF{ex:dor:baby} may correspond to a negative Perfect, a negative Stative, or a negative Imperfective:\footnote{The Negative realis is also the counterpart of certain less frequent \textsc{tamp} categories shown in \tabref{tab:dor:tamp}, such as the Dilatory aspect \textit{qra} ‘only then (in the past or future)’ [see Footnote \ref{fnt:ftn9}, and \REF{ex:dor:qra}], or its derivative the~Immediate past \textit{qra}… \textit{ti} ‘just recently’ [see \REF{ex:dor:wrisris}].}

\ea\label{ex:dor:baby} 
    \gll O \={m}er\={m}er \textbf{s}-\={n}or tavul \textbf{tēmē}. \\ 
         \textsc{art} child \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{1}}-sleep well \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{2}}\\
    \glt ‘The baby \{did not sleep \textasciitilde\ doesn’t sleep \textasciitilde\ is not sleeping\} well.’
\z 

\noindent The distinctions made in positive statements are neutralised under a single, semantically vague category of “Negative realis”, which encompasses most of the tense and aspect values found in the affirmative within the semantically realis domain.\footnote{The only two \textsc{tam} markers of the realis domain that have an exact correspondence between positive and negative polarities [cf. \tabref{tab:dor:tamp}] are the two phasal aspects “iami\-tive” and “continu\-a\-tive”; these will be examined in \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.7}.}

In sum, the semantic space of verbal aspect is cut up differently in the positive and in the negative – a configuration typical of northern Vanuatu languages in general.\footnote{See François (\citeyear[33–37]{François2003}, \citeyear[132]{François2005b}) for similar observations about the language Mwotlap; \citet[31, 52, 95]{Schnell2011} about Vera’a; \citet[461]{Malau2016} about Vurës.} This lack of a one-to-one correspondence between positive and negative polarities, which was obvious in~\tabref{tab:dor:tamp} [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.1}], is known as \textit{paradigmatic asymmetry} (\citealt{Miestamo2005,Miestamo2013-wals113}). When it involves the neutralisation of certain semantic distinctions, as happens here for realis \textsc{tam} categories, it is called \textit{paradigmatic neutralisation} \citep[54]{Miestamo2005}, abbreviated {“A/Cat/\nobreakdash TAM/Neutr”} (\citeyear[123]{Miestamo2005}).

\subsubsection{Is there an asymmetry in reality status?}\label{sec:dor:2.1.5}

As explained at the end of \sectref{sec:dor:1.2}, the analysis I favour is to consider all bipartite \textsc{tamp} markers, whether positive or negative, as unitary morphemes, even though they appear to consist – at least etymologically – of two elements.

Now, if we were to analyse the Negative realis \textit{s(o)-}… \textit{tēmē} into its components, we could not help noticing that it seems to combine the negation \textit{tēmē} (“indicative” negation, often associated with the realis domain) with a prefix \textit{s(o)-}, which is the same form as the positive “Irrealis”. Indeed, when used alone in positive clauses, the prefix \textit{s(o)-} is usually devoted to predicates with a meaning of conditional~\REF{ex:dor:bewitch}, future \REF{ex:dor:give}, potential \REF{ex:dor:sorcerer}, imperative \REF{ex:dor:irrimp} or deontic \REF{ex:dor:tuqatiti}: this is the textbook definition of an Irrealis marker. Under this literal analysis, one could be tempted to present a sentence like \REF{ex:dor:home2} with the tentative glosses in \REF{ex:dor:perfneg}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:perfneg} 
    \gll  Na \textbf{s}-tek \textbf{tēmē} ni a gvur.\\ 
          \textsc{1sg} \textbf{\textsc{irr}}-see \textbf{\textsc{neg.ind}} \textsc{3sg} \textsc{loc} home\\
    \glt ‘I didn’t see him at home.’
\z 

It may come as a semantic oddity that an Irrealis morpheme should be used in~statements about semantically ‘realis’ situations, whether past \REF{ex:dor:home2} or present (\ref{ex:dor:statneg2}, \ref{ex:dor:koro}). Yet~this is arguably due to a paradox inherent to negation itself: even when set in a realis (past or present) situation, the state-of-affairs that is being negated remains virtual, and indeed un-realised. In his major typological survey of negation, \citet[96]{Miestamo2005} discusses this type\footnote{For other general references, see also \citet{Elliott2000} and \citet{Cristofaro2012}.} under the label “paradigmatic asymmetry in reality status” (realis vs. irrealis), and explains it in these words: 

\begin{quote}
    “[T]he association between negation and non-reality on the formal level iconically reflects the association between negation and non-reality on the functional level.” (ibid: 96)
\end{quote}

Using the label “A/NonReal”, \citeauthor{Miestamo2005} (\citeyear[192]{Miestamo2005}, \citeyear{Miestamo2013-wals114}) observes the distribution of this asymmetry across languages, and finds it in 13\% of his sample (40~languages out of 297). While the pattern is well known across the world, in northern Vanuatu it is only found in Dorig: the other 16 languages of the Torres-Banks fail to show any link between negation and the irrealis. In~that sense, Dorig is locally unique in enforcing this pattern, whereby negative statements impose an irrealis verb in semantically realis (past, present) contexts.

In languages with “A/NonReal” asymmetry, the typical pattern is one where the contrast between realis and irrealis exists in the affirmative, but is neutralised under negation, in~favour of the irrealis. Thus for the Australian language Mawng, \citet{Miestamo2013-wals113} notes: “the negative clause is obligatorily marked for the \mbox{irrealis} category, whereas the affirmative can make a distinction between realis and irrealis”; such a configuration constitutes a case of paradigmatic neutralisation \citep[97]{Miestamo2005}.
%     \hc{@LSP here also Miestamo should not be hyphenated here. Mies-ta-mo. adding it to the localhyphenation did not help.}
Yet this is not what happens in Dorig: the~combination \textit{s(o)-}… \textit{tēmē}, even though it is originally based on an irrealis morpheme \textit{s(o)-}, can in~fact only receive a realis interpretation – that is, an anchoring in past or present situations. Thus in \tabref{tab:dor:realis}, the counterpart of perfect \REF{ex:dor:saw} \textit{na m-tek nēr}, namely \REF{ex:dor:sawneg} \textit{na s-tek tēmē nēr}, is strictly used for realis reference (past ‘I didn’t see them’, or present ‘I’m not seeing them’); it~cannot have any \mbox{irrealis} interpretation (*\textit{I~won’t see them}). The~latter meaning can only be expressed using one of the negations pertaining to the irrealis domain proper [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.6}], e.g. the Negative future \textit{vte} … \textit{tēmē} – as in \REF{ex:dor:wontsee}.

The system shown in \tabref{tab:dor:realis} could be explained using two very different analyses. Under one hypothesis, the negative sentence (\ref{ex:dor:sawneg}) would be seen as the symmetric counterpart of (\ref{ex:dor:willsee}) – at least if one considers only the surface forms, since it consists in adding to it the negative particle \textit{tēmē}. Yet that formal symmetry comes along with a semantic asymmetry, because the future interpretation of the affirmative (\ref{ex:dor:willsee}) corresponds to a past reading in the negative. Such a configuration is called “paradigmatic displacement”  by \citet[98]{Miestamo2005}, as he describes the facts of the Papuan language Alamblak:

\begin{quote}
    “The paradigm is asymmetric but has no neutralization; realis negation uses the irrealis form but the irrealis is negated differently (…). There is thus paradigmatic displacement rather than neutralization and the asymmetry is of type \textit{A/NonReal/Displc}.”
\end{quote}

While such an interpretation would be acceptable for Dorig, I will favour a different approach. In my view, the analytical tool of “symmetry” is only meaningful if we are comparing two sentences whose sole semantic difference is polarity. Faced with a dataset like \tabref{tab:dor:realis}, it does not appear useful to assess whether there is symmetry, formal or semantic, between sentences (\ref{ex:dor:willsee}) and (\ref{ex:dor:sawneg}), simply because these two sentences do not form a pair in terms of polarity. Likewise, we do not want to assess the symmetry of negation by comparing, say, English sentences \textit{He laughed} vs. \textit{He won’t laugh}, because they do not form a polarity pair. The only sentences relevant to this assessment are those consisting of a statement in the affirmative vs. its counterpart in the negative: that is, the pair (\ref{ex:dor:saw}--\ref{ex:dor:sawneg}) on the one hand; and the pair (\ref{ex:dor:willsee}--\ref{ex:dor:wontsee}) on the other hand. For a speaker of Dorig, there is no pragmatic context in which a sentence like (\ref{ex:dor:sawneg}) would form the negative counterpart to (\ref{ex:dor:willsee}). These two distinct sentences, involving different truth-value conditions, do not enter any form of productive polarity contrast in the \mbox{modern} language. 

In sum, I believe that the better analysis of the Dorig facts is to analyse the Negative realis marker as a single morphological unit \textit{s(o)‑… tēmē}, albeit a discontinuous one. Synchronically, that negative construction has no tie whatsoever with the positive irrealis \textit{s(o)‑} – apart from some partial homophony; yet this homophony is best ignored by the linguist, because it’s irrelevant to the speaker. Under this new analysis, what \tabref{tab:dor:realis} shows is rather the solidity of the contrast between realis and irrealis in the language, which remain formally distinguished both in the affirmative and in the negative. This comes in contrast with the cases of asymmetry we saw in \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.4} above, where certain paradigmatic contrasts in the domain of tense and aspect are neutralised under negation. 

This construction-based analysis [see \sectref{sec:dor:1.2}] is reflected in the glosses I use in \tabref{tab:dor:realis}. Its logical conclusion is that Dorig really doesn’t have A/NonReal asymmetry, because its Negative realis morpheme \textit{s(o)‑… tēmē}, synchronically, actually counts as [+realis], regardless of its etymology. In the remainder of this paper, I will continue to gloss the discontinuous morpheme \textit{s(o)-}… \textit{tēmē} as a single \mbox{semantic} category of “Negative realis”, irrespective of its etymological connection with the positive irrealis.

\subsubsection{Declarative statements in the irrealis domain}\label{sec:dor:2.1.6}

In the affirmative, the \textsc{tamp} category of Irrealis encoded with \textit{s(o)-}, like \REF{ex:dor:give}, may express intention, promise, threat – in a way equivalent to an English future:

\ea\label{ex:dor:give}
     \gll  Na \textbf{s}-la āt min kmur.\\ 
              \textsc{1sg} \textsc{irr}-take thither \textsc{dat} \textsc{2du}\\
     \glt ‘I will give it to you.’ \quad [\textsc{af.bp3}.29a]
\z

As we saw in Table~3 [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.5}], the negative equivalent of this positive Irrealis \textit{s(o)-} is not the Negative realis \textit{s(o)-}… \textit{tēmē}, but one of the irrealis negative morphemes – for example, the Negative future \textit{(v)te} … \textit{tēmē}, as in \REF{ex:dor:wontgive}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:wontgive}
    \gll   Na \textbf{vte} la \textbf{tēmē} āt min kmur.\\
              \textsc{1sg} \textsc{neg.fut\textsubscript{1}} take \textsc{neg.fut\textsubscript{2}} thither \textsc{dat} \textsc{2du}\\
    \glt ‘I won’t give it to you.’  \quad [\textsc{af.bp3}.29b]
\z 

The preverbal element \textit{vte} or \textit{te} never occurs alone. It~only exists as the first ele\-ment in~three discontinuous morphemes of negation, all with irrealis \mbox{semantics}: see Table~\ref{tab:irrealis}.\footnote{Note that only the first two of these correspond to declarative sentences, and thus to standard negation proper; as for prohibitive morphemes, they will be presented in~\sectref{sec:dor:2.2.2}.}

\begin{table}\small
% \renewcommand*{\arraystretch}{1.2}
         \begin{tabular}{lll}
            \textit{(v)te … tēmē} & Negative future & ‘I~won’t V…’\\
            \textit{(v)te … late} & Negative potential & ‘I can’t V…’\\
            \textit{(v)te … te} & Prohibitive & ‘Don’t V…!’\\
        \end{tabular}
    \caption{The three main negative markers of the irrealis domain}
    \label{tab:irrealis}
\end{table}

If we tried to analyse these three discontinuous negations into their components, we~might propose a generic gloss ‘\textsc{neg.irr}’ for \textit{(v)te}, without too much difficulty; but then, the \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2} particle would be impossible to gloss accurately. If~\textit{tēmē} were glossed ‘\textsc{fut}’ (so that \textsc{neg.irr + fut} results in a negative future), this would be incompatible with the other uses of \textit{tēmē} in past or present contexts. Likewise, \textit{late} cannot be glossed ‘\textsc{pot}’, as it only occurs in combination with the negation. Even more problematic would be to try and gloss separately \textit{(v)te … te}, assum\-ing we tried to achieve a compositional meaning of prohibitive. All things considered, the only elegant analysis -- which is also more realistic in terms of modelling speakers’ competence -- is to adopt a constructional approach, and consider each of these combinations as bipartite, unanalysable morphemes. The~three negations above will thus be glossed, respectively, \textsc{neg.fut\textsubscript{1}}… \textsc{neg.fut\textsubscript{2}}; \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{1}}… \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{2}}; and \textsc{proh\textsubscript{1}}… \textsc{proh\textsubscript{2}}. 

The Negative future, illustrated in \REF{ex:dor:wontgive}, is used to negate two types of future: the one encoded by the Irrealis \textit{s(o)-}, but also the Dilatory future marked by \REF{ex:dor:qra1} \textit{qra} [Footnote \ref{fnt:ftn9} p.\pageref{bkm:Ref130472068}]:
% \hc{@Sebastian: is it ok to have smaller textsize for the source of examples so that it fits on the line?}


\ea\label{ex:dor:qra}
    \ea\label{ex:dor:qra1}
        \gll  Dār s-sim bas, dār \textbf{qra} gāngēn.\\ 
              \textsc{1incl.du} \textsc{irr}-drink finish \textsc{1incl.du} \textsc{dilat} eat.\textsc{intr}\\
        \glt ‘We’ll first drink, and (only later) we will have dinner.’  {[Drg.d04:34]} %AP
    \ex\label{ex:dor:qra2}
        \gll  Dār s-sim, la dār \textbf{te} gāngēn \textbf{tēmē}.\\
              \textsc{1incl.du} \textsc{irr}-drink but \textsc{1incl.du} \textsc{neg.fut\textsubscript{1}} eat.\textsc{intr} \textsc{neg.fut\textsubscript{2}}\\
        \glt ‘We’ll drink, but we won’t be having dinner.’ 
    \z
\z 


\noindent This is another instance of paradigmatic asymmetry, as a semantic contrast made in the affirmative (between the Irrealis and the Dilatory future) is neutralised under negation.

In reality, the Negative future is very rare in my corpus: I~was only able to hear it under elicitation. Much more common are the two other types of irrealis negation: the Negative potential (21 instances in my corpus), and the various forms taken by the Prohibitive (35~instances) – for which, see \sectref{sec:dor:2.2.2}.

In the affirmative, potential statements of the type ‘I~can~V’ are expressed using the discontinuous Potential morpheme \textit{s(o)-… lala} – see \REF{ex:dor:lala} and \REF{ex:dor:follow1} above, or \REF{ex:dor:potential}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:potential} 
    \gll  O \={m}at ne\={n} ni \textbf{s}-daw rōrōw \textbf{lala} o tdun.\\ 
          \textsc{art} snake \textsc{dist} \textsc{3sg} \textsc{pot\textsubscript{1}}-do wrong \textsc{pot\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{art} person\\
    \glt ‘That snake can be harmful to people.’  \quad {\small [Drg.q.Anemol.03]}
\z 

The positive Potential \textit{s(o)-… lala} combines the Irrealis prefix \textit{s(o)-} with a \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2} particle \textit{lala}; the latter results from the grammaticalisation of a former postverb [\sectref{sec:dor:1.2}] \textit{lala} meaning ‘(do)~successfully, e.g. when hunting’. Yet synchronically, \textit{s(o)-… lala} must be analysed as a single (albeit discontinuous) morpheme coding for potential modality; hence the gloss \textsc{pot\textsubscript{1}}-… \textsc{pot\textsubscript{2}}. One reason to consider \textit{s(o)-… lala} as grammaticalised is precisely the form of its negative counterpart. Instead of a putative form *\textit{(v)te… lala tēmē} (which would be expected if \textit{lala} were still a postverb), the Negative potential is an unpredictable morpheme \textit{(v)te… late}. Thus, the negative equivalent of \REF{ex:dor:lala} above is \REF{ex:dor:late}:

\protectedex{\ea\label{ex:dor:late} [\textsc{negative} \textsc{potential}] \\
    \gll  Kmār \textbf{vte} bri\={n} \textbf{late} nēk.\\ 
          \textsc{1excl.du} \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{1}} help \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{2sg}\\
    \glt ‘We can’t help you.’
\z }


\noindent The \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2} particle \textit{late} results from the contraction of \textit{lala} with *\textit{te}, which is essentially a particle of negation [see \sectref{sec:dor:5}]; synchronically, it is unanalysable. 

Whether in the positive or negative, the potential mood may refer semantically to a situation in the present or future – as in (\ref{ex:dor:lala}/\ref{ex:dor:late}) or (\ref{ex:dor:follow1}--\ref{ex:dor:follow2}). It can also refer to a habitual possibility – as in \REF{ex:dor:potential}, or the clause \textit{nēk {te} tek {late} ni} in \REF{ex:dor:sorcerer}:


\ea\label{ex:dor:sorcerer}
    \gll  Tuar qō\={n} ni s-van nēk \textbf{te} tek \textbf{late} ni ni t-van ti.\\ 
          \textsc{indf} day \textsc{\textsc{3sg}} \textsc{irr}-go {\db}\textsc{\textsc{2sg}} \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{1}} see \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{\textsc{3sg}} \textsc{\textsc{3sg}} \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{1}}-go \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{2}}\\
    \glt ‘Sometimes [the sorcerer] can just walk around without being seen.’ \newline 
(lit. ‘Sometimes he’ll walk \textit{you can’t see him} yet he’s walking.’)  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003195#S38}{\{\texttt{⚓}3195\#S38\}} 
\z 

\subsubsection{The subsystem of phasal aspects}\label{sec:dor:2.1.7}

As we examined the paradigmatic asymmetries of the Dorig system, we saw how several aspect distinctions made in the affirmative are neutralised in the negative. One type of semantic boundary, though, is solid enough to be preserved across polarities: these are the contrasts involving phasal aspects with pragmatic presuppositions: ‘already’ vs. ‘not~yet’, ‘still’ vs. ‘no longer’.\footnote{For general references on phasal aspects, see \citet{Löbner1989}, \citet{Auwera1993}, (\citeyear{Auwera1998}), \citet{Baar1997}, \citet{Krifka2000}.} These aspects form a subsystem of their own, with grammaticalised constructions, both in the positive and negative.

Let us call \textit{t} the moment when a state of affairs \textit{P} changes into its opposite state \textit{Q} (e.g. \textit{alive} \textrm{${\rightarrow}$}~\textit{dead}; \textit{sick} \textrm{${\rightarrow}$}~\textit{cured}; \textit{single} \textrm{${\rightarrow}$}~\textit{married}; \textit{wet} \textrm{${\rightarrow}$}~\textit{dry}; etc.). If~I~wish to express that \textit{t} has taken place already, I~may formulate this by reference to the new state Q (‘the shirt is dry already’), by using what is known as a~\textsc{iamitive} \mbox{aspect}.\footnote{The term “iamitive“ (from Latin \textit{iam} ‘now, already’) is a type of perfect that focuses on change-of-state, and often builds on speaker’s expectations (\citealt{Olsson2013}; \citealt{DahlWälchli2016}).} Alternatively, I~may use a pragmatically equivalent formulation, this time making reference to the initial state \textit{P} (‘it’s no longer wet’). The latter construction, sometimes called \textsc{discontinuative} (\citealt[44]{Auwera1998}), involves a phasal negation ‘not any more, no longer’.
Another possibility is that the event \textit{t} (the change from \textit{P} to \textit{Q}) has not happened yet. Again, I may choose to express this by reference to \textit{P} (‘it’s still wet’), which is a \textsc{continuative}; or by reference to \textit{Q}, by employing what I’ll call a \textsc{nondumitive} (‘it’s not dry yet’).

Dorig constrasts two types of perfect aspects: the Perfect \textit{m(e)-} and the Iamitive \textit{m(e)-}… \textit{nok}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:married} 
    \ea\label{ex:dor:married1}
        \gll  I ntu-k \textbf{m}-lāg le tuar s\={n}ar.\\

    \z

The semantic contrast between perfect and iamitive, which is pervasive in northern Vanuatu (\citealt[118–130]{François2003}), has to do with the handling of information.\footnote{See also \citet{KlokMatthewson2015} \textsf{f}or a discussion of a contrast between ‘perfect’ and ‘already’ in Javanese\textsf{.}} In~a~Perfect sentence like \REF{ex:dor:married1}, the whole predicate brings new information. By contrast, \REF{ex:dor:married2} entails a pragmatic presupposition: due to local cultural expectations, the~event ‘\textit{get married (at some point)}’ is~presupposed or “pre-defined”, and here the focal information is whether that expected event has yet happened, or not. 

Admittedly, the contrast between \REF{ex:dor:married1} and \REF{ex:dor:married2} is encoded through a postverb \textit{nok} rather than through distinct \textsc{tamp} morphemes. That said, these two constructions entertain clear paradigmatic relations with their negative counterparts, which form \textsc{tamp} categories of their own [see \tabref{tab:dor:phasal}]. I~thus propose to take a constructional perspective here again, and consider these different combinations as \textsc{tamp} categories in their own right.

Indeed, the contrast between perfect and iamitive finds its mirror image in the negative polarity. A clause in the Perfect, with no internal hierarchy of information (old vs. new), would simply be negated with the Negative realis [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.4}]:\footnote{If \REF{ex:dor:notmarried} didn’t have a time complement, its Negative realis could also receive a present (stative) interpretation: \textit{I~ntu-k s-lāg tēmē} ‘My child isn’t married’ [see \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.4}].}

\ea\label{ex:dor:notmarried}  [\textsc{neg. realis}]\\
    \gll  I ntu-k \textbf{s}-lāg \textbf{tēmē} le tuar s\={n}ar.\\ 
          \textsc{pers} child-\textsc{1sg} \textsc{neg.r\textsubscript{1}}-marry \textsc{neg.r\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{loc} other month\\
    \glt ‘My child didn’t get married last month.’
\z 


The negative counterpart of the iamitive, on the other hand, is a specific construction equivalent to English ‘not~yet’. Among various names, that construction has sometimes been called \textit{nondum}, after its Latin equivalent (\citealt{VeselinovaDevos2021}); I propose to label it \textit{nondumitive}, to highlight the mirror-relationship with the \textit{iamitive}. In Dorig, the nondumitive is a discontinuous morpheme of the form \textit{sowse … te} – as in \REF{ex:dor:fire} above, or \REF{ex:dor:notyetmarried}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:notyetmarried}  [\textsc{nondumitive}]\\
    \gll  I ntu-k \textbf{sowse} lāg \textbf{te}.\\ 
          \textsc{pers} child-\textsc{1sg} \textbf{\textsc{ndum}\textsubscript{1}} marry \textbf{\textsc{ndum}\textsubscript{2}} \\
    \glt ‘My child isn’t married yet.’
\z 


The nondumitive also comes with pragmatic presuppositions – the very same ones we saw with the iamitive. Thus, \REF{ex:dor:notyetmarried} implicitly refers to the expectation that one should marry some day; the nondumitive states that such a predefined moment has not materialised yet at the moment of utterance. Likewise in \REF{ex:dor:fire1}, in a context where the subject was supposed to be cooking food, the (predefined) moment of lighting the fire had not taken place yet. 

As far as the morphology is concerned, one must note here a puzzling case of opacity between the ordinary Realis negation \textit{s(o)-… tēmē} on the one hand, and the Nondumitive \textit{sowse}…\textit{ te} on the other hand [\tabref{tab:dor:tamp}]. While English simply contrasts \textit{not} with \textit{not yet}, Dorig treats the two morphemes as formally unrelated with each other. The first element \textit{sowse} is opaque, being found exclusively in this context; as for the second element \textit{te}, it clearly bears a relation with the negative domain, yet not in a way that would make it easy to gloss on its~own.

Finally, another example of a phasal aspect with pragmatic implications is the Continuative, expressed in English with \textit{still} – see \REF{ex:dor:contin} above. Dorig expresses the positive continuative with a postverb \textit{mlēti} ‘still’ (glossed \textsc{cont} for ‘continuative’), as in \REF{ex:dor:alive}.\footnote{In (\ref{ex:dor:alive}), the dash encodes the elongated vowel [ɛːː] typical of the word \textit{le} in questions.}

\ea\label{ex:dor:alive} 
    \gll  Ni m-mat nok, le— ni va-ēs \textbf{mlēti}?\\ 
          \textsc{3sg} \textsc{prf}-dead \textsc{iam} or \textsc{3sg} \textsc{stat-}alive \textsc{cont}\\
    \glt ‘Is he dead already, or is he still alive?’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0007437#S64}{\{\texttt{⚓}7437\#S64\}}
\z  

The continuative particle \textit{mlēti} is generally incompatible with negation.\footnote{The only case when the continuative \textit{mlēti} can combine with a negation is when forming a sentential reply “Not yet” – see \sectref{sec:dor:3.1.2} below.} As we saw in \tabref{tab:dor:phasal}, the polarity counterpart of the Continuative is the Discontinuative ‘no~longer, not any more’. The latter obtains by combining the Realis negation \textit{s(o)-… tēmē} [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.5}] with the iamitive postverb \textit{nok}, as in (\ref{ex:dor:sick}):

\ea\label{ex:dor:sick}

        \gll  Obek, ni \textbf{s}-se\={m} \textbf{nok} \textbf{tēmē}.\\

\z

\subsubsection{Synthesis}\label{sec:dor:2.1.8}

To summarise our observations so far, standard negation in Dorig involves a wealth of morphological elements in which polarity is inextricably mixed with semantic dimensions of tense, aspect, modality – or even pragmatic presuppositions, in the case of phasal aspects. Negation is expressed by discontinuous morphemes which can hardly be given a compositional analysis, and are best understood as unanalysable constructions.

As detailed in \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.2}, the Dorig system is characterised by different forms of asymmetry across polarities: constructional asymmetry in the expression of tense-aspect-mood; paradigmatic asymmetry regarding tense and aspect; and possibly asymmetry with respect to reality \mbox{status}. In fact, there are very few areas where Dorig maintains some form of stability across polarities: the contrast in reality \mbox{status} has resisted neutralisation, in a pattern of “paradigmatic displacement”; and phasal aspects form a neat “rectangle”, with regular one-to-one correspondences between affirmative and negative.

All in all, there are so many differences between positive and negative \textsc{tamp} markers that Dorig could be analysed as having two distinct sub-systems. Each polarity has its own cut-up of the semantic space, with no easy way to find correspondences across polarities -- a type which \citet[54]{Miestamo2005} calls “different-system asymmetry” (DiffSys).

\subsection{Negation in non-declaratives}
\subsubsection{Questions}

Little needs to be said about questions. Interrogative sentences make use of the same verbal categories as we saw in the declarative: e.g. Negative Realis \REF{ex:dor:seeher}, Nondumitive \REF{ex:dor:mether}, Negative Potential \REF{ex:dor:phone}. 
In these examples, interrogation is only marked by prosody.

\z

\subsubsection{Prohibition}\label{sec:dor:2.2.2}

In the positive, an order can be encoded by an Imperative. If the subject is dual or plural, it~is encoded by a special pronoun \REF{ex:dor:imper2} \textit{ar} ‘\textsc{imp.2nsg}' (imperative non-singular) – contrasting with \REF{ex:dor:imper1} Ø for \textsc{2sg}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:imper} [\textsc{imperative}] \\
    \ea\label{ex:dor:imper1} 
        \gll (Ø) sēw ma! \\ 
             (\textsc{imp.2sg}) descend hither \\
        \glt ‘Come down!’ 
    \ex\label{ex:dor:imper2} 
        \gll \textbf{Ar} sēw ma!\\ 
             \textsc{imp.2nsg} descend hither\\
        \glt ‘Come down (y’all)!’ 
    \z 
\z 
\tabref{tab:dor:tamp} [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.1}] represented the Imperative category as \textit{[ar]}…: this stands for the alternation between preverbal Ø and \textit{ar}.


Another common way to formulate an order is simply to use an Irrealis clause in~\textit{s(o)-}, as in (\ref{ex:dor:irrimp1}--\ref{ex:dor:irrimp2}):

\ea\label{ex:dor:irrimp} [\textsc{irrealis}] 
    \ea\label{ex:dor:irrimp1} 
        \gll Nēk \textbf{s}-sēw ma! \\ 
             \textsc{2sg} \textsc{irr-}descend hither \\
        \glt  ‘Come down!’ 
    \ex\label{ex:dor:irrimp2} 
        \gll Kmur \textbf{s}-sēw ma!\\ 
             \textsc{2du} \textsc{irr-}descend hither\\
        \glt ‘Come down (you two)!’ 
    \z 
\z 

\noindent Except for the imperative prosody, such clauses are formally identical to the declarative sentences in the Irrealis – cf. \REF{ex:dor:bewitch}, \REF{ex:dor:give}. As for the prohibitive, it involves three constructions. Speakers describe them as perfectly synonymous; and indeed, they appear to be interchangeable in all contexts. The first construction is the discontinuous morpheme \textit{(v)te… te}. This requires the overt presence of a subject pronoun – unlike the imperative \REF{ex:dor:imper1} – plus reduplication of the verb, as in~\REF{ex:dor:negimp}:

\protectedex{\ea\label{ex:dor:negimp} [\textsc{negative imperative}] \\
    \gll  Nēk \textbf{vte} sēwsēw \textbf{te} ma!\\ 
         \textsc{2sg} \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{1}} descend{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{2}} hither \\
    \glt ‘Don’t come down!’ 
\z }

A non-singular subject of a Prohibitive can be either an ordinary pronoun or a special imperative pronoun. Thus, the dual equivalent of \REF{ex:dor:negimp} can be \textit{Ar~(v)te…~te} as in \REF{ex:dor:point} or \REF{ex:dor:throw} below, but it can also take the form \textit{Kmur (v)te…~te} as in \REF{ex:dor:tvilag}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:tvilag}
    \gll  Kmur \textbf{vte} vanvan tvilag \textbf{te} vak!\\ 
          \textsc{2du} \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{1}} go{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} beyond \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{dir}\\
    \glt ‘Don’t you (two) ever walk beyond that point over there!’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003254#S7}{\{\texttt{⚓}3254\#S7\}}
\z 

\noindent In terms of morphology, the Prohibitive can thus be seen as the negative counterpart of the Imperative \textit{[ar]}… of \REF{ex:dor:imper}, but also of the Irrealis with imperative reading \textit{s(o)-}… of \REF{ex:dor:irrimp}. This double correspondence was represented in \tabref{tab:dor:tamp} in~\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.1}.

The second construction consists of a clause-initial prohibitive particle \textit{tog} and a \textit{v(a)-} prefix, following the atypical template \REF{ex:dor:tog subject}.

\ea\label{ex:dor:tog subject}
\textbf{\textit{Tog}} subject \textrm{${\langle}$}\textbf{\textit{v}}\textit{(a)-} verb …~\textrm{${\rangle}$} …
\z 

\noindent Even though it is homophonous with the Stative, the prefix \textit{v(a)-} is likely to represent here a different morpheme, namely the Counterfactual [\sectref{sec:dor:2.4}]. In any case, the best analysis here again is to assign a single meaning ‘Prohibitive’ to the construction as a whole (i.e. \textit{tog… v(a)-} ‘\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{1}\ldots\ \textsc{proh}\textsubscript{2}’):


\ea\label{ex:dor:showoff}
    \gll \textbf{Tog} nēk \textbf{v-}savāg nēk vat\={m}e sa ne\={n}!\\
         \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{1}} \textsc{2sg} \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{2}}-boast \textsc{2sg} like \textsc{foc} \textsc{dist}\\
    \glt ‘Stop showing off like that!’ \quad [\textsc{af.bp3}.34b]  
\z 

\ea\label{ex:dor:underworld} 
    \gll  Kmur s-van, \textbf{tog} nēk \textbf{va}-vavgat min i Wrisris.\\ 
          \textsc{2du} \textsc{irr}-go \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{1}} \textsc{2sg} \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{2}}-talk\textsc{{\textasciitilde}red} with \textsc{pers} (name)\\
    \glt ‘As you walk together [to the Underworld], don’t talk to Wrisris.’ \\
     \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003197#S12}{\{\texttt{⚓}3197\#S12\}}
\z 

While (\ref{ex:dor:showoff}--\ref{ex:dor:underworld}) illustrate the prohibitive with a second person, \REF{ex:dor:women} shows it may also be used with third person subjects:


\ea\label{ex:dor:women}
    \gll \textbf{Tog} ra=rqa \textbf{v-}van gin o qāti bē!\\
         \textsc{proh\textsubscript{1}} \textsc{pl=}woman \textsc{proh\textsubscript{2}}\textsc{-}go \textsc{obl} \textsc{art} source water\\
    \glt ‘Women must not go to the river source.’ \quad [\textsc{af.bp3}.30b]
\z 

Finally, a third construction exists, that is somewhat a hybrid of the first two. It~takes the form of a sequence \textit{tog… te}, which I also gloss ‘Prohibitive’. The coding of its second-person subjects is parallel to the positive counterpart we saw in \REF{ex:dor:imper}: if the subject is singular \REF{ex:dor:worry}, it may be encoded with a zero; but a non-singular subject \REF{ex:dor:vak} would involve \textit{ar}: 

\ea\label{ex:dor:worry} 
    \gll  \textbf{Tog} dōdōm mawmawis \textbf{te} aē!\\ 
          \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{1}} think{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} suffer{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} \textbf{\textsc{proh}}\textbf{\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{adv.anaph}\\
    \glt ‘Don’t worry about it!’ \quad { \small [Drg.d04.Kava:41]} 
\z 

\ea\label{ex:dor:vak}
    \gll Ar \textbf{tog} vanvan rās \textbf{te} vak!\\
         \textsc{imp.2nsg} \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{1}} go{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} far \textbf{\textsc{proh}\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{dir}\\
    \glt‘Don’t you (two) walk too far over there!’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0007437#S29}{\{\texttt{⚓}7437\#S29\}} 
\z  

Dorig’s three prohibitive constructions are used in the same pragmatic contexts, and appear to be perfectly interchangeable -- as shown in \REF{ex:dor:prohibs}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:prohibs}
    \ea\label{ex:dor:40a}
        \gll  Nēk (v)\textbf{te} simsim \textbf{te}!\\ 
              \textsc{2sg} \textsc{proh\textsubscript{1}} drink{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} \textsc{proh\textsubscript{2}}\\
    \ex\label{ex:dor:40b}
        \gll  Nēk \textbf{tog} simsim \textbf{te}!\\
              \textsc{2sg} \textsc{proh\textsubscript{1}} drink{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} \textsc{proh\textsubscript{2}}\\
    \ex\label{ex:dor:40c}
        \gll  \textbf{Tog} nēk \textbf{v-}sim!\\
              \textsc{proh\textsubscript{1}} \textsc{2sg} \textsc{proh\textsubscript{2}}\textsc{-}drink\\
        \glt ‘Don’t drink it!’
    \z
\z

\noindent This diversity of forms for the prohibitive adds to the profusion of negative morphemes we~had seen already.\footnote{In addition, Dorig also has a marker of apprehensive modality, which in some contexts may be used as an indirect form of prohibitive: this will be briefly discussed in \sectref{sec:dor:4.5}.}

These three constructions can be situated within the typology of prohibitive patterns proposed by \citet{AuweraEtAl2013}. Dorig belongs to their subtype~\#4, labelled “special imperative + special negative”: 

% \aut{@LSP: can we reduce the spacing in this itemize list?}
\begin{itemize}

\item \textit{special imperative}: the three prohibitives involve morphosyntactic patterns specific to them, and not found in the positive imperative (obligatory reduplication, obligatory exponence of the subject);

\item \textit{special negative}: the three prohibitives employ (bipartite) negators that are all reserved to the expression of the prohibitive, and never used in declaratives.

\end{itemize}

\noindent \citegen{AuweraEtAl2013} typological study included a sample of six Vanua\-tu languages, which pertain to different subtypes. Among that sample, the language geographically closest to Dorig, namely Mwotlap, was also assigned to their subtype \#4.

\subsection{Negation in stative predications}\label{sec:dor:2.3}

The previous sections examined verbal clauses. Following the structure of the reference questionnaire (\citetv{chapters/questionnaire}), we now turn to \textit{stative predication}. As we’ll see, this umbrella category encompasses quite different types of negation again.

\subsubsection{Equative and ascriptive predicates}\label{sec:dor:2.3.1}

In the absence of a copula like English \textit{be}, noun phrases in Dorig are directly predicative.\footnote{This is true of other languages in north Vanuatu – e.g. Mwotlap (\citealt[128]{François2005b}), Vera’a \citep[32]{Schnell2011}, Vurës (\citealt[68]{Malau2016}), Hiw (\citealt[326]{François2017}) – and widespread in Oceanic (\citealt{Lier2016}; \citealt{FrançoisForthcominga}).} In~\REF{ex:dor:major}, the predicate NP is shown between brackets \textrm{${\langle}$}…\textrm{${\rangle}$}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:major} 
    \gll  Ni \textrm{${\langle}$}o tdun vi-lwo nami kma\textrm{${\rangle}$}.\\ 
          \textsc{3sg} \textsc{art} person \textsc{attr-}great \textsc{poss} \textsc{1excl.pl}\\
    \glt ‘He is a major figure for us.’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003197#S35}{\{\texttt{⚓}3197\#S35\}}
\z 

Such nominal predicates are negated using the negator \textit{tēmē}. Whereas verbs only use it in combination with a pre-verbal \textsc{tamp} element – e.g. \textit{s(o)-… tēmē} or \textit{vte… tēmē} – non-verbal predicates feature \textit{tēmē} as the sole marker of negation. When \textit{tēmē} occurs alone like this, I~propose to gloss it \textsc{neg.ind} [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.3}]. Thus compare the positive noun predicate \REF{ex:dor:toy1} (\textit{X is an N}) with its negative counterpart \REF{ex:dor:toy2} (\textit{X is \textbf{not} an N}):

\ea\label{ex:dor:toy}
    \ea\label{ex:dor:toy1}
        \gll  O masa \textrm{${\langle}$}oror nami \={m}er\={m}er\textrm{${\rangle}$}.\\ 
              \textsc{art} knife toy \textsc{poss} child\\
        \glt ‘A knife is a toy for children.’ 
    \ex\label{ex:dor:toy2}
        \gll   O masa \textrm{${\langle}$}oror nami \={m}er\={m}er \textbf{tēmē}\textrm{${\rangle}$}.\\
              \textsc{art} knife toy \textsc{poss} child \textsc{neg.ind}\\
        \glt ‘A knife is \textbf{not} a toy for children.’ \quad {\small [Drg.d05.Naef:43]}  
    \z
\z 

\subsubsection{Negation of attributive predicates}\label{sec:dor2.3.2}

Dorig has a category of adjectives. Unlike verbs, adjectives can modify nouns, by~means of the ‘Attributive’ prefix \textit{v(e)-} (cf. \REF{ex:dor:major} above). In spite of their structural difference, adjectives behave the same as stative verbs in predicate position, and take the same array of \textsc{tamp} markers. Thus if the meaning is stative as in \REF{ex:dor:vawe}, the adjective inflects for the Stative aspect \textit{v(a)-}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:vawe}
    \gll \textbf{Va}-wē.\\
         \textsc{stat-}good\\
    \glt ‘It’s okay / That’s fine / It’s beautiful.’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003189#S14}{\{\texttt{⚓}3189\#S14\}}
\z 

In principle, adjectival predicates are negated following the same rules as for verbs [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1}]. Thus the Stative, Perfect, or Imperfective aspects in the positive are all negated with the Negative Realis \REF{ex:dor:ache} \textit{s(o)-… tēmē}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:ache} 
    \gll  Na bē-k \textbf{s}-wē \textbf{tēmē}.\\ 
          \textsc{art} body-\textsc{1sg} \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{1}}-good \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{2}} \\
    \glt ‘My body is aching.’ (lit. my body is not well) \quad {\small [Drg.d02.Krae:06]} 
\z 

\noindent However, my corpus shows several examples, like \REF{ex:dor:ok}, where a negated adjectives has kept the stative \textit{v(a)-}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:ok}
    \gll \textbf{Va}-wē \textbf{tēmē}!\\
         \textsc{stat-}good \textsc{neg.ind}\\
    \glt ‘That’s not okay.’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0002306#S67}{\{\texttt{⚓}2306\#S67\}}
\z 

\noindent Such a combination is excluded with stative verbs – see \REF{ex:dor:statneg} above – but it is allowed with adjectives. This is coherent with our earlier observation about nominal predicates [\sectref{sec:dor:2.3.1}], suggesting that \textit{non-verbal predicates} follow simpler rules than verbal ones. Negating a non-verbal predicate only takes adding the negator \textit{tēmē}. This is the only domain where Dorig negation shows full “symmetry” between polarities. 

This principle also works with a handful of adjectives that happen to be incompatible with the stative prefix -- e.g. \REF{ex:dor:far} \textit{arās} ‘remote, far away’. They are simply negated by adding \textit{tēmē}:


\ea\label{ex:dor:far}
    \begin{xlist}
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:dor:far1}
        \gll  Arās soqsoq sa!\\ 
               far \textsc{ints} \textsc{dist} \\ 
        \glt ‘That’s really far!'
    \exi{B: }\label{ex:dor:far2}
        \gll  Bek! Arās \textbf{tēmē}.\\ 
               \textsc{negex} far \textsc{neg.ind}\\
        \glt ‘Not at all! It’s not far.’  \quad
     {\small [Drg.q.d01.Road:21]}  
    \end{xlist}
\z 

Finally, Dorig has a similative predicate (\citealt[1052]{FrançoisForthcominga}) \textit{ta\={m}rag} ‘be like…’ -- derived from \textit{\={m}rag} ‘like…’ -- that behaves neither like an adjective nor like a verb. As \REF{ex:dor:similative} shows, this similative takes the same negation as other non-verbal predicates, namely \textit{tēmē}: 

\ea\label{ex:dor:similative}
    \gll Ta\={m}rag \textbf{tēmē} aēsa le Vanuatu.\\
        be.like \textsc{neg.ind} here \textsc{loc} Vanuatu\\
    \glt ‘It’s not like here in Vanuatu.’ \quad {\small [\textsc{af.bp3}.28a]}  
\z

\subsubsection{Existential, possessive, locative predicates}\label{sec:dor:2.3.3}

In the affirmative, Dorig usually forms its existentials using the word \REF{ex:dor:exist} \textit{aē}:\footnote{The original use of \textit{aē} is as an oblique anaphoric ‘about~it, with~it, at~it, there’, used in adjunct position – see \REF{ex:dor:worry}. When used in predicate position, that adverb has grammaticalised into an existential operator (compare English \textit{there} → \textit{be~there}). A similar path can be reconstructed in various other Oceanic languages: e.g.~Mwotlap (\citealt[128]{François2005b}, \citeyear[1055]{FrançoisForthcominga}), East Uvean (\citealt[305]{MoyseFaurie2018}).}

\ea\label{ex:dor:exist}
    \gll O tne vre \textrm{${\langle}$}\textbf{aē}\textrm{${\rangle}$}, Diwtag.\\
         \textsc{art} location.of village \textsc{ex} Diwtag\\
    \glt‘There is an abandoned village, (called) Diwtag.’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003195#S45}{\{\texttt{⚓}3195\#S45\}}
\z

The negation of an existential predicate \REF{ex:dor:beka} employs a dedicated negator, namely \REF{ex:dor:bekb} \textit{bek} or \textit{obek} ‘\textsc{negex}’ (Negative existential):

\ea\label{ex:dor:bek0}
    \ea\label{ex:dor:beka}
        \gll O bē \textrm{${\langle}$}aē\textrm{${\rangle}$}. \\ 
             \textsc{art} water \textsc{ex}  \\
        \glt ‘There is water.’ 
    \ex\label{ex:dor:bekb}
        \gll  O bē \textrm{${\langle}$}\textbf{obek}\textrm{${\rangle}$}.\\
             \textsc{art} water \textsc{negex} \\
        \glt ‘There is no water.’
    \z 
\z 


Existential constructions are also used to encode predicative possession. The equivalent of English \textit{I~have an~N} is a structure meaning literally “There is my~N” {\textasciitilde} “My~N exists”. This may refer to alienable \REF{ex:dor:canoe} or to inalienable \REF{ex:dor:child} possession:

\ea\label{ex:dor:canoe} 
    \gll  Namo-n o ak sōsō vi-lwo \textbf{aē}.\\ 
          \textsc{poss}-\textsc{3sg} \textsc{art} ship paddle{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} \textsc{attr}-big \textsc{ex}\\
    \glt ‘He had a large canoe.’  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0002306#S1}{\{\texttt{⚓}2306\#S1\}}
\z 


\ea\label{ex:dor:child} 
    \gll  I nti kmār nok \textbf{aē}.\\ 
          \textsc{pers} child.of \textsc{1excl.du} \textsc{iam} \textsc{ex}\\
    \glt ‘We already have children.’ \quad {\small [Drg.q.d12.Sintia:36]}  
\z 

\noindent Such possessive predicates are also negated using \textit{(o)bek} – see \REF{ex:dor:nochild}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:nochild} 
    \gll  Nēk magse-\={n}, i ntō-\={n} \textbf{obek}.\\ 
          \textsc{2sg} alone-\textsc{2sg} \textsc{pers} child.of-\textsc{2sg} \textsc{negex}\\
    \glt  ‘You are alone, you don’t have children.’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0002306#S41}{\{\texttt{⚓}2306\#S41\}}
\z 

With a definite subject, a Negative existential \textit{obek} also serves to negate a locative predicate such as \REF{ex:dor:village1}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:village}
    \ea\label{ex:dor:village1} 
        \gll  Ni le mo-n o vre. \\ 
             \textsc{3sg} \textsc{loc} \textsc{poss-}\textsc{3sg} \textsc{art} village \\
        \glt ‘He is in his village.’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003197#S8}{\{\texttt{⚓}3197\#S8\}}
    \ex\label{ex:dor:village2}
        \gll  Ni \textbf{obek} le mo-n o vre.\\ 
             \textsc{3sg} \textsc{negex} \textsc{loc} \textsc{poss-}\textsc{3sg} \textsc{art} village\\
        \glt ‘He isn’t in his village.’ 
    \z 
\z 


\noindent We’ll see in \sectref{sec:dor:3.1.1} how the Negative existential \textit{obek} is also used for negative replies.\footnote{The syntactic and phraseological behaviour of Dorig \textit{obek} is parallel to that of equivalent morphemes in~northern Vanuatu languages – Hiw \textit{tego}, Vurës \textit{odia\={n}} (cf. \citealt[66]{Malau2016}), Mwotlap \textit{tateh}, Lemerig \textit{niv}, etc.; see the comparison in \citet[214, 219--221]{François2011}.}

\subsection{Negation in non-main clauses}
\label{bkm:Ref130736429}\label{sec:dor:2.4}
The rules of negation are identical in main and non-main clauses. Example \REF{ex:dor:dodge} has two clauses in a causal relation \{\textit{P~because~Q}\}. The second clause uses the Negative realis, just like an independent clause would (cf. \ref{ex:dor:home2}):


\ea\label{ex:dor:dodge} 
    \gll  Kmur me-bri\={n} na \textit{sur} o āv \textbf{s}-gān \textbf{tēmē} na.\\ 
          \textsc{2du} \textsc{prf}-help \textsc{1sg} \textsc{csl} \textsc{art} fire \textsc{neg.r\textsubscript{1}}-burn \textsc{neg.r\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{1sg}\\
    \glt ‘You helped me dodge the fire.’ \\ (lit. ‘You two helped me \textit{so} the fire didn’t burn me.’) \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0002306#S68}{\{\texttt{⚓}2306\#S68\}}
\z 


In a relative clause, the subordinator \textit{ka} inserts between the clause’s subject and predicate. The relative clause in \REF{ex:dor:point} features a nondumitive:

\ea\label{ex:dor:point} 
    \gll  Ar te vanvan vga te vak gēn ne\={n} sa  \\
          \textsc{imp.2nsg} \textsc{proh\textsubscript{1}} go{\textasciitilde}\textsc{proh} beyond \textsc{proh\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{dir} \textsc{foc} \textsc{dist} \textsc{top} \\
          
    \gll  \{gēn ka \textbf{sowse} van \textbf{te} aē\}.  \\
          ~\textsc{1incl.pl} \textsc{sub} \textsc{ndum\textsubscript{1}} go \textsc{ndum\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{adv.anaph} \\
    \glt ‘Don’t you two walk beyond the point over there, \\~where \textit{we haven’t been~yet}!’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0007437#S20}{\{\texttt{⚓}7437\#S20\}} 
\z 


The morphosyntax of negation is here identical to the one found in an independent sentence (cf. 8). Section \sectref{sec:dor:4.5} below will examine a type of quasi negation in quasi subordinate contexts: namely, the apprehensive \textit{tekor} ‘so that not…; for~fear that…’.

I will here focus on one particular type of syndesis: conditional systems. Conditional systems in Dorig present two semantic subtypes: \textsc{hypothetical} vs. \textsc{counterfactual} systems. As \tabref{tab:dor:conditional} shows, these two types of conditionals require different negations when the conditional protasis is negated.

In \textsc{hypothetical} systems like \REF{ex:dor:mosquito}, the conditional subordinator (English~\textit{if}) is the complementiser \textit{kak}, usually followed (in the affirmative) by a verb in the Perfect \textit{m(e)-}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:mosquito} 
    \gll  \textit{Kak} o d\={m}ug \textbf{m}-kot nēk, nēk s-gār nēk  s-dē\={n} o mrān.\\ 
    \textsc{comp} \textsc{art} mosquito \textsc{prf-}bite \textsc{2sg} \textsc{2sg} \textsc{irr}-scratch \textsc{2sg} \textsc{irr}-reach \textsc{art} daylight\\
    \glt ‘If you’re bitten by mosquitoes, you’ll scratch yourself all night.’
\z 

If the protasis is negative as in \REF{ex:dor:ritual}, the Perfect marker \textit{m(e)-…} is replaced by a combination \textit{mtē… tēmē} in the usual \textsc{tamp} slots:

\newpage
\ea\label{ex:dor:ritual} 
    \gll  \textit{Kak} nēk \textbf{mtē} vrisa wālōg \textbf{tēmē} mi (…), \\
     \textsc{comp} \textsc{2sg} \textsc{hyp.neg} run round \textsc{neg.ind} with.it \\
     
    \gll   nēk s-gān o \={m}la ne\={n}, v-marmar.\\
    \textsc{2sg} \textsc{irr}-eat \textsc{art} scrubfowl \textsc{dem} \textsc{stat}-hard \\
    \glt [\textit{a magic ritual to make meat tender}] ‘If you don’t run in circles while holding it, then when you eat the~scrubfowl, [its meat] will be too hard.’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003189#S41}{\{\texttt{⚓}3189\#S41\}}
\z 

The \textsc{tamp} marker \textit{mtē}… \textit{tēmē} is only found in this context (although see ex.~\ref{ex:dor:sleep}). The use of the negator \textit{tēmē}, normally reserved to realis or “indicative” modality, is somewhat paradoxical in the case of a hypothesis; but it is coherent with the use of a (realis) Perfect in the affirmative equivalent \REF{ex:dor:mosquito}.

As for \textsc{counterfactual} hypotheses, they involve a dedicated counterfactual system \textit{vit}… \textit{\={m}rag} [\tabref{tab:dor:conditional}], as seen in \REF{ex:dor:blind} with two positive clauses:

\protectedex{\ea
    \label{ex:dor:blind}
    \gll Ni \textbf{\textit{vit}} ttuw na mta-n, \\ 
     \textsc{3sg} if.\textsc{cntf} hit \textsc{art} eye-\textsc{3sg} \\
    
    \gll \textbf{\textit{\={m}rag}} na mta-n \textbf{v}-qel ni!\\
    then.\textsc{cntf} \textsc{art} eye-\textsc{3sg} \textsc{cntf}-blind \textsc{obl.ana} \\
    \glt ‘If he had hit her eyes, she would have become blind.’ \quad {\small [Drg.d08.Rao:15]}
\z }


In such a system, a negative protasis requires a special negation, namely \textit{(v)te…~te} -- as in \REF{ex:dor:squid}:

\ea
    \label{ex:dor:squid}
    \gll Na \textbf{\textit{vit}} \textbf{te} lōblōb \textbf{te} o wrēt sa, \textbf{\textit{\={m}rag}} \textbf{v}-marmar.\\
        \textsc{1sg} if.\textsc{cntf} \textsc{neg.cntf\textsubscript{1}} pound{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} \textsc{neg.cntf\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{art} squid \textsc{top} then.\textsc{cntf} \textsc{cntf-}hard \\
    \glt ‘If I hadn’t pounded this squid, it would be too hard.’ \quad {\small [\textsc{af.bp3}.33a]}
\z 


It is noteworthy that the negation \textit{(v)te… te} is used both for the prohibitive [\sectref{sec:dor:2.2.2}] and for a negative Counterfactual hypothesis. Indeed, those are two contexts when the speaker elaborates a virtual situation in contrast with reality.\footnote{Likewise, a language like Latin would use the subjunctive in both cases: the Counterfactual (\textit{si~eum occidisset} ‘if she had slain him…’) and the Prohibitive (\textit{ne facias} ‘don’t do!’).}

\subsection{Negative lexicalizations}\label{sec:dor:2.5}

The notion of “negative lexicalization” \citep{Veselinova2013lexicalization} refers to the case when a negative meaning is expressed by lexical rather than morphological means. In other words, negative polarity is baked into a word's lexical semantics, rather than being part of a compositional expression. For example, English \textit{refuse} can be seen as the negative counterpart of \textit{accept}.


Except for the contrast between positive and negative existentials [\sectref{sec:dor:2.3.3}], Dorig does not have clearcut cases of such a pattern. Among Dorig’s neighbours, some languages show lexicalisation for meanings such as ‘not want’ (Teanu \textit{mene}), or ‘not know’ (Hiw \textit{yi\={n}etog}, Teanu \textit{mui}: \citealt{François2021}). But in such cases, Dorig would use a phrasal negation: \textit{so-mrōs tēmē} ‘not want’,\textit{ so-vrēgēl tēmē} ‘not know’.

\subsection{Other clausal negation constructions}\label{sec:dor:2.6}

Somewhat peripheral to the domain of negation proper is the frustrative postverb \textit{mtēl} ‘(do) in vain’ -- e.g. \REF{ex:dor:vain}. A common translation is often a negative construction in English, such as ‘be unable to, can’t’:

\protectedex{\ea\label{ex:dor:vain}
    \gll  Sō sag ne\={n}, t-rev mlē namon o ak ne\={n} ti, \\
        paddle up \textsc{dist} \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{1}}-tow again \textsc{poss}.\textsc{3sg} \textsc{art} canoe \textsc{dist} \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{2}}  \\
          
    \gll  la t-\textit{revrev} \textbf{\textit{mtēl}} ti. \\
        but \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{1}}-tow{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} in.vain \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{2}} \\
    \glt ‘Once he reached the shore, he tried again to tow his boat, but \textit{didn’t~manage to}.’ (lit. ‘but \textit{he towed in vain}’ = he tried to tow it but~was~\textsc{not} able~to) \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0002306\#S35}{\{\texttt{⚓}2306\#S35\}}%added \ before # in the link, that is not needed in the others, but here it created an error. but the link still works (20.11.2023)
\z }

In spite of its English translation, this frustrative construction cannot be considered a proper instance of a negative structure in the grammar of Dorig.

\ea\label{ex:dor:stillwet}
    \begin{xlist}
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:dor:stillwet1}

The negation \textit{(o)bek} may contradict a negative statement or question uttered by the addressee, in which case it may translate in English as a strong ‘yes’ (Fr.~\textit{si!}, Germ. \textit{doch!}):

\ea\label{ex:dor:nono}
    \begin{xlist}
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:dor:nono1}
        \gll   Kmur vte bri\={n} late na! \\ 
              \textsc{2du} \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{1}} help \textsc{neg.pot\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{2sg}\\ 
        \glt ‘You won’t be able to help me!'
    \exi{B: }\label{ex:dor:nono2}
        \gll  \textbf{Obek}, va-wē!\\ 
               \textsc{negex} \textsc{stat-}good\\\
        \glt ‘\textbf{Yes} (we will), that’s fine!’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0002306#S21}{\{\texttt{⚓}2306\#S21\}}
    \end{xlist}
\z



\noindent A dialogue like \REF{ex:dor:nono} shows that Dorig behaves like Japanese, in that its negative replies disagree with the polarity of the previous utterance, rather than with its propositional content (see \citealt{Holmberg2015}; \citealt{Miestamo2017}). In that sense, it~serves as a “polarity-reversing particle” \citep[23]{Moser2018}.

\subsubsection{Equivalent of a Nondumitive clause}
\label{sec:dor:3.1.2}\label{bkm:Ref66558480}
The standalone equivalent of the nondumitive \textit{sowse… te} ‘not yet’ [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.7}] is a combination of \textit{(o)bek} with the continuative marker \textit{mlēti}. 
Such a combination reads literally as shown in \REF{ex:dor:it is still the case}.


\ea\label{ex:dor:it is still the case}
\{~it is still the case~\}\textsc{\textsubscript{continuative}} that [\textit{no}]\textsc{\textsubscript{negex}} 
\z

\noindent This is in fact parallel to English \textit{not yet} or French \textit{pas encore}.
Note that the negative reply \textit{bek mlēti} can also be used as a tag in the question (\ref{ex:dor:bek1}).

\ea\label{ex:dor:bek}
    \begin{xlist}
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:dor:bek1}
        \gll  Ni m-lāg nok, le \textit{bek} \textit{mlēti}?\\ 
          \textsc{3sg} \textsc{iam\textsubscript{1}}-marry \textsc{iam\textsubscript{2}} or \textsc{negex} \textsc{cont}\\ 
        \glt ‘Is she married, or not yet?' 
    \exi{B: }\label{ex:dor:bek2}
        \gll  \textbf{Bek} \textbf{mlēti}.\\ 
                \textsc{negex} \textsc{cont}\\
        \glt ‘Not yet.’ \quad {\small [Drg.d12.Sintia:33]}  
    \end{xlist}
\z 

This combination of the negative existential \textit{bek} with the continuative \textit{mlēti} provides a standalone equivalent to the nondumitive \REF{ex:dor:notyetmarried2}:


\ea\label{ex:dor:notyetmarried2} 
    \gll  Ni \textbf{sowse} lāg \textbf{te}.\\ 
          \textsc{3}sg \textbf{\textsc{ndum}\textsubscript{1}} marry \textbf{\textsc{ndum}\textsubscript{2}} \\
    \glt ‘She isn’t married yet.’
\z 


\noindent In English, the relation between the clausal construction (\textit{not… yet}) and the standalone equivalent (\textit{Not yet.}) is formally transparent; in Dorig, it is quite opaque – compare (\ref{ex:dor:bek}--\ref{ex:dor:notyetmarried2}).

\subsubsection{Equivalent of a Prohibitive}\label{sec:dor:3.1.3}

A standalone prohibitive uses the interjection \REF{ex:dor:throw} \textit{tog!} ‘don’t!’. This is the same word as the formative found in \textit{tog \ldots\ v(a)-}, one of the \textsc{tamp} markers for prohibitive – see \REF{ex:dor:underworld} in \sectref{sec:dor:2.2.2}.

\ea\label{ex:dor:throw} 
    \gll  \textbf{Tog}! Ar te qāgqēg vtē te!\\ 
      \textbf{\textsc{proh}} \textsc{2nsg}.\textsc{imp} \textsc{proh\textsubscript{1}} throw{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} away \textsc{proh\textsubscript{2}}\\
    \glt ‘Don’t! Don’t you throw it away!’ \quad {\small [Drg.d09.Karen:41]} 
\z 

Dorig also has a special interjection \REF{ex:dor:tuqatiti} \textit{tuqa (titi)} for what can be called the “dilatory prohi\-bi\-tive”, i.e.~‘Not~yet!’ or ‘Wait!’:

\ea\label{ex:dor:tuqatiti} 
    \gll \textbf{Tuqa} titi! So-wdō\={n} \={m}o o āv. \\ 
          \textbf{\textsc{dilat.proh}} \textsc{pol.imp} \textsc{irr-}set.up before \textsc{art} fire\\
    \glt ‘\textit{Not yet / Wait!} You must first set up the fire.’ \quad  {\small [Drg.d10.Bekem:10]} 
\z 

\noindent This sort of interjection is a common feature in northern Vanuatu – see Table~\ref{tab:dor:standalone} in the Appendix.

\subsection{Negative indefinites and quantifiers}\label{sec:dor:3.2}

Dorig does not have inherently negative indefinites or adverbs equivalent to \mbox{English} \textit{never}, \textit{nobody}, \textit{nothing}, \textit{no~X}, etc. These meanings are expressed by combining the expected negation with a generic noun (hyperonym) such as:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textit{o~tdun} ‘(a)~person’ + \textsc{neg} \textrm{${\rightarrow}$} ‘nobody’

\item \textit{o~sa(v)} ‘(a)~thing, what’ + \textsc{neg} \textrm{${\rightarrow}$} ‘nothing’

\end{itemize}


In the typology of negative indefinites proposed by \citeauthor{Haspelmath2013-wals46} (\citeyear{Haspelmath2013-wals46}, \citeyear{Haspelmath2013-wals115}), \textit{sav} and \textit{tdun} would be “generic-noun-based indefinites”. \REF{ex:dor:desert} shows the equivalent of \textit{nobody} in an existential clause:

\ea\label{ex:dor:desert}
    \gll A\={m}o, \textbf{\textsc{o}} \textbf{tdun} \textbf{obek}. \\
         in.past \textsc{art} person \textsc{negex}\\
    \glt ‘In the olden days, [in this island] there was \textit{nobody}.’ \\(lit. ‘there was not a person') \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003195#S7}{\{\texttt{⚓}3195\#S7\}}
\z 


\noindent The negated participant can be the syntactic subject as in \REF{ex:dor:desert}, or an object as in \REF{ex:dor:nothing}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:nothing} 
    \gll  Kmār s-tek tēmē \textbf{\textsc{o}} \textbf{sa} aēsei.\\ 
          \textsc{1excl.du} \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{1}-see \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{2} \textsc{art} what here\\
    \glt ‘We haven’t seen \textit{anything} here.’ \quad {\small [Drg.d05.Naef:08]}  
\z 

Just like other nouns, the NP heads \textit{tdun} and \textit{sa(v)} take the common noun article~\textit{o}. As~we’ll see in \sectref{sec:dor:4.3} for noun phrases in general, that article \textit{o} remains unchanged whether the sentence is affirmative or negative. 

\subsection{Negative derivation and case-marking}\label{sec:dor:3.3}

Patterns of negative derivation (such as English \textit{un-friendly}, \textit{im-possible}, \textit{time-less}) are rare in Oceanic languages, and evidently absent in Dorig.

Likewise, Dorig has no adposition similar to English \textit{without}. In order to express a caritive meaning, one would resort to a complex sentence with a negative existential. For example, \textit{without a child} or \textit{childless} would be expressed by a sentence like \REF{ex:dor:nochild} above – a strategy which is typologically very common (\citealt[118]{Veselinova2013lexicalization}).

\section{Other aspects of negation}\label{sec:dor:4}
\subsection{The scope of negation}\label{sec:dor:4.1}

Dorig does not have grammaticalised devices to specify the scope of negation. As~a rule, the negation is carried by the predicate head (generally, a verb) regardless of which constituent is semantically the focus of the negation: 


\ea\label{ex:dor:wrisris} 
    \gll La Wrisris, ni \textbf{s}-mat \textbf{tēmē} \textit{attua} soqsoq, \\ 
         but Wrisris \textsc{3sg} \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{1}-die \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{2} long.ago \textsc{ints} \\
         
    \gll Wrisris ni qra mat wor ti. \\
        Wrisris \textsc{3sg} \textsc{recpst\textsubscript{1}} die just \textsc{recpst\textsubscript{2}}  \\
    \glt ‘[Our god] Wrisris \textit{didn’t die} a very long time ago, he died just recently.’  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003197#S36}{\{\texttt{⚓}3197\#S36\}}
\z 

\noindent In \REF{ex:dor:wrisris}, the negation formally surrounds the verb \textit{mat} ‘die’, even though its semantic scope is really the time adjunct \textit{attua} ‘a long time ago’ – in a way similar to its English translation.

Because the negation is only marked on the predicate head, sentence \REF{ex:dor:plant} would be ambiguous between three readings (a, b, c). Only prosody can here be used as a clue to identify the scope of negation.

\ea\label{ex:dor:plant} 
    \gll  O tdun sa \textbf{so}-vsōg \textbf{tēmē} o wiag ne\={n}.\\ 
         \textsc{art} person this \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{1}-plant \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{2} \textsc{art} yam that \\
    \glt (lit. ‘This person here didn’t plant those yams.’)\\
    \ea ‘It was \textit{not this man} who planted those yams.’ \\
    \ex ‘This man \textit{did not plant} those yams (he bought them).’\\ 
    \ex ‘This man didn’t plant \textit{those yams} (he planted these other ones).’
    \z 
\z 

That said, the scope of negation is sometimes specified using a strategy, namely topicalisation by left-dislocation. Thus, Dorig commonly has complex predicates that involve more than two lexemes – either a serial verb \{V+V\}, or a verb and its modifier \{V+Adjective\}, \{V+Postverb\}; such complex predicates invariably share the same \textsc{tamp} marking. If~that marking is negative, it~has scope over the whole predicate: see the examples \REF{ex:dor:follow1}, \REF{ex:dor:statneg2}, \REF{ex:dor:baby}, \REF{ex:dor:tvilag}, \REF{ex:dor:worry}. In a sentence like \REF{ex:dor:correctly}, the negation is thus shared by the action verb \textit{daw} ‘do’ and the postverb \textit{tavul} ‘well, correctly’:

\ea\label{ex:dor:correctly} 
    \gll  Na \textbf{s}-daw tavul \textbf{tēmē}.\\ 
          \textsc{1sg} \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{1}}-do well \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}}\textbf{\textsubscript{2}} \\
    \glt ‘I’m not doing it correctly.’ 
\z 

Dorig can sometimes break up these complex predicates, and distribute them across two separate clauses – one being topicalised, the second under focus. In~such cases, each predicate head recovers its own autonomous \textsc{tamp} marking. Thus compare \REF{ex:dor:correctly} with its biclausal variant \REF{ex:dor:notcorrect}:\footnote{Because \textit{tavul} is a postverb (‘well, properly’), it cannot head a predicate; its clausal equivalent is the adjective \textit{wē} (‘good, proper’).}

\ea\label{ex:dor:notcorrect} 
    \gll  Na t-daw t’ sa, \textbf{va}-wē \textbf{tēmē}!\\ 
          \textsc{1sg} \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{1}}-do \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{top} \textbf{\textsc{stat}}-good \textbf{\textsc{neg.ind}} \\
    \glt ‘[The way] I’m doing it, that’s not correct!’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0002306#S67}{\{\texttt{⚓}2306\#S67\}}
\z 

\noindent Breaking apart a complex predicate may be seen as a way to specify the exact scope of the negation.


\subsection{Negative polarity items}\label{sec:dor:4.2}

So-called \textit{negative polarity items} \citep{Baker1970}, or \textit{scale reversal items} \citep[34]{Haspelmath1997}, are words – such as English \textit{any} or \textit{ever} – that occur typically in negative contexts, but are also found in other forms of non-assertive sentences, such as questions, hypotheses, generic statements, etc. Dorig does not have such morphemes.

For example, the generic noun \textit{(o)~tdun} ‘person’ combines with a negation to yield the equivalent of ‘nobody’ as in \REF{ex:dor:desert} or \REF{ex:dor:steal}; but it is also found in affirmative statements, as in \REF{ex:dor:potential} or \REF{ex:dor:major}. The same would be true of the inanimate \textit{(o)~sa(v)} ‘what/​anything’ – see \REF{ex:dor:nothing}.

\subsection{Marking of NPs in the scope of negation}\label{sec:dor:4.3}

Dorig has the following noun determiners \citep{François2007}:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textit{i} – ‘personal article’, reserved to human nouns with high individuation such as proper names [\textrm{${\rightarrow}$}~ex. \REF{ex:dor:underworld}] or kin terms [\textrm{${\rightarrow}$}~(\ref{ex:dor:married}--\ref{ex:dor:notyetmarried}), \REF{ex:dor:nochild}, \REF{ex:dor:ghost}] 
\item
\textit{na} – ‘possessive article’ for common nouns (i.e. non-human, or human with low individuation) that are inalienably possessed (suffixed) [\textrm{${\rightarrow}$}~\REF{ex:dor:tavul}, \REF{ex:dor:koro}, \REF{ex:dor:ache}]
\item 
\textit{o} – ‘common article’ for common nouns that are unbound: either alienably possessed as in \REF{ex:dor:canoe}, or simply unpossessed as in \REF{ex:dor:fire}, \REF{ex:dor:baby}, \REF{ex:dor:potential}.
\item 
\textit{tuar} – ‘indefinite article’ for all nouns, as in \REF{ex:dor:sorcerer}, \REF{ex:dor:steal}.
\end{itemize}

The function of these articles is mostly syntactic, that of a determiner: it’s a D in a~DP. Crucially, the first three articles are underspecified with respect to the features [\textrm{${\pm}$}definite] or [\textrm{${\pm}$}referential]: depending on the context, they may refer to an indefinite (‘a’, ‘some’) or a definite article (‘the’), to a specific entity or a generic one. This explains why the same articles are compatible both with positive and negative clauses, whether they are to be interpreted as referential or not. A~noun marked by one of these determiners will be ambiguous in its interpretation. The~same sequence \textit{o~masa} ‘(a/the)~knife’ is thus found in positive \REF{ex:dor:masa1} or negative \REF{ex:dor:masa2} statements alike:\footnote{While Dorig here behaves like its immediate neighbours, it contrasts with several languages of Vanuatu that employ different NP articles in positive vs. negative sentences. Thus Hiw (\mbox{Torres~Is.}) contrasts two indefinite articles, one [+ref] and one [-ref] \citep{François2016b}; further south, Araki also forces the use of \textit{partitive} determiners in irrealis and/or negative clauses (\citealt[54--67]{François2002}).}

\ea\label{ex:dor:masa}
    \ea\label{ex:dor:masa1}
        \gll  Na m-tek \textbf{o} \textbf{masa} allon.\\ 
              \textsc{1sg} \textsc{prf-}see \textsc{art} knife inside\\
        \glt [\textrm{${\pm}$}def] [+ref] \quad ‘I saw a/the knife inside.’ 
    \ex\label{ex:dor:masa2}
        \gll  Na s-tek tēmē \textbf{o} \textbf{masa} allon.\\
              \textsc{1sg} \textsc{neg.r\textsubscript{1}}-see \textsc{neg.r\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{art} knife inside\\
        \glt [-def] [-ref] \quad ‘I didn’t see any knife inside.’ 
    \z
\z 

The default reading of \textit{o~masa} in \REF{ex:dor:masa2} is non-referential (English \textit{any knife}); but the presence of another modifier, like a possessor \REF{ex:dor:knife} or a demonstrative, can override this interpretation by forcing a [+definite] reading:

\ea\label{ex:dor:knife} 
    \gll  Na s-tek tēmē \textbf{namo-\={n}} \textbf{o} \textbf{masa} allon.\\ 
          \textsc{1sg} \textsc{neg.r\textsubscript{1}}-see \textsc{neg.r\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{poss-}\textsc{2sg} \textsc{art} knife inside\\
    \glt [+def] [+ref] \quad ‘I didn’t see your knife inside.’
\z 

In sum, noun phrases bear the same determiners in positive and negative contexts. In this respect, the Dorig system shows perfect symmetry across polarities.%\footnote{Some languages of northern Vanuatu differ from Dorig in this respect, as they show different noun determiners depending on the clause’s polarity: see \citet[62–69]{François2002} for the language Araki (Santo), or \citet{François2016b} for Hiw (Torres~Is.).}

\subsection{Reinforcing negation}\label{sec:dor:4.4}

In order to reinforce its negative statements, Dorig uses an auxiliary \textit{tē} ‘Negation intensifier’ (\textsc{ints.neg}), of unknown origin.\footnote{The form \textit{tē} [tɪ] is unrelated with the \textit{te} [tɛ] we have seen as a formative in several negative morphemes [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.3}].} The reason it can be analysed as a (verb-like) auxiliary is that it bears the \textsc{tamp} marking instead of the lexical verb, which follows it immediately.

The ordinary negation \REF{ex:dor:intens1} can be compared with the intensified negation \REF{ex:dor:intens2}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:intens}
    \ea\label{ex:dor:intens1}
        \gll  Ni \textbf{s}-vit \textbf{tēmē} o sav.\\ 
             \textsc{3sg} \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{1}-say \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{2} \textsc{art} thing\\
        \glt ‘He didn’t say anything.’
    \ex\label{ex:dor:intens2}
        \gll  Ni \textbf{so}-\textbf{\textit{tē}} vit \textbf{tēmē} o sav, ni so mōl.\\
              \textsc{3sg} \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{1}-\textsc{ints.neg} say \textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{2} \textsc{art} thing \textsc{3sg} \textsc{seq} return\\
        \glt ‘He didn’t \textbf{even} say anything, and left.’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0002306#S26}{\{\texttt{⚓}2306\#S26\}}
    \z
\z 


This auxiliary is also attested with the perfect \textit{m(e)-}: 

\ea\label{ex:dor:sleep} 
    \gll  Tōlnēr so \={n}or, i rār \textbf{m-\textit{tē}} \={n}or tavul \textbf{tēmē}.\\ 
          \textsc{3tri} \textsc{seq} sleep \textsc{pers} \textsc{3du} \textsc{prf}-\textsc{ints.neg} sleep well \textsc{neg.ind}\\
    \glt ‘The three of them went to sleep, but the two (brothers) didn’t manage to sleep \textbf{at} \textbf{all}.’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0003107#S20}{\{\texttt{⚓}3107\#S20\}}
\z 

This sequence \textit{m-tē… tēmē} may well be the origin of the homophonous negation we saw in hypothetical sentences [\sectref{sec:dor:2.4}].

\subsection{Negation in complex clauses: the case of the apprehensive}\label{sec:dor:4.5}

Dorig does not have any coordinator that would be specialised for negation, such as Latin \textit{neque}, or English \textit{neither}… \textit{nor}. As for subordination, special mention must be made of negative purposives, or rather their pragmatic equivalent. 

When a clause P is meant to avoid the realisation of an event Q, many languages – like English – employ a negation in the subordinate clause, in a pattern \{P,~\textit{so that not~}Q\} – e.g. \textit{Stand firm, so you don’t fall}. In Vanuatu languages, such meanings are usually expressed by a special construction called “apprehensive” – of the type \{P,~\textit{lest~}Q\}. 

In Dorig, the apprehensive linker is a form \textit{tekor}, followed by a positive irrealis:

\ea\label{ex:dor:steal} 
    \gll   Na t-\={n}or gor ti \textbf{\textit{tekor}} tuar tdun s-bāl.\\ 
          \textsc{1sg} \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{1}}-sleep over \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{appr} \textsc{indf} person \textsc{irr}-steal\\
    \glt ‘I sleep on it [my money] so nobody can steal it.’ \\(lit. ‘I sleep on it \textit{lest} anyone steals it’)  {\small [Drg.d05.Naef:14]}
\z 

\noindent This apprehensive particle \textit{tekor} is grammaticalised from a verb \textit{tekgor} [tɛkɔr] ‘beware, look out’ – etymologically ‘watch (\textit{tek}) over (\textit{gor})’. So a sentence like \REF{ex:dor:steal} arguably involves three underlying predicates: “I~sleep on it, [\textit{bewaring}] someone might steal it”.

Even though \textit{tekor} appears to serve as a subordinator in \REF{ex:dor:steal}, the very same word also routinely surfaces sentence-initially as in \REF{ex:dor:ghost}, as a morpheme coding for apprehensive modality:

\ea\label{ex:dor:ghost} 
    \gll   Ar te vanvan vga te vak gēn ne\={n} sa \\
     \textsc{imp.2nsg} \textsc{proh\textsubscript{1}} go{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} beyond \textsc{proh\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{dir} \textsc{foc} \textsc{dist} \textsc{top} \\

    \gll gēn ka sowse van te aē.  \\
     \textsc{1incl.pl} \textsc{sub} \textsc{ndum\textsubscript{1}} go \textsc{ndum\textsubscript{2}} \textsc{anaph} \\

    \gll \textbf{Tekor} kmur s-van wōn i tbi-kmur.\\  
    \textsc{appr} \textsc{2du} \textsc{irr-}go find \textsc{pers} ancestor-\textsc{2du} \\
    \glt ‘Don’t you two walk beyond the point over there, where we haven’t been yet! \textit{You might come across} [the ghost of] your ancestor.’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0007437#S21}{\{\texttt{⚓}7437\#S21\}}
\z 

Even if \textit{tekor} does not, strictly speaking, encode syntactic subordination, it~does encode a form of pragmatic dependency between the two sentences. \mbox{Indeed}, the main function of the apprehensive modality is to present a scenario as undesirable (‘you might meet an evil ghost’); this utterance, in turn, serves as a justification for an imperative or a prohibitive, whether the latter is made explicit or not.\footnote{I have developed this argument about the apprehensive of Mwotlap (\citealt[301--312]{François2003}; \citealt{FrançoisForthcomingb}); see also \citet[679--680]{Malau2016} for Vurës. For a typology of apprehensives, see \citet{VuillermentEtAlforthcomingA}.} As~a corollary, the apprehensive is sometimes used -- e.g. in \REF{ex:dor:bones} -- as a polite or indirect variant of a prohibitive [\sectref{sec:dor:2.2.2}]:

\ea\label{ex:dor:bones}
    \gll \textbf{Tekor} nēk so-dlōm o sri-n!\\
         \textsc{appr} \textsc{2sg} \textsc{irr}-swallow \textsc{art} bone-\textsc{3sg}\\
    \glt‘[Make sure you] \textit{don’t swallow} the bones!’ \quad {\small [Drg.q.Rerem.04]}
\z

Yet crucially for our purposes, it bears highlighting that apprehensive modality does not, in fact, pertain to negation. Such constructions are relevant to a discussion of negative polarity only insofar as they constitute a pragmatic equivalent of constructions which, in~other languages, might involve negative morphology (cf. ‘so \textit{nobody} can steal it’); yet the apprehensive does not, strictly speaking, belong to the set of negative constructions.

\subsection{Miscellaneous aspects of negation}\label{sec:dor:4.6} 
\subsubsection{Contrastive negation}\label{sec:dor:4.6.1}

In contrastive systems of the form \{\textit{not P (but)~Q}\}, some languages employ a \mbox{special} conjunction for ‘but’ (e.g. German \textit{sondern}, Spanish \textit{sino}). In such cases, illustrated in \REF{ex:dor:steam}, Dorig simply uses parataxis:

\ea
    \label{ex:dor:steam}
    \gll Bek, o \textit{gasi} \textit{āv} \textbf{tēmē}, o sawi o naw wor.\\
         \textsc{negex} \textsc{art} smoke fire \textsc{neg.ind} \textsc{art} steam \textsc{art} salt.water just\\
    \glt ‘No, that’s not smoke, that’s just steam!’ \quad {\small [Drg.d10.Bekem:26]}
\z

\subsubsection{Non-negative uses of negatives}\label{sec:dor:4.6.2}

Clausal negation is always semantically negative or prohibitive. One case, though, deserves mention, where a formally negative morpheme is routinely assigned a meaning that cannot be reduced to negation strictly speaking.

We saw in \sectref{sec:dor:3.1} how the negative existential \textit{bek} {\textasciitilde} \textit{obek} is commonly used as a negative declarative reply (‘No!’). The same negation can also commonly take a broader meaning, that of politely contradicting the relevance of the addressee’s utterance, even when it was not a yes/no question. This is shown in the dialogue \REF{ex:dor:invain}:

\ea\label{ex:dor:invain}
    \begin{xlist}
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:dor:invainA}
        \gll  Nēk t-daksa ti?\\ 
              \textsc{2sg} \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{1}}-do.what \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{2}}\\ 
        \glt ‘What are you doing?
    \exi{B: }\label{ex:dor:invainB}
        \gll  \textbf{Bek}, na m-mōl kēl ma ti na t-rev namu-k o ak ti, la na t-revrev mtēl ti.\\ 
               \textsc{negex} \textsc{1sg} \textsc{prf}-return back hither \textsc{coord} \textsc{1sg} \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{1}}-tow \textsc{poss}-\textsc{1sg} \textsc{art} canoe \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{2}} but \textsc{1sg} \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{1}}-tow{\textasciitilde}\textsc{red} in.vain \textsc{ipfv\textsubscript{2}}\\
        \glt \textbf{\textit{No}} (=\textit{nothing in particular, don’t worry)}. Just that I was trying to tow my boat on my~way back home, and I was unable to do it!’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0002306#S18}{\{\texttt{⚓}2306\#S18\}}
    \end{xlist} 
\z 

\noindent This polite use of sentential negation is common in the daily phraseology of Vanuatu languages (\citealt[221]{François2011}).

\subsubsection{Diachronic notes}\label{sec:dor:5} 
In the absence of ancient documents in Dorig, the language’s history must be reconstructed based on language comparison with its immediate neighbours. In that perspective, the appendix provides a comprehensive list (so far unpublished) of negative morphemes in all 17 Torres–Banks languages [see the map in \sectref{sec:dor:1.1}], based on my firsthand data.

While a full comparison would go beyond the purpose of the present study, I~will at least mention here the main paths of change that can shed light on the origin of Dorig negative morphemes.

\subsubsubsection{Jespersen’s cycle in the Banks islands’ languages}\label{sec:dor:5.1}

The comparison of north Vanuatu languages shows that standard negation was initially (i.e.~at~the level of \textsc{Ptb} ‘Proto Torres–Banks’) a simple proclitic *\textit{ate=}. Mota, a~conservative language spoken north of Dorig, has kept that simple \mbox{system}: \textbf{\textit{ate}}\textit{~aras} <\textsc{neg}~far> ‘It’s~not far’. Out of the 15 languages of the Banks islands, twelve later added a postverbal element, resulting in discontinuous markers. The data in \REF{ex:dor:jespersen} is a list (in~IPA) of Realis negations in a few Banks languages. These are all semantically and structurally equivalent to Dorig \textit{s(o)-… tēmē}, including their distribution across two slots \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{1}… \textsc{tamp}\textsubscript{2} [see~\sectref{sec:dor:1.2}]:

\eabox{\label{ex:dor:jespersen} 
    \begin{tabular}{ll}
    Lehali & /tɛt… \textbf{tæ}/ \\
    Löyöp &  /tɛ… \textbf{ʧɛ}/ \\
    Mwotlap, Volow &  /ɛt-… \textbf{tɛ}/ \\
    Lemerig &  /(e)… \textbf{ʔæ}/ \\
    Vera’a &  /(ɪʔ)… rɔs/ \\
    Nume &   /veta… \textit{mi}/ \\
    Dorig &  /s(ɔ)-… tɪmɪ/ \\
    Koro &  /t-… wʊs-\textit{mɪ}/ \\
    Olrat &  /tɛ… wʊs/ \\
    Lakon &  /tɪ… avʊh/ \\
    Mwerlap &  /ti… \textbf{tɛa}/ \\
    \end{tabular}
} 

In five of the languages cited in \REF{ex:dor:jespersen}, the element in bold reflects a proto-form *\textit{tea}. This~word *\textit{tea} goes back to a former numeral ‘one’, found for example in the form *\textit{lavea-tea} ‘six’, literally ‘[five]-one’ (\citealt[496]{François2005a}). Some modern languages, like Mwotlap in \REF{ex:dor:par}, still reflect that form *\textit{tea} as an indefinite or partitive (‘some’): 

\ea\label{ex:dor:par} 
    \gll  Kimi ne-myōs ne-gengen \textbf{\textit{te}} en, ami lep.\\ 
          \textsc{2pl} \textsc{stat-}want \textsc{art}-food \textsc{par} \textsc{top} \textsc{2pl.imp} take\\
    \glt ‘[If] you want \textit{some}/\textit{any} food, help yourselves.’ \quad  \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0007413#S250}{\{\texttt{⚓}7413\#S250\}}
\z 

That partitive grammaticalised into the second element of a double negation (‘not … even a little’ → ‘not’): see \REF{ex:dor:jespersen2} for Mwotlap (\citealt[317]{François2003}).

\ea\label{ex:dor:jespersen2} 
    \gll  Imam \textbf{et}-ēglal \textbf{te}. \\ 
          father \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{1}}-know \textbf{\textsc{neg.r}\textsubscript{2}}\\
    \glt ‘Father doesn’t know.’ \quad \href{https://doi.org/10.24397/pangloss-0007413#S27}{\{\texttt{⚓}7413\#S27\}}
\z 

Several languages of north Vanuatu went through the same grammaticalisation path, whereby a former partitive (‘some, any’ < *\textit{tea} ‘one’) became an obligatory component of a bipartite negation.\footnote{Further south on Ambae island (Vanuatu), \citet[260]{Hyslop2001} describes the double negation \textit{hi} … \textit{tea} in Lolovoli. For an overview of negation in several languages of Vanuatu, with an emphasis on the language Lewo, see \citet[89]{Early1994}.} 
This is an instance of Jespersen’s \mbox{Cycle}.\footnote{About Jespersen’s cycle, see \citet{Auwera2009} for a general account; \citet{VossenAuwera2014} for a comparison of Austronesian languages. For case studies dedicated to other Oceanic languages, see \citet{Barbour2015}, \citet{RoversiNaess2019}.} In some languages, the cycle has even reached its ultimate consequence – i.e. the loss of the first component of negation. Thus in~\REF{ex:dor:jesp}, the negative meaning ends up being carried by the reflex of *\textit{tea} on its own:

\ea\label{ex:dor:jesp} Mwotlap \quad \citep[318]{François2003}\\ 
    \gll Ino \textbf{te}, ikē!\\
        \textsc{1sg}.\textsc{pred} \textbf{\textsc{neg}} \textsc{3sg}.\textsc{pred}\\
    \glt ‘It’s not me, it’s him.’ 
\z 

With the form \textit{te} /tɛ/ found in neighbouring languages, the reader will have recognised the postverbal element \textit{te} we had observed earlier in various negative constructions of Dorig: e.g. the nondumitive \textit{sowse…} \textbf{\textit{te}} [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.7}], the prohibitive \textit{(v)te…} \textbf{\textit{te}} or its variant \textit{tog…} \textbf{\textit{te}} [\sectref{sec:dor:2.2.2}], the counterfactual protatic \textit{vit (v)te…} \textbf{\textit{te}} [\sectref{sec:dor:2.4}].
%\aut{If you wish, you can join the two paragraphs here. In that case, please add the linker That said, : vte... te [§2.4]. That said, while a historical demonstration...}
That said, while a historical demonstration can show that \textit{te} has its \mbox{ultimate} origin in a former quantifier *\textit{tea}, this is no longer perceptible to Dorig speakers: in synchrony, the only function that could be assigned to \textit{te} is a \mbox{general} sense of “negation”. Strictly speaking, \textit{te} is not even a full-fledged \textit{morpheme}, since it never occurs on its own: it is no more than a formative in several compound morphemes, which are semantically non-compositional.


\subsubsubsection{Morpheme coalescence as the source of Dorig negators}\label{sec:dor:5.2}

Among the many morphological elements associated with negation in Dorig, \mbox{several} result from processes of coalescence, or contraction, between two formerly separate morphemes.

Thus in the negative potential \textit{(v)te …} \textbf{\textit{late}} [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.6}], the second element \mbox{arguably} results from a contraction of negative \textit{te} with the former postverb *\textit{la} or \textit{lala} coding for the potential: *\textit{la} + \textit{te} \textrm{${\rightarrow}$}~\textit{late}. This reduplicated form \textit{lala} is itself cognate with a postverbal morpheme *lai found in some Banks languages, to encode \mbox{Potential} modality, of the form \textit{lai} or \textit{le} – see the forms of the Negative potential in \tabref{tab:dor:clausal} of the appendix.

The other common marker of negation, namely \textbf{\textit{tēmē}}, can also be explained if we follow the path of Jespersen’s cycle in north Vanuatu, and pursue our cross-linguistic comparison. Among the 12 Banks languages that have reinforced their initial negation with a second element, \REF{ex:dor:jespersen2} showed not only reflexes of *tea, but also of other strengtheners: /rɔs/; /wʊs {\textasciitilde} avʊh/; /mi {\textasciitilde} mɪ/, all of unknown etymology. 

My proposal is that the Dorig negation \textit{tēmē} /tɪmɪ/ results from the contraction of \textit{te} /tɛ/ (marker of negation < quantifier *tea) and of a second form *\textit{mē} /mɪ/. The latter is not a morpheme in modern Dorig, but is attested (as /mi/ or /mɪ/) as a negative formative in Dorig’s two neighbours Nume and Koro. Considering the contrast between the negations in \textit{…te} and those in \textit{…tēmē} (see \tabref{tab:dor:tamp} in \sectref{sec:dor:2.1.1}), it appears that \{\textit{te}+*\textit{mē}\} would combine only in declarative utterances (as opposed to prohibitives), and under so-called “indicative” modality – covering realis contexts (past, present) as well as the rare declarative future [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.6}]. (Note however that the nondumitive, which is semantically realis or indicative, shows the unexpected form \textit{te} instead of expected \textit{tēmē}.)

The hypothesis of a coalescence \{\textit{te}+*\textit{mē}\} is confirmed if we compare Dorig with its close neighbour Koro (François, field notes). In those contexts where Dorig would have \textit{te}, Koro has a form \textit{wōs} /wʊs/ (which it shares with Olrat /wʊs/ and Lakon /avʊh/); whereas Dorig \textit{tēmē} systematically corresponds in Koro to an augmented negation of the form \textit{wōsmē}. The~morphomic parallelism\footnote{For the notion of \textit{morphomic pattern}, see \citet{Aronoff1994}, \citet{Maiden2005}.} between the two languages is striking: see \tabref{tab:dor:koro}.

In sum, the history of negative morphemes in Dorig instantiates the Jespersen cycle in~three steps:

\begin{itemize}
    \item In Pre-Dorig, a quantifier *tea (‘one, some’) was grammaticalised into the 2\textsuperscript{nd}~element of negation in several bipartite combinations (*X… tea > \textit{X… te}), to the point of becoming the main marker of negation.
    \item While some bipartite combinations in Dorig kept the bare form \textit{te} /tɛ/, other constructions, found in declarative utterances, reinforced that \mbox{second} element with a suffix *\textit{mē}, yielding an augmented negation \textit{tēmē} (\mbox{parallel} to the augmented negation \textit{wōsmē} of neighbouring Koro).
    \item In some contexts – especially, non-verbal predicates [\sectref{sec:dor:2.3}] – the augmented form \textit{tēmē} now functions as the sole exponent of negation: this constitutes the final stage of a Jespersen cycle.
\end{itemize}

The 17 Oceanic languages of the Torres–Banks linkage of northern Vanuatu vary considerably in the forms of their words, yet share a number of structural and typo\-logi\-cal features in the internal organisation of their grammars \citep{François2011}. This is true for the semantic domain of negation.

Thus, all Torres–Banks languages draw a formal contrast between (\textit{a})~a set of clausal negators carried by the predicate phrase (\textsc{Drg}~\textit{s(o)-… tēmē}, etc.), and (\textit{b})~a “Negative existential” word (\textsc{Drg}~\textit{obek}), which is itself a predicate of its own. That \textsc{negex} word [\sectref{sec:dor:2.3.3}] is used in existential, locative and possessive clauses, and also forms negative replies (“No !”).


In most Torres–Banks languages, standard negation takes the form of bipartite morphemes, resulting historically from a Jespersen Cycle. Those morphemes are portmanteau forms that combine polarity with semantic features of Tense, Aspect, Mood: this results in a \textsc{tamp} system, with often non-compositional morphemes [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1}]. A~widespread configuration in the region is the lack of one-to-one correspondence between positive and negative \textsc{tamp} morphemes, either in form or in meaning – an asymmetry known as “A/Cat” in typological work (\citealt{Miestamo2005,Miestamo2013-wals114}).

Compared to its neighbours, Dorig is original in using irrealis modality in semantically \mbox{realis} contexts, at least etymologically [\sectref{sec:dor:2.1.5}]. Also unique to this language is the contrast between \textit{te} and \textit{tēmē} negations, bearing strong links with clausal modality (declarative vs. imperative; “indicative” vs. “subjunctive”), and only paralleled by its neighbour Koro [\sectref{sec:dor:5.2}]. Equally noteworthy is the general insensitiveness of noun phrases and determiners to the polarity of the clause [\sectref{sec:dor:4.3}].

In a sense, Dorig constitutes an extreme case: that of a language in which the complexities of negative constructions are all concentrated in the predicate phrase, yet virtually absent from the rest of the clause.

\section*{Appendix: Negation in the Torres–Banks languages}\label{sec:dor:6}




While the present study was dedicated to negative constructions in the Dorig language, the very same linguistic categories can be consistently observed across all seventeen languages of the Torres and Banks Islands. In line with a very common configuration in the region \citep{François2011}, this near-perfect isomorphism of structures goes along with an intense diversity of phonological forms.
