\title{Negation in Teotitlán del Valle Zapotec}
\abstract{Negation in Zapotec (Otomanguean) languages is expressed by a variety of constructions that have not yet been broadly explored, perhaps due to the phonological and morphosyntactic complexity of the several dialects, as well as their extensive variation. In this chapter, I will discuss several negative constructions in Teotitlán del Valle Zapotec, a variety spoken in the Central Valley of Oaxaca, Mexico. I will show that in this language, as in other Zapotec languages, there are different negative constructions and negators that convey specific functions: standard negation; \textit{not yet expressions}; negation in existential clauses; focus negation; \textit{not even} (or emphatic) negative constructions, among others. As I  demonstrate, most of the negative markers in this language are clitics and some of the negative constructions exhibit structural and paradigmatic asymmetries \citep{Miestamo2005, Miestamo2007}.
\keywords{negation, prohibitives, clitics, Zapotec, indefinite pronouns}

\section{The language}\label{sec:zap:1}
In this chapter, I discuss various negative constructions that occur in Teotitlán del Valle Zapotec (TdVZ)
(ISO: \href{https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/zap}{zap}, Glottocode: \href{https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/teot1238}{teot1238}).\footnote{The data presented here comes from the author, who is a native speaker of Teotitlán Zapotec and has been doing research on this language since 2011. Also, a corpus of at least 10 hours of transcribed and translated material have been used in addition to direct elicitation to explore the phenomenon discussed here.}
This language is spoken by approximately five thousand speakers in Teotitlán del Valle, a town located in the Valley of Oaxaca, Mexico. It is part of the Otomanguean family, and within the Zapotec family, it is considered to belong to the central group \citep{Smith-Stark2007}. For my discussion, the relevant features of TdVZ are the following.

TdVZ has  VSO word order, and it follows most of the predicted correlations for VO languages \citep{Dryer1997sixway}. The verb takes an obligatory TAM prefix, and it can optionally be followed by a comitative suffix or an affective morpheme. After these, free function word (FFW) adverbials may appear followed by adverbial enclitics. Also, between the TAM prefix and the verb, the grammaticalized verb roots \textit{æ} ‘go’\footnote{/æ/ alternates with /e/ in non-accented syllables (\citealt{UchiharaGutiérrez2020}).}  or \textit{ǣ̰d} ‘come’ may appear, glossed as \textsc{and} and \textsc{ven}, respectively, in the verb template in \figref{fig:zap:1}.
These indicate the movement of the subject to perform the action. In addition, if the subject is pronominalized, the clitic form attaches to the verb complex after the adverb enclitic(s) if any. The template in \figref{fig:zap:1} summarizes the structure of the verb, and an illustrative example is given in \REF{ex:zap:1}. \medskip

    \textbf{\textsc{tam}}{}-(\textsc{ven}/\textsc{and}{}-)\textbf{\textsc{verb.stem}}(-\textsc{com})(-\textsc{afft}) (\textsc{ffw} \textsc{adverb}(\textsc{s}))(=\textsc{adverb}(\textsc{s}))(=\textsc{subj})
    }
\caption{Morphosyntactic structure of TdVZ verb} 
\label{fig:zap:1}

\ea\label{ex:zap:1} 
    \glll Re.ˈkī’.zá.dān ˈxhǔb.\\ 
          r-e-kī'=zá=dān xhǔb\\
          \textsc{hab}{}-go-toast=also=\textsc{3pl.f} corn\\
    \glt ‘They also go (and/to) toast the corn.’
\z 

The preverbal position, on the other hand, contains at least three positions that may be filled by a subordinator, a focused noun phrase, and by the clausal negator, as in \REF{ex:zap:2}. If these cooccur, they must take the order shown below (i.e., subordinator – focused element – negation).

\ea\label{ex:zap:2} 
    \glll Kom ˈJwáyn kēd.rá.ˈzuyn.di, ˈtyéz.rú ˈzyó.pún.\\ 
         kom Jwáyn kēd=ra-zuny=di tyéz=rú z´-yōp=ūn \\
         because Juan \textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-arrive=\textsc{neg} good=more \textsc{prog}{}-come:\textsc{1pl}=\textsc{1pl.excl} \\
    \glt ‘Because Juan is/was not arriving, (we decided that) it was better for us to come.’
\z 

In TdVZ, vowels present a three-way distinction in phonation, that is, modal vowels /a, æ, i, ɨ, u, o/ contrast with creaky /a̰, æ̰, ḭ, ɨ̰, ṵ, o̰/ and glottalized /aʔ, æʔ, iʔ, ɨʔ, oʔ, uʔ/ vowels. Creaky vowels are represented in this chapter with the IPA symbol  ( ̰) while glottalization is represented with an apostrophe (’). Also, the language has five contrastive tones (\citealt{UchiharaGutiérrez2019}), which are represented here as follows: high (´), mid ( \={}), rising ( \v{}), falling (\^{}), while low tone is unmarked. In addition, there are several tonal processes, but three of them are important to mention here since they occur in most of the examples shown in this paper.

Firstly, High Tone Sandhi (HTS)\footnote{The way in which the High Tone Sandhi (HTS) affects the following syllable depends on the lexical tone of this element, and whether this element is prominent or non-prominent. In the first case, the high sandhi affects low and mid tone, but it does not affect high, falling, or rising tones. On the other hand, the HTS affects the following low or mid tone differently. That is, if the following syllable is prominent and has a low tone, the HTS would result in a falling tone (HTS + a $\rightarrow$ â), but if the following syllable is non-prominent and has a low tone, the HTS would be expressed as a high tone (HTS + a $\rightarrow$ á). Nevertheless, with mid-tone, the HTS always triggers high tone on the following syllable whether this is prominent or non-prominent (HTS + ā $\rightarrow$ á).} of mid tone assigns a high tone to the following syllable within the same phonological utterance, as shown below. Notice that in \REF{ex:zap:3a}, the mid-tone of the verb root assigns a high tone to the pronominal clitic that lexically has a low tone (second line). In \REF{ex:zap:3b}, the mid-tone of the third person formal pronominal clitic assigns a high tone to the beginning syllable of the following noun \textit{bada̰w} ‘baby’, which lexically has a low tone.

\ea\label{ex:zap:3}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:3a}
        \glll  Ri.ˈdxā̰.gán ˈluy. \\ 
              ri-dxā̰g=\textbf{an} luy\\
              \textsc{hab}{}-get.along.with=\textsc{3sg.if} 2\textsc{sg}.\textsc{if} \\
        \glt ‘S/he gets along with you.’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:3b}
        \glll  Ru.ˈyā̰.nān bá.ˈda̰w.\\
             ru-ya̰n=ān \textbf{ba}da̰w\\
              \textsc{hab}{}-feed=\textsc{3sg.f} baby\\
        \glt ‘S/he feeds the baby.’
    \z
\z 

Secondly, potential and counterfactual moods are marked with a prefix and a high or rising tone that typically docks onto the verb root. In the examples below, I show how the high tone that comes with these moods change the lexical tone of the verb root (second line), as in \REF{ex:zap:4a} and \REF{ex:zap:4b}. This high tone can also be anchored to the first element of a compound, as in \REF{ex:zap:4c}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:4}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:4a}
        \glll  ga.ˈzûyn\\ 
              ga´-zuny\\
              \textsc{pot}{}-arrive\\
        \glt ‘will arrive’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:4b}
        \glll  gu.ˈkí’\\ 
            gu´-kī’\\
            \textsc{pot}{}-toast\\
        \glt ‘will toast’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:4c}
        \glll  gu.kwá.ˈdyag\\
        gu´-kwadyag\\
        \textsc{pot}{}-listen\\
        \glt ‘will listen’
    \z
\z 

Thirdly, the completive prefix has an allomorph that triggers a tone change in the verb root (glossed here as \textsc{compl}\textsubscript{2}). This allomorph occurs in relativization, focus constructions, interrogatives, and clausal negation (see \citealt{GutiérrezEtAl2021} for more information on this topic). In \REF{ex:zap:5}, I show an example of a focus construction where this allomorph occurs.

\ea\label{ex:zap:5} 
    \glll ˈBǽ.dēn bá.ˈllṵ̂b lo.ˈnæz.\\ 
          Bǽd=ēn ba´-\textbf{llṵb} low=næz\\
          Pedro=\textsc{foc} \textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-sweep \textsc{rn}.face=road\\
    \glt ‘(It was) Pedro (who) swept the Street.’%HC
\z 

In the following, I describe various negative constructions in TdVZ and define the morphosyntactic status of the negators involved, using the negation questionnaire provided by the editors  \parencitetv{chapters/questionnaire}. Also, I discuss the asymmetries observed, if any. This  situates TdVZ negative constructions into a broader typological perspective.

\section{Clausal negation} \label{sec:zap:2}
\subsection{Standard negation} \label{sec:zap:2.1}

Standard negation (\citealt{Payne1985}, \citealt{Miestamo2005}) in TdVZ is expressed with the markers \textit{kēd=} and \textit{=di}. The proclitic \textit{kēd}= appears pre-verbally while =\textit{di} is attached post-verbally, where adverbial enclitics may occur.\footnote{\textit{Kēd}= and =\textit{di} are clitics since they present most of the phonological and morphosyntactic characteristics of this type of element in TdVZ (i.e., they are non-prominent elements, thus, there is no vowel lengthening even when \textit{kēd}= is followed by a lenis consonant, and =\textit{di} does not have coda; also, both morphemes have level, or non-complex tones: mid and tone respectively). In \sectref{sec:zap:2} I discuss their clitic morphosyntactic behavior.} In \REF{ex:zap:6} and \REF{ex:zap:7}, I show an affirmative clause and its negative counterpart. As noted, no asymmetries are observed in standard negation. The tone change on the prefix in \REF{ex:zap:7} is due to Tone Sandhi (see \sectref{sec:zap:1}).

\ea\label{ex:zap:6} 
    \glll Ru.ˈllṵb ˈJwáyn lo.ˈnæz.\\ 
          ru-llṵb Jwáyn low=næz\\
          \textsc{hab}{}-sweep Juan \textsc{rn}.face=road\\
    \glt ‘Juan sweeps the road / the street.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:7} 
    \glll Kēd.rú.ˈllṵb.di ˈJwáyn lo.ˈnæz.\\ 
          \textbf{kēd}=ru-llṵb=\textbf{di} Jwáyn low=næz\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-sweep=\textsc{neg} Juan \textsc{rn}.face=road\\
    \glt ‘Juan doesn’t sweep the road / the street.’
\z 

The proclitic \textit{kēd=} may be separated from the verbal word (TAM + verb stem) if adverbial clitics\footnote{In closely related varieties, these adverbial clitics have been discussed as second position clitics \citep{Munro2004}.}  modify the negated clause. Also, the enclitic \textit{=di} may be separated from the verb if free function word adverbials occur in the clause, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:8}. Note that =\textit{di} must occur before the NP, or pronominal clitic, arguments.

\ea\label{ex:zap:8} 
    \glll Kēd.zá.bi.ˈdzɨ̂yn ˈga.dyan ˈnna̰y.\\ 
          kēd=\textbf{zá}=bi´-dzɨny \textbf{ga}=di=an ˈnna̰y\\
          \textsc{neg}=also=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-arrive soon=\textsc{neg=3sg.if} yesterday\\
    \glt ‘S/he didn’t arrive early/soon yesterday either.’
\z 

\textit{Kēd=} and \textit{=di} are obligatory in monoclausal declarative sentences. If either \textit{kēd}= or \textit{=di} is omitted, the construction becomes ungrammatical. This is not the case in prohibitives, where the proclitic negator alone forms this negative construction (see \sectref{sec:zap:2.2.1})

Even though there are no asymmetries observed in relation to the form of the construction when the negative markers occur, the relation between negation and the aspectual markers is paradigmatically asymmetric. That is, not all declarative clauses have a negative formal counterpart, as I discuss below. 

\largerpage
TdVZ has six productive TAM prefixes; three of which encode aspect (habitual, completive, progressive) and three encode modality (future, potential, and counterfactual). Any clause containing a verb with an aspectual prefix has a negative formal counterpart, as shown in the contrasted examples (\ref{ex:zap:9}--\ref{ex:zap:11}) below.


\ea\label{ex:zap:9}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:9a}
        \glll  ˈRæ’n ˈnis.\\ 
               r-æ’=an nis\\
              \textsc{hab}{}-drink=\textsc{3sg.if} water \\
        \glt ‘S/he drinks water.’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:9b}
        \glll  Kēd.ˈr\^{æ}’.dyan ˈnis.\\
              kēd=r-æ’=di=an nis\\
               \textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-drink=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if} water\\
        \glt ‘S/he doesn’t drink water.’
    \z
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:10}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:10a}
        \glll  Gwæ’n ˈnis.\\ 
               gu-æ’=an nis\\
             \textsc{compl}{}-drink=\textsc{3sg.if} water \\
        \glt  ‘S/he drank water.’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:10b}
        \glll Kēd.ˈgw\^{æ}’.dyan ˈnis.\\
             kēd=gu´-æ’=di=an nis\\
               \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-drink=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if} water\\

    \z
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:11}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:11a}

        \glt  ‘S/he is drinking water.’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:11b}
        \glll  Kēd.ká.ˈyæ’.dyan ˈnis.\\
              kēd=káy-æ’=di=an nis\\

    \z

However, a clause containing those prefixes that encode modality has certain characteristics in declarative as well as in negative constructions. First, in TdVZ a verb, or clause containing the potential, or the counterfactual prefix generally occurs in subordinated clauses where the negation of the main clause (predicate) involves the negation of the subordinate clause\footnote{A clause with potential or counterfactual prefix in a non-subordinated construction has an optative mood reading, as in \REF{ex:zap:fn:i}, and not a declarative. In fact, the constructions below could be considered fronted complement clauses of an omitted desiderative verb.

\ea\label{ex:zap:fn:i}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:fn:ia}
        \glll ˈg\^{æ}’n ˈnis \\
            g´-æ’=an nis \\
            \textsc{pot}{}-drink=\textsc{3sg.if} water \\
        \glt ‘May s/he drink water.’ \\
    \ex\label{ex:zap:fn:ib}
        \glll ˈny\^{æ}’n ˈnis \\
            ni´-æ’=an nis \\
            \textsc{cntf}{}-drink=\textsc{3sg.if} water \\
        \glt ‘May s/he have drunk water.’\\
    \z
\z

} (see negative transport \sectref{sec:zap:4.6}).

Second, although an indicative (main) clause with a verb prefixed with the future is common, as in \REF{ex:zap:12}, this does not have a formal negative counterpart.\footnote{This TAM marker exhibits several restrictions in TdVZ; for instance, in \textit{Serial Verb Constructions} (SVC), two verbs marked with the \textit{future} are not possible \citep{Gutiérrez2014}. In fact, various of these restrictions hold in other Central Zapotec varieties such as the San Dionisio Ocotepec Zapotec \citep{Broadwell2012} and San Pablo Güilá Zapotec (López Cruz, personal communication).} That is, the negative marker \textit{kēd}= and the future prefix do not coexist, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:12b}. To negate a future event, the potential prefix must occur on the verb as shown in \REF{ex:zap:13}. Therefore, the negative counterpart of a declarative clause with a future prefix is one with a potential prefix.

\ea\label{ex:zap:12}
    \ea[]{\label{ex:zap:12a}
        \glll  ˈZæ’n ˈnis.\\ 
               z-æ’=(a)n nis\\
               \textsc{fut}{}-drink=\textsc{3sg.if} water\\
        \glt  ‘S/he will drink water.’}
    \ex[*]{\label{ex:zap:12b}
        \glll Kēd.ˈzæ’.dyan ˈnis.\\
              kēd=z-æ’=di=an nis\\
              \textbf{\textsc{neg}}=\textbf{\textsc{fut}}{}-drink=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if} water\\
        \glt Intended reading: ‘S/he won’t drink water.’}
    \z
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:13} 
    \glll Kēd.ˈg\^{æ}’.dyan ˈnis.\\ 
          kēd=g´-æ’=di=an nis\\
           \textsc{neg}=\textsc{pot}{}-drink=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if} water\\
    \glt  ‘S/he won’t drink water.’
\z 

Third, although the counterfactual prefix does not occur in main clauses, a negative (non-subordinated) clause that involves the counterfactual prefix is common in TdVZ. In fact, a negative clause with a counterfactual prefix may alternate with a negative clause with a completive prefix. That is, a clause containing the counterfactual, or one with a completive prefix are both potential answers for a polar question whose answer is negative. For instance, if someone asks 'Did s/he drink water?' and the answer is negative, both \REF{ex:zap:10b} above or \REF{ex:zap:14} are potential answers.

\ea\label{ex:zap:14} 
    \glll Kēd.ˈny\^{æ}’.dyan ˈnis.\\ 
           kēd=ni´-æ’=di=an nis\\
            \textsc{neg}=\textsc{cntf{}-}drink=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if} water\\
    \glt   ‘S/he didn’t drink (the) water.’ 
\z

Also, due to the clitic status of the negative marker, it is clear that TdVZ exhibits syntactic negation and not morphological \citep{Dahl1979}. Clitics are elements that attach at the phrase level. They can have different hosts and their position is not consistent. As mentioned, \textit{kēd=} does not always precede verbs since adverbial clitics can interrupt the sequence \textit{kēd}= + verb stem, as in \REF{ex:zap:15}; also, \textit{kēd}= can appear with other lexical categories such as adverbials, as in \REF{ex:zap:16}, or interrogative pronouns, as in \REF{ex:zap:17}. This latter example also demonstrates the same clitic status for \textit{=di}. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:15} 
    \glll Kēd.pká.ru.ˈsyā.dyán ˈli.zan.\\ 
          kēd=\textbf{pká}=ru-syā=di=an liz=an\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textbf{always}=\textsc{hab}{}-clean=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{poss}.house=\textsc{3sg.if}\\
    \glt ‘He never cleans his home.’ / ‘He doesn’t clean his home at all.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:16} 
    \glll Kēd.ˈǽs.tá ˈrṵ.núx.\\ 
          \textbf{kēd}=ǣstá r-ṵn=u=x\\
          \textsc{neg}=later \textsc{hab}{}-cry=\textsc{2sg.if}=\textsc{de}\\
    \glt ‘Don’t be crying later!’ (lit. ‘Don’t later you cry then!’)
\z  

\ea\label{ex:zap:17} 
    \glll Kēd.ˈká.lī.dí ˈbyô’n.\\ 
          \textbf{kēd}=kālī=di b-yô’=un\\
          \textsc{neg}=where=\textsc{neg} \textsc{compl}{}-go:1\textsc{pl}=1\textsc{pl}\\
    \glt ‘We went nowhere.’
\z  

Finally, a particular characteristic of standard negation is that it selects for the allomorph of the completive prefix that causes a tone change on the verb root \citep{GutiérrezEtAl2021}. This tone change can be observed with verb roots that have a lexical low or mid tone, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:18}. The verb \textit{-llṵb} ‘sweep’ lexically has a low tone (second line in the example), and it takes a high falling tone (first line) when occurring with the completive prefix in standard negation. No other negative construction in TdVZ selects for this completive prefix allomorph.

\ea\label{ex:zap:18} 
    \glll Kēd.bá.ˈ\textbf{llṵ̂b}.di ˈJwáyn lo.ˈnæz.\\ 
          kēd=ba´-llṵb=di Jwáyn low=næz\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-sweep=\textsc{neg} Juan \textsc{rn}.face=road\\
    \glt ‘Juan didn’t sweep the road / the street.’
\z 


‘Not yet’ expression is a subtype of standard negation (\sectref{sec:zap:2.1}) since it exhibits structural and functional characteristics of the latter in TdVZ. That is, the negator appears preverbally and \textit{=di} is required post-verbally. Also, it negates a monoclausal predicate, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:19}. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:19} 
    \glll Gád.gútx.ˈnyâ̰.di.dán.\\ 
          \textbf{gád}=g´-utxnyâ̰=\textbf{di}=dán\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{pot}{}-get.married=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3pl.if}\\
    \glt ‘They haven’t gotten married yet.’
\z 

This negative construction, however, differs from the main type of clausal negation in two ways: 1) the proclitic negator is \textit{gád}= instead of \textit{kēd=}, and 2) the negated predicate/verb must be marked with the potential prefix, as shown above. This construction, as noted, is exclusive for events that have not occurred but will potentially do. Therefore, the meaning of the negative marker \textit{gád=} is interpreted as ‘not yet’. 

Interestingly, this type of negative construction does not have an affirmative counterpart. Formally, its negative counterpart would be a declarative clause with the potential marker. However, as mentioned above, this type of predicate gets an optative mood reading, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:20}. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:20} 
    \glll Gútx.ˈnyâ̰.dán.\\ 
          g´-utxnyâ̰=dán\\
          \textsc{pot}{}-get.married=\textsc{3pl.if}\\
    \glt ‘That they get married (I say/suggest).’
\z  

Semantically, the affirmative counterpart of a ‘not yet’ expression may be a clause that expresses a (possible) completive event, as in \REF{ex:zap:21}, or a clause that indicates a previous state/process of an event that is expected to occur, as in \REF{ex:zap:22}. In the first case, the construction requires the adverbial marker \textit{á}= ‘already’ and the completive prefix on the verb. In the second case, a verb with a stative prefix and the adverb \textit{zegāk} ‘still’ must occur in the construction. In both cases, the negative counterpart varies from the affirmative one. Thus, this type of negation shows functional and structural asymmetries. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:21} 
    \glll Á.bitx.ˈnyâ̰.dán. \\ 
          á=bi-utxnyâ̰=dán \\
          already=\textsc{compl}{}-marry=\textsc{3pl.if}\\
    \glt ‘They are married.’ / ‘They have married.’ (lit. ‘Already they married.’)
\z  

\ea\label{ex:zap:22} 
    \glll Ze.ˈgāk zu.ˈgwá’.nǽ.zī ˈsâ’.dán.\\ 
           zegāk zugwā'-nǽ=zī sa'=dán\\
          still \textsc{stat}.dwell-\textsc{com}=without.motives same.type=\textsc{3pl}.\textsc{if}\\
    \glt  ‘They are still living together with each other just because (without being married).’
\z 

A particular characteristic of this negative construction is that it has an alternative form. That is, the marker \textit{gáti} alternates with \textit{gád=} and \textit{=di}, as shown in the examples (\ref{ex:zap:23}--\ref{ex:zap:24}) below. However, note that \textit{gáti} is a prominent element, then, a phonological and morphosyntactic word, while \textit{gád=} and \textit{=di} are clitics. I posit that \textit{gáti} is the conflation of \textit{gád=} and \textit{=di} since both negative constructions shown are in free variation in TdVZ.

\ea\label{ex:zap:23} 
    \glll ˈGá.ti gútx.ˈnyâ̰.dán.\\ 
          gáti g´-utxnyâ̰=dán\\
          \textsc{neg} \textsc{pot}{}-get.married=\textsc{3pl.if}\\
    \glt ‘They haven’t gotten married yet.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:24} 
    \glll Gád.gútx.ˈnyâ̰.didán.\\ 
          gád=g´-utxnyâ̰=di=dán\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{pot}{}-get.married=\textsc{neg=3pl.if}\\
    \glt‘They haven’t gotten married yet.’ 
\z 

\subsection{Negation in non-declaratives}\label{sec:zap:2.2}
\subsubsection{Negation of imperatives}\label{sec:zap:2.2.1}

Imperatives in TdVZ have two forms depending on the number of the addressee. Singular imperatives are formed with the completive prefix on the verb as in \REF{ex:zap:25a}. Plural imperatives are formed with the proclitic \textit{gūl}= and potential prefix on the verb, as in \REF{ex:zap:25b}. Either imperative construction does not allow a subject as in a declarative clause. This, in addition to the tone allomorphy of the completive prefix, helps disambiguate the completive aspect from the singular imperative since in the former the subject NP or enclitic is obligatory, and the imperative prefix does not show an allomorph with a tone change.

\ea\label{ex:zap:25}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:25a}
        \glll Gu.ˈdow ˈgæt! \\ 
               gu-dow gæt\\
               \textsc{imp.sg}{}-eat tortilla\\
        \glt  ‘Eat (the) tortilla (you.\textsc{sg})!’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:25b}
        \glll  Gūl.ˈgâw ˈgæt.\\
              gūl=g-aw gæt\\
               \textsc{imp.pl}=\textsc{imp.pl}{}-eat tortilla\\
        \glt ‘Eat the tortilla (y’all).’
    \z
\z  

In the negation of imperatives (or \textit{prohibitives,} \citealt{AuweraEtAl2013}), a habitual prefix must appear on the verb if the action has started; otherwise, the potential marker is required. Also, prohibitive constructions require the subject on the verb, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:26}. As can be observed, in prohibitives, only the negator \textit{kēd=} is used; if \textit{=di} is added, the negative construction is interpreted as the negation of a declarative statement, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:27}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:26}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:26a}
        \glll   Kē.ˈdrô̰w ˈnd\v{æ}n!\\ 
              \textbf{kēd}=r-aw=u nd\v{æ}n\\
              \textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-eat=\textsc{2sg.if} that\\
        \glt  ‘Do not eat that!’ / ‘Stop eating that!’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:26b}
        \glll  Kēd.ˈgô̰w ˈnd\v{æ}n.\\
             \textbf{kēd}=g´-aw=u nd\v{æ}n\\
              \textsc{neg}=\textsc{pot}{}-eat=\textsc{2sg.if} that\\
        \glt ‘Do not eat that!’
    \z
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:27}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:27a}
        \glll  Kē.ˈdrôw.dyu ˈnd\v{æ}n.\\ 
               \textbf{kēd}=r-aw=\textbf{di}=u nd\v{æ}n\\
               \textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-eat=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{2sg.if} that\\
        \glt  ‘You do not eat that.’ 
        \glt *‘Do not eat that!’ 
    \ex\label{ex:zap:27b}
        \glll  Kēd.ˈgów.dyu ˈnd\v{æ}n.\\
             \textbf{kēd}{}-g´-aw=\textbf{di}=u nd\v{æ}n\\
              \textsc{neg}=\textsc{pot}{}-eat=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{2sg.if} that\\
        \glt  ‘You won’t eat that.’
    \z
\z

\subsubsection{Negation of interrogatives}\label{sec:zap:2.2.2}

In TdVZ, polar questions are formed using the proclitic (\textit{l)á=}, as in \REF{ex:zap:28}. Notice that when this type of construction contains a negative clause in it, this requires both both \textit{kēd=} and \textit{=di}, as in \REF{ex:zap:29}. If any of these markers are omitted, the construction becomes ungrammatical, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:30}. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:28} 
    \glll (L)á.row ˈbǣkw.rǣ ˈdzɨ̂t?\\ 
          (l)á=r-aw bækw=rǣ dzɨt\\
          \textsc{q}=\textsc{hab}{}-eat dog=\textsc{dist.dem} bone\\
    \glt ‘Does that dog eat bone(s)?’ 
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:29} 
    \glll (L)á.kē.ˈdrôw.di ˈbǣkw.rǣ ˈdzɨ̂t?\\ 
          (l)á=\textbf{kēd}=r-aw=\textbf{di} bækw=rǣ dzɨt\\
          \textsc{q}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-eat=\textsc{neg} dog=\textsc{dist.dem} bone\\
    \glt ‘Doesn’t that dog eat bone(s)?’ / ‘That dog doesn’t eat bone(s)?
\z 

\ea[*]{\label{ex:zap:30} 
    \glll (L)á.kēd.rôw ˈbǣkw.rǣ ˈdzɨ̂t?\\ 
          (l)á=\textbf{kēd}=r-aw bækw=rǣ dzɨt\\
          \textsc{q}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-eat dog=\textsc{dist.dem} bone\\
    \glt Intended reading: ‘Doesn’t that dog eat bone(s)?’}
\z 

When a polar question acquires a rhetorical connotation (from a context in which an agreement is expected rather than an affirmative or negative answer), it is introduced by the interrogative marker \textit{tā}=. In these types of interrogatives, \textit{=di} becomes optional, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:31}. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:31} 
    \glll Tā.kédˈrôw(di) ˈbǣkw.rǣ ˈdzɨ̂t?\\ 
          tā=kēd=r-aw(=di) bækw=rǣ dzɨt\\
          \textsc{q}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-eat(=\textsc{neg}) dog=\textsc{dist.dem} bone\\
    \glt ‘That dog doesn’t eat bones?’ / ‘Doesn’t that dog eat bones?’
\z 

Interestingly, in a potential answer to the previous question, \textit{=di} does not appear when the answer is introduced by \textit{nī}=,\footnote{I have not been able to define the functions of this element, it is identical in form to the proclitic \textit{nī}= that occurs in ‘not even one’ constructions (see \sectref{sec:zap:4.4}), but it’s function in this construction suggest that it may be another morpheme (with the same form) that adds the speaker’s ‘negative connotation’ to the phrase.} which I gloss here as 'not even', as in \REF{ex:zap:32}. Nevertheless, if \textit{nī}= doesn’t occur, =\textit{di} is obligatory, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:33}. Thus, polar rhetorical questions allow for the lack of \textit{=di,} and when the potential answer of these is introduced by \textit{ni}=, =\textit{di} cannot occur. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:32} 
    \glll Nī.kē.ˈdrôw ˈbǣkw.rǣ ˈdzɨ̂t.\\ 
          nī=kēd=r-aw bækw=rǣ dzɨt\\
          not.even=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-eat dog=\textsc{dist.dem} bone\\
    \glt ‘That dog doesn’t even eat bones.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:33} 
    \glll Kēd.ˈrôw.di ˈbǣkw.rǣ ˈdzɨ̂t.\\ 
          kēd=r-aw=di bækw=rǣ dzɨt\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-eat\textsc{=neg} dog=\textsc{dist.dem} bone\\
    \glt ‘That dog doesn’t even eat bones.’
\z 

On the other hand, open questions in TdVZ are formed with the various interrogative elements, i.e., \textit{tū}= ‘who’, \textit{xī}= ‘what’, \textit{kālí} ‘where’, \textit{xá}=\footnote{This interrogative was not tested since it is semantically awkward to ask 'How didn’t you do something?'.} ‘how’, \textit{gǔk} ‘when’, and \textit{xíxh} ‘why’, that occur in the preverbal position, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:34}. These clauses are also negated with both \textit{kēd=} and \textit{=di}, as in \REF{ex:zap:35}. If any of these markers are omitted, the construction becomes ungrammatical. Thus, one could conclude that interrogatives are negated just like declaratives in TdVZ.

\ea\label{ex:zap:34} 
    \glll Tū.ri.ˈka̰z ˈtxǽ?\\ 
           tū=ri-ka̰z tx´-æ?\\
           who=\textsc{hab}{}-want \textsc{pot}{}-go\\
    \glt  ‘Who wants to go?'
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:35} 
    \glll Tū.kēd.rí.ˈka̰z.di txǽ?\\ 
           tū=\textbf{kēd}=ri-ka̰z=\textbf{di} tx´-æ?\\
           who=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-want=\textsc{neg} \textsc{pot}{}-go\\
    \glt  ‘Who doesn’t want to go?'
\z 

Interestingly, as with polar rhetorical questions, the interrogative \textit{xíxh} ‘why’, which introduces the (clausal) question with the subordinator \textit{té}=, can omit =\textit{di}, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:36}. The subordinator \textit{té}= is the subordinator for purpose and reason adverbial clauses \citep{Gutiérrez2021}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:36} 
    \glll ˈXíxh té.kēd.ˈgwǽ.(dy)u ˈxkwíly nna’.ˈdxi?\\ 
          xíxh \textbf{té}=kēd=gu´-æ(=\textbf{di)}=u xkwíly nna’dxi\\
          why \textsc{sub}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-go(=\textsc{neg})=\textsc{2sg.if} school today\\
    \glt ‘Why (is the case that) didn’t you go to school today?’\\
    (The speaker sees that a student is at home during school hours)
\z 

The negation of non-declarative clauses differs from standard negation since prohibitives do not contain =\textit{di}, and polar rhetorical questions and the interrogative with \textit{xíxh}= ‘why’ allow for the lack of =\textit{di}. However, polar and content questions (with \textit{tū}= ‘who’, \textit{xī}= ‘what’, \textit{kālí} ‘where’, \textit{gǔk} ‘when’) behave just like standard negation.

\subsection{Negation in stative predications}\label{sec:zap:2.3}
\subsubsection{Nominal and adjectival predication}\label{sec:zap:2.3.1}

In TdVZ adjectival or nominal predicate constructions occur with the copula \textit{nā,} as shown in \REF{ex:zap:37a} and \REF{ex:zap:38a} respectively. As noted, in declarative sentences, the noun or adjective is first in the construction followed by the copula, and the subject takes the last position. The negation of this predication is expressed by the same markers as standard negation, that is, \textit{kēd}= and =\textit{di} cliticize to the copula, as in \REF{ex:zap:37b} and \REF{ex:zap:38b}. Interestingly, the order of the elements varies in the negative counterpart since the copula (together with the negative clitics) must occur first in the construction. Therefore, this type of negation exhibits a constructional asymmetry.

\ea\label{ex:zap:37}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:37a}
        \gll  ˈMwǽs ˈ\textbf{nā} ˈBǽd. \\ 
              teacher \textsc{cop} Pedro\\
        \glt  ‘Pedro is (a) teacher.’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:37b}
        \glll  Kēd.ˈná.dí ˈBǽd ˈmwǽs.\\
                kēd=\textbf{nā}=di Bǽd mwǽs.\\
                \textsc{neg}=\textsc{cop}=\textsc{neg} Pedro teacher\\
        \glt ‘Pedro is not (a) teacher.’
    \z
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:38}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:38a}
        \glll Na.ˈsínny ˈnǎn. \\ 
              nasínny \textbf{nā}=an \\
             intelligent \textsc{cop}=\textsc{3sg.if}\\
        \glt  ‘S/he is intelligent.’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:38b}
        \glll  Kēd.ˈná.dyán na.ˈsínny.\\
               kēd=\textbf{nā}=di=an nasínny\\ 
               \textsc{neg}=\textsc{cop}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if} intelligent\\
        \glt  ‘S/he is not intelligent.’
    \z
\z 

\subsubsection{Locative predication}\label{sec:zap:2.3.2}

Locative predicates in TdVZ involve the use of a positional verb + a locative phrase that generally contains a body part relational noun, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:39}. Thus, in addition to stating existence and location, they also specify the position of the predicated entity. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:39} 
    \glll ˈZǔb ˈxhǐt.kī kyæ.ˈyu’.\\ 
           zǔb xhǐt=kī kyæ=yu’\\
         \textsc{stat.}sit cat=\textsc{temp.dem} \textsc{rn}.head=building \\
    \glt  ‘The cat is/was sitting on top of the roof.’
\z 

In order to negate this type of predicate, just as with standard negation, \textit{kēd}= and =\textit{di} cliticize to the verb stem, such as in \REF{ex:zap:40}. Since the negators cliticize to the positional verb, but the scope of negation is over the whole phrase, two possible readings are triggered: one that negates the place and one that negates the position. To specifically negate the location, focus negation must occur, this type of negation, however, is discussed in \sectref{sec:zap:3.3}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:40} 
    \glll Kēd.ˈzǔb.di ˈxhǐt kyæˈyū’kī.\\ 
          kēd=zǔb=di xhǐt kyæ=yu’=kī\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{stat.}sit=\textsc{neg} cat \textsc{rn}.head=bulding=\textsc{temp.dem}\\
    \glt  ‘The cat is not sitting on that roof (is somewhere else).’
    \glt ‘The cat is not sitting on that roof (but lying/ standing).’
\z 

\subsubsection{Possessive predication}\label{sec:zap:2.3.3}

TdVZ, like many other languages, distinguishes between alienable and inalienable nouns. In this section, I describe one specific case of possessive predication with each type of noun. I look at cases that exhibit a more ‘prototypical’ possessive situation: the possessor is close to an object over which s/he has control \citep{Stassen2009}. Thus, I show possessive predications of alienable nouns that occur with the verb -\textit{āp} ‘have’ and possessive predication with body parts that occur with positional verbs. The first type occurs as shown in \REF{ex:zap:41}, while the second as in \REF{ex:zap:42}. As noted, the second construction requires a specific positional verb to denote the attachment of the body{}-part to the body (of the possessor). This last type of predication can also occur with a verb of existence and can be negated as such; this is discussed in \sectref{sec:zap:2.3.5}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:41} 
    \glll ˈRāp ˈBǽd ˈgû’n.\\ 
          r-āp Bǽd gû’n\\
          \textsc{hab}{}-have Pedro bull \\
        \glt ‘Pedro has bull(s).’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:42} 
    \glll ˈZǔb ˈtyōp ˈnyâ̰n̄. \\ 
          zǔb tyōp nyâ̰=ān\\
          \textsc{stat.}sit two \textsc{poss}.hand=\textsc{3sg.f}\\
    \glt  ‘S/he has two arms.’ (lit. ‘There is placed two hands of his/hers (a doll, for example).’)
\z 


Both types of possessive predications are negated similarly to standard negation, that is, \textit{kēd}= and =\textit{di} attach to the verb, as in \REF{ex:zap:43} and \REF{ex:zap:44} respectively.

\ea\label{ex:zap:43} 
    \glll Kēd.ˈráp.dí ˈBǽd ˈgû’n.\\ 
            kēd=r-āp=di Bǽd gû’n\\
           \textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-have=\textsc{neg} Pedro bull\\
    \glt  ‘Pedro doesn’t have bull(s).’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:44} 
    \glll ˈKēdzǔbdi ˈte ˈnyâ̰n̄. \\ 
           kēd=zǔb=di te nyâ̰=ān\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{stat.}sit=\textsc{neg} \textsc{indf} \textsc{poss}.hand=\textsc{3sg.f} \\
    \glt ‘S/he doesn’t have an arm.’ (lit. ‘There isn’t placed a hand of his / her (a doll, for example).)
\z

\subsubsection{Existential predication}\label{sec:zap:2.3.5}
\label{bkm:Ref126504315}
Existential predication is expressed by the verb -\textit{u’} ‘exist’. This verb has a suppletive form in the stative aspect, i.e., \textit{yū’} \textsc{stat.ex}, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:47}. Even though both forms belong to the same verb root, their morphosyntax and function are different. \textit{Yū’} \textsc{stat.ex} differs from {}-\textit{u’} ‘exist’ since it has mid tone and its negative counterpart does not contain a verb, but the marker \textit{kěty} \textsc{neg.ex}, as in \REF{ex:zap:48}. Unlike the negator(s) in standard negation, this negative marker is not a clitic but a phonologically and morphosyntactically independent element.

\ea\label{ex:zap:47} 
    \glll ˈYū’ ˈnîs nna’.ˈdxi.\\ 
          yū’ nis nna’dxi\\
          \textsc{stat.ex} water today\\
    \glt ‘There is water (service) today.’
\z 

\protectedex{\ea\label{ex:zap:48} 
    \gll ˈKěty ˈnis nna’.ˈdxi.\\ 
          \textbf{\textsc{neg.ex}} water today\\
    \glt ‘There is no water (service) today.’
\z }
On the other hand, -\textit{u}’ ‘exist’ has a low tone and conjugates with the full TAM paradigm, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:49} with the completive prefix. Interestingly, the latter is negated with the same markers that occur in standard negation; that is, \textit{kēd}= and =\textit{di} attach to the verb stem, as in \REF{ex:zap:50}. Thus, in TdVZ, \textit{yū’} \textsc{stat.ex} is a lexicalized form of the verb that is used to predicate existence without indication of an initial or end point of that existence. On the other hand, {}-\textit{u}’ ‘exist’ is a verb that is used to indicate an existence that has ceased or that will start sometime in the future.

\ea\label{ex:zap:49} 
    \glll ˈGu’ ˈnis ˈnna̰y.\\ 
           g-u’ nis nna̰y\\
           \textsc{compl}{}-exist water yesterday\\
    \glt  ‘There was water (service) yesterday.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:50} 
    \glll Kēd.ˈgû’.di ˈnis ˈnna̰y.\\ 
          kēd=g´-u’=di nis nna̰y \\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-exist=\textsc{neg} water yesterday\\
    \glt  There was not water (service) yesterday.’
\z 

Note that the negator for existential predicates \textit{kěty} has some resemblance to the clausal negator \textit{kēd=}.\footnote{In closer Zapotec varieties (i.e., San Pablo Guilá Zapotec (SPGZ), and San Lucas Quiaviní Zapotec (SLQZ)) \textit{kěty} (or \textit{kɨ̌ty} or \textit{ke’ity} respectively) is the negator in standard negation. This suggests that in TdVZ \textit{kēd}= may be the evolved form of \textit{kěty,} which has maintained its form in existential negation. Also, since the negation with \textit{kěty} does occur with =\textit{di} one could hypothesize that \textit{kěty} may be the conflation of \textit{kēd= + =di}, but in the closer varieties, the homologous of \textit{kěty} does coexist with =\textit{di}.}
This is a typologically common pattern for this type of predicate \citep{Veselinova2013neg.exist}. In TdVZ, the existential negator resembles the clausal negator, but it is not formally identical to it, and the phonological and morphosyntactic status of each element differs, as well as the constructions in which they appear.\footnote{Nevertheless, historically these two negators may have had one single source.} 

A particular characteristic of \textit{kěty} \textsc{neg.ex} is that it can host pronominal enclitics, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:51}. No other negator exhibit this property in TdVZ.

\ea\label{ex:zap:51} 
    \glll ˈKē.tyán ˈgwâ’.\\ 
          kěty=an gu-{â’}\\
          \textsc{neg.ex}=\textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{compl}{}-go:\textsc{1sg}\\
    \glt ‘He (was) not (there) (when) I went.’
\z 

\subsection{Negation in non-main clauses}\label{sec:zap:2.4}

Most subordinate clauses are negated in the same way as monoclausal constructions. That is, the negator \textit{kēd}= occurs preverbally and =\textit{di} occurs post-verbally, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:52} with a complement clause, in \REF{ex:zap:53} with a relative clause, and in \REF{ex:zap:54} with an adverbial clause. If =\textit{di} is omitted in these constructions, they become ungrammatical.


\ea\label{ex:zap:52} 
    \glll Gu.dixh.ˈlæ’n kēd.ˈgǽ̰d.dyán.\\ 
               gu-dixhlæ’=an \textbf{kēd}=g´-ǣ̰d=\textbf{di}=an\\
              \textsc{compl}{}-tell=\textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{neg}=\textsc{pot}{}-come=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if}\\
        \glt  ‘He told (us) that he will/would not come.’\\
    \glt 
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:53} 
        \glll Gu.ˈnnæ̰z.dān ˈbénny ní.kēd.gú.ˈdîxh.dī.kī.\\ 
              gu-nnæ̰z=dān bēnny ni=\textbf{kēd}=gu´-dixh=\textbf{di}=kī\\
              \textsc{compl}{}-catch=\textsc{3pl.f} person \textsc{sub}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}\textsc{{}-compl}:pay=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{temp.dem}\\
        \glt ‘They caught the person who didn’t pay.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:54} 
        \glll Ba.ˈtyu’.nan kom.kēd.bá.ˈzûyn.dyan.\\ 
              ba-tyu’n=an kom=\textbf{kēd}=ba´-zuyn=\textbf{di}=an\\
              \textsc{compl}{}-lose=\textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{sub}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-arrive=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if}\\
        \glt ‘He lost (in the game) because he didn’t arrive.'
\z 

There are, however, various negative subordinated clauses in which \textit{=di} is non-obligatory. For instance, in complement clauses used for reported speech,\footnote{The last construction is considered by \citet{Lee1999} as being derived from an indirect imperative.} as in \REF{ex:zap:55}; in head fronted relative clauses, as in \REF{ex:zap:56}; and in adverbial conditional clauses, as in \REF{ex:zap:57}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:55} 
    \glll ˈRæby dok.ˈtór ˈLlúpy kēd.ˈgáw.(dy)an bǣl.ˈkǔtx.\\ 
          ræby doktór Llúpy kēd=g´-aw(=\textbf{di})=an bǣ̰l-kǔtx\\
          \textsc{compl}.say.to doctor Lupe \textsc{neg}=\textsc{pot}{}-eat(=\textsc{neg})=\textsc{3sg.if} meat-pig\\
    \glt ‘The doctor told (recommended) Lupe not to eat meat pork.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:56} 
    \glll ˈDbénny ní.kēd.gú.ˈdâw(di) ˈndǣ ˈzû ˈrǣ.\\ 
          d´=bēnny ni=kēd=gu´-daw(=\textbf{di}) ndǣ zu rǣ\\
          \textsc{pl}=person \textsc{sub}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-eat(=\textsc{neg}) that \textsc{stat}.stand \textsc{loc.adv}\\
    \glt ‘(The) people that didn’t eat are standing there.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:57} 
    \glll Txi.kēd.ˈr\^{æ}.(di) ˈBǽd ˈxkwíly, ˈræ̰n loˈkǎntx.\\ 
          txi=kēd=r-æ\textbf{(=di}) Bǽd xkwíly r-æ=an low=kǎntx\\
          \textsc{sub}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-go(=\textsc{neg}) Pedro school \textsc{hab}{}-go=\textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{rn}.face=court\\
    \glt ‘When/if Pedro doesn’t go to school, he goes to the court field.’
\z 

Also, there are counterfactual subordinated expressions, as the one shown in \REF{ex:zap:58}, which can also omit \textit{=di}. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:58} 
    \glll ˈBlé kēd.ˈnyáw.(di)á ˈbǣlˈkǔtx téˈnna’…\\ 
          Blé kēd=ni´-aw(=di)=a bǣ̰l+kǔtx té=nna’\\
          hopefully \textsc{neg}=\textsc{cntf}{}-eat(=\textsc{neg})=\textsc{1sg} meat+pig \textsc{sub}=now\\
    \glt ‘I wish I hadn’t eaten pork because now...’
\z 

In addition, there are subordinate clauses in which \textit{=di} restricts the type of clause that may follow it. In \REF{ex:zap:59}, just as with \textit{prohibitives}, relative clauses followed by a clause expressing a request do not allow \textit{=di}. However, \textit{=di} is optional in a similar construction if the following clause (the main clause) expresses a (declarative) consequence, as in \REF{ex:zap:60}. This suggests that \textit{=di} contrasts with deontic modalities, specifically directive modalities. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:59} 
    \glll ˈDdxâp ni.kē.dríˈzḭ, ˈgúyn.dán pa.ˈbúr bī.ˈká.dán.\\ 
          d´=dxap ni=\textbf{kēd}=ri-zḭ g´-uny=dán pabúr bīká=dán\\
          \textsc{pl}=girl \textsc{sub}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-buy \textsc{pot}{}-do=\textsc{3pl.if} favor \textsc{pot.}be.removed=\textsc{3pl.if}\\
    \glt ‘Girls that would/do not buy, please do the favor of moving away.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:60} 
    \glll ˈDdxâp ni.kē.drí.ˈzḭ(di), ˈruyn ˈgán.\\ 
          d´=dxap ni=kēd=ri-zḭ(=di) r-uny gán\\
          \textsc{pl}=girl \textsc{sub}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-buy(=\textsc{neg}) \textsc{hab}{}-do win\\
    \glt ‘Girls that would/do not buy, win.’
\z 

Furthermore, there are subordinated adverbial conditional clauses in which the addition of \textit{=di} results in ungrammaticality, as can be observed when comparing the examples below.

\ea\label{ex:zap:61} 
    \glll ˈBǣll.ké.drí.ˈka̰.zan gā.ˈdxâ.gan, ˈsyæ̰̂.nēn.\\ 
          bǣll=\textbf{kēd}=ri-ka̰z=an ga´-dxag=an sḭ̂=an=ēn\\
          if=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-want=\textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{pot}{}-get.tired=\textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{pot}.buy=\textsc{3sg.if}=\textsc{3sg.inan}\\
    \glt ‘If he doesn’t want to get tired, he better buy it (something to help).’
\z 

\ea[*]{\label{ex:zap:62} 
    \glll Bǣll.ké.drí.ˈka̰.za.dyan ga.ˈdxâ.gan, ˈsyæ̰̂.nēn.\\ 
          bǣll=\textbf{kēd}=ri-ka̰z=\textbf{di}=an ga'-dxag=an sḭ̂=an=en\\
          if=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-want=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{pot}{}-get.tired=\textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{pot}.buy=\textsc{3sg.if}=\textsc{3sg.inan}\\
    \glt Intended reading: ‘If he doesn’t want to get tired, he better buy it (something to help).’}
\z 

This fuzzy behavior of \textit{=di} in subordinated clauses may not be defined by the syntax but by the semantics of the construction. That is, as I showed in (\ref{ex:zap:53}--\ref{ex:zap:55}), there are complement, relative and adverbial subordinated clauses in which \textit{=di} is obligatory, but there are many others in which =\textit{di} is not because there is no certainty as to whether the event expressed in the subordinate clause will occur or not. 

As I have shown, \textit{kēd=} is obligatory in all negative clausal constructions and in the three mood distinctions (declarative, interrogative, imperative) while \textit{=di} is not. Therefore, \textit{kēd}= is a negator in TdVZ, but the status of =\textit{di} is less clear.\footnote{Diachronically =\textit{di} may have been a marker that contributed to the declarative mood of the constructions it appeared. This is because the type of sentences that are not compatible with \textit{=di} (\textit{e.g}., prohibitives, requests, \textit{bǽll} ‘if’ conditionals) or that show =\textit{di} optionally (counterfactuals, interrogatives, and \textit{masy} ‘even if’ \& \textit{txi} ‘when’ conditionals), all involve possible events, but not facts.} However, since \textit{=di} is obligatory in monoclausal declarative constructions, I consider it to be a negative marker in a double negative construction.

\section{Non-clausal negation}\label{sec:zap:3}
\subsection{Negative replies}\label{sec:zap:3.1}

The negative reply in TdVZ is \textit{ā'ā} ‘no’. This, however, is generally followed by the negated clause, as shown in the answer to the question in \REF{ex:zap:6364}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:6364}
    \begin{xlist}[B: ]
    \exi{Q: }\label{ex:zap:63} 
        \glll (L)á.ˈgwæ ˈBǽd ˈLlwâ’ ˈnna̰y?\\ 
          (l)á=gu-æ Bǽd Llwâ’ nna̰y \\
          \textsc{q}=\textsc{compl}{}-go Pedro Oaxaca yesterday\\
    \glt  Did Pedro go to Oaxaca yesterday?
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:zap:64} 
        \glll ˈĀ'ā', kēd.ˈgwǽ.dyan.\\ 
           ā'ā kēd=gu´-æ=di=an\\
           \textsc{neg} \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-go=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if}\\
    \glt ‘No, he didn’t go.’
    \end{xlist}
\z 

Interestingly, if the question contains a negative clause, as in (\ref{ex:zap:65}Q), \textit{ā'ā} ‘no’ would be used as an answer to indicate that the proposition is correct, as shown in (\ref{ex:zap:66}A1). If the answer, on the other hand, contradicts the negative proposition, a declarative clause would occur as the answer, as shown in (\ref{ex:zap:67}A2). In this type of negative construction then, the \textit{ā'ā} ‘no’ agrees with the polarity of the question’s proposition. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:656667} 
    \begin{xlist}[A2: ]
    \exi{Q: }\label{ex:zap:65} 
        \glll (L)á.kēdˈgwǽdi ˈBǽd ˈLlwâ’ ˈnna̰y?\\ 
          (l)á=kēd=gu-æ=di Bǽd Llwâ’ nna̰y\\
           \textsc{q}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-go=\textsc{neg} Pedro Oaxaca yesterday\\
    \glt  Didn’t Pedro go to Oaxaca yesterday?
    \exi{A1: }\label{ex:zap:66} 
        \glll ˈĀ'ā, kēd.ˈgwǽ.dyan.\\ 
           ā'ā kēd=gu´-æ=di=an\\
           \textsc{neg} \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-go=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if}\\
    \glt  ‘No, he didn’t go (you are right).’
    \exi{A2: }\label{ex:zap:67} 
        \glll Gwæ̰n.\\ 
           gu-æ=an\\
           \textsc{compl}{}-go=\textsc{3sg.if}\\
        \glt  ‘He went (you are/were wrong).’
    \end{xlist}
\z 

The existential negator \textit{kěty} (\sectref{sec:zap:2.3.5}) may also be used as a negative reply, as in \REF{ex:zap:68B}, and this can optionally be followed by the negated clause. However, \textit{kěty} generally occurs in replies that refer to future events, and which generally are directed to the interlocutor, as noted in the examples below.

\ea\label{ex:zap:68}
    \begin{xlist}[A: ]
    \exi{Q: }\label{ex:zap:68A} 
        \glll (L)á.ˈzḛw ˈLlwâ’?\\ 
               (l)á=z-æ=u Llwâ’\\
               \textsc{q}=\textsc{fut}{}-go=2\textsc{sg.if} Oaxaca \\
        \glt  Will you go to Oaxaca?
    \exi{A: }\label{ex:zap:68B} 
        \glll Kěty, kēdtxâ’dya.\\ 
               kěty kēd=tx´=â’=di=a\\
               \textsc{neg} \textsc{neg}=\textsc{pot}{}-go:\textsc{1sg}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{1sg}\\
        \glt  ‘No, I am not going to go.’
    \end{xlist}
\z 

\subsection{Negative indefinites and quantifiers}\label{sec:zap:3.2}

In TdVZ, the indefinite pronouns \textit{nobody}, \textit{nothing}, and \textit{nowhere} are formed using the monoclausal negation markers (i.e., \textit{kēd=/gád=} and \textit{=di}) and the interrogative clitics, as I show in \REF{ex:zap:69}. These forms, however, are used only as short answers that do not contain a verb.

\ea\label{ex:zap:69}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:769a}
        \glll  kē.ˈtú.dí \\ 
               kēd=tū=di\\
               \textsc{neg}=who=\textsc{neg}\\
        \glt ‘nobody’ 
    \ex\label{ex:zap:69b}
        \glll  kē.ˈtxí.dí\\
               kēd=xī=di \\
               \textsc{neg}=what=\textsc{neg}\\
        \glt  ‘nothing’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:69c}
        \glll  kēd.ˈká.(lī)dí\\
              kēd=kā(lí)di\\
              \textsc{neg}=where=\textsc{neg} \\
        \glt ‘nowhere’
    \z
\z 

If the (questioned) verb occurs in the answer, the position of \textit{=di} is now after the verb, as in \REF{ex:zap:70a}. Indefinite constructions can also occur with the potential mood clause negator \textit{gád}=, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:70b}. This feature confirms the proposal that the negation of potential mood clauses is a subtype of clausal negation in TdVZ.

\ea\label{ex:zap:70}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:70a}
        \glll  Kēd.tú.ˈbǽ̰d.dí. \\ 
                \textbf{kēd}=tū=b-ǣ̰d=\textbf{di}\\
               \textsc{neg}=who=\textsc{compl}{}-come=\textsc{neg}\\
        \glt  ‘Nobody came.’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:70b}
        \glll  Gád.ˈtú.gǽ̰d.dí.\\
               \textbf{gád}=tū=g´-ǣ̰d=\textbf{di}\\
                 \textsc{neg}=who=\textsc{pot}{}-come=\textsc{neg}\\
        \glt   ‘Nobody has come yet.’
    \z
\z 

In addition, when the question or the indefinite construction focalizes some specific noun, as in \REF{ex:zap:71}, the meaning as well as the construction are modified, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:72}. In this case, note that =\textit{di} cliticizes right after the negated constituent.

\ea\label{ex:zap:71} 
    \glll (L)á.yū’ xí.ˈg\^{æ}t ba.ˈdæ̰̂d.dān ˈlǎ̰n?\\ 
          lá=yū’ xī=\textbf{gæt} ba´-dæ̰d=dān lǎ̰n?\\
          \textsc{q}=\textsc{stat.ex} what=tortilla \textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-give=\textsc{3pl.f} \textsc{3sg.if}\\
    \glt ‘Did they give him some/any tortilla?’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:72} 
    \glll Kē.txī.ˈg\^{æ}t.di ba.dæ̰̂d.dān lǎ̰n.\\ 
           kēd\textbf{=}xī\textbf{=gæt}=\textbf{di} ba´-dæ̰d=dān lǎ̰n\\
           \textsc{neg}=what=tortilla=\textsc{neg} \textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-give=\textsc{3pl.f} \textsc{3sg.if}\\
    \glt  ‘They didn’t give him/her any tortilla.’ (lit. ‘Any/no tortilla they gave him.’)
\z 

The use of \textit{kēd=} in these last examples does not seem to be negating nouns in a contrastive way but instead, their existence. Then, in this construction =\textit{di} seems to add emphasis to the non-existence of the entity, like the negative item ‘at all’ in English.

Other means to form an indefinite pronoun in TdVZ do not involve negation. Therefore, I do not discuss them here. 

\subsection{Negative derivation}\label{sec:zap:3.3}

The present state of analysis does not allow me to say anything about negative derivation such as English -\textit{less} or \textit{un}{}-. Thus, in this section, I discuss focus negation since it is one of the most common strategies for negating nominals in Zapotec languages.

In focus negation, the marker \textit{ádí=} is used to negate focused elements that appear in the preverbal position. The negated element can be a noun phrase or some other type of phrase, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:73} and \REF{ex:zap:74} respectively. This element has all the properties of clitics in this language even though it may be a compound element as I discuss below. 

\ea\label{ex:zap:73} 
    \glll Á.dí.ˈJwáyn(īn) ba.ˈllṵ̂b lo.ˈnæz. \\ 
          \textbf{ádí}=Jwáyn(=īn) ba´-llṵb low=næz\\
          \textsc{neg}=Juan(=\textsc{foc}) \textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-sweep \textsc{rn}.face-road\\
    \glt ‘It wasn’t Juan who swept the street (but someone else).’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:74} 
    \glll Á.dí.nna’.ˈdxi ˈtxô’ūn.\\ 
          \textbf{ádí}=nna’dxi tx´-æ=ūn\\
          \textsc{neg}=today \textsc{pot}{}-go=\textsc{1pl.excl}\\
    \glt ‘We won’t go today (but some other day).’
\z 

\textit{Ádí}= seems to be the remnant of a more morphologically complex structure that involves \textit{ád}= ‘the negator’, a base (i.e., \textit{lā̰})\footnote{\textit{lā̰} is a grammatical element that is used as a base to host clitics \citep{Foreman2006}, but its origin and meaning is no longer transparent. \textit{lā̰} also appears in the formation of independent pronouns (lā̰ + an = lǎ̰n ‘\textsc{3sg.if}’).} and \textit{=di}, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:75}. \textit{Ádlā̰dí} is in free variation with \textit{ádí}= in more conservative registers.

\ea\label{ex:zap:75} 
    \glll Á.ˈdlā̰.dí ˈJwáyn(īn) ba.ˈllṵ̂b lo.ˈnæz.\\ 
          \textbf{ád}=\textbf{lā̰}=\textbf{di} Jwáyn(=īn) ba´-llṵb low=næz\\
          \textsc{neg}=lā̰=\textsc{neg} Juan(=\textsc{foc}) \textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-sweep \textsc{rn}.face-road\\
    \glt ‘It is not the case that Juan swept the road / It wasn’t Juan who swept the road.’
\z 

I showed above that \textit{gád}=, the negator in ‘not yet’ expressions, may merge with \textit{=di} to form \textit{gáti}. Thus, I assume that the same type of process derived the focus negator \textit{ádí}=. I consider this to be the case since \textit{ád}= and \textit{=di} can appear separately, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:76}. In addition, when \textit{ádí}= and =\textit{di} co-occur the construction is ungrammatical, as in \REF{ex:zap:77}. Thus, \textit{ádí}= contains \textit{di=}, and the high tone of this element may be a remnant of the high tone this element acquired (and maintained) from the more complex form (\textit{ádlā̰dí}) in which \textit{=di} was preceded by a mid-tone.

\ea\label{ex:zap:76} 
    \glll Ád.ˈdxap.di.rǣ gú.ˈzḭīn.\\ 
          \textbf{ád}=dxap\textbf{=di}=rǣ gu´-zḭ=īn\\
          \textsc{neg}=girl=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{dist.dem} \textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-buy=\textsc{3sg.inan}\\
    \glt ‘It wasn’t that girl who bought it (but someone else).’
\z 

\ea[*]{\label{ex:zap:77} 
    \glll Á.dí.ˈdxap.di.rǣ gú.ˈzḭ̂īn.\\ 
          \textbf{ádí}=dxap=\textbf{di}=rǣ gu´-zḭ=ēn\\
          \textsc{neg}=girl=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{dist.dem} \textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-buy=\textsc{3sg.inan}\\
    \glt Intended reading: ‘It wasn’t that girl who bought it (but someone else).’}
\z 

Also, in \REF{ex:zap:79}, I demonstrate that \textit{ád=} and \textit{=di} are also used to negate other aspects of a nominal phrase; compare \REF{ex:zap:78} vs \REF{ex:zap:79} and notice that these elements surround the quantifier that modifies the noun.

\ea\label{ex:zap:78} 
    \glll ˈRá ˈbēnny rú.ˈkḭn ˈbǣ̰̰l.\\ 
         rá bēnny ru-kḭn bǣ̰̰l \\
          all person \textsc{hab}{}-consume meat\\
    \glt ‘All people consume meat.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:79} 
    \glll Ád.ˈrá.di ˈbēnny rú.ˈkḭn ˈbǣ̰̰l.\\ 
          ád=rá=di bēnny ru-kḭn bǣ̰l\\
          \textsc{neg}=all=\textsc{neg} person \textsc{hab}{}-consume meat\\
    \glt ‘Not all people consume meat.’
\z 

Moreover, notice that \textit{ádí=} constructions generally result in contrastive focus negative readings, as in \REF{ex:zap:80a}. Also, when \textit{ádí=} negates a verbal phrase, it is possible to move the negated verbal phrase after the verbal phrase that contrasts with it, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:80a} vs. \REF{ex:zap:80b}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:80}
    \ea\label{ex:zap:80a}
        \glll Á.dí.gu.ˈzyǣ̰.nēn, ba.ˈdǣ̰d.zī.dā.nēn ˈlǎ̰n. \\ 
               ádí=gu-zḭ=an=ēn ba-dæ̰d=zī=dān=ēn lǎ̰n\\
               \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}{}-buy=\textsc{3sg.if}=\textsc{3sg.inan} \textsc{compl}{}-give=only=\textsc{3pl.f}=3\textsc{sg.inan} \textsc{3sg.if}\\
        \glt  ‘It is not the case that s/he bought it, they gave it to him/her for free.’
    \ex\label{ex:zap:80b}
        \glll  Ba.ˈdǣ̰d.zī.dā.nēn ˈlǎ̰n, á.dí.gu.ˈzyǣ̰.nēn.\\
              ba-dæ̰d=zī=dān=ēn lǎ̰n ádí=gu-zḭ=an=ēn\\
               \textsc{compl}{}-give=only=\textsc{3pl.f}=\textsc{3sg.inan} \textsc{3sg.if} \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}{}-buy=\textsc{3sg.if}=\textsc{3sg.inan}\\
        \glt  ‘They gave it to him/her for free, it is not the case that s/he bought it.’
    \z
\z 

There are two more syntactic aspects of \textit{ádí}= that I should mention; first, there are instances in which an NP in the topic position can precede \textit{ádí}=, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:81}. Second, as mentioned above, \textit{ádí}= and standard negation can cooccur, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:82}. This last example demonstrates that the scope of each negator is defined and specific to the phrase they appear in.


\ea\label{ex:zap:81} 
    \glll ˈBǽd, á.díˈJwáyn ba.ˈllṵ̂b lo.næz.  \\ 
          Bǽd \textbf{ádí}=Jwáyn ba´-llṵb low=næz\\
          Pedro \textsc{neg}=Juan \textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-sweep \textsc{rn}.face=road\\
    \glt ‘Pedro, not Juan, swept the street.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:82} 
    \glll Á.dí.ˈdxāp.rǣ kēd.ˈgwǽ.di.\\ 
          \textbf{ádí}=dxap=rǣ \textbf{kēd}=gu´-æ=di\\
          \textsc{neg}=girl=\textsc{dist.dem} \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-go=di\\
    \glt ‘Not that girl didn’t go.’ / ‘It isn’t that girl that/who didn’t go.’ 
\z 

Finally, just as the clausal negator \textit{kēd=}, \textit{ádí}= is not compatible with the future aspectual marker, as I show in \REF{ex:zap:83}. In order to negate a possible future event with a focused element, the potential marker should appear on the verb, as in \REF{ex:zap:84}.

\ea[*]{\label{ex:zap:83} 
    \glll Á.dí.ˈdxāp.rǣ ˈzûyn ˈzṵyn.\\ 
          \textbf{ádí}=dxap=rǣ \textbf{z}{}-uny zṵyn\\
          \textsc{neg}=girl=\textsc{dist.dem} \textsc{fut}{}-do work\\
    \glt Intended reading: ‘That girl (is) not (the one who) will work.’}
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:84} 
    \glll Á.dí.ˈdxāp.rǣ ˈgúyn ˈzṵyn. \\ 
          \textbf{ádí}=dxap=rǣ \textbf{g´}{}-uny zṵyn\\
          \textsc{neg}=girl=\textsc{dist.dem} \textsc{pot}{}-do work\\
    \glt ‘That girl (is) not (the one who) will work.’
\z 

\textit{Ádí=,} then, is a remnant of a compound morpheme with complex morphology, but it does not acquire an accent and remains non-prominent since neither of its vowels lengthens as expected in this environment (/á/ is before a lenis consonant and /í/ is in syllable-final position). Also, the fact that \textit{ádí}= or \textit{ád}= + =\textit{di} attach to various lexical categories confirms that this morpheme is a (pro)clitic. However, \textit{ádí}= perhaps is a more advanced stage of fusion than \textit{gáti,} the ‘not yet’ expression negator, since it is more common for the speakers to use the merged form than the separated one. 

\section{Other aspects of negation} \label{sec:zap:4}
\subsection{The scope of negation: position and scope of the negative marker \textit{kēd}=}\label{sec:zap:4.1}

In most Zapotec languages there is a preverbal position for focused elements. One might argue then that the negative proclitic \textit{kēd}= occupies this focus position. The negative marker \textit{kēd}=, however, can appear together with a focused NP, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:85}. Notice that the position of \textit{kēd}= is fixed and cannot appear before the focused element assuming that it would move to the focus position, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:86}. Thus, the position of \textit{kēd=} is before the verb, and due to its clitic status, \textit{kēd=} would cliticize to it if no other proclitics appear.

\ea\label{ex:zap:85} 
    \glll [ˈJwáyn]\textsubscript{FOC} kēd.bá.ˈllṵ̂b.di lo.ˈnæz.\\ 
          \textbf{Jwáyn} \textbf{kēd}=ba´-llṵb=di low=næz\\
          Juan \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-sweep=\textsc{neg} \textsc{rn}.face=road\\
    \glt ‘(It was) Juan (who) didn’t sweep the street.’
\z 

\ea[*]{\label{ex:zap:86} 
    \glll Kēd ˈJwáyn ˈba.llṵ̂b.di lo.ˈnæz. \\ 
          \textbf{kēd}= \textbf{Jwáyn} ba´-llṵb=di low=næz\\
          \textsc{neg} Juan \textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-sweep=\textsc{neg} \textsc{rn}.face=road\\
    \glt Intended reading ‘(It was) not Juan (who) didn’t sweep the street.’}
\z 
Also, the scope of \textit{kēd=} is over the verbal predicate, and not over constituents or focused elements. When a focused element is negated, the negative marker \textit{ádí=} is required, as shown in \REF{ex:zap:87}. This type of negative construction was discussed in \sectref{sec:zap:3.3}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:87} 
    \glll Á.dí.ˈJwáyn kēd.bá.ˈllṵ̂b.di lo.ˈnæz.\\ 
          ádí=Jwáyn kēd=ba´-llṵb=di low=næz\\
          \textsc{neg}=Juan \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-sweep=\textsc{neg} \textsc{rn}.face=road\\
    \glt ‘(It wasn’t) Juan (who) didn’t sweep the street.’
\z

So far, I have identified two negative polarity items in TdVZ, i.e. \textit{nītúy} ‘not even one’ and \textit{nēkl(ā)} ‘nor’. These are discussed in \sectref{sec:zap:4.4}. and \sectref{sec:zap:4.5} respectively.

\subsection{Reinforcing negation: emphatic negative constructions (\textit{not even} \textit{one} expressions)}
\label{sec:zap:4.4}
In TdVZ, there is an emphatic negative marker \textit{nītúy=}\footnote{This construction is also discussed by \citet{GalantForeman2010} for other Zapotec varieties.} (\textit{nī=} + numeral 'one'), which modifies a phrase that functions as an argument or as an oblique of a negated clause, as in \REF{ex:zap:88} and \REF{ex:zap:89} respectively. \textit{Nītúy=} ‘not even one’, then, together with the negated clause, emphasizes that in a negated clause/event, not even one member of a category participates in it or that the event does not take place even once. Thus, \textit{nītúy=} ‘not even one’ is a negative polarity item that only appears in negative clauses. In \REF{ex:zap:88}, note that \textit{nītúy=} is generally followed by the free function word intensifier \textit{tæ̰}, which further emphasizes this expression.

\ea\label{ex:zap:88} 
    \glll Kēd.bá.ˈzûyn.di nī.túy(ˈtæ̰)ˈbēnny.\\ 
           kēd=ba´=zuyn=di nītúy(tæ̰)=bēnny\\
           \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-arrive=\textsc{neg} not.even.one(\textsc{ints})=person\\
    \glt  ‘Not even one person arrived.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:89} 
    \glll Nī.túb.dxi kēd.gwǽ.dyan.\\ 
           nītúy=dxi kēd=gu´-æ=di=an\\
          not.eve.one=day \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-go=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3sg.if} \\
    \glt ‘Not even one day he went.’
\z 

\textit{Nītúy} ‘not even one’ is polymorphemic, since it can get morpho-syntactically complex. That is, \textit{nī}= ‘not even’ can be separated from \textit{tuy} ‘one’ by other elements, as in \REF{ex:zap:90}.

\ea\label{ex:zap:90} 
    \glll Nīk.txá.ˈtuy (ˈtæ̰) ˈbēnny kéd.bá.ˈzûyn.di.\\ 
           nī=kā=txá=tuy (tæ̰) bēnny kēd=ba´=zuyn=di\\
           not.even=\textsc{aff}=maybe=one (\textsc{ints}) person \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}=arrive=\textsc{neg}\\
    \glt  ‘Not even one single person arrived.’
\z 

Given that \textit{nītúy} is an emphatic negator, it typically appears in the preverbal position together with the modifying noun as was shown in \REF{ex:zap:89} and \REF{ex:zap:90}. Also, the fact that \textit{nītúy} ‘not even one’ emerges only in negative constructions differentiates this type of negation from other negative constructions discussed in this chapter.

\subsection{Negation, coordination and complex clauses}\label{sec:zap:4.5}

In addition to \textit{nītú}y ‘not even one’, another element that could be considered a negative polarity item is \textit{nēkl(ā)} ‘nor’. This element could be the conflation of the conjunctive element conjunction \textit{nǣ}= ‘and’ and the enclitics =\textit{k(ā)} ‘as expected’ and =\textit{lā} ‘also’. \textit{Nēkl(ā)}, which I translate as ‘nor’, occurs within a negated clause to coordinate two or more NPs (or other type or phrases),\footnote{\citet{Salaberri2022} refers to this type of constructions as Emphatic Negative Coordination.} as shown in \REF{ex:zap:91}. This element is much less common coordinating two clauses, cases such as in \REF{ex:zap:92} were not completely accepted as grammatical for various speakers.

\ea\label{ex:zap:91} 
    \glll Kē.drúˈrak.di ˈgæl nē.kl(ā).bí.ˈza̰, nē.kl(ā).ˈgît.\\ 
           kēd=rú=r-ak=di gæl nēkl(ā)=biza̰ nēkl(ā)=git\\
          \textsc{neg}=more=\textsc{hab}{}-be.done=\textsc{neg} milpa \textsc{conj}=bean \textsc{conj}=pumpkin\\
    \glt  ‘Milpa is no longer harvested (good), nor beans, nor pumpkin.’
\z

\ea\label{ex:zap:92} 
    \glll Kē.dbá.ˈyá’.dī.dān né.kl(ā).kéd.ˈgw\^{æ}’.dī.dān nē.kl(ā)… \\ 
           kēd=ba-yā’=di=dān nēkl(ā)=kēd=gu´{}-æ’=di=dān nēkl(ā)=\\ 
           \textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-dance=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{3pl.f} \textsc{conj}=\textsc{neg}=\textsc{compl}\textsc{\textsubscript{2}}{}-drink=di=\textsc{3pl.f} \textsc{conj}=\\
    \glt  ‘They didn’t dance nor (did they) drink, nor…’
\z 

\subsection{Miscellaneous aspects of negation: negative transport}\label{sec:zap:4.6}

A case of negative transport that I have identified occurs in complement constructions, specifically with complement taking predicates that select for less finite complements. Less finite complements in TdVZ are those that are restricted to occur with the potential or counterfactual prefixes on their verbs, or they copy the aspectual marker of the main clause/predicate. Thus, negative transport occurs with achievement (e.g., {}-\textit{yexh} ‘dare’) and desiderative (e.g., {}-\textit{ka̰z} ‘want’) verbs, among others (see \citealt{Gutiérrez2021} for more information on this topic). In \REF{ex:zap:93} and \REF{ex:zap:94}, I show an example of negative transport with the predicates {}-\textit{yexh} ‘dare’ and {}-\textit{ka̰z} ‘want’ respectively.

\ea\label{ex:zap:93} 
    \glll Kēd.ˈgyéxh.dyan gú.nya.nnēn.\\ 
           kēd=g´-yexh=di=an g´-uny=an=ēn\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{pot}{}-dare=\textsc{neg=3sg.if} \textsc{pot}{}-do=\textsc{3sg.if=3sg.inan}\\
    \glt  ‘S/he won’t dare to do it.’
\z 

\ea\label{ex:zap:94} 
    \glll Kēd.ri.ˈka̰z.di ˈJwáyn ˈny\^{æ}’n ˈnis.\\ 
           kēd=ri-ka̰z=di Jwáyn ni´-æ’=an nis\\
          \textsc{neg}=\textsc{hab}{}-want=\textsc{neg} Juan \textsc{cntf}{}-drink=\textsc{3sg.if} water\\
    \glt ‘Juan didn’t want to drink water.’
\z

Also, I have shown that standard negation is syntactic \citep{Dahl1979} in TdVZ, and I have described the paradigmatic asymmetries that this construction exhibits. Therefore, I consider it to belong to type A/CAT/TAM in \citeauthor{Miestamo2005}’s categorization (\citeyear{Miestamo2005, Miestamo2007}).

Moreover, I have discussed other negative constructions that have not received much attention in Zapotec languages: a subtype of standard negation (i.e., ‘not yet’ expressions), existential negation and focus negation. All these constructions have different negators. However, the negator for ‘not yet’ expressions and the focus negator (\textit{gád}= and \textit{ád}= respectively) seem formally similar, but their function and scope are different: \textit{gád}= is used to negate clauses that express events that have not occurred while \textit{ád}= is used in focus constructions to mainly negate noun phrases. However, both types of negators occurs with \textit{=di} as a merged element or as a morpheme that occur after the negated element. On the other hand, the existential negator in TdVZ resembles the clausal negator but is not identical to it.
